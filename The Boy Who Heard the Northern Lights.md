# The Boy Who Heard the Northern Lights

*a novel*

---

> In 1859, the sky caught fire.
>
> A storm on the surface of the sun flung a wave of charged particles at the Earth, and for two nights the Northern Lights burned so brightly that people in Cuba read their newspapers by them at midnight, and telegraph machines sparked and chattered and printed out messages that nobody had sent.
>
> Nobody ever found out what those messages said.
>
> *— true, mostly*

---

## One

The first time I heard a voice come out of my Grandad's wireless, I was lying on the floor of his front room with a custard cream halfway to my mouth, and I was so surprised I bit my own thumb.

I'm telling you that bit first because my English teacher, Miss Okonkwo, says you should always *hook your reader*, and a boy biting his own thumb in fright is about as hooked as I can manage on page one. But it isn't really the start. The start is greyer than that. The start is a Tuesday in May, a wheelie suitcase with one dodgy wheel, and a town I had been to exactly twice in my whole life.

The town is called Skerrenby. You won't have heard of it, and I don't blame you. It sits on the Northumberland coast like something the sea spat out and then forgot to take back: a curve of grey houses round a stone harbour, a stubby white lighthouse on the point, an amusement arcade with half its bulbs dead, and a chip shop called the Harbour Lights that does the second-best chips I have ever eaten in my life. (The best are from Greggs in Gateshead, but Greggs does a sausage roll, not a chip, so it doesn't really count and I shouldn't have brought it up.)

I am from Gateshead. Or I *was*, until that Tuesday in May, when my whole life got picked up by the scruff and carried forty miles up the coast to a place where the Wi-Fi reaches about as far as your own front teeth.

Here is what happened, in order, because Miss Okonkwo also says you should *start at the start and not gallop off like a startled whippet*, which is a funny thing for her to say because she's never met my Grandad's dog.

My mam needed an operation.

I should say straight away that she is fine. She's fine, she's fine, she's going to be completely fine — I'm only telling you because I spent about three weeks being absolutely certain she was *not* going to be fine, and lying in the dark doing sums in my head about it, and I don't want to do that to you. So: she's fine. They were going to fix a valve in her heart. The doctor drew me a picture of it on a bit of paper, a little flappy door inside the heart that had stopped shutting properly, and he said, "We're just going to pop in and mend the hinge, Finlay, like a washing machine," which is a strange thing to say about a person's heart but I appreciated it, because a washing machine is a thing that gets mended all the time and then carries on washing for years.

The trouble was the *recovery*. Six weeks, minimum, of Mam not lifting anything heavier than a kettle, and a lad of twelve counting, apparently, as heavier than a kettle.

"There's only Grandad," Mam said. We were sitting in the kitchen and she had her hands wrapped round a mug of tea she wasn't drinking, which is how I knew it was serious. "I know it's not — I know you two have never really —"

"It's fine," I said.

"It's the summer term, you'll miss your mates—"

"It's *fine*, Mam."

It was not fine. But you say that, don't you. You say *it's fine* the way you put a custard cream in your mouth when there's nothing left to say.

The thing you have to understand about my Grandad Wilf is that I barely knew him. Here is the complete list of facts I had about my own grandfather, written out, because I like a list and you'll have to get used to it:

1. His name is Wilf, short for Wilfred, which he hates.
2. He used to work on ships. The radio on ships, sending messages in Morse code, dot dot dash. They called him *Sparks*. Everybody on a ship gets a daft name like that, apparently.
3. My Nana Eunice was married to him and she died when I was little and I don't remember her except for a smell of violet sweets.
4. He lives on his own in Skerrenby in a house full of stuff.
5. Him and my mam fell out about something years ago and never properly un-fell-out, and nobody would ever tell me what it was, which meant I had spent a fair bit of my childhood imagining it was something brilliant, like a bank robbery, when in fact it was probably about a sofa.

That was it. That was everything. And now I was going to live with him.

Dad, in case you're wondering, was not an option. Dad lives in Spain now, in a town with a name like a sneeze, with a woman called Carole and a jet-ski. He left when I was six. He sends me a message on my birthday that always says *Happy Birthday champ have a good one* with no full stops, as if full stops cost money in Spain. So. No. Not Dad.

We drove up the coast with my whole life in the boot, and the further north we went the smaller the world got, until the dual carriageway turned into a normal road and the normal road turned into a lane, and the lane went over a little bridge and there was Skerrenby, sat in its bay with the tide out and the mud shining like the back of a fish.

Grandad was waiting at the door.

I'd forgotten how big he was. Not fat — *built*, like something they used to make properly and don't any more. He had a face like a cliff that the weather had been working on for sixty-odd years, and hands the size of dinner plates, and standing next to his leg was the ugliest, skinniest, most miserable-looking dog I have ever seen.

"This is Bess," Grandad said. It was the first thing he said to me. Not *hello*, not *haway in*, just: "This is Bess."

Bess looked at me as if I'd come round to read the gas meter and she'd rather I didn't.

"She's a lurcher," he said. "She'll not bother you if you'll not bother her. That's the arrangement."

I learned later that this is basically the arrangement my Grandad has with the entire world.

Mam couldn't stay. She had to be back for the hospital, and there was a whole thing on the doorstep where she hugged me too hard and I could feel her heart going under my ear, that little broken door flapping away, and she said into my hair, "You be good for your Grandad," and then quieter, just for me, "and don't let him make you sad. He's not a sad man, he just looks like one." And then she got in the car and drove away down Coble Row, and I stood there with my dodgy-wheeled suitcase and a granddad I didn't know and a dog who hated me, and the wind came straight off the North Sea and went right through my coat like I wasn't wearing one.

"Right," said Grandad. "You'll want your tea."

---

Tea was fish, obviously. The house smelt of it, and of coal, and of something else underneath that I couldn't place — old, warm, electrical, like the inside of a telly. The whole place was crammed. Not dirty, just *full*: shelves bowing under books, a barometer, jars of screws, a stuffed seagull that I genuinely thought was alive for the first ten minutes, charts of the sea pinned over the wallpaper, and everywhere, *everywhere*, bits of radio. Valves and dials and coils of wire, and a soldering iron sitting in a saucer like it lived there.

And in the front room, taking up most of one wall, the biggest one of all.

It was a great wooden cabinet, taller than me, the colour of conkers, with a cloth-covered speaker and a row of buttons and a glowing dial across the front like a tiny lit-up city. It hummed. Even sitting there doing nothing, it gave off a low warm hum, and a glow, and that smell of warm dust I'd noticed on the doorstep.

"What's that?" I said.

"That," said Grandad, "is the wireless." He said it the way you'd say *that is the Queen*. "And you'll not touch it."

"I wasn't going to—"

"You'll *not touch it*," he said again, and there was something in his voice that made Bess look up from her basket. "Not the dial, not the buttons, not any of it. It's not a toy and it's not for kiddies. Are we clear?"

"We're clear," I said.

He looked at me for a long second. Then his face did something that was almost, *almost*, but not quite, a smile.

"There's a fair bit of me in you," he said. "More than your mam'd like, probably." And he went to put the kettle on.

I should have left it there. I want that on the record. I should have eaten my fish and gone to bed in the little cold room at the back with the model ship on the windowsill, and that would have been that.

But the thing is, while he was in the kitchen, I had a proper look round the front room, the way you do in a strange house when nobody's watching, touching nothing but looking at everything. And on the mantelpiece, above the gas fire, there was a row of photos in frames — Nana Eunice in a flowery dress, my mam aged about ten with a fringe like a helmet, a ship — and at the end, one frame that was lying face-down.

I don't know why I picked it up. I think I thought it had just fallen over.

It was a photo of a boy. He was about my age, maybe a bit younger, with a gap in his front teeth and ears that stuck out and a grin all over his face, and he was holding up a fish nearly as big as he was, on a beach I half-recognised, with the stubby Skerrenby lighthouse behind him on the point. On the white border at the bottom, in faded biro, someone had written one word.

*Sid.*

"Put that down."

I hadn't heard him come back. Grandad was in the doorway, and his face wasn't a cliff any more. It had gone grey and still, like the tide-flats outside, and his big hands were hanging at his sides, and he looked, for just a moment, like the saddest man in the entire world — which, you'll remember, my mam had told me he wasn't.

He came across the room and took the photo off me. Not roughly. Almost gently. He looked at it for a second, the boy and the fish and the grin, and then he opened a drawer in the sideboard and put it inside, face-down, and shut the drawer.

"Bed," he said, not looking at me. "School in the morning."

And that was the end of my first night in Skerrenby. But it was not the end of Sid. Not by a long way.

---

## Two

Skerrenby Middle School had forty-one pupils in the whole school and one of them was a goat.

That's not a joke. There was a goat, called Nigel, who lived in the bottom field and was technically a "wellbeing animal," and on my first morning I watched a boy in Year 6 give Nigel half a Twix through the fence, and I thought: *this is a different planet to Gateshead, and I am the alien.*

I'll be quick about the school, because school is school and you've got your own. I'll just say that being the new kid in a place that small is like being a new word that gets added to a language everybody already speaks. Everybody knows everybody. Everybody's grandad knew everybody's grandad. There were two lads who'd been best friends since they were *born*, in the actual same hospital, on the actual same day, and they finished each other's sentences, and I sat at the back and ate my sandwich and thought about my mates back home doing PE without me, and about my mam in a hospital bed with her heart held together by a hinge, and I did the thing where you look very hard at the table so your eyes have got an excuse.

And then a voice next to me said, "You're the Charlton lad. Wilf's grandbairn."

I looked up. The girl had braids with little gold beads on the ends, and a Skerrenby Middle jumper two sizes too big, and she was eating a packet of pickled-onion crisps in a way that suggested she'd happily fight you for them.

"It's Robson," I said. "Finn."

"Aye, but your *mam's* a Charlton," she said, as if I'd lost an argument. "Everybody's a something, round here. You can't get away with it. I'm Bex. My nana owns Neptune's." She said it proudly. When I looked blank she rolled her eyes. "The *arcade*. On the front. The one with the two-pence machines. You'll have walked past it, it's got a giant fibreglass Neptune on the roof who's lost his trident."

"Right," I said. "Yeah. I saw him."

"He looks like he's hitch-hiking, since the trident went," said Bex, and despite everything I laughed, an actual laugh, the first one in about a week, and Bex looked extremely satisfied with herself, like she'd been trying to get one out of me and had succeeded.

"I'm only here for the summer," she said. "Normally I'm in Newcastle but me mam's working away so I'm at me nana's. So we're the same, you and me. Blow-ins."

"Blow-ins?"

"Folk the wind blew in," she said. "Skerrenby's full of folk it blew in and never blew back out again. You'll see."

---

After school she walked me down to the front, which was on the way to nowhere but she said she'd nothing better to do, and I learned more about Skerrenby in twenty minutes than I'd have learned in a year on my own.

She showed me Neptune's, which smelled of warm copper and disappointment, and her nana, a tiny fierce woman called Mrs Okoro who gave me a free go on the grabber machine and watched me fail to win a unicorn with great sympathy. She showed me the harbour, where two old men were doing something complicated to a boat and stopped to watch us go past, the way old men do. She showed me the chip shop, the Harbour Lights, and the woman behind the counter, who was about a hundred, leaned over and said, "You'll be Wilf's, then," and gave me a free scoop of scraps, which are the crunchy bits of batter that fall off the fish, and which are, I have decided, the finest food in England.

And she showed me the lighthouse.

It sat out on the point where the land ran out, a stubby white tower with a black top, and the path to it ran along the top of the low cliffs with the sea on one side, smashing itself to bits on the rocks, and the town on the other. It was the exact spot, I realised, from the photo. The photo of the boy and the fish.

"My grandad's got a photo of a kid on this beach," I said, before I'd really decided to. "Holding a massive fish. It says *Sid* on it. But when I picked it up he went all weird and put it in a drawer."

Bex stopped walking.

"You didn't know?" she said.

"Know what?"

She looked at me, and then she looked out at the lighthouse, and then she looked back at me, and I could see her deciding whether to tell me, the way you do when a thing is a bit too big and a bit too good and you're not sure it's yours to hand over.

"You know the lights are coming," she said instead. "The Northern Lights. It's all over the space-weather, my nana's got an app. Biggest storm on the sun for twenty-odd years, they reckon. Everybody's going to be up the point with their phones." She paused. "Last time the lights came this strong over Skerrenby, properly strong, like the whole sky green — that was a long time before us. Nineteen-eighty-five."

The wind had got up. The lighthouse stood there saying nothing.

"There was a lad," said Bex, quiet now, all the cheek gone out of her. "A Skerrenby lad. Our age. The night the lights came, the big night, the whole town came out to watch them, right here on the point. And in the morning the lights had gone and so had he. They never found him. Not a shoe, not nothing. My nana was a young woman then and she still won't talk about it. They've got a name for it, the old folk." She lowered her voice even though there was no one for half a mile. "*The night the sky came down.*"

I felt something cold go down the back of my neck that had nothing to do with the wind off the sea.

"What was his name?" I said. But I think I already knew.

Bex looked at me, and something in my face must have answered the question before she did, because her eyes went wide.

"Sid," she said. "Sid Charlton."

*Charlton.*

Everybody's a something, round here. You can't get away with it.

---

I don't really remember walking back to Coble Row. I remember letting myself in, and the smell of warm dust and coal, and Bess lifting her head an inch off her basket to confirm it was only me and not worth getting up for.

I remember that the house was dark, which was strange, because it was only just getting dim outside.

And I remember the hum.

It was coming from the front room. I stopped in the hall. The door was open a crack, and through it I could see that the big wireless was *on* — its dial glowing that tiny-city glow, its speaker giving off a soft sea of static, sshhhhh, like the inside of a shell. And sitting in front of it, in the dark, in his armchair, not moving, was my Grandad Wilf.

He didn't have the volume up loud. He had it just loud enough. And he was leaning forward with his elbows on his knees and his head bowed, listening to the static the way you'd listen for a footstep on the path, and his big hands were folded together so tight the knuckles had gone white.

I must have made a sound. The floorboard, probably; the house was full of boards that grassed you up.

"You're back," he said, without turning round.

"Grandad," I said, "are you alright?"

For a while he didn't answer. The static washed in and out. Somewhere under it, far away, there was a thin high whistle that rose and fell, rose and fell, like a kettle a hundred miles off.

"Some nights," he said at last, so quietly I almost missed it, "it nearly comes through." Then he seemed to wake up. He reached out and pressed a button, and the glow died, and the hum died, and the static stopped, and the room was just a room again, an old man in the dark.

"Get yourself to bed, son," he said. "The lights are coming."

I went. But I lay awake a long time, in the cold little room with the model ship, listening to the sea, and thinking about a boy with a gap in his teeth who walked into the green and never walked back out, and a granddad who sat in the dark waiting for something to *come through*, and what on earth either of those things had to do with the great glowing wireless I'd been forbidden, on pain of death, to touch.

---

## Three

You'd think I'd have asked him. About Sid. About the wireless. About *some nights it nearly comes through*.

But you have to understand what my Grandad Wilf was like by then. Asking him a direct question about something that mattered was like trying to grab smoke. He'd go quiet, or he'd find a job that needed doing that very second, or he'd answer a completely different question instead, and you'd give up. So I didn't ask. I did the other thing, the thing I'm better at. I watched, and I waited, and I let him come to it sideways.

It took two days. Two days, and the wireless.

I'd caught him at it again the second night, and the third — sitting in front of the thing in the dark, listening, his head bowed. And on the fourth evening, when he caught *me* watching *him*, instead of sending me to bed he sighed, a big sigh that came all the way up from his boots, and he said: "Howay, then. Sit down. If you're going to gawp at it you may as well understand it."

So I sat down on the rug, and my Grandad Wilf, ship's radio operator, retired, taught me how a voice can travel further than it has any right to.

"Up there," he said, pointing at the ceiling, by which he meant the sky, "miles up, there's a layer of the air that's got a charge to it. Electrified. They call it the ionosphere. And the clever bit, the bit that fed my family for thirty year, is this." He held up one dinner-plate hand, flat. "A radio wave goes up, and instead of carrying on out into space and getting lost, it hits that layer and it *bounces*. Comes back down to the Earth, hundreds of miles away. Then it bounces *off the ground* and goes back up, and bounces again. Skip, they call it. A voice can skip its way clean round the world, if the conditions are right." He looked at the dial, fond. "On a good night on the *Carrall Star*, off the coast of Africa, I've sat in my little radio room and talked to a man in Whitley Bay clear as I'm talking to you. The sky was carrying us. People don't credit it. They think the sky's just *weather*."

"What makes it a good night?" I asked.

And this is the bit. This is the bit I should have paid more attention to.

"The sun," said Grandad. "It's all the sun. When the sun's quiet, the sky's quiet, and your voice goes where you point it and no further. But every so often — every eleven year or so, on a cycle, like the tides but bigger — the sun goes *mad*. Storms on it the size of a hundred Earths. Throws out a great gust of — call it weather, solar weather, a wind of bits-of-atoms — and a day or two later it hits us, and it lights up the sky, and it does *strange things* to that electric layer." He wasn't looking at me now. He was looking at the dark window, at the sky behind it. "Strange things to radio. Whole stations vanish. And other times — signals come through that have no business coming through. From too far. From — " he stopped.

"From where?" I said.

"From a long way off," he said. And that was the end of that, because his voice had gone thick, and he got up, suddenly busy, and put the kettle on, and I knew the door had shut.

But before he went, he said one more thing. He said it from the kitchen doorway, with his back to me, and I've thought about it every single day since.

"There was a fella," he said, "wrote it all down, the first one to see it happen. Eighteen-fifty-nine. Carrington, his name was. Biggest solar storm there's ever been recorded. The sky burned red as far south as the tropics, and the telegraph wires — that's the radio of its day, son, wires across the country — the telegraph wires sparked and ran *on their own*. The operators unplugged the batteries and the messages kept coming through. Powered by the sky itself." He paused. "Some of them messages, they reckon, came from no station at all. Words nobody'd sent." He shook his head, slow. "I never believed that. For sixty year I never believed that."

"And now?" I said.

He didn't answer. He just looked out at the sky, where, very faintly, right at the edge of seeing, the first thin curtain of green was beginning to shimmer up over the black line of the sea.

"The lights are here," he said.

---

The whole town went up the point.

It was the strangest, best thing I'd ever seen. Mrs Okoro shut Neptune's and came up with a flask. The two old men from the harbour came. The chip-shop woman came, and Bex, who found me in the dark and grabbed my sleeve and said "*Look at it, look at it*," and we all stood on the cliff in the cold with our heads tipped back, and the sky — the whole enormous sky over the North Sea — came *alive*.

It wasn't like the photos. Photos make it look solid, like paint. It wasn't solid. It *moved*. Great slow curtains of green, hanging down from the top of the sky, rippling, the way a net curtain moves in a draught, and through the green there were veins of pink and a deep dark red, and it all shifted and breathed and poured across the stars without a sound, and forty-one people and a goat called Nigel stood underneath it and forgot, for a bit, to be cold or sad or anything at all.

The old folk weren't taking photos. They'd gone quiet. And I noticed — I couldn't help noticing — that one or two of them weren't looking up at the lights at all. They were looking down. At the rocks below the point, where the sea was breaking. As if the lights reminded them of something they'd rather not be reminded of.

I looked for my grandad in the crowd. He wasn't there.

And I knew, the way you sometimes just *know* a thing, exactly where he was. He was home, in the dark front room, sitting in front of the wireless, with his big hands folded tight, listening to a sky gone mad and waiting for something to come through.

So I made an excuse to Bex, and I slipped away down the path, back to Coble Row, to be near him. That's all I meant to do. Be near him.

But the house was empty.

The front room was dark and silent. No hum. No glow. And no grandad. There was a note on the kitchen table in big shaky capitals: *GONE TO HELP GET MRS O'S FLASK BACK, BACK IN HALF HOUR, BEHAVE.*

I stood in the dark front room, on my own, with the green light pouring in through the window and lying across everything — across the stuffed seagull and the sea-charts and the drawer where a photo of a boy lay face-down — and across the great wooden wireless, glowing now with reflected sky.

*You'll not touch it.*

I want you to know I heard him say it. I heard it as clear as if he was stood next to me.

And then I reached out, in the green light, and I turned it on.

---

It took a minute to warm up. They do, the old valve ones; you can't just have them, you have to wait, the way you wait for a kettle. The dial lit up its little city. The hum came up through the floor and into my feet. And then the static arrived, that sea-sound, *sshhhhhhh*, filling the dark room.

I knelt down in front of it, the way he did. I didn't really know what I was doing. There was a big knob on the front for tuning, and I took hold of it, and very slowly, the way you'd open a door you weren't sure about, I began to turn.

The static changed as I turned it. It swelled and faded. A snatch of music went by, far away, and was gone. A man's voice, a few words in a language I didn't know, gone. The whistle I'd heard before, rising and falling. I went past it all, hardly breathing, hunting through the noise for I didn't know what, while the green light rippled across my hands.

And then I turned the dial one more notch — and under the hiss, faint, *so* faint, the way you hear your name from another room and aren't sure you heard it — there was a voice.

A girl's voice.

I froze. I turned the dial back a hair. The static parted, just for a second, the way fog parts, and the voice came through, thin and crackly and absolutely, definitely real, a girl about my own age, and she said:

"—anybody there? Hello? Is anybody — oh, this is *useless*—" a crackle, a hiss, and then, clear as a bell, half cross and half hopeful, into the dark of my grandad's front room, forty years too late: "*Hello? Can you hear me? Is somebody there? Over.*"

That's when I bit my own thumb.

---

## Four

I didn't answer her. Not at first. I knelt there in the green light with my heart going like a drum, staring at the glowing dial, and I genuinely wondered if I'd gone mad. People do, don't they. Maybe missing your mam and being shipped up the coast and standing under a sky gone mad just *does* that to a person.

The voice came again.

"*I know there's somebody, I can hear you breathing, you great wet weekend. If you're a lad from school doing a daft voice I'll batter you. Over.*"

There is something about being told off by a stranger that makes you answer whether you want to or not. It's a reflex. My mouth opened before my brain had voted.

"I'm not from your school," I said.

There was a silence. A long one, full of static. And then the voice came back, and it had changed — the crossness had dropped out of it, and what was left was a kind of careful astonishment, the same thing I was feeling, coming back at me through forty years of sky.

"*You're not Skerrenby,*" she said slowly. "*You don't talk Skerrenby. You talk Tyneside. Are you a ship? Are you one of the ships? Over.*"

"I'm not a ship." This was the maddest conversation I had ever had and we were about four lines into it. "I'm in a house. In Skerrenby. On Coble Row."

Another silence.

"*Don't be daft,*" she said. "*I'm on Coble Row.*"

And there it was. That cold thing down the back of my neck again, the same one I'd felt at the lighthouse, except this time it went all the way down to my feet.

"What number?" I said.

"*You first,*" she said, because she was clearly the kind of person who always made you go first, and I'd find out over the next however-long that she was exactly that kind of person, all the way through, like a stick of rock.

"Eleven," I said.

The static hissed. When she spoke again her voice was very small.

"*That's the Charltons' house,*" she said. "*That's Sid's house.*"

I gripped the dial so I wouldn't fall over, even though I was already kneeling down.

"Who's Sid?" I whispered.

"*Sid Charlton. Me best mate. We're going up the point the night to see the lights, the whole town's saying they'll be the best ever — here, who* are *you? Are you one of his cousins? You staying there? Where'd they get a radio? Mr Charlton'd never have a radio in the house, not since* —" she stopped herself. "*What's your* name*?*"

"Finn," I said. "What's yours?"

"*Maggie,*" she said. "*Maggie Dunn.*" And then, suspicious again: "*Finn what? There's no Finns at our school. There's no Finns in the whole of Skerrenby.*"

I didn't answer that. I'd just noticed something. It had been niggling at me all the way through, the way a word you can't spell niggles, and now it stepped right out into the open.

*We're going up the point the night to see the lights.*

But the lights were tonight. *My* tonight. And the whole town was up the point right now, with Bex and Mrs Okoro and a flask, and Nigel the goat. I'd just come down from there. I'd left them up there, under the green.

"Maggie," I said. My voice came out funny. "What's the date? Today. What's today's date?"

"*What kind of a question's that?*"

"Just — please. What's the date today?"

A pause. "*The thirteenth,*" she said, like I was simple. "*Saturday the thirteenth.*"

It wasn't Saturday. It was Thursday. I'd had double maths.

"What month?"

"*Are you having me on?*"

"*What month, Maggie?*"

"*July,*" she said. "*It's July, you numpty. It's flaming summer.*"

It was May. Outside the window of my grandad's front room it was the seventh of May and it was freezing and the daffodils were over. I could see them, dead, in the window box, lit up green.

I should have stopped. I knew, I think, what the next answer was going to be, and I knew that once I'd heard it I couldn't un-hear it, the way once you've seen the trick you can never see the magic again. But I had to. You always have to. That's the whole problem with being a person.

"Maggie," I said, very quietly, into a wireless I'd been forbidden to touch, in the dark, with the Northern Lights pouring over my hands. "What year is it?"

The static breathed in and out. A snatch of far-off music drifted by and was gone. And then Maggie Dunn, who was twelve years old and had braids — no, that was Bex — who I had never seen and never would, who was about to go up the point with her best mate to watch a sky gone mad, said, in a tone that told me she thought *I* was the one being thick:

"*It's nineteen-eighty-five,*" she said. "*Course it is. What year do you think it is?*"

---

I don't know how long I knelt there. Long enough for the green light to dim and brighten and dim again. Maggie was still talking — I could hear her, far away, saying "*Finn? Finn, are you there? You've gone all quiet, have I lost you?*" — but I couldn't make my mouth work.

Nineteen-eighty-five.

*Last time the lights came this strong over Skerrenby, that was a long time before us. Nineteen-eighty-five.*

*The night the sky came down.*

*And in the morning the lights had gone, and so had he.*

I was talking to a girl on the night Sid Charlton disappeared.

No. Not the night. Maggie said the thirteenth, Saturday the thirteenth of July. I didn't know the date Sid vanished, not yet, but I knew it was *the* big night, the once-in-a-lifetime night, the night the whole town came out — and that hadn't happened to Maggie yet. She was going *the night*, she said. Tonight, her tonight. So either this was the night, or — or it was the days *before*. The wireless had reached the *days before*.

Which meant it hadn't happened yet.

Which meant —

"Finn?" Maggie's voice, sharp with worry now, fading as the static rose. "*Finn, I think I'm losing you, the lights are mucking it up, me dad says the lights wreck the radio — Finn, are you a ghost? You're not a ghost, are you? Because I'll tell you now, I don't believe in—*"

"Maggie," I said. My voice cracked right down the middle. "Maggie, listen. Don't —"

But the green outside the window was fading. The great curtains were thinning, drawing back up into the top of the sky, and as they went the static swelled and rose and swallowed her voice, *sshhhhhhhhh*, louder and louder, until there was nothing left in the speaker but the sea-sound of a sky going quiet, and Maggie Dunn was gone, forty years away, with a warning still stuck in my throat that I never got to give.

And from the hallway, behind me, in a voice I had never heard him use, my Grandad Wilf said:

"*What have you done.*"

---

## Five

He was standing in the doorway with the green light dying on his face, and he was looking at the wireless, and then at me, and then at the wireless, and his big hands were shaking.

I'd never seen a grown man frightened before. Not really. You see them cross, you see them upset, but you don't see them *frightened*, not the proper white kind, and seeing it on my own grandad was about the worst thing that had happened in a week that had a lot of competition.

"I told you," he said. "I told you never. *Never.* Did you—" he could hardly get the words out. "Did you *hear* something?"

And I should have lied. I should have said no, Grandad, it was just static, I was only fiddling, I'm sorry. That's what a sensible boy does. But I was twelve and I was tired and I had just spoken to someone who'd been dead, or gone, or *something*, for forty years, and it all came up out of me at once like sick.

"There was a girl," I said. "Maggie. Maggie Dunn. She's on Coble Row. She's twelve. She said it's nineteen-eighty-five and she's going up the point tonight to see the lights with her best mate and her best mate is *Sid* — Grandad, she said his best mate is *Sid Charlton* and he lives in *this house* and—"

I stopped.

Because my Grandad Wilf had reached out and taken hold of the doorframe to keep himself standing up.

All the colour had gone out of him. He looked at me as if I'd reached into his chest and taken something out and shown it to him, and for a long, long moment the only sound in the whole house was Bess's claws on the kitchen lino as she got up, uneasy, and came and pressed herself against his leg.

"Maggie Dunn," he said. Barely a whisper. "Little Maggie Dunn, with the plaits. She used to sit on our wall." His eyes had gone somewhere a long way off. "She moved away, after. Couldn't stand it. None of us could stand it." He looked at me. "She'll be an old woman now, son. Older than me. If she's still—"

"But on the wireless she's *twelve*," I said.

"Aye," he said. "Aye." And he closed his eyes.

---

He told me, in the end. We sat in the dark, the two of us, with the wireless cold and silent between us and the dog at his feet, and he told me. It came out slow and rusty, like a tap that hasn't been turned in years, and I didn't say a word the whole time, because I'd finally, *finally* learned that the way to get smoke is to stop grabbing at it.

Sid was his little brother.

There were twelve years between them, which is a lot, which is nearly the same as me and a baby, and Wilf had half-raised him, because their dad worked away and their mam was poorly. Wilf taught Sid to fish off the point. Wilf taught Sid Morse code, dot dot dash, tapping it out on the kitchen table. And then Wilf had gone to sea himself, *Sparks* on the *Carrall Star*, off the coast of Africa, because that's what you did, there was nothing else, and he'd left his little brother behind with a promise to bring him something back from every port.

He was three thousand miles away the night the sky came down.

"It was the storm of the century," Grandad said. "Bigger than this one, even. The whole town went up the point. And our Sid — he loved the radio, see, he'd got it off me, he loved the *idea* of it, voices coming out of nowhere from the other side of the world. And he'd heard — somebody'd told him, some daft tale — that on a night like that, with the sky like that, you could hear *anything* on the radio. Voices from anywhere. From *any* — " his voice caught. "He had this little set he'd built himself. From bits I'd sent him. And he didn't go up the point with the rest. They found his set up there the next morning, switched on, the batteries dead flat. But they never found *him*. Not that day. Not ever." He stopped. "Forty year. Forty year I've sat with this thing, on every storm, every aurora, turning the dial, because if our Sid found a voice that night — if he heard something come through — then maybe — "

He couldn't finish it. He didn't have to. *Some nights it nearly comes through.* He'd been sitting in the dark for forty years, waiting to hear his little brother's voice come back through a sky gone mad.

And now his grandbairn had turned the dial, and got Maggie Dunn instead. Maggie Dunn, who in nineteen-eighty-five was *alive*, and *twelve*, and going up the point — and whose best mate, Sid Charlton, was *still here*. Still in the days before. Still, somewhere on the other side of the static, switching on his little homemade set on the point and waiting to hear a voice come through from the other side of the world.

A voice from anywhere.

A voice from *any time*.

I sat very still in the dark, and I felt the whole thing turn over inside me like a key in a lock.

"Grandad," I said. "She said tonight. *Her* tonight. The big night. But I don't think it's happened yet. I think the wireless reached the *days before*. Maggie was going to see the lights tonight but she didn't say it like it was the worst night of her life. She said it like — like it was just a brilliant night coming up." I was talking faster and faster. "What if it's not nineteen-eighty-five *the night Sid vanished*? What if it's nineteen-eighty-five a few days *before*? What if there's still—"

"Don't," said Grandad. And his voice wasn't angry. It was worse than angry. It was the voice of a man who has hoped so hard for so long that he's not sure he can survive being made to hope again. "Don't you do that. Don't you say it."

But it was already said. It was already out in the room with us, in the dark, glowing like a dial.

If the wireless could reach the days before — if it would warm up again, the next time the sun went mad and the sky came down — then somewhere on the other side of all that static was a gap-toothed boy with ears that stuck out, who hadn't vanished yet, switching on a little radio he'd built himself, listening for a voice from anywhere.

And I knew his name. And I knew his street. And I knew, now, exactly which night he must never, ever go up the point.

I just had to be on the wireless when it counted. I had to find out when. And the trouble — the enormous, ticking, impossible trouble — was that you can't *order* the sky to go mad. The aurora comes when it comes, once in a blue moon, once in a lifetime, on the sun's own clock and nobody else's, and tonight's was fading even as we sat there, drawing back up into the dark over the North Sea, taking Maggie's voice with it, taking my chance with it, until the next time. Whenever the next time was.

It had to be soon. In Maggie's world the big night was *coming*. The clock was already running in nineteen-eighty-five, and I had no way to stop it and no way to know how many days were left.

Outside the window, the very last thread of green shivered once over the sea, and went out, and the stars came back, ordinary and cold.

My Grandad Wilf put his enormous hand over his face, and his shoulders shook, just once, and Bess pressed in against him, and I sat on the rug in the dark next to the great cold wireless and made a promise to a boy I had never met and a granddad who couldn't speak.

I was going to bring Sid Charlton home.

I just had to wait for the sky to come down again — and pray it came down in time.


---

## Six

I did not sleep, in case you were wondering.

I lay in the cold little room with the model ship and I went over it and over it. A girl in 1985. A boy who hadn't vanished yet. A great-uncle I'd never been told about, the same age as me, switching on a homemade radio on a night that was coming for him like a tide.

In the morning Grandad was at the kitchen table with a pot of tea gone cold beside him, and Bess's chin on his knee, and the drawer with the photo in it open.

He'd taken Sid out and stood him up against the milk jug. The boy and the fish and the grin.

"Sit down," he said. He poured me a tea I didn't want. For a while he didn't say anything, just looked at the photo. Then: "You'll have questions."

"About a thousand," I said.

"You'll get one a day," he said. "I can't do more than that. It's like — " he turned his big hands over, looking for the word. "It's like lifting. You can't lift everything at once or you'll do your back."

So I picked my one carefully.

"Can the wireless only do it when the lights are out?"

He nodded slowly. "Aye. That's my reckoning, for what it's worth, and I've had forty year to reckon. It's the storm. The sun throws its weather at us, the sky goes — wrong, somehow, *open* — and the set can reach where it can't normally reach." He tapped the table. "No lights, no road. The rest of the time it's just a wireless, picking up the shipping forecast and Radio 2 and nowt that matters."

I thought about that. About the great curtains drawing back up into the dark last night, taking Maggie with them.

"Then I need to know when," I said, half to myself.

"When what?"

"When Sid goes." I made myself look at him. "If I know the exact night, then when the lights come, I can — I can try to —"

"Don't," he said again. But it was a tired *don't* this time, a *don't* with no fight in it, and his eyes had gone wet, and we both knew it was already too late for don't.

That was the morning my Grandad Wilf and I stopped being two strangers in a house and became something else. I'm not sure what. Two people standing at the edge of the same impossible thing, maybe, working out whether to jump.

---

## Seven

I told Bex everything at second break, behind the bins where Nigel the goat couldn't earwig.

She didn't believe me. Obviously. I wouldn't have believed me. She stood there with her arms folded and her braids whipping in the wind and she said, "Right. So you're telling me. That your grandad's got a *magic radio*. That talks to the *olden days*."

"Not magic. The ionosphere—"

"You've been up here five minutes," she said, "and the sea air's got into your head. It happens. My nana says it sends folk doolally."

So I told her the bit she didn't know. The bit nobody knew except me and a girl forty years away. I told her Maggie Dunn said Sid's house was Number Eleven Coble Row — Grandad's house — and that Sid Charlton was Grandad's little brother, and the whole town had a name for the night he vanished, and that name was —

"*The night the sky came down,*" Bex said, very quietly. The arms came uncrossed.

"You said it yourself," I said. "On the cliff. You told *me* about it. I didn't know any of it. How would I make up the name your nana won't say?"

The wind blew. Somewhere on the field, Nigel said *meh*.

"Show me," said Bex. "The next time the lights come. You show me, or I'll tell the whole school you talk to ghosts and you'll never live it down, not in a town this size, you'll be Ghost Lad till you're ninety."

"Deal," I said.

And just like that, there were two of us.

---

## Eight

Here is a thing about small towns: nothing ever gets thrown away.

The library in Skerrenby is one room in the back of the community centre, open Tuesdays and Thursdays, run by a man called Mr Fenwick who is roughly the same age as the building. But in a cupboard, under a dust sheet, behind a broken accordion, Bex knew there was a machine for reading old newspapers off little reels of film, because she'd hidden behind it during a wet-play once.

It took Mr Fenwick twenty minutes to find the reels for 1985, and he kept stopping to tell us things, the way old men do, but eventually the machine whirred and a page of the *Skerrenby & District Courier* swam up out of the dark, black and grey, the print all wobbly.

We wound forward. Spring. Summer. Adverts for things that don't exist any more. A photo of the carnival. And then —

Bex stopped winding.

The headline filled half the front page. I'm not going to write it out, because writing it out felt like saying a swear word in church. But it had Sid's name in it, and the word *MISSING*, and a date.

The thirtieth of August, 1985.

The night of the greatest display of the Northern Lights anyone in Skerrenby had ever seen, the paper said. The whole town up on the point. And in the photograph, grainy and grey, a gap-toothed boy with ears that stuck out, grinning, holding a fish.

The same photo. The exact same photo that was sitting against the milk jug at home.

I worked it out on my fingers, there in the dark of the library, with Bex breathing beside me.

Maggie was talking to me on the thirteenth of July. The thirtieth of August was forty-eight days after that.

Forty-eight days. That was all the time Sid Charlton had left. And it was already ticking.

---

## Nine

Mrs Okoro had an app on her phone for the lights. It had a needle on a dial, like a speedometer, that told you how stirred-up the sky was, and a forecast, and a little alarm she'd set so she'd never miss a show.

I made her show me, and I watched the dial, and my heart sank.

Because here is the cruel bit, the bit I hadn't understood. The aurora doesn't run on a timetable. It runs on the sun, and the sun doesn't care about a boy and a forty-eight-day countdown. The big one last week had been a once-in-twenty-years monster. After it, the dial dropped right down into the green calm and *stayed* there, flat as the tide-flats, day after day after day.

I could only talk to 1985 when the lights came. And the lights might not come again for weeks. Or months. Or — the thought arrived in the night like cold water — they might not come strong enough, in time, ever again.

I had forty-eight days in 1985 to save Sid Charlton.

And I had no idea how many of them the sky was going to give me.

---

## Ten

The next aurora came nine days later, and it was a weak one. Mrs Okoro's app barely twitched. Bex was at her cousin's in Newcastle and missed it, which she has never fully forgiven me for, and Grandad was asleep in his chair, so it was just me and the wireless and a thin smear of green over the sea that you'd have missed if you didn't know to look.

I warmed the set up. I found the place on the dial. And under the hiss, faint and wobbling, there she was.

"*Finn? Is that you, you weird lad? Where'd you go last time? You just disappeared—*"

I nearly said it. I had it right there on my tongue. *Maggie, listen, on the thirtieth of August Sid is going to die, you have to stop him.*

But I'm glad I didn't, and I'll tell you why. Imagine a stranger's voice comes out of your radio, a voice with a funny accent that claims to be from the future, which on the face of it is the daftest thing in the world — and the very second thing it ever says to you is that your best friend is going to be killed in seven weeks. You wouldn't believe it. You'd be *frightened* of it. You'd switch the radio off and never switch it on again, and you'd tell your mam there was a horrible man on the CB, and that would be the end of the only road I had to Sid.

So I bit my tongue, and instead I said, "Sorry. The lights went. I can only talk when the lights are out — that's how it works. Did you see them, on the night? The thirteenth?"

"*Course I did, the whole town did. They were canny but me dad says they've been better.*" A crackle. "*Here, are you really from — you said — you weren't having me on?*"

"What's the biggest pop star in the world right now?" I said.

She told me. I'd never heard of him.

There was a silence then, a long one, and when Maggie spoke again her voice had gone small and amazed, the way mine must have gone, and she said, "*You really don't know who that is. Everybody knows who that is.*"

"Not where I am," I said. "Not when I am."

The green flickered and began to die.

"Maggie," I said quickly, "next time, can you bring Sid? To the radio?"

"*Why d'you want our Sid?*"

The lights were going. I had a second, maybe two.

"Because he's the one who'll understand it," I said, which was true, and not the whole truth, but it was all I had time for before the static rose up and swallowed her.

---

## Eleven

That was my Grandad-question for the next day, and the day after, and the day after that, because once he started on Sid he couldn't always stop.

Sid was a Tuesday's child, he said, which is "full of grace," though Grandad said the only graceful thing about Sid was the way he could skim a flat stone — eleven bounces, the town record, never beaten. Sid collected birds' eggs and then felt bad and tried to put them back. Sid could do impressions of every teacher at the school and one of the Prime Minister. Sid was frightened of moths and not of the sea, which Grandad said was exactly the wrong way round.

And Sid loved the radio.

"It was my fault, that," Grandad said, not like it was a confession, just like it was a fact. "I'd send him bits home from the ports. A valve here, a coil there. He built himself a little set on the kitchen table out of a cigar box and a bedspring and whatever I sent him, and the first time he got a voice out of it — some fella in Norway, going on about fish prices — he ran the length of Coble Row in his pyjamas to tell the whole street." He smiled, and the smile had a crack in it. "He used to say, our Sid, that the best thing about the radio was you never knew who'd come out of it. 'A voice from anywhere, Wilf,' he'd say. 'Could be anybody. Could be anywhere.'"

He stopped.

"He didn't know how right he was," he said. "A voice from anywhere. He never reckoned on a voice from *anywhen.*"

And he got up to see to the dog, even though the dog didn't need seeing to, which is a thing my Grandad does when his face is about to give him away.

---

## Twelve

That night I couldn't settle, and I worked something out, lying in the dark, and it landed in my stomach like a stone.

Maggie didn't believe me. Maggie thought I was a weird lad on the CB. I could spend my forty-eight days slowly, carefully convincing Maggie I was real — and even then, what was she going to do? March up to Sid and say a voice from the future told her he was going to die? Sid would laugh. Everyone would laugh.

There was only one person in 1985 who might actually believe a voice from anywhere, because he'd been waiting his whole short life to hear one.

Sid himself.

I had to talk to Sid. Not Maggie. Sid. The boy with the cigar-box radio, who ran down the street in his pyjamas because a voice came out of nowhere. He'd believe me. He was *built* to believe me.

But every single time the sky had opened, I'd got Maggie. Never Sid. As if the wireless had picked its own line and stuck to it.

I lay there and stared at the ceiling, at the dark where the sky was, and I thought: *How do I reach a boy who's been dead for forty years, in time to stop him dying?*

I didn't have an answer. But I had forty-seven days to find one. And the sun, that great mad furnace ninety-three million miles away, had no idea I was even waiting.

---

## Thirteen

Grandad's old logbooks were in a tin trunk under his bed, and he let me read them, which Bex said was a bigger deal than I knew.

They were full of his neat sloping handwriting and columns of numbers and times and call-signs, the diary of a man whose job was talking across an ocean. And folded inside the front of one was a yellowed sheet of paper covered in dots and dashes, with a boy's clumsier hand underneath turning them into letters.

"That's Sid's," Grandad said, looking over my shoulder. "His Morse. I was teaching him by post. I'd send a message in code in my letters home and he'd have to crack it and send the answer back." He touched the paper, light as anything. "He got fast. Faster than me, by the end."

I learned the code that week. Bex and I both did, tapping it out on the dinner table, *dit-dit-dit, dah-dah-dah, dit-dit-dit*, until Mr Fenwick at the library asked us to stop because we were upsetting the accordion.

I didn't fully know why I was learning it. I just had a feeling — the kind of feeling you get when a thing is going to matter and you don't yet know how — that a code two brothers used to talk across an ocean might one day need to talk across something a lot wider than that.

---

## Fourteen

The lights came back the following Friday, stronger this time, a proper green flush over the whole northern sky, and Bex was there, and that's the night she stopped being able to call me Ghost Lad.

She knelt next to me in the dark front room and she watched me warm up the wireless, and she heard the static, and she watched me find the place on the dial — and then a voice, a girl's voice, forty years old and clear as cold water, said, "*Finn? You there?*" — and Bex sat back on her heels with her hand over her mouth and her eyes like dinner plates, and she didn't say a single word for about a minute, which for Bex is a personal best.

"Maggie," I said, "I've got a friend here. She's called Bex."

"*A lass?*" said Maggie, instantly interested. "*Put her on, then.*"

So Bex leaned in, shy as anything, which I'd never seen, and said "...hiya," and Maggie said "*hiya*" back, and the two of them, forty years apart, talked for a minute about absolutely nothing — about hair, and a teacher, and whether the chips at the Harbour Lights were still the best in the world — and it was, I think, the strangest and nicest thing I have ever heard.

But Maggie's voice had a weight under it that night. She told us, without making a thing of it, that her dad had lost his job — "*the pit shut, didn't it, they all shut, after the strike*" — and that things were "*tight*" at home, and that her and Sid spent half their time out of the house and down the harbour because it was easier than being in. She said it light, the way you say *it's fine*. But I heard it.

There was a whole world down that radio. Not just Sid. A whole grey, hard, brilliant world that I was starting, against all sense, to love.

---

## Fifteen

I want to put one good bit in here before things get heavy, because they do get heavy, and because the good bits are the ones that broke my heart in the end.

Over the auroras that came that fortnight — and they came kindly, that fortnight, three of them, as if the sky was on our side for a while — me and Maggie just *talked*. About her world and mine.

She could not get her head round phones. "*You're telling me,*" she said, "*everyone walks round with a telephone in their pocket. A telephone. With no wire. And it's also a camera. And a telly. And it can answer any question in the world.*" A long pause. "*And you use it to look at — what did you say — videos of cats?*"

"Mostly, yeah."

"*The future is wasted on you lot,*" said Maggie Dunn, and I laughed so hard I had to hold the dial.

And I couldn't get my head round hers. No internet. Three telly channels and they all stopped at midnight. You had to be *in* when a programme was on or you missed it forever. If you wanted to know a thing you went to a library or you just — didn't know it. If a friend moved away you wrote them a *letter*, on paper, and waited a *week*.

"*How d'you ever find anyone?*" I asked.

"*You just — knew where they'd be,*" she said, like it was obvious. "*You knew everyone. You all just — were where you were.*"

I thought about my mates back in Gateshead, who I could message any second of any day and somehow never really talked to.

"*I think,*" I said slowly, "*we might've lost something, getting the cats.*"

"*Aye,*" said Maggie. "*Well. You can come and live in 1985 if you want. We've got better music.*"

I would have, you know. By then. I really would have.

---

## Sixteen

And then, on a wild green night at the end of my third week, the sky gave me what I'd been asking for.

I warmed the set. I found the place. And the voice that came out of the dark was not Maggie.

It was a boy. Quick, and bright, and breathless, with the same accent as Maggie but somehow more *up*, like a kettle just before it whistles, and it said:

"*Is it you? Is it you, the future lad? Maggie said — she said there's a voice on our wavelength reckons it's from years off, and I said she's having me on, she's got me up at midnight for a wind-up, but it's not a wind-up, is it? Say something. Say something only a future person would know.*"

My heart was going so hard I could barely speak.

"Hello, Sid," I said.

A silence. A long one. Just the static, breathing.

"*You know me name,*" said Sid Charlton, and his voice had changed — gone soft and amazed, the exact way mine had, the exact way Maggie's had, the way everybody's does, I think, the first time the impossible turns out to be true. "*You said me name. How d'you know me name?*"

"Because everybody round here is a something," I said, "and you can't get away with it."

And forty years away, in a kitchen on Coble Row, with a cigar-box radio he'd built with his own hands, my great-uncle Sid started to laugh — a great delighted whoop of a laugh, the laugh of a boy who has waited his whole life for a voice from anywhere and has just, finally, beautifully, got one.

I'd found him.

Now I just had to keep him alive.

---

## Seventeen

I didn't tell him. Not that night.

I lay awake afterwards arguing with myself about it. Part of me said: tell him *now*, you've got him, you've not got long, every wasted aurora is a night gone — *tell him.*

But I kept hearing Grandad. *You can't lift everything at once or you'll do your back.* And I kept thinking about what it would do to a kid — to a brilliant, breathless, eleven-bounces, frightened-of-moths kid — to be told by a disembodied voice that he had a date stamped on him like a sell-by.

He'd believe me. That was the trouble. Sid would believe me completely, and the fear would get into him, and I'd seen what forty years of that fear had done to my Grandad. I wasn't going to do it to Sid until I had a *plan*. Until I could say not just *you're going to die* but *and here is exactly how we stop it.*

And I didn't have that yet. I knew the date. I didn't know how. The newspaper said *missing*, not *how*. Up on the point, the whole town there, and then gone, like a stone into deep water.

To save him I had to know what I was saving him *from*.

So that night I just talked to Sid like a mate. I asked him about the radio. I asked him about the eleven-bounce stone. I let him ask me whether we had flying cars yet (we don't; he was disgusted) and whether we'd been back to the moon (we hadn't; more disgusted). I made a friend of the great-uncle I never had.

And the whole time, behind my teeth, I held the terrible date, the thirtieth of August, like a hot coal I wasn't ready to put down.

---

## Eighteen

I made my first big mistake on day twenty-five, and I want you to learn from it so you never make it yourself, in case you ever get a magic radio, which, statistically, you won't, but still.

I wanted to *test* it. To know, for certain, that you could change the past — that this whole mad effort wasn't pointless. Because what if you couldn't? What if it was all fixed, like a film you can shout at but can't alter?

So I picked something small. Something that couldn't possibly matter. Sid had mentioned, laughing, that his mate Banger Bell was going to ride his bike off the harbour ramp on Saturday for a dare and would "*definitely come a cropper.*"

"Tell Banger not to do the ramp," I said. "Tell him a voice from the future said he'll break his arm." I thought it'd be funny. I thought it'd prove the point. A broken arm, un-broken. Where's the harm?

Next aurora, Sid was buzzing. "*It worked! I told Banger and he bottled it, and his cousin did the ramp instead and went straight over the bars, proper yard-sale, so Banger reckons you saved his life and now he wants to talk to you an' all—*"

I should have been pleased. But that morning, when I'd gone to the library, Mr Fenwick had said, sad, that he'd be shutting early all week, because his old friend down the coast had had a fall and broken his hip and he was off to see him in hospital. And Mr Fenwick's friend's name, he mentioned, in passing, the way old men do, was Bell. Used to be a tearaway, in his day. They called him Banger.

Banger Bell didn't break his arm at twelve. His cousin broke his collarbone instead, and grew up frightened of bikes, and never became the cycling instructor he'd otherwise have been, and so a hundred kids never learned road safety from him, and — I don't know. I don't know all of it. That's the *point*. You never know all of it. You pull one thread and the whole jumper moves and you can't see where.

I'd changed the past to save a boy from a broken arm. And somewhere down the line, decades away, that change had reached up and broken an old man's hip.

I sat in the library and I felt sick to my stomach.

---

## Nineteen

"You can't just *do* things," Bex said. We were on the harbour wall, the tide coming in below us, slap and suck against the stone. "You can't just pull threads to see what happens."

"I know that now."

"Every little thing you change, it's like —" she chucked a pebble in, and the rings spread out and out until they hit the wall and came back, all tangled. "It doesn't stop. It just goes."

"I *know*, Bex."

"So when it comes to Sid," she said, and her voice went serious, the cheek all gone, "you don't get to make it up as you go. You change *one thing*. The smallest thing that saves him. You change one thing, on purpose, knowing why — and you change nothing else. Promise me."

"I promise."

It was a good promise. It was the right promise. The trouble, as you'll see, is that on the night, with the sea coming in and the sky on fire, *one thing* turns out to be the hardest number in the world to stop at.

That night Grandad caught me at the wireless and we had our worst row. He'd come down for a glass of water and found me on my knees in front of the set with the green light on my face, mid-sentence with a dead boy, and something in him just went.

"I told you NEVER—"

"He's your *brother*, Grandad—"

"You think I don't KNOW that?" he roared, and Bess shot under the table, and the whole house seemed to flinch. "You think I haven't sat where you're sitting? Forty year, son. Forty year of nearly. It's not a toy. It's not an adventure. It's a *wound*, and you've gone and stuck your hand right in it—"

"Then let me FIX it!" I shouted back. And the house went quiet.

He stood there in his pyjamas, this huge old man, breathing hard, with the dead light of the dial on him.

"You can't fix it," he said at last, and all the roar had gone out of him, and what was left was worse. "Nobody can fix it. I'd have fixed it in a heartbeat. I'd have given anything." He turned to go. "Don't break your heart on it, son. That's all I'm saying. Don't do what I did and break your heart on a thing that won't break."

And he went up the stairs slow, one at a time, holding the rail.

---

## Twenty

He came down again twenty minutes later.

I was still on the floor. I hadn't moved. The wireless had gone quiet — the aurora had faded — and I was just sitting there in the dark feeling about two inches tall.

He sat down in his chair with a great sigh, and Bess crept back out and put her chin on his knee, and for a long time he didn't say anything. Then:

"I was off Lagos," he said. "When it happened. Three thousand mile away. We got the cable four days late — they had to find which ship I was on, see, and signal it, and it all took time, back then." He stared at the cold dial. "Four days, our Sid had been missing, before I even *knew*. I was sat in my little radio room talking to the whole world and I never heard the one thing I'd have given my arm to hear." His voice cracked right across. "I always thought — if I'd been *home*. If I'd been here, instead of chasing the sea. He'd have come to me. He came to me with everything. I'd have known he was up to something. I'd have stopped him."

"It wasn't your fault, Grandad."

"No," he said. "It never is, is it. And it always is." He looked at me, and his eyes were wet and old and terribly kind. "I'm not angry at you, son. I'm angry at the sky. I've been angry at the sky for forty year and it's never once noticed." He reached out and, awkward, like a man who's forgotten how, he put his huge hand on the top of my head, just for a second. "If you're going to do this daft, impossible thing," he said, "then God help me, you're not doing it on your own. I know that set better than any man alive. And I know that boy." He took his hand back. "We'll do it together, or not at all. Agreed?"

"Agreed," I said, and my voice didn't work properly either.

That's the night it stopped being my secret mission and became *ours*. Me, and Bex, and a heartbroken old man, and a dog, against the sea and the sky and forty years.

I fancied our chances a bit more after that. Not much. But a bit.

---

## Twenty-One

I have to put Mam in here, because the whole time this was going on, she was forty miles away with her chest held shut by little wire stitches, and I'd be lying if I said the radio crowded her out. It didn't. She was under everything, like the hum under the static.

She rang every evening on the house phone, the proper old phone in the hall with the curly wire, because my mobile got no signal at Grandad's, which I'd started to be glad of. She sounded tired but herself. She'd ask about school and I'd tell her about Nigel the goat and she'd laugh, and I'd not tell her about the wireless, obviously, because what would I even say.

Then one evening she didn't ring at the usual time, and Grandad and I sat looking at the phone, and it didn't ring, and it didn't ring, and I felt that old cold thing climbing up my back — the doing-sums-in-the-dark thing — and Grandad, watching my face, said quietly, "It'll be nowt. They keep them in late for all sorts."

She rang at nine. There'd been a thing — a "*little setback*," she called it, in the carefully light voice, the *it's fine* voice — her heart had gone too fast for a bit and they'd had to give her something and watch her, and she was fine now, she was *fine*, but they were keeping her in longer to be sure.

I sat on the bottom stair after, in the dark.

And here is the thing I understood, sitting there, that I hadn't understood before. I knew exactly how Grandad felt. Exactly. Because I had a person whose heart might stop, miles away where I couldn't reach, and there was nothing — *nothing* — I could do but sit by a wire and wait for a voice.

That's all he'd had. For forty years. A wire, and a wait, and a voice that never came.

"Grandad," I said into the dark.

"Aye, son."

"We have to save him."

"Aye," said my Grandad Wilf, from his chair. "I know. Now more than ever, I know."

---

## Twenty-Two

Here is what I knew, and didn't know, on day twenty-eight, with twenty days left in 1985 and the sun keeping its own counsel.

I knew the date Sid vanished. The thirtieth of August.
I knew where: up the point, the whole town there, watching the greatest lights anyone ever saw.
I did *not* know how. *Missing.* Gone into the dark like a stone.

And you can't save a boy from a danger you can't name. I could tell Sid not to go up the point at all — but the whole town was going up the point; how do you keep a curious eleven-bounce kid away from the greatest sky in living memory? And worse — the cold thought that kept coming back — what if it wasn't the point that took him? What if it was something I couldn't see, and by keeping him from the point I just moved it somewhere else, the way I'd moved Banger Bell's broken arm onto an old man's hip?

I needed to *know*. I needed someone who was *there*, on the night, who saw, who remembered — someone who'd carried that night for forty years and could finally set it down in front of me.

There was exactly one person in the whole world like that.

Maggie Dunn was twelve, on the radio. But Maggie Dunn was also — if she'd lived, if the lights hadn't taken her too — an old woman now. Sixty-seven. Somewhere. Out there in my own time, walking around, with the worst night of her life folded up inside her.

"Bex," I said, the next morning, behind the bins. "We have to find Maggie. The *old* Maggie. The one who's still alive now."

Bex looked at me for a long moment, the wind in her braids.

"You want to find a woman," she said slowly, "who's been carrying this since before either of us was born. And ask her about the worst thing that ever happened to her."

"Yeah," I said. "I do."

---

## Twenty-Three

Finding a person who doesn't want to be found is hard. Finding a person who's spent forty years trying not to be reminded of something is harder. But Skerrenby has a secret weapon, and her name is Mrs Okoro, and she knows everything about everyone, and so does the hundred-year-old woman at the Harbour Lights, whose name, it turned out, was Vi.

"Dunn," said Vi, going still over the fryer. "Maggie Dunn." She looked at me a long time, the way they all do. "You'll be asking about *that*, then. The Charlton business. It's all coming up at once, this summer. Like the sea coughing it back."

She told us what she knew. Maggie Dunn's family moved away the spring after, because the mother couldn't pass the point any more without coming apart, and you can't live in Skerrenby and not pass the point. They went inland first. Then Maggie grew up, and married a joiner called Eddie Surtees, and they'd come back up the coast eventually — folk do, the sea calls them — and she was widowed now, and living, last Vi heard, in the sheltered flats at Warkhaven, two towns up, the ones by the church.

"She kept herself to herself," said Vi. "Wouldn't ever come to Skerrenby, all those years. Not for owt." She wrapped our chips. "If you're going to see her, son, you go gentle. That lass loved that lad like a brother. Worse than a brother. And she's never put it down."

---

## Twenty-Four

We got the bus to Warkhaven on the Saturday. Grandad wanted to come and then didn't — "She'll not want a great lump like me looming. You and the lass'll do. But you tell her—" and then he couldn't think what to tell her, forty years of things, so he just said, "tell her Wilf says hello," in a voice like a door closing.

The sheltered flats were low and pebble-dashed, by a church, with a little garden where two old men were arguing about a hanging basket. We found Number 9. There was a name slot by the bell. It said *M. SURTEES.*

I stood there with my finger over the bell and I couldn't press it.

"What if it makes it worse?" I said. "What if we're just — making an old lady remember the worst thing, for nothing?"

Bex took my hand off the bell and held it, which she'd never done before.

"We're not doing it for nothing," she said. "We're doing it to bring him back. And if it works — if it actually works — then we're not making her remember the worst thing." She pressed the bell herself. "We're about to un-happen it."

Footsteps. Slow ones. A shape behind the frosted glass.

The door opened.

---

## Twenty-Five

She was small, and white-haired, and sharp-eyed, in a cardigan the colour of the sea on a calm day, and she looked at the two of us on her step — a Gateshead lad and a Newcastle lass, neither of us hers — with the polite blankness of someone expecting the gas man.

"Yes, pet?" she said.

And I'd planned what to say, I'd planned it on the whole bus, but it all went out of my head, and what came out instead, in a sort of croak, was just:

"Mrs Surtees, my name's Finn."

She went absolutely still.

The colour drained out of her face the exact way it had drained out of Grandad's, that first night, and her hand came up to the doorframe to hold herself, the exact way his had, and she stared at me — at a twelve-year-old boy she had never seen in her life — as if I were a ghost standing on her welcome mat.

"*Finn,*" she whispered.

"Yes," I said.

"There was a Finn once," she said. Her voice was shaking. "A long time ago. When I was a girl. A voice. On Sid's radio. A lad with a Tyneside voice that came out of the wireless on the nights the lights were out, and reckoned — he reckoned he was from *years away*, and we thought it was a wind-up, some lad mucking about on the CB, except—" her eyes were filling, "—except he knew things. He knew our *names*. And he tried to tell us something, right at the end, something about Sid, and we never — we never understood it in time—" She put her hand over her mouth. "I have wondered. For forty years. Whether I imagined him. Everyone said I imagined him. The grief, they said. You imagined a voice."

She looked at me, this tiny old woman, with her whole long life behind her and the worst night of it suddenly standing on her doorstep with chips going cold in a bag.

"I didn't imagine you," she said. "Did I. You're real. You were always real."

"I'm real," I said. "And Mrs Surtees — I'm still trying to tell you. I never stopped trying. I've come to finish what I couldn't say."

And Maggie Surtees, née Dunn, who was twelve years old forty years ago and was, somehow, still hearing the same boy now, stood back from her own door, and said, in a voice full of tears and forty years of waiting:

"Then you'd best come in, pet. Both of you. And I'll put the kettle on."

---

## Twenty-Six

I won't write everything she told us. Some of it's hers.

But she told us about the night. Sitting in her little flat with the tea going cold and a photo of Sid on the mantel — the *same* photo, the boy and the fish, of course it was — she told us, in a voice gone back to being twelve.

The whole town went up the point. The lights were like nothing on earth — "*the sky came down, pet, there's no other words, it came right down to meet us.*" Everyone watching. And Sid — Sid had his radio with him, his little cigar-box set, because he'd got it into his head that on a night like that, the biggest storm in living memory, he'd hear *anything*. "*A voice from anywhere, he kept saying. He was that excited. He'd hardly look at the sky, he was that busy with the dial.*"

And then he'd heard something.

"*He went all — strange,*" said Maggie. "*Quiet. He had the earphone in, and he went quiet, and then he said, 'There's a voice. There's a voice coming in, Mags. From down by the water. It's calling — I think it's calling me.' And he got up, and he went off down the path. Toward the steps. Down off the point, toward the rocks. And I said, Sid, don't, the tide, but he said he'd only be a minute, he had to get a clearer signal, the voice was breaking up and he had to get *closer*—"* 

She stopped. Her hands were knotted in her lap.

"*And I let him go,*" she said. "*I was a bairn. I let him go. And the fret came in — the sea-fret, the fog, it came in that fast, off the water, it just — swallowed the rocks. And by the time anyone thought to look *down* instead of *up*—*" She couldn't finish it.

"They searched the cliffs," I said quietly.

"*The cliffs, the dunes, the whole point. For weeks. Divers, in the end.*" She shook her head. "*Nobody thought he'd gone *down*. Why would a sensible lad go down onto the rocks with the tide coming in? It made no sense.*" Her eyes found mine, and they were terrible and clear. "*Unless there was a voice. Calling him. From down by the water.*"

The room was very quiet.

"*Was it you?*" said Maggie Surtees. "*The voice from down by the water. Was that you, pet, all along?*"

And I didn't have an answer. Because I'd never said a word about water. But I felt the cold come up my back all the same.

---

## Twenty-Seven

I was quiet the whole bus ride home. Bex let me be.

Because here is what was going round and round in my head, like the static, like the tide:

*A voice from down by the water. Calling him.*

I'd been talking to Sid for weeks now, on the wireless, on the nights the lights were out. My voice came out of *his* radio. And on the night — the biggest storm in living memory, the strongest the connection would ever be — what if my voice came in *too* strong? What if I was up at Grandad's, talking to him, and on his end it sounded like it was coming from — I don't know — everywhere, anywhere, from out of the sky, from down by the sea?

What if Sid Charlton went down onto those rocks, into that fret, into that tide, chasing *me*?

What if every aurora I'd spent making friends with him, every night I'd said *hello, Sid*, was teaching him to follow my voice — straight down to the water, on the one night he should never, ever have gone?

I felt sick the whole way home, watching the grey sea slide past the bus window.

I'd come to save him. And I was starting to be horribly, coldly afraid that I was the thing he needed saving from.

---

## Twenty-Eight

I didn't tell Grandad that fear. Not yet. I couldn't. It was too big and too sharp and I didn't have the shape of it yet.

But I made a decision that night, lying in the dark with the model ship.

I'd been holding the date behind my teeth like a hot coal, waiting until I had a plan. But I was nearly out of time — eighteen days left in 1985, and who knew how many auroras the sun would give me. I couldn't wait for the perfect plan. I had to give Sid the one thing that might save him even if everything else went wrong.

The truth.

Not a vague *be careful*. The truth, plain. *On the thirtieth of August, the night of the great lights, a voice is going to call you down to the rocks below the point. Whatever it sounds like — whoever it sounds like — even if it sounds like me — DO NOT GO DOWN. Stay up high. The tide and the fret are coming. The voice can't help you from the water. Stay up.*

If he believed nothing else, if every clever plan fell apart, I needed that one sentence buried in him so deep that on the night, in the dark, with the sky on fire and a voice in his ear, it would stop his feet at the top of those steps.

The next time the lights came, I was going to tell Sid Charlton exactly how he died.

And I was going to beg him not to.

---

## Twenty-Nine

The lights came three nights later. Not the big ones — those were still to come, the paper had taught me that — but enough. Grandad sat with me this time, in the dark, his hand on Bess. We'd agreed I'd do the talking. Sid knew me.

"*Finn! Where you been, I've had stuff to tell you, Banger reckons—*"

"Sid," I said. "I need you to listen to me. Really listen. This is the most important thing I'll ever say to you."

Something in my voice stopped him. The static breathed.

"*...go on,*" he said.

So I told him. All of it. The thirtieth of August. The greatest lights anyone would ever see. The whole town on the point. A voice, on his radio, coming in strong, seeming to come from down by the water. And how, if he followed it down onto the rocks, the fret and the tide would come in and take him, and they'd search the cliffs for weeks and never think to look down, and he'd be gone, forever, and it would break his brother's heart for forty years.

I said it all in a rush, before I could lose my nerve.

Then there was a silence so long I thought I'd lost the signal.

"*Is that why,*" said Sid at last, very quietly, not breathless any more, "*you knew me name. Right at the start. Is that why you came. To tell me this.*"

"Yes," I said.

"*Because I'm — I'm already gone. Where you are.*"

"Yes," I whispered. "But you're not gone yet. Where you are, you're alive, and I'm talking to you, and that means we can change it. I've changed things before. We can change this. You just have to do one thing, Sid. One thing. On the night — don't go down. However it sounds. Don't go down to the water."

The static washed in and out.

And then Sid Charlton, twelve years old, frightened of moths and not of the sea, said the bravest thing I ever heard anyone say.

"*Right then,*" he said. His voice was shaking, but it was steady underneath, like a kettle that's decided not to boil over after all. "*If a voice from the future's come all this way to tell us, then a voice from the future must reckon it can be done. So we'll do it. Me and you. We'll beat it.*" A breath. "*I'm not going down. I promise you, Finn. Whatever I hear. I'm staying up top.*"

And beside me in the dark, my Grandad Wilf made a sound I'd never heard a person make, half a sob and half a laugh, at the sound of his little brother's voice, forty years away, promising to live.

---

## Thirty

For about a day, I thought we'd done it.

It seemed so simple, when Sid said it. *I'm not going down.* He'd promised. He was brave and he was clever and now he *knew*. All he had to do, on the night, was stay at the top of the point with the whole town, and watch the lights, and not follow a voice down some steps. People do harder things than that every day.

"We've cracked it," I told Bex, almost giddy. "He knows now. He just stays up top. That's it. That's the whole thing."

Bex didn't look as pleased as I felt.

"If it's that easy," she said slowly, "then why didn't it already work?"

"What d'you mean?"

"You said history's stubborn. You said you pull one thread and the jumper fights you." She frowned out at the sea. "Sid's just promised, in 1985, not to go down. So — has the newspaper changed? Go and check. If he keeps his promise, the paper shouldn't say *missing* any more, should it. It should say — I don't know — nowt. He should just've watched the lights and gone home."

I went to the library that Thursday with my heart in my mouth. Mr Fenwick, back from his hip-broken friend, set up the machine. The reel whirred. The thirtieth of August, 1985, swam up out of the dark.

*MISSING.*

The same headline. The same grey grainy boy with the fish. Nothing had changed. Not a word.

Sid had promised not to go down. And the past said he went down anyway.

---

## Thirty-One

I didn't understand it, and not understanding it frightened me more than anything had yet.

We talked it round and round, the three of us — me and Bex on the harbour wall, Grandad in his chair of an evening. Bex had a theory.

"Maybe it's not changed *yet*," she said. "Maybe the paper only shows what's true *so far*. And Sid's promise is real, but the night hasn't *happened* yet, in his time — he's still got two weeks to go, in 1985. Maybe the paper won't change until the night actually comes and he actually does or doesn't go down." She chewed her lip. "Maybe nothing's decided till it's decided."

Grandad had a darker thought, and he said it to the fire, not to me.

"Or maybe," he said, "a promise isn't enough. Maybe the lad means it with his whole heart, here, now, in the calm. But you've not heard it, son. The voice. On the night." He shook his head slowly. "I've heard things come out of that set in a storm that'd freeze your blood. Voices that seem to come from inside your own skull. If something's calling our Sid down to the water, with the sky come down to meet him and a fret rolling in — a promise made a fortnight before, in a warm kitchen—" He stopped. "I don't know it'll hold. That's all. I don't know a boy can keep a promise against a thing like that."

The fire cracked.

"Then we don't just rely on the promise," I said. "We've got two weeks. We work out *exactly* what happens, exactly when, exactly where — so that on the night, we're not begging him, we're *guiding* him. Step by step. Through it."

"Aye," said Grandad. "That's better. That's a sailor's answer. You don't pray the storm away. You know your tides, and you keep your head, and you bring her in." He looked at me. "Then we'd best learn this storm by heart. Every minute of it. Starting now."

---

## Thirty-Two

So we became students of one night.

The thirtieth of August, 1985, from teatime to dawn. We knew it better, in the end, than we knew our own lives.

From the newspaper, and from old Maggie on a second careful visit, and from Vi at the Harbour Lights, and from two more old folk Grandad knew who'd been on the point that night, we built it up, minute by minute, like a model ship in a bottle.

Teatime: the lights starting, faint, everyone coming out.
Dark: the whole town on the point, the sky ablaze.
Late: Sid with his radio, hearing the voice, going strange.
The fret coming in off the water — Grandad found the actual weather records, a "sea fog, sudden, dense," logged at the coastguard station.
The tide. This was the one that mattered. Grandad got the old tide tables down, the real ones, and worked it on his fingers and a bit of paper, his sailor's hands sure. On the thirtieth of August, 1985, low water at the point was at half past ten at night. The rocks below the steps would have been *bare* — walkable — at half ten. And the tide turned, and came in, fast on that coast, racing over the flat rocks, and by midnight those rocks were six feet under cold black water.

Half ten, the rocks were bare and tempting. By midnight, they were a death trap. An hour and a half. That was the window the sea gave Sid Charlton to make the worst mistake of his life.

"So that's it," I said, staring at Grandad's pencil marks. "That's the danger. Half ten to midnight. If we keep him off those rocks for ninety minutes — just ninety minutes — the tide covers them, and the danger's gone, and he can't go down even if he wants to."

"Aye," said Grandad. He was looking at the tide table like it was an old enemy. "Ninety minutes. From half ten." He set the pencil down. "We keep that boy up top from half past ten to midnight, son, and we'll have done it. We'll have beaten the sea at its own game, forty year too late." His jaw was tight. "I've waited a long time to beat that sea."

---

## Thirty-Three

That should have made me feel better. A plan. A window. A number to hold onto: ninety minutes.

But that night the other fear came back, the one I'd been pushing down, and this time I couldn't push it down, because the plan had a hole in it shaped exactly like me.

*A voice. From down by the water. Calling him.*

We'd been so busy with tides and frets we'd skated right past the worst question of all: *whose voice?*

Because I talked to Sid through that radio. My voice came out of his set. And on the night — the biggest storm the connection would ever know — what if the connection came in so strong, so strange, so *everywhere*, that to Sid it sounded like I was calling from out of the sea itself? What if, on the night, I tried to talk to Sid to *save* him — and the very sound of me, distorted by the monster storm, was the voice that drew him down?

What if I was the voice from the water?

What if the harder I tried to reach him on the night, the more I called him to his death?

I lay there with that, and it was the coldest I'd ever been.

Because if it was true — if my voice was the danger — then there was only one way to be sure of saving Sid Charlton.

I'd have to go silent on the night. Switch off. Disappear. Leave him with no voice to follow. Give up the one thing I had — the connection, the friendship, the chance to guide him through — and just *vanish*, and pray that a boy with no voice in his ear stays up top on his own.

I'd have to lose Sid to save him. Lose Maggie. Lose all of it.

I didn't tell anyone that fear yet. It was too heavy. I carried it on my own, the way Grandad had carried his, and I started, just a little, to understand him.

---

## Thirty-Four

The big aurora — not *the* big one, but the biggest since I'd arrived — came on day thirty-three, and the sun was generous, and the connection held strong and clear for nearly an hour, which had never happened.

We used every second of it.

I had Sid walk me through the geography while he could. Where exactly the steps were. How many. Where the voice had seemed to come from. (He didn't know yet that it might be me; I couldn't bring myself to say it.) Grandad leaned in and talked to his brother about the point — "*you know the third step, Sid, the one that's broke?*" — and Sid said "*aye, course,*" and the two of them, an old man and a boy, mapped a piece of coast across forty years like it was nothing, like Sid had just been out and was telling Wilf what he'd seen.

And at the end, when the lights began to thin, Sid said, "*Wilf. Is that really you. You sound — you sound* old*.*"

"I am old, our kid," said Grandad. His voice was rough as rope. "I'm an old, old man. And you're twelve. Isn't that a thing."

"*Did you have a good life?*" Sid asked. Just like that. The way only a kid would ask it. "*After. Did you have a good life, our Wilf?*"

And Grandad looked at the dying green light for a long moment, and I watched him decide not to lie.

"I had a life," he said. "It had a hole in it. A boy-shaped hole." He swallowed. "But I'm hoping — I'm hoping very hard, son — that we're about to mend it. So you hold on. You hold on to half past ten to midnight, and you stay up top, and we'll fill that hole back in. Both of us. Together. Agreed?"

"*Agreed,*" said Sid, and the lights went out, and took him with them.

Grandad sat in the dark a long time. Then he said, "Thirteen days," meaning in 1985. "Thirteen days left, for him." He put his hand flat on the cold cabinet of the wireless. "Hold on, our kid," he said, to the silence. "Just hold on."

---

## Thirty-Five

The next morning, Mam rang with good news, and it nearly ruined everything.

She was past the setback. Her heart had settled, the wire stitches were doing their job, the little flappy door was mended and shutting sweet, and the doctors were "*delighted*" — and she was coming *home*. And because she was coming home, and because she could manage now with a bit of help, she was going to come and *fetch* me. Back to Gateshead. Back to school, to my mates, to a flat with proper Wi-Fi and no sea wind and no goat.

"They reckon I'm well enough to have you back, love," she said, warm and tired and happy down the curly wire. "I've missed your face. I'll come up at the weekend and bring you home."

The weekend.

I stood in the hall with the phone against my ear and the bottom dropped out of my stomach. Because the thirtieth of August, in 1985, was still nine days off in parallel time — nine days, and the weekend was *three*. If Mam took me home on Saturday, I'd be forty miles from the wireless on the night that mattered most in the world.

I'd lose Sid because my mam's heart got *better*.

"That's — brilliant, Mam," I made myself say. "I can't wait." And then, casual as I could, the lie sliding out before I'd decided to tell it: "Although — Grandad's been showing me loads of stuff. About the radios. And there's a thing — a school thing — at the end of next week I'd hate to miss. Could I maybe — could I stay just a bit longer? Just till the week after?"

A pause.

"You want to stay?" said Mam, and there was something in her voice I couldn't read — surprise, and something underneath it, something almost like hurt, and something almost like hope. "With your Grandad?"

"Yeah," I said. "Me and Grandad — we get on, Mam. We really do."

Another pause, longer.

"Well," she said softly, and her voice had gone thick. "Well, there's a thing. There's a thing I never thought I'd hear." A breath. "Aye, love. Stay on, then. Stay till the week after. I'll come and we'll all—" she stopped. "I'll come up. We'll see."

I'd bought us the time. Nine days. But I'd bought it with a lie, and lies, as I was learning, are like changing the past: you pull one thread, and you never know what moves.

---

## Thirty-Six

Grandad knew the lie would cost something, and he did a brave thing. He decided to make it true.

"If your mam's coming up the week after," he said, "then it'll not be for a school thing, because there isn't one, and she'll find that out in five minutes, your mam's not daft." He looked at me steady. "So when she comes, son, we tell her. Not all of it — not the wireless, she'd have us both certified — but the rest. About Sid. About me. The thing her and me never talk about. It's time. Past time. She's a grown woman and she's lost her uncle her whole life and never been let to know him, and that's *my* doing, and it's wrong." His jaw worked. "I should never have shut him in a drawer. Not the photo. Not the boy. Not from her."

I didn't know what to say to that, so I said nothing, which is sometimes right.

Then he did the thing that mattered most. "But the wireless," he said, "is too important to leave to a voice that crackles and cuts. On the night, when the storm's at its worst, the signal'll come and go, and a word lost at the wrong second could lose the whole thing. We need a way to get a message through clean, that the storm can't muddy. We need a code." He looked at me, and the ghost of a smile crossed his cliff of a face. "And it just so happens, son, that there's a code two Charlton brothers know that nobody else in the world can read."

He got Sid's Morse sheet out of the tin trunk, the dots and dashes, the boy's clumsy hand turning them into letters.

"Sid can do Morse in his sleep," said Grandad. "I taught him. And now I'm going to teach you. Properly. Fast. Because on the night, when the voice fails — and it will fail — we're going to tap it through to him in dots and dashes, brother to brother, the way it's always been." He put the pencil in my hand. "Sit down, son. You've got nine days to learn to talk in lightning."

---

## Thirty-Seven

We practised Morse till my dreams went *dit-dit-dah*.

And on the fourth evening of it, with the kitchen table covered in our scribbles, Grandad went quiet over his tea, and then he said a thing he'd never said before, a thing he'd carried even longer than the rest.

"There's summat I've never told a soul," he said. "Not your nana. Not your mam. Because it sounds mad, and I'm not a mad man, whatever I look like." He turned his mug round and round. "The night our Sid went. I told you I was off Lagos. I was in my radio room, doing the midnight watch, all alone with the sea." He stopped. "And I got a transmission. On a wavelength there shouldn't have been owt on. A voice, broke up, far off, in a storm — there was a magnetic storm that night, see, a bad one, the whole band was a mess. And the voice—" his own voice failed, and he made it go again — "the voice was a lad. A young lad. And through all the static, all I caught — all I ever caught — was two words. Me name. *Wilf.* And then — *help.*" He looked up, and his eyes were forty years deep. "And then nowt. Gone. I told meself it was a fluke, some skip off another ship, some lad somewhere in trouble, nowt to do with me. I had to tell meself that, son, or I'd not have stayed sane. But I've wondered. Every night since. Whether — somehow — across all that ocean — I heard our Sid call me, the very night he went, and I couldn't make it out in time."

The kitchen was dead silent.

And the cold came up my back, slow, because I was starting to see the shape of something enormous and circular and terrible, a thing folding back on itself across forty years — but I couldn't see all of it yet, only the edge, only enough to know it was there.

"Grandad," I said carefully. "On the night. The thirtieth. If we're all on the wireless — you, here, talking to Sid — is there any chance your voice, from here, could've reached you, *there*, in 1985, off Lagos? Could the storm have — bounced it that far?"

Grandad stared at me.

"I don't know," he said slowly. "I'd have said never. But I'd have said all of this was never." He set his mug down with a hand that wasn't quite steady. "What are you thinking, son?"

"I'm not sure yet," I said. "But I think — I think this night might be more tangled than we know. I think we might already be *in* it. I think we might always have been."

---

## Thirty-Eight

Here is what I worked out, in the small hours, with the model ship and the dark and the sea.

Forty years ago, the night Sid vanished, three things happened at once, in three places, joined by one monster storm in the sky.

On the point: Sid heard a voice from down by the water, and followed it down onto the rocks, into the fret and the tide, and was lost.

Off Lagos: a young Grandad, alone on the midnight watch, caught a broken transmission on a dead wavelength — a lad's voice, his own name, *help* — and never understood it.

And — this was the new, cold, enormous piece — *here*. In this house. On this very wireless. Was somebody, on that night, transmitting?

Because the wireless reaches across forty years. We knew that. *I* knew that, in my present, talking to 1985. But what if it had always worked both ways, always, even back then? What if, on the night, there'd been a voice from *the future* coming out of Sid's radio — a voice that, in the monster storm, seemed to pour out of the whole sky, out of the sea, out of everywhere — calling Sid by name, sounding for all the world like it was coming from down by the water?

And what if that voice — distorted, doubled, thrown about by the worst storm in living memory — had been *mine*?

What if I was always going to be on the wireless on the night? What if the voice that called Sid down to the rocks, forty years before I was born, was a voice I hadn't even *made* yet — was the voice I would make, this summer, this very week, trying to save him?

What if I had always been the thing that killed him — and the thing that, if I got it exactly, perfectly right, might just save him instead?

I sat up in the dark with my heart going like the Morse, *dit-dit-dah, dit-dit-dah*, and I understood, finally, the whole terrible circle. The voice from the water was me. It had to be me. And on the night, in nine days' time, I was going to be holding the very dial that, forty years ago, had killed the boy I was trying to save.

The same storm. The same voice. The same night. Looping back on itself like the tide turning.

And the only question left — the only one that mattered now — was whether, this time, I could make the voice say *up* instead of *down*.

---

## Thirty-Nine

I told them all of it the next day. Grandad, and Bex, and — because she deserved to know, because it was hers too — old Maggie, on the phone Grandad held while I talked.

I told them my voice was the voice from the water. That I'd always been part of the night. That the thing that took Sid was, somehow, going to be me.

I thought Grandad would be horrified. He wasn't. He'd had a lifetime to learn that the world is stranger and crueller and more circular than anyone wants it to be, and when I finished he was quiet for a bit, and then he said:

"So you're the voice that calls him down." A pause. "Then you're the voice that can call him up."

"That's what I keep telling myself," I said. "But what if I get it wrong? What if I make it *worse*? Grandad — there's a part of me that thinks the only safe thing is to *not be there*. To go silent on the night. No voice, no call, no danger. Just — disappear, and let him stay up top on his own."

"No," said old Maggie down the phone, fierce and instant, before Grandad could speak. "*No.* You listen to me, pet. I've had forty years of him going down to that water with no one to call him back. Forty years of silence. Silence is the thing that *took* him." Her voice cracked. "Don't you dare go quiet on him. A voice took him down — then by God a voice can bring him up. You be *louder*. You be *clearer*. You don't leave that boy alone in the dark with no one calling his name." She was crying now, and so was I. "I left him go down on his own once. I will not have it happen twice. You will be *there*. You will *call* him. And this time, this time, pet — you call him *home*."

And that decided it. Not the science, not the logic. An old woman who'd waited forty years, telling me that the answer to a voice that calls you to the dark is not silence.

It's a better voice.

---

## Forty

The sun, which had been so kind to us all summer, chose the worst possible moment to turn its back.

Five days before the night, with everything ready — the tide tables learned, the Morse drilled, the plan as tight as we could make it — the aurora forecast went flat. Mrs Okoro's app dropped into the deep green calm and *stayed* there. Day after day. No lights. No connection. No way to reach Sid, to steady him, to go over the plan one last time before the worst night of his life.

And the wireless itself was getting worse.

It had been straining for weeks — I'd noticed it, the hum gone rough, a smell of hot dust that was a bit too hot, the dial flickering. Grandad had it half in bits on the table, frowning, testing valves, his big hands surprisingly delicate.

"She's old," he said. "Older than me. These valves were made when your nana was a bairn. And I've run her hard, every storm, forty year." He held one up to the light, the filament inside burned thin as a hair. "She's tired, son. And the night that's coming—" he shook his head. "The thirtieth, here, our time — the app's saying it now, look — it's not just *an* aurora. It's a monster. Biggest predicted in twenty years. A proper Carrington-class brute. The whole sky'll come down."

"That's good," I said. "Isn't it? Strong storm, strong connection—"

"It's good and it's terrible," said Grandad grimly. "Strong enough to reach him, aye. And strong enough to *kill her*." He nodded at the wireless. "A storm like that puts a surge through every wire it touches. Carrington burned the telegraph out, remember, set the offices on fire. A set this old, in a storm this big—" He set the valve down, gentle. "She might give us the night. But I'll not promise she'll give us the morning. We could lose her, right when we need her most. Halfway through. With the tide coming in and our Sid on the rocks."

I looked at the old wireless, in bits on the table, glowing faintly even now, even dying.

"Then we'd better get her ready," I said, "to give us everything she's got. One last time."

"Aye," said Grandad. He picked up the soldering iron. "One last storm, old girl. Let's see you home."

---

## Forty-One

I behaved badly, the day before the night, and I owe Bex an apology in writing, so here it is, in a book, where it'll last.

I was wound so tight I could've snapped. The plan, the wireless dying, my voice being the danger, Mam coming, the whole impossible weight of it — and Bex came round to Grandad's all cheerful, to help, and I bit her head off. Told her it wasn't really *her* problem, was it, she'd be off back to Newcastle at the end of the summer anyway, it wasn't her great-uncle, it wasn't her family, why was she even—

I saw her face change and I knew, the second it was out, that I'd said the cruellest thing I had.

She didn't shout. She went very quiet, which from Bex is much worse.

"You're right," she said. "It's not my family." She picked up her coat. "I just thought, when somebody's about to do the hardest thing in their life, they shouldn't have to do it on their own. Because I know what that feels like. Being on your own. I'm a blow-in, remember? Folk the wind blew in and never blew back out." Her chin was up but her eyes weren't right. "I thought we were the same, you and me. I thought that's why we were mates." She got to the door. "But maybe you've got a family now, and a great-uncle, and a grandad, and you don't need the lass from the arcade any more."

And she went.

And I stood there in Grandad's kitchen feeling like the smallest, worst person in the entire history of the world. Because she was the one who'd believed me first. She was the one who held my hand at old Maggie's door. She was the one who said *we're not making her remember the worst thing — we're about to un-happen it.* She was, I realised, standing there in the wreckage of what I'd said, my best friend. My actual best friend. And I'd told her she didn't count.

"You'd best go after her," said Grandad quietly, from the doorway. "Right now. Before the tide of it goes out too far to bring back." He almost smiled. "I've some experience, son, of letting a thing go unsaid too long. Forty years' experience. Don't you do it. Go on."

---

## Forty-Two

I caught her at the harbour wall, where she always went, with the tide coming in below, slap and suck against the stone.

"Bex."

"Don't," she said, not turning round.

"I'm sorry," I said. "I'm so sorry, Bex. It was the worst thing I've ever said to anyone and I didn't mean it, I was just — I'm scared. I'm so scared about tomorrow night I made it land on you, and that's not fair, and you're not a blow-in, or — you are, but so am I, and that's the whole point, that's *exactly* the point — we found each other. You're not the lass from the arcade." I ran out of clever words, so I just said the true one. "You're my best mate. You're the best mate I've ever had. And I can't do tomorrow without you. I don't want to."

She was quiet for a long moment, watching the water come in.

"Say it again," she said. "The best-mate bit. I want to hear it again so I can hold it over you forever."

"You're my best mate," I said. "There. Witnessed by the sea."

"And the goat," said Bex, because Nigel had wandered down the harbour and was watching us with great interest. And then she laughed, a wet sort of laugh, and shoved me, and it was alright. It was alright again.

"Right then," she said, wiping her eyes, all business. "Tomorrow night. The biggest storm in twenty years. A dying radio. Your voice that might save him or might kill him. The whole town up the point. Your mam liable to turn up. And ninety minutes, half ten to midnight, to keep a boy off some rocks forty years ago." She sniffed. "Just an ordinary Saturday, then."

"Just an ordinary Saturday," I agreed.

We sat on the wall, the two of us, with the tide coming in, and we watched the sun go down behind the lighthouse, the way it had gone down on Sid Charlton's last evening, forty years before. Tomorrow the sky was going to come down. And we were going to be ready. Or we weren't. But we were going to be there.

Together.

---

## Forty-Three

The morning of the night, the wireless stood whole again on its table, and she was beautiful.

Grandad had been up till three putting her back together, and he'd done more than mend her. He'd nursed every valve, cleaned every contact, run a thick new earth wire out the window and down to a copper spike he'd driven into the ground by the coal house — "to give the surge somewhere to *go*, son, other than through her heart, or ours." She hummed low and steady, and her dial glowed its little lit-up city, and she looked, for all her age, ready.

"Will she hold?" I asked.

"She'll hold long enough," said Grandad, which is not the same as yes, and we both knew it.

Mrs Okoro's app had the storm building all day, the needle climbing out of the green calm, hour by hour, into the amber, toward the red. *Severe geomagnetic storm expected this evening,* it said. *Aurora may be visible as far south as southern England. Possible disruption to power and radio.* And under that, a little line that made the hair stand up on my arms: *Largest event since the records began.*

The same storm. Forty years on, the same monster sun throwing the same fire at the same patch of sky, on the same night, over the same point.

"It's like the sky's keeping an appointment," said Bex, looking at the climbing needle. "Like it's been circling back to this for forty years, same as us."

"Aye," said Grandad. "Well. Let's not keep it waiting."

---

## Forty-Four

Mam arrived at half past three, in the little car, with a smaller suitcase than she'd brought me up with, because half my stuff was going home in it.

She looked thin, and there was a carefulness to how she moved, but her colour was good and her eyes were bright and when she hugged me on the doorstep I could feel her heart going steady and sweet under my ear — *tick, tick, tick* — that little door, mended, shutting clean. I held on a lot longer than I meant to.

"Eh, give over, you'll squash me stitches," she laughed, but she didn't let go either.

And then she looked up, over my shoulder, at Grandad, standing in the doorway of his cluttered house, this huge old man, and the two of them just *looked* at each other, across the doorstep and across the years, and nobody said anything, and the wind came off the sea, and I understood that they hadn't stood this close in a very long time.

"Dad," said Mam.

"Donna," said Grandad.

And that was all, for now. But it was more than I think they'd managed in years.

I had a panic, briefly, about the night — how would I get to the wireless with Mam here, how would I explain being up till midnight — but Grandad caught my eye over her head, and gave the smallest shake of his head, *leave it to me*, and I did, because I'd learned by then that my Grandad Wilf, who'd said about nine words to me on my first day, had been quietly carrying the whole weight of this for longer than I'd been alive, and knew what he was doing.

"Donna," he said. "Come in. Sit down. There's a pot on." He took a breath, the kind you take before you go into cold water. "There's a thing I should've told you thirty years ago, hinny. About my brother. About your uncle. About a lad called Sid."

Mam went still.

"I never had an uncle," she said.

"Aye," said Grandad. "You did. And it's my fault you never knew it. Sit down, love. Let your old dad tell you. It's past time."

---

## Forty-Five

I'll not write all of what Grandad told Mam, because it was theirs, father and daughter, forty years overdue, and some things shouldn't be written down even in a book.

But I sat on the stairs and I heard the shape of it. I heard him tell her about Sid — the eleven-bounce stone, the cigar-box radio, the boy frightened of moths and not of the sea. I heard him tell her about the night the sky came down, and the search that found nothing, and the drawer he'd shut the photo in, and how he'd shut his whole heart in there with it, and shut her out too without meaning to, because a man who's frozen one room of himself can't always keep the cold from spreading.

I heard Mam cry. I heard her say "*why did you never tell me, Dad, all these years, why,*" and I heard him say "*because if I never said it out loud it didn't have to be true, and that's a coward's reason, and I'm sorry,*" and I heard a long quiet, and then I heard a chair scrape, and I think — I couldn't see, I was on the stairs — but I think my mam went and put her arms around her old dad for the first time in a very long time.

He didn't tell her about the wireless. We'd agreed. *That* she'd never have believed, and it would only have frightened her, and besides — as Grandad said, low, when she'd gone up to wash her face — "some of tonight she's better not knowing. If it goes wrong, she's no part of it. And if it goes right—" he almost smiled — "if it goes right, hinny, she'll have an uncle by the morning, and she can meet him then, and we'll none of us have to explain a thing."

I didn't fully understand that last bit. I would, by morning. But the way he said it — *she'll have an uncle by the morning* — went through me like a current, because it was the first time anyone had said out loud that if we won, the world would actually *change*.

---

## Forty-Six

The lights came early, and they came like the end of the world, in the best way.

We were at the tea table when Bex hammered on the door, breathless, having run the whole length of Coble Row. "It's starting! It's *starting*, come and see, the whole sky—"

We went out into the street, all of us, Mam too, and looked north, out over the rooftops to the sea, and the sky was already going. Not the thin smear I'd seen my first weak night. This was the sky from the photographs and worse — great green curtains hauling themselves up from the horizon, rippling, shot through with pink and a deep blood red, brightening even as we watched, pouring across the early stars without a sound.

The whole town was coming out. Doors opening up and down the row. Mrs Okoro locking Neptune's and Vi shutting the fryer and the two old men from the harbour and forty-odd kids from the school and a goat — the whole of Skerrenby, drawn up toward the point, the way they'd been drawn up the point forty years before, on a night exactly like this one.

"I'm going up," said Mam softly, her face tipped to the sky, lit green. "I've never seen owt like it. I'm going up the point." She looked at Grandad, and something passed between them. "For Sid. He loved the lights, you said. I'll go up for Sid."

"Aye, hinny," said Grandad gently. "You go up for Sid. We'll — we'll be along. Finn's showing me summat with the radios first. We'll be along."

And Mam went, up the row with the rest of the town, up toward the point and the burning sky, and she didn't know that down in the front room behind her, her old dad and her son and a lass from the arcade were about to try to reach across forty years and pull her uncle out of the sea before she ever got there.

"Right," said Grandad, when the door shut. The green light poured through the window onto the old wireless, the way it had that first night, the way it must have on the night itself. "She's safe out the way. The storm's near its peak. Sid'll be on the point now, his time, with his little set." He sat down in front of the wireless and cracked his big knuckles, and laid the Morse key — a real brass one, from the tin trunk — on the table beside the dial. "Half past nine, his time. We've an hour till the rocks go bare at half ten." He looked at me, and at Bex. "Warm her up, son. Let's go and get my brother."

---

## Forty-Seven

The wireless took longer to warm than ever, as if she knew what we were asking of her, and the static, when it came, was a roar — not the gentle sea-sound of the quiet nights but a great surging crashing wall of it, the storm at full fury, the whole band a chaos.

I found the place on the dial. I lost it. I found it again. And under the roar, faint, fighting, came a voice.

"*—inn? FINN? Are you—*" crash of static "*—can't hardly—*"

"Sid! Sid, I'm here, we're all here, can you hear me?"

"*—aye! Aye, just! It's mad up here, Finn, the sky's—*" the signal swelled, suddenly clear, and his voice came through bright and scared and alive: "*—you should see it, man, the whole sky's come down, everyone's out, the whole town, I've never seen owt like it—*"

"Sid, listen, what time is it where you are? Exactly?"

A pause. "*Just gone half nine. Why?*"

Grandad leaned in to the set, and his voice when he spoke was the steadiest I ever heard it, a sailor's voice, a voice for a storm. "Sid. It's Wilf. Now you listen to your big brother. In one hour, at half past ten, the tide's going to turn and those rocks below the steps are going to come bare. And a voice is going to call you down to them. And whatever that voice sounds like, however much it sounds like it needs you — you are going to stay UP TOP. You hear me? From half ten to midnight, you do not go below the top step. Not for anything. Not for anyone. You stay up where Maggie is, where the town is, in the light, till midnight. And at midnight the tide'll have those rocks under six foot of water and you'll be safe and it'll be over and we'll have *beaten* it. Can you do that for me, our kid? One hour. Stay up."

The static breathed.

"*Aye, Wilf,*" said Sid, and his voice wobbled but it held. "*I'll stay up. I promise. Half ten to midnight. I'll not go down.*"

"Is Maggie with you?"

"*She's right here. She's not leaving me side, she says.*"

"Good lass," said Grandad. "You keep hold of him, Maggie. You don't let go of him, not for one minute, this whole hour. You hold onto Sid Charlton like the world depends on it. Because tonight, hinny — it does."

---

## Forty-Eight

The hour between half nine and half ten was the longest of my life, and the worst part was that nothing happened, which gave the fear room to grow.

The lights blazed. The static surged and ebbed. We kept Sid talking, all of us, to keep him steady and to keep him *up* — Bex telling him daft things about the future to make him laugh, me going over the plan again and again, Grandad just being there, a big steady brother-voice in the storm. Sid was scared, you could hear it, but he was holding, up top, with Maggie's hand in his, the whole town around them, the sky on fire.

"*It's funny,*" Sid said, during a clear patch, his voice gone thoughtful. "*Knowing. That this is the night. That I'm meant to — you know.*" He didn't say the word. "*I keep thinking — what if you hadn't come, Finn? What if there'd been no voice from the future? I'd be stood here right now, having the best night of me life, with no idea. And then I'd've—*" he stopped. "*And me brother'd've had a hole in him for forty years. And he'd never even've known I was about to—*" His voice cracked. "*Thank you. For coming all this way. Even if it doesn't work. Thank you for not letting me go on me own.*"

"It's going to work," I said. "We're nearly there, Sid. Half an hour. You're doing it. You're beating it."

And for a moment — for one warm, fragile moment, with twenty-five minutes to go — I let myself believe we'd already won. Sid was up top. He was holding. All he had to do was *not move* for half an hour.

Then Mrs Okoro's app, sitting on the table where Bex had left it, gave a little chime, and we all looked at it, and the needle had gone past red. Off the scale. Into a colour I'd never seen it show before, a deep pulsing violet, and a new message had come up on the screen, and it said:

*EXTREME EVENT IN PROGRESS. Widespread power and communications disruption likely. This is the strongest geomagnetic storm on record.*

And at that exact moment, the wireless made a sound she had never made before — a deep, sick, rising whine — and the dial flared, too bright, and the smell of hot dust became the smell of hot *metal*, and Sid's voice in the speaker suddenly doubled, and trebled, and came pouring out of the set from everywhere at once, huge and distorted and strange, as if it were coming not from a radio but from the whole sky, from the sea, from under the floor.

"It's peaking," said Grandad, and for the first time all night, his steady sailor's voice had fear in it. "The storm. It's peaking *now*. Forty years early or forty years late, I can't tell, but it's here, and she—" he put his hand flat on the shaking cabinet "—she'll not take much of this. Hold on, old girl. *Hold on.*"

And in the speaker, through the rising howl, small and far and terrified, came Sid's voice:

"*Finn. Finn, there's a voice. There's a voice coming in. From down by the water. It's — it's calling me name. Finn, it's calling me—*"

---

## Forty-Nine

"DON'T GO DOWN!" I shouted at the set. "Sid, that's it, that's the voice, DON'T GO DOWN, it's not real, it's the storm, STAY UP—"

But here was the horror, arriving exactly as I'd feared it, dropping into place like the last piece of a puzzle I'd been dreading to finish:

The voice from the water was *me*.

I understood it in a flash, cold and total. My own voice — my shouting, my *don't go down* — was pouring into Sid's set so loud and so distorted by the monster storm that on his end it sounded like it came from everywhere, from the sky, from the sea, from down by the rocks. The harder I shouted to keep him up, the louder the voice from the water became. Every word I said to save him was the lure that called him down.

I was the voice. I had always been the voice. Forty years before I was born, the thing that drew Sid Charlton onto the rocks was *me, trying to save him,* on this exact night.

"Grandad," I said, and I could hardly get it out, "it's me. *I'm* the voice from the water. The more I talk, the more I call him down. If I keep shouting at him to stay up — he'll hear it as the voice from the rocks — I'm *doing it*, I'm doing the thing right now—"

Grandad's face went white as bone. Because he saw it too. The whole circle. Closing.

"Then stop," he said. "Stop talking. Go quiet—"

"*No!*" — and old Maggie's voice was in my head, fierce, from days ago: *Silence is the thing that took him. Don't you dare go quiet on him.* "No, if I go quiet he's alone in the dark with the lure still calling — Maggie said — silence took him once, I can't—" I was coming apart. "If I talk I call him down, if I go quiet I leave him alone, there's no — there's no way to—"

"*FINN!*" Sid's voice, ragged, over the howl. "*I can hear two of you! There's you, and there's the one down by the water, and they're saying — I can't tell which is which — I'm coming down to get a clearer—*"

"NO—"

And the wireless screamed, and the dial went black, and the speaker died, and Sid Charlton was gone.

The connection had cut. At the worst possible second. With Sid moving toward the steps and the tide about to turn and the fret rolling in, forty years away, in the dark, alone.

---

## Fifty

"NO no no—" I was scrabbling at the dial, at the dead black dial, at the silent set. "Get her back, Grandad, GET HER BACK—"

"She's surged—" Grandad was already at the back of her, both hands in, his face lit by sparks I didn't like the look of. "The earth wire — the surge went through — Bess, *out*, get the dog out—" because Bess was barking, and the room was full of that hot-metal smell, and a thin smoke was rising off the top of the cabinet. "She's not dead, she's *fainted*, the main valve's tripped — I have to bring her back without the surge taking her for good — Finn, the time, what's the *time*—"

I looked at the app. The clock on it. "Twenty-eight minutes past ten."

"Two minutes," said Grandad, low and terrible, working, working. "Two minutes till the rocks go bare. And he's already on his way down." His hands moved in the guts of the wireless, sure and desperate. "Come on, old girl. Come *on*. I've nursed you forty year for this one night. Don't you dare die on me now. Not now. Not with our Sid on those steps. *Come on.*"

Bex had her hands pressed over her mouth. I had stopped breathing. The smoke curled. The dead set sat there, black and silent, and somewhere on the other side of it, forty years down, a boy was walking down stone steps toward bare wet rocks with a voice in his ears, and we couldn't reach him, we couldn't reach him, we couldn't—

The dial flickered.

Went dark.

Flickered again.

And then, with a deep sick shuddering hum, the little lit-up city came back, dim and guttering but *back*, and the static crashed in like a wave, and through it, far and frantic and forty years away, a girl was screaming a name.

"*SID! SID, COME BACK UP! SID!*"

Maggie. On the point. Screaming down into the fret.

We'd got the wireless back. But we were too late to stop him going down.

He was already on the rocks.

---

## Fifty-One

"Maggie!" I shouted. "Maggie, talk to me, where is he, what's happening—"

"*He's gone down!*" Her voice was wild with terror. "*The fret came in, it came in that fast, I can't see him, I can hear him on the rocks but I can't SEE him, and the water — Finn, the water's coming, it's coming over the flat rock, oh God, he won't come back up, he keeps saying the voice is right there, just a bit further, he keeps going FURTHER OUT toward the—*"

"Sid!" Grandad roared at the set. "SID, GET BACK UP THE STEPS, THE TIDE'S COMING—"

But it was happening again — I watched it happen — the moment Grandad shouted, the moment *I* shouted, our voices poured into Sid's set, doubled and distorted by the storm, and became the voice from the water, calling him *further out*, away from the steps, toward the sound.

"*It's working against us!*" I grabbed Grandad's arm. "Every word we shout goes out as the lure — we're calling him further onto the rocks — Grandad, our voices are KILLING him, we have to—"

And Grandad's eyes met mine, and something cleared in them, some old hard sailor's certainty, and he reached past me and put his hand on the brass Morse key.

"Then we'll not use words," he said.

---

## Fifty-Two

"The storm muddies a *voice*," said Grandad, fast, his hand on the key, "because a voice is a great fat sloppy thing, all tones and edges, easy to smear. But a dot and a dash—" his eyes were burning now "—a dot and a dash is just *on* and *off*. Clean. Sharp. The storm can't blur a thing that's only on or off. It's how we talked across oceans in the worst weather God ever made. It'll cut through where a voice can't." He looked at me. "And there's not a soul on those rocks who'd mistake Morse for a voice from the water. It's not a voice at all. It's a *hand*. *My* hand. The hand that taught him. He'll know it. Out of anything in the world, our Sid will know his brother's hand on the key."

"But how does he hear it—"

"His set's still on, isn't it — he's still got the earphone in — he'll hear the tone, the beeps, plain as anything, *cleaner* than our voices—" Grandad was already poised. "Finn — you tell me the way up. You and Bex, you learned that point by heart, the steps, the broken third step, the safe ledge. You feed me the words and I'll send them in code, fast and clean, and we will walk that boy up off those rocks one dot at a time. *Now.* The tide. Go."

And so we did the maddest, hardest, most beautiful thing I have ever been part of.

I called out the way up — "tell him: STOP. then: TURN. then: STEPS BEHIND YOU—" — and Bex checked it against the map of the point we'd drilled for two weeks, and Grandad's big hand came down on the little brass key, *dit-dah, dit, dah-dit-dah*, fast and sure and clean, forty years of a sailor's skill pouring out through his fingers — and out across the storm, down through forty years, into a cigar-box radio on the wet rocks, in the fret, in the rising tide, went a brother's hand, spelling out a road home.

Not a voice. Not the lure. A hand.

And the speaker went quiet — Maggie had stopped screaming — and we waited, all of us frozen, for any sign at all that on the other side of the dark, a boy had heard.

---

## Fifty-Three

For three seconds — the longest three seconds in the history of the world — there was nothing but static.

And then, faint, under the roar, came a sound that made my Grandad Wilf's whole face break apart.

*Dit-dit-dit. Dah-dah-dah. Dit-dit-dit.*

Then a pause.

Then: *dit-dit. dah. dit-dah-dit. dit.* 

"He's answering," Grandad whispered. His hand was shaking on the key, his eyes streaming, but his sending stayed clean. "He's sending back. He heard me. He *heard* me." He read the dots as they came, his lips moving. "He says — he says—" and his voice cracked right open — "he says, '*Wilf. Is that you. I can't see.*'"

"Tell him yes," I said, crying now, all of us crying. "Tell him yes, it's you, and you're going to walk him up, and not to listen to the voice, only to the key—"

Grandad sent it. *Dit-dah. Dit. Dit-dit-dit.* YES. Then: ONLY THE KEY. NOT THE VOICE. THE VOICE IS A FRIEND BUT IT CANT HELP YOU FROM THE WATER. THE KEY WILL TAKE YOU UP. TRUST THE KEY.

And the answer came back, slow, a frightened boy's clumsy hand, the same clumsy hand from the yellowed sheet in the tin trunk, forty years on the other side of the dark:

*I TRUST YOU WILF.*

"Right," said Grandad, and wiped his face with the back of his sending hand, and bent to the key like a man rowing for a drowning child. "Right. Feed me the way up, son. Every step. We're bringing him home."

---

## Fifty-Four

I'm going to slow right down here, because this was the heart of it, and it deserves to be slow, even though it was the fastest, most frightening few minutes of my life.

Bex held the map. I called the moves. Grandad sent them in clean sharp lightning. And on the rocks, in the fret, with the cold sea climbing his legs, a boy followed his brother's hand.

STOP, Grandad sent, when Bex said Sid was heading the wrong way, out toward the deep water and the sound. STOP NOW.

And the answer: *STOPPED.*

TURN ABOUT, Grandad sent. THE STEPS ARE BEHIND YOU. NORTH. TOWARD THE LIGHTHOUSE. CAN YOU SEE THE LIGHT.

A long pause. Then: *FRET TOO THICK. CANT SEE LIGHT. WATER AT MY KNEES.*

And I watched the fear take Grandad, just for a second, and then I watched him beat it, because that's what you do for the people you love.

LISTEN THEN, he sent. DONT LOOK. LISTEN. THE WAVES. THEY BREAK ON YOUR RIGHT. KEEP THE BREAKING WATER ON YOUR RIGHT HAND AND WALK. THE STEPS ARE STRAIGHT AHEAD. GO SLOW. FEEL WITH YOUR FEET.

The pause stretched. The static howled. The app said 10:47. Thirteen minutes of rising tide. I didn't dare breathe.

Then: *WALKING.*

Then, after a horrible gap: *FOUND THE STEPS. FIRST STEP.*

"YES," I shouted, and Grandad sent it, YES YES CLIMB, and Bex was gripping my arm hard enough to bruise, and we were all leaning toward the dim glowing dial as if we could push him up the steps with the force of our wanting.

THIRD STEP IS BROKE, Grandad sent, his hand flying. SKIP THE THIRD STEP. STEP OVER IT. ITS BROKE. DONT TRUST IT.

And in the speaker, suddenly, breaking through even the storm, came a real voice — Sid's voice, not in code now, gasping, half-laughing, half-sobbing, climbing:

"*I remember the broke step, Wilf! You told me when I was little! Don't trust the third step, you said! I REMEMBER!*"

---

## Fifty-Five

The tide came in fast, that night, the way Grandad's old tables had promised it would, faster than a boy can climb when he's cold and blind and frightened.

He was on the steps now, climbing, and Maggie was above him in the fret screaming encouragement — "*COME ON SID, I CAN NEARLY REACH YOU, COME ON*" — and Grandad sent the steps one by one, FOURTH, FIFTH, KEEP GOING, ALMOST THERE, and Sid called them back, on the seventh step, on the ninth, climbing up out of the sea and the fog and the forty years —

And then the wireless screamed again.

The storm gave one last enormous surge — the biggest yet, the very peak, the crest of the whole monster — and the dial flared white, and the smoke off the top of the cabinet turned thick and dark, and Grandad cried out and snatched his hand half off the key, and the speaker filled with a roar like the inside of the sun.

"She's going!" Grandad shouted. "The surge — she can't take it — Finn, she's dying, we've got seconds—"

"Where is he?!" I screamed. "Bex, where is he on the steps?!"

"Eleventh!" Bex shouted, white-faced. "There's thirteen! He's on the eleventh, he's nearly up, he just needs—"

"TWO MORE STEPS," I yelled, "Grandad SEND IT, two more steps, tell him, before she dies, TELL HIM—"

And my Grandad Wilf, with the wireless screaming and burning under his hands, with smoke in his eyes and the dying dial searing white, put his hand down one last time on the brass key that had carried his voice across every ocean in the world, and sent, clean and sharp through the screaming storm, the last message that wireless would ever send:

*TWO MORE. THEN MAGGIE HAS YOU. CLIMB SID. I LOVE YOU. CLIMB.*

---

## Fifty-Six

And here is the thing I have to tell you, the thing that closed the whole circle, the thing I only understood as it happened.

As that last message went out — *CLIMB SID I LOVE YOU CLIMB* — it went out on the biggest storm the Earth had ever recorded. A storm that bounced a signal not hundreds of miles but, that night, around the whole curve of the world. And the message didn't only go down to the rocks below the point.

It went *out*. Across the sea. Around the planet. To every dead wavelength, to every wire, to every set still switched on under that burning sky.

And somewhere, forty years ago, off the coast of Lagos, a young man named Wilf Charlton sat alone on the midnight watch in a ship's radio room, with a magnetic storm wrecking the whole band, and out of the chaos, on a wavelength there shouldn't have been anything on, he caught — for the first and only time in forty years he caught it clearly now, I understood — not just *Wilf* and *help*, the fragments that had haunted him. He caught his own hand. His own sending. His own brother's name. *Climb, Sid. I love you. Climb.*

The transmission that young Wilf heard off Lagos, the one he never understood, the one he carried for forty years — it was *this one*. It was old Wilf, tonight, sending to Sid. The call for help and the answer to it, the lost brother and the saving brother, had always been the same signal, circling the world and circling the years, waiting forty summers for the night when both ends could finally, finally meet.

Grandad had been trying to save Sid since before he even knew Sid was in danger. He'd heard himself doing it, off Lagos, in 1985, and not known. The help had always been on its way. It had just taken forty years, and a grandson, and a dying wireless, to arrive on time.

I didn't say any of this out loud. There wasn't time, and anyway Grandad's whole life had just bent into a circle in front of him, and some things you leave a man to feel on his own.

The wireless screamed its last.

And up on the point, forty years away, a boy reached the top of the steps and a girl's hands closed around his wrists and pulled.

---

## Fifty-Seven

"*I'VE GOT HIM!*"

Maggie's voice tore through the dying speaker, through the smoke, through the screaming storm, and it was the most beautiful sound I have ever heard or ever will.

"*I'VE GOT HIM, I'VE GOT HIM UP, HE'S UP, HE'S OFF THE ROCKS — SID! SID, YOU'RE UP, YOU'RE SAFE, I'VE GOT YOU—*"

And then Sid's voice, gasping, soaking, shaking, *alive*, up top, above the tide line, on the grass of the point with the whole burning sky above him:

"*I'm up. Wilf — Finn — I'm UP. I can see the lighthouse. The fret's — I'm above it, I'm on the grass, the water can't — I'M UP!*" He was laughing and crying at once, the way we all were. "*I did it. We did it. I'm not — I'm not going to—*" He couldn't say the word, even now, even safe. "*I'm going to be alright. I'm going to grow up. WILF, I'M GOING TO GROW UP!*"

And my Grandad Wilf — sixty-four years old, with a burning radio under his hands and tears pouring down the cliff of his face — threw back his head and *laughed*, a huge, broken, joyful, forty-years-coming laugh, and shouted at the screaming, dying, glorious set:

"AYE YOU ARE, OUR KID! AYE YOU ARE! YOU GROW UP, SID CHARLTON! YOU GROW UP AND YOU HAVE A LIFE, A GREAT BIG LIFE, AND DON'T YOU DARE WASTE A DAY OF IT, D'YOU HEAR ME? NOT ONE DAY!"

"*I HEAR YOU, WILF!*" Sid shouted back, through the storm, through the years. "*I'LL NOT WASTE A DAY! I PROMISE!*"

And the wireless gave one final, rising, shuddering wail —

— and the dial flared so bright it lit the whole room white —

— and then, all at once, with a soft electrical sigh, like a held breath finally let go, the light went out, and the hum stopped, and the static stopped, and the smoke curled up gently into the dark, and the great wooden wireless that had waited forty years for one single night went quiet, and stayed quiet, forever.

---

## Fifty-Eight

The room was very dark, and very silent, after.

The only light was the green of the aurora, still pouring through the window, softer now, the storm beginning, at last, to pass. Grandad sat with both hands resting on the dead, warm, smoking cabinet, his head bowed, his shoulders going up and down. Bess crept out from under the table and pushed her nose into his hand. Bex was crying without making any sound. And I knelt on the rug where I'd knelt that first night, and I didn't know — none of us knew — whether it had worked.

Because that's the thing nobody tells you about saving someone across forty years. You don't get to *see*. The connection was gone. The wireless was dead. The last we'd heard, Sid was up top, alive, soaked and laughing on the grass of the point — but that was *then*, forty years ago, and between that moment and this one lay four whole decades that I had no way to look into. Had he stayed up? Had the tide taken him anyway? Had something else, some other thread I'd pulled, reached up out of the dark? Had I changed it, or had I just *heard* it, the way Grandad heard it off Lagos, helpless, after the fact?

I didn't know. I couldn't know. Not tonight.

"Grandad," I whispered. "Did we do it? Is he — did it work?"

Grandad lifted his head. His face in the green light was wet and old and unreadable.

"I don't know, son," he said quietly. "I've waited forty year to know, and I don't know yet." He looked at the dead wireless, and laid his huge hand flat on her one last time, gentle, the way you'd settle a horse, or close a friend's eyes. "She gave us the night. Like I asked. She gave us everything she had." He patted her, once. "Good girl. Rest now."

And then, from up the row, through the open window, over the soft hiss of the passing storm, we heard the town coming back down off the point — voices, footsteps, the ordinary sound of a crowd that's seen something wonderful — and somewhere in it, getting closer, my mam's voice, calling my name into the green dark.

"*Finn? Finn, love, are you in? You'll never guess — you'll never guess who I—*"

And she stopped.

In the middle of the sentence, she stopped, out there in the street, as if she'd run into a thought too big to get round.

And then her voice came again, and it was strange — wondering, shaking, like someone waking from a dream into a different room than the one they fell asleep in.

"*Dad?*" she called. "*Dad — there's a man here. He says — he says he's my—*"

---

## Fifty-Nine

I want to tell you what came through that door. But first I have to tell you what happened *inside* me, in the second before it did, because it's the strangest thing that has ever happened to me and I still don't properly understand it.

As Mam's voice broke off in the street — *he says he's my* — I felt the world go soft around me. Not dizzy. *Soft.* Like the moment when you're not quite asleep and a dream is rearranging the furniture of the day. And for one impossible heartbeat I had two sets of memories in my head at once, both completely true, lying over each other like two photographs on the same piece of film.

In one, I had no great-uncle. There was a drawer with a face-down photo in it, and a name nobody said, and a hole in my family forty years wide.

In the other —

In the other, I'd known him my whole life.

In the other, *Great-Uncle Sid* was the one who'd taught me to skim stones on the beach at Whitley Bay when I was six (eleven bounces, he'd done, showing off, and I'd never beaten it). Great-Uncle Sid sent me a fiver in a card every birthday with a daft joke in it and *actual full stops*. Great-Uncle Sid was the reason I knew anything about radios at all, the funny gap-toothed old man with the ears that stuck out who'd grown up loving voices from anywhere, who'd had a whole long life, a wife, kids, who'd grown old in a world that hadn't lost him —

— and then the two photographs slid together and became one, and the first one faded like breath off glass, and I was left, kneeling on the rug, with only the second.

Sid Charlton had grown up.

I was the only person in the world who remembered that he ever hadn't.

---

## Sixty

The door opened, and the town's green-lit faces and chatter spilled in behind two figures, and Mam came through first, her face lit up like the sky, half laughing and half crying and completely bewildered by her own happiness, and behind her —

Behind her came an old man.

He was tall, and stooped a bit, and he had white hair and ears that stuck out and a gap in his front teeth when he grinned, which he was doing, hugely, helplessly, the grin of a boy who has waited his whole long life for something and just got it. He had a soaked-through look to him still, somehow, deep down, as if a part of him would always be a little bit wet from a sea forty years ago. And he stood in the doorway of the house he'd grown up in, Eleven Coble Row, and he looked across the dark front room at the huge old man rising from the chair by the dead wireless, and his face crumpled.

"Hello, our kid," said Grandad. His voice didn't work at all. "You grew up, then."

"Aye, Wilf," said Great-Uncle Sid Charlton, seventy years old, alive, *alive*. "I grew up." The tears were running down into his grin. "Took your advice, didn't I. Didn't waste a day." He looked around the cluttered, green-lit room, at the dead radio, at the dog, at the lass from the arcade, and last of all at me, kneeling on the rug. And something flickered across his old face when his eyes landed on me. A question. A very old question, half-remembered, from a very long time ago.

"And you'll be young Finn," he said slowly, wondering, "that I never could place. The lad whose voice I heard, when I was a boy. On the wireless. On the worst night of me life." His eyes searched my face. "I've wondered about you for sixty years, son. Whether you were real. Whether I dreamed you, on those rocks, in the fog." His voice dropped to a whisper. "Was it you? The voice that called me home?"

And I looked at my great-uncle, who was alive because of a dying radio and a brother's hand and a code two boys used to talk across an ocean, and I said the truest thing I have ever said:

"It was all of us," I said. "But mostly it was your brother. He never stopped calling you home, Sid. Not for forty years. It just took us a while to get the message through."

---

## Sixty-One

That was a strange and golden night, in the cluttered house on Coble Row.

The kettle never stopped. Half the town squeezed in at one point or another, because in a place that small the news that Wilf Charlton's long-lost brother had turned up went round faster than the aurora, and nobody could quite say *how* he'd turned up, or where he'd been, and nobody quite asked, because the North East is full of people who know that some things are better celebrated than explained. Vi brought chips. Mrs Okoro brought a cake she'd had in for "an occasion," and said this was clearly it. Even Mr Fenwick came, and shook Sid's hand for about a minute and a half.

And in the middle of it all sat two old brothers, side by side on the settee, not saying much, just *being* next to each other, which is a thing they'd been robbed of for forty years and weren't about to waste a second of now. Every so often Grandad would look across at Sid as if to check he was still there. And every time, Sid was still there. And every time, something in Grandad's old face settled a little more, like a tide coming gently back into a harbour that's been dry too long.

Mam couldn't stop touching Sid's sleeve, marvelling. "I had an *uncle*," she kept saying, dazed and delighted. "All this time. Uncle Sid. I had an *uncle.*" She didn't seem to find it strange that she'd somehow always had him and was meeting him for the first time; the new memories had folded into her smooth as cream into tea, and only I knew there'd ever been a drawer, a silence, a hole. She just thought tonight was the night the family finally got round to a proper do.

Bex caught my eye across the room, over the cake and the chatter, and raised her eyebrows, and I knew exactly what she was asking. *Are you alright? You did this. You actually did this.* And I gave her a little nod that meant *yes* and *I know* and *thank you* and *I couldn't have without you*, all at once, the way you can with a best mate, in one nod, across a crowded room.

It was the happiest night of my life.

And it was, in a small quiet way that I told no one about for a long time, also a little bit one of the saddest. I'll explain why in a minute. But first I want you to have the happiness, whole, the way we had it that night, before I tell you what it cost.

---

## Sixty-Two

Here is what it cost.

The wireless was dead.

I went and stood in front of her, late on, when the do had thinned out, when Sid had gone to sleep in the spare room — *his old room*, he'd said, wondering — and Mam was washing up and Grandad was sat with a whisky he wasn't drinking, just looking at the door Sid was asleep behind. I stood in front of the great dark wooden cabinet, cold now, the dial black, the smell of burnt dust gone faint, and I understood, properly, what we'd given up.

The connection was gone. Forever. She'd burned herself out giving us the night, and no one was ever going to fix a thing that old, that broken, even Grandad. Which meant there'd be no more voices from anywhere. No more nights kneeling on the rug, finding the place on the dial, hearing a girl with plaits I'd never seen say *Finn? You there?*

I was never going to talk to *my* Sid again. The breathless, eleven-bounce, flying-cars-disgusted Sid. The boy. He'd grown up — beautifully, wonderfully, into the lovely old man asleep down the hall — but the *boy*, the boy on the radio who was my mate, my own age, my secret friend across forty years — he was gone. Grown out of. I'd saved his life and lost his friendship in the very same act, because the whole point of saving him was that he didn't stay twelve.

And Maggie.

That was the one that got me, standing there in the dark. *My* Maggie. The fierce, funny, pickled-onion-crisps, *the-future-is-wasted-on-you-lot* Maggie. The first friend I made in this whole impossible summer. I was never going to hear her voice again. There was an old Maggie out there, I knew — and I'd go and see her, I'd already decided — but it wouldn't be *the same*, it couldn't be, she was sixty-seven, she'd had a whole life, she wasn't twelve and cross and brilliant on the other end of a dial any more.

I'd saved them by letting them grow up and grow old and grow away from me. That's what saving someone across time *is*, I think. You don't get to keep them. You just get to give them all the years you yourself will never share.

I put my hand flat on the dead wireless, the way Grandad did, and I said, very quietly, so no one could hear, "Bye, Maggie. Bye, Sid." To the boy and the girl who'd been twelve, who'd be twelve now forever, in nobody's memory but mine.

And then I went and sat with my Grandad, and we didn't talk, and that was alright.

---

## Sixty-Three

I went to see Maggie Surtees three days later, on the bus, the way I had before. Bex came too.

It was strange, going back to the sheltered flats at Warkhaven, because some of my memories of the place were now the only ones that existed. In the world everyone else lived in, I'd never knocked on this door before. In the world everyone else lived in, there'd been no grieving widow who couldn't pass the point, no forty years of carrying a dead boy. There'd just been Maggie — Sid's lifelong best friend, the two of them thick as thieves their whole lives long, godmother to his kids, the pair of them still arguing about whose turn it was to buy the chips at seventy.

But I knocked, and the door opened, and it was her — older, white-haired, sharp-eyed in a sea-blue cardigan — and her face, when she saw me, did something I'll never forget.

It lit up.

Not the white shock of the first time. Not the ghost-on-the-doorstep terror. It lit up with a great warm astonished *delight*, and she clapped her hands together and said, "Well, I never! It's the *radio lad*! It's young Finn! Sid said you'd grown up funny — said you'd turn up looking like you do — eh, come in, come in, pet, I've waited about sixty years to get a proper look at you!"

And I understood that in this world — the world I'd made — Maggie remembered me too. But she remembered me *happy*. She remembered the night of the great lights as the night a voice on the radio helped save her best friend's life, the most wonderful and impossible thing that ever happened to her, the start of a story she and Sid had told and retold and laughed over for sixty years. *The radio lad.* The boy who called Sid home.

She made tea. She showed me photos — a whole long life of them, her and Sid down the decades, weddings and beaches and grandkids — and I drank it all in, the life that the original Maggie, the grieving one, never got to have. And only I knew there'd ever been another version of her, alone in a sea-blue cardigan, carrying a stone.

"You alright, pet?" she said, catching me with my eyes wet over a photo of her and Sid, ancient and laughing, on the point.

"Yeah," I said. "Yeah. I'm just glad. I'm just really, really glad."

"So am I, son," said Maggie Surtees, and patted my hand. "Every single day. So am I."

I never told her about the other Maggie. Some things you carry on your own, so the people you love don't have to. I'd learned that off my Grandad.

---

## Sixty-Four

The thing that healed, that summer, wasn't only Sid.

My mam and my Grandad — who'd fallen out, years before, about a thing nobody would ever name — un-fell-out. And I worked out, watching them, that the thing they'd fallen out about had never really been a sofa, or whatever I'd imagined as a kid. It had been *grief*. Grandad's grief, frozen and locked in a drawer, that had spread cold through the whole house Mam grew up in, until she couldn't breathe in it and left and they stopped being able to reach each other across all that ice.

And now the ice was gone. Because the grief was gone. Because Sid had come home, and the boy-shaped hole in my Grandad was filled back in, and a man whose wound has finally closed is a different man — warmer, softer, *thawed*. Mam noticed it before she could even name it. "He's different, your Grandad," she said to me, wondering, in the car. "He's — lighter. I've not seen him like this since I was little. Since before—" and she stopped, not knowing what the "before" was, only feeling it.

I knew what the "before" was. It was before the thirtieth of August, 1985.

We'd reached back forty years and mended a man at the root, and the mending had grown up through all the years between, straightening everything as it came, the way you'd splint a young tree and find the whole grown trunk standing true.

My mam got her dad back. My Grandad got his brother back. And I — the blow-in, the lad from Gateshead with the absent dad and the mam with the mended heart — I got a whole family I hadn't had in June. A great-uncle who sent fivers with full stops. A grandad who wasn't the saddest man in the world any more. And a mam and a grandad who could sit in the same room and laugh.

You pull one thread, and the whole jumper moves. I'd learned that the hard way, with Banger Bell's broken arm. But it turns out it works the other way too. Mend one thing, the right thing, on purpose, knowing why — and the whole thing knits back together, all the way down the years.

One thing. The smallest thing that saves him. I'd kept the promise to Bex after all.

---

## Sixty-Five

Summer ended, the way it does, all at once, when you're not looking.

Bex had to go back to Newcastle for school. We stood on the harbour wall the day before she left, with the tide coming in below, slap and suck against the stone, the way it had all summer, the way it had on Sid's last evening and on the night we brought him home.

"So," said Bex. "That was a summer."

"That was a summer," I agreed.

"Most people just get a wasps' nest and a sunburn," she said. "We got a dead lad un-dead." She squinted out at the sea. "Nobody's ever going to believe us, you know. Not ever. Not for the rest of our lives."

"Nope."

"Good," said Bex, fiercely. "It's ours, then. Just ours." She looked at me. "And the goat's."

And then, because we'd both got soft and neither of us could stand it, she pulled out her phone and made me put my number in, and this time — this time, with proper signal back in Newcastle and Gateshead, in a world full of phones that Maggie thought we wasted on cats — this time the technology *worked*. We could message any second of any day. And here's the thing I'd learned from a girl forty years ago: I wasn't going to waste it. I wasn't going to be one of those people who can reach anyone and never really talks to them. Bex was my best mate. I was going to *use* the future, properly, the way Maggie Dunn would've wanted, to hold onto the people the wind blew in.

"Text me the second you're home," I said.

"Text me when your mam's heart does owt," she said. "And when your Grandad does owt. And when the goat does owt."

"I'll send you hourly goat updates."

"Good," said Bex, and hugged me, hard and quick, and went off up the row with her bag, turning round twice to wave, and then she was gone round the corner, and Skerrenby felt suddenly enormous and quiet.

But not lonely. That was the difference. It didn't feel lonely any more.

## Sixty-Six

The night before *I* went home to Gateshead, Grandad and Sid took me up the point.

It was a clear, calm, ordinary evening, the sky going pink and gold over the sea, no aurora, nothing but the lighthouse beginning to turn and the gulls going home. We walked out along the cliff path, the three of us and Bess, to the top of the steps — *those* steps — and we stood there, and nobody said anything for a while.

Then Sid took something out of his coat pocket and held it out to me.

It was a small wooden box. A cigar box, old and soft-cornered and mended with tape in about nine places, with a tangle of wire and a single earphone and a length of bedspring poking out of it, and a dial made from a bottle top.

"Me first set," said Sid. "The one I built when I was a bairn, out of the bits our Wilf sent me from the ports. The one I had on the rocks, that night." He turned it over in his old hands, fond. "I kept it me whole life. Sixty year. Never knew quite why. And now I do." He held it out further. "I want you to have it, son. The lad whose voice came out of it, the night it mattered most." His old eyes were bright. "A voice from anywhere, I used to say. Could be anybody, could be anywhere." He smiled his gap-toothed smile. "I never reckoned on a voice from *anywhen*. From me own great-great-nephew, that wasn't even born. Calling me home." He pressed the box into my hands. "You hold onto that. And one day, when you're an old, old man, and some bairn doesn't believe a word of it — you let them hold it. And you tell them the daftest, truest story they'll ever hear."

I took the cigar-box radio. It weighed almost nothing and it weighed everything.

"And here," said Grandad, gruff, and put something cold and heavy in my other hand. The brass Morse key. The one he'd carried across every ocean in the world. The one that sent *climb, Sid, I love you, climb* through the biggest storm there ever was. "You learned the code," he said. "You earned the key. A Charlton always has, in this family. I had it off my dad, Sid had it off me—" he glanced at his brother "—well. He will have done. Now. In the world we made." He cleared his throat hard. "And now you've got it. So you keep the code alive, son. Long after I'm gone. Long after we're all gone. You keep tapping. You never know who'll come out of the dark."

I stood on the point, between my Grandad and my great-uncle, with a cigar box in one hand and a brass key in the other, and the lighthouse turning, and the sea going gold, and I couldn't speak at all.

So I didn't. I just stood there with them, and let it be enough.

---

## Sixty-Seven

I went home to Gateshead the next day, in the little car, with Mam, with my suitcase, with the cigar box and the brass key wrapped in a jumper so they wouldn't rattle.

And here's the strange thing about going home. I'd spent the whole start of the summer dreading being sent *to* Skerrenby — torn away from my mates, my flat, my proper Wi-Fi, dumped in a town the size of a stamp with a grandad who hated me and a dog who hated me more. And now, going the other way, back to all the things I'd missed — I was sad to leave.

Because it wasn't a town the size of a stamp any more. It was the place where my family was. It was Coble Row, and the Harbour Lights, and Neptune's with his lost trident, and the lighthouse, and the point. It was where my Grandad lived, who I now loved, who I'd nearly never known. It was where Sid was. It was where the whole impossible summer had happened, the summer nobody would ever believe, the summer that was just ours, and the goat's.

"You can come up whenever you want, you know," Mam said, watching the road, reading me the way she always could. "It's not far. And your Grandad—" she smiled "—your Grandad would have you up every weekend if you'd go. He told me. He said the house is too quiet now, with just him and Bess and Sid round the corner. He said—" her voice went soft "—he said it's been a long time since that house had a young person in it who *understood* it. I don't know what he meant by that. Do you?"

I looked out the window, at the grey sea sliding away behind us.

"Yeah," I said. "I think I do."

"You and your Grandad," said Mam, shaking her head, smiling. "Who'd have thought. The pair of you. Thick as thieves." She reached over and squeezed my knee. "I'm glad I sent you to him. I worried about it the whole way up, in May. I thought I was doing a terrible thing." She laughed. "Turns out it was the best thing I ever did. For both of you."

*For all of us,* I thought. *For more of us than you'll ever know.*

But I didn't say it. I just put my hand over hers, on my knee, and we drove home down the coast, away from the sea, into the rest of our lives.

---

## Sixty-Eight

I go up most weekends, now.

The cigar box sits on my windowsill in Gateshead, next to the model ship Grandad let me keep, and some nights I take the earphone out and hold it to my ear, even though the set's long dead and there's nothing in it but silence. I don't know what I'm listening for. The boy, maybe. My Sid. The breathless one. Or Maggie, fierce and twelve, telling me the future's wasted on me.

I never hear anything. The wireless that could reach across the years burned out on the night it mattered, and you don't get a thing like that twice in a life, or even once, really, except I did.

But here's a thing I've started to wonder, and I'll leave it with you, because I think about it a lot.

The aurora comes round, you know. On a cycle. Every eleven years or so the sun goes mad again. And one day, years from now, when I'm older — maybe even old — the big storms will come back, the Carrington kind, the kind that lights the whole sky and does strange things to radio, the kind where voices come through that have no business coming through.

And I've got a brass key. And I know the code.

I don't think the cigar box will ever work again. I really don't. But on the night the sky comes down again — and it will, one day — I think I might just take that old brass key out, and set it on a table by a window, under a burning sky, and tap out a message into the dark. Dot dot dot. Dash dash dash.

You never know who'll come out of the dark. That's the best thing about the radio.

Sid taught me that. Across forty years. The first time, and the last, that anyone ever has.

## Sixty-Nine

Grandad's not the saddest man in the world any more.

I want that written down, because of what my mam said to me on my very first day, on the doorstep, with the wind coming off the sea. *Don't let him make you sad,* she said. *He's not a sad man. He just looks like one.*

She was right and she was wrong. He *was* a sad man. He'd been a sad man for forty years, frozen round a boy-shaped hole, sitting in the dark in front of a wireless waiting for a voice that never came. He just hid it so well, under the gruffness and the dog and the nine words a day, that even his own daughter half-believed it was only how his face fell.

But you should see him now.

You should see him at the Harbour Lights on a Friday with Sid, the two of them arguing about the football and stealing each other's chips. You should see him on the point on a clear evening, teaching me and Bess to skim stones, never beating Sid's eleven bounces and pretending he doesn't care. You should see him with my mam, the two of them sat in the cluttered front room — where the dead wireless still stands, because none of us could ever move her, she's earned her place — laughing about something, easy as anything, forty years of ice all melted away.

You should see his *face*. The cliff that the weather worked on for sixty years. It's the same face. But the weather's changed. The sun's come out on it.

My Grandad Wilf got his brother back, and his daughter back, and a grandson he never expected, and a house that isn't quiet any more. He'd tell you he doesn't deserve it. He'd tell you he was off Lagos when it counted, three thousand mile away, and couldn't hear the one voice he'd have given his arm to hear.

But he could, in the end. He *did* hear it. Forty years late, on the biggest storm there ever was, with a dying radio and a grandson and a brass key — he heard his little brother, and he answered him, and he brought him home.

The help was always on its way. It just took the long way round. Forty years, the whole curve of the world, and one summer.

But it got there.

## Seventy

My English teacher, Miss Okonkwo, says the hardest part of any story is the end. She says you should land it like a gull landing on water — clean, and quiet, and like you meant to.

I'm not sure I can do that. There's too much. But I'll try.

I started this by telling you about the night I bit my own thumb, kneeling on the floor of my Grandad's front room with a custard cream halfway to my mouth, the first time a voice came out of his wireless. And I told you that wasn't really the start — that the start was greyer than that, a Tuesday in May, a suitcase with a dodgy wheel, a town I'd been to twice in my life.

And it was. That was the start. A scared lad being carried up the coast to a grandad he didn't know, into a house full of old radios and a wound forty years deep, with a mam whose heart had a broken hinge and a great-uncle in a drawer he'd never been told about.

But you know what? I don't think a story really has *one* start. I think it's all loops, in the end. Mine started in a car in May. And it started off the coast of Lagos in 1985, when a young man heard a voice he couldn't place. And it started on a cigar-box radio on the rocks, with the fret coming in. And it started on the surface of the sun, ninety-three million miles away, with a storm getting ready, forty years before I was born, to keep an appointment with a patch of sky over a town called Skerrenby.

The book at the front of this — the bit about 1859, the year the sky caught fire, when telegraph machines printed out messages nobody had sent, and *nobody ever found out what those messages said* — I put that there on purpose. Because for a hundred and sixty years, nobody knew what those messages said. Voices coming out of a sky gone mad, from no station at all, and not a soul could read them.

Well. I know what one of them said. One of them, at least, on one night, on the biggest storm there ever was.

It said: *Climb, Sid. I love you. Climb.*

That's what the sky was carrying, all that time. That's what comes through, on the nights the sky comes down and the radio does strange things and a voice arrives that has no business arriving. It's just people. Reaching for each other, across whatever's in the way. Across an ocean. Across forty years. Across a wound that everyone said would never close.

Love, mostly. Trying to get the message through.

It takes the long way round, sometimes. It takes a dying radio, and a brass key, and a code two boys used to talk across the sea. It takes a girl with plaits and a girl with braids and a goat called Nigel and a dog called Bess and a grandad who only ever said nine words to you on your first day.

But it gets there.

Look up, on the night the sky comes down. Everybody else will be filming it on their phones. But you — you listen. Put the earphone in. Tap out a message into the dark.

You never know who'll come out of it.

I never did.

*Finn Robson*
*Skerrenby, on the point, in the gold of an ordinary evening*
