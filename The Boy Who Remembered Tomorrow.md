## Part One — Tomorrow Comes Early

---

## Chapter One — The Thursday I Remembered Friday

Here is something I have learned, and I am only twelve, so you can take it or leave it: the worst day of your life almost never tells you it's coming. It doesn't knock. It doesn't send a calendar reminder. It just turns up, like our dog Sputnik turns up when you open a packet of anything, and by then it's already too late to pretend you're not at home.

But for me, it was different. For me, the worst day of my life sent a memory ahead of itself, the way a wave sends that thin, cold lick of water up the beach a full second before the rest of it arrives and soaks your socks.

I just didn't know what it was yet.

I'm going to start with the maths test, because that's where it started for me, and because if I start with the other thing — the real thing, the Pip thing — you'll think I'm exaggerating, and I am not exaggerating, and that's important, so just stick with the maths test for now.

It was a Thursday in May. I know it was a Thursday because Thursday is bins, and Mam had shouted up the stairs, "Nico, the bins!" in the voice she uses that makes my full name sound like a swear word, and I had dragged the wheelie bin down our path in the half-dark with my school trousers pulled on over my pyjama top, which is a perfectly normal thing to do and I will not be taking questions.

We live at number 9 Sefton Terrace, in a town called Saltmere Bay, which is on the cold knuckle of the North East coast where England sort of gives up and lets the sea have a go. If you've never been, picture this: a long grey beach, a longer grey sea, a promenade with a chip shop, an amusement arcade called Codd's that smells of doughnuts and ten-pence pieces, and, sticking out into the water like a broken finger, a thin causeway of rock that leads to a little island called the Brigg. On the Brigg there's a ruined chapel and a bell that doesn't ring any more.

Remember the bell. I know I keep telling you to remember things. That's sort of my whole problem, as it turns out. Hang on, I'm getting ahead of myself, which — ha — is also sort of my whole problem.

The maths test.

So I'd taken the bins out, and I'd gone back to bed for the eleven minutes I have between bins and breakfast, and I'd fallen asleep, the way you do, that thick gluey ten-o'clock-feeling sleep even though it's seven in the morning.

And I dreamed.

Except it wasn't a dream. I want to be really clear about this, because I have had dreams, normal ones, where I'm sitting an exam in my pants, or where Sputnik can talk and he's very disappointed in me. Those are dreams. You wake up and they slither away and you can't hold onto them, like trying to pick up a single chip with a plastic fork.

This was not like that. This was a *memory*. It had the texture of a memory. It had that boring, ordinary, true feeling that memories have — the feeling that you didn't make it up, because if you'd made it up you'd have made it more interesting.

I remembered sitting in Mr Pegg's maths room. I remembered the radiator under the window ticking. I remembered Mr Pegg saying, in his flat voice that makes algebra sound like a weather warning, "Right, you horrible lot, pens down in forty minutes, no talking, and Dale Ferris, if I see your eyes go anywhere near Priya's paper I'll have them out with a teaspoon."

I remembered the first question on the test.

*A train leaves Saltmere at 14:06 travelling at 72 miles per hour —*

I remembered every single question. I remembered getting the fourth one wrong, the one about the cylinder, because I always get the volume of a cylinder mixed up with the area of a circle and Mr Pegg has told me eleven times that one has an extra letter on the end and I never remember which.

I remembered Macca Dodds being sent out for flicking a rubber.

And then Mam shouted, "NICO, YOUR TOAST IS GOING COLD AND I AM NOT MAKING ANOTHER ONE," and I sat bolt upright in bed with my heart going like a trainer in a tumble dryer, because I had just remembered, in perfect detail, a maths test.

That we were sitting that afternoon.

That hadn't happened yet.

---

Now. I'm a sensible person. I know that sounds like the kind of thing only a deeply un-sensible person says, like how the lads who do the most dangerous things always start with "watch this," but I am, genuinely, quite a careful, suspicious sort of boy. I don't believe in much. I don't believe in ghosts, or horoscopes, or that the bloke off the telly can really bend spoons, or that my Auntie Sandra's healing crystals did anything for her knee except give her something to lose down the back of the settee.

So my first thought was not *I can see the future.* My first thought was: *I must have heard about the test. Someone must have told me the questions.*

Except they hadn't. Nobody had. The test was a surprise test — Mr Pegg's favourite kind, the cruel kind, the kind he springs on you while wearing the small private smile of a man who has very little else going on in his life.

My second thought was: *coincidence. You half-remembered some old questions and your brain stitched them together.*

That one lasted until 2:15 that afternoon.

Because at 2:15 that afternoon I was sitting in Mr Pegg's maths room, and the radiator under the window was ticking, and Mr Pegg said, "Right, you horrible lot, pens down in forty minutes, no talking, and Dale Ferris, if I see your eyes go anywhere near Priya's paper I'll have them out with a teaspoon," and the first question on the sheet, in Mr Pegg's smudgy photocopying, said:

*A train leaves Saltmere at 14:06 travelling at 72 miles per hour.*

And the room went all swimmy and far away, like when you stand up too fast, and I thought, very calmly, the way you think things when you're actually about to be sick:

*Oh. So that's real, then.*

I got full marks. Well — I got everything right except the cylinder, because by the time I got to question four I was so busy panicking about how I knew the answers that I forgot to actually do the maths, and I put down the area of a circle, like an idiot, exactly like I'd remembered doing.

You'd think that, of all the things, would be the bit that convinced me.

It was. Getting it *right* could be luck. Getting it *wrong* — getting it wrong in exactly the way I'd remembered getting it wrong, a day before I got it wrong — that's not luck. That's something else entirely.

Macca Dodds got sent out for flicking a rubber.

I went home, let myself in, fed Sputnik, told Pip her drawing of a horse looked exactly like a horse and not at all like a brown sofa with legs, and then I went and sat on my bed and looked at my hands for a really long time, the way you do when you've just found out something about yourself that you cannot un-find-out.

I could remember tomorrow.

I want to tell you that I was scared. I want to tell you that some wise little voice in me said: *Nico, this is going to cost you more than you can imagine; put it down, walk away.*

But I was twelve, and I'd just got full marks on a surprise maths test without revising, and Macca Dodds had been sent out, and the chips were on, and downstairs my little sister was alive and arguing with the dog about whose turn it was on the beanbag.

So no. I wasn't scared.

That came later.

That came on a Friday I remembered far, far too well.

## Chapter Two — A Brief History of Me (and Why I'm Not Lying)

Before we go any further, I should probably tell you who I am, in case you've wandered into this book by accident, looking for one about dragons or football.

My name is Nico Kostas. I'm twelve, nearly thirteen, which my Auntie Sandra says is "a difficult age," as if there's an easy one. I'm in Year 7 at Brindley High, which is a big concrete school on the hill above Saltmere Bay that looks, from the beach, like something that fell off the back of a lorry in 1974 and was never collected.

The Kostas bit is from my dad. My dad was Greek — Greek-Cypriot, properly, from a village near a town called Paphos that I have never been to and that I can only ever picture as warm, which is the exact opposite of everything else in this story. He came to Saltmere when he was nineteen because his uncle had a boat here, and the plan was to stay one summer, and then he met my mam in the queue at the chip shop, and that was that. He always said it was the only good decision he ever made standing up. My mam always said it was the only decision he ever made full stop, and then she'd laugh, before. She doesn't make that joke any more.

You've probably worked out the "before" by now. I'm not going to make a big thing of it, because Dad would have hated that — he was a man who, when he cut his hand open on a winch and you could practically see the bone, said, "It is fine, it is only a small bit of me," and wrapped it in a tea towel and finished bringing the pots in.

My dad is dead. He died two years ago this summer. He was a volunteer on the Saltmere lifeboat — the RNLI, the proper orange-and-blue one — which means that when other people's boats got into trouble in weather that would make you wet your pants just looking at it, my dad and a handful of other completely mental, completely brilliant people would get out of bed, run down to the boathouse, and go *out into it*.

And one night in August, two years ago, there was a yacht in trouble off Gallows Nab, which is the black headland at the north end of our bay, and the sea was doing the thing it does maybe twice a year where it stops being water and starts being a kind of grey animal, and they got the three people off the yacht, all three of them, alive — and on the way back in, a wave that the inquiry later called "anomalous," which is a long word for *we don't want to say the sea just decided to,* took my dad over the side.

They found him two days later, down past the Brigg, near the bell.

There. I've said it. I'm not going to say it like that again, so if you needed to put the book down and have a biscuit, now's the time.

I'll wait.

---

Right. Here's the rest of us.

There's my mam, Dawn. She's a nurse at North Tyne General, which is forty minutes away, and she does nights, three a week, which is why a lot of this story happens with no adults in the house, which I promise was not my idea and which Childline would probably have something to say about, except we manage, and there's our neighbour Mrs Okafor who has a key and a thing about us eating vegetables.

Mam since Dad has gone a sort of quieter version of herself, like someone turned her down two notches and lost the remote. She works too much. She thinks I don't notice that she works too much, and I don't tell her I notice, because that's the deal we've made without ever saying it out loud, which is the only way our family ever makes deals.

Then there's Pip.

Pip is my little sister. She's six. Her real name is Philippa but nobody has called her that since the day she was born and decided, by the look on her face, that Philippa was far too long a word for someone with that much to do. She has hair that does whatever it likes, a gap where her front teeth used to be, and a pair of red wellies she wears in all weathers, including, on one memorable occasion, to bed.

Pip remembers Dad in the way you remember things from when you were four, which is to say she remembers about six things, very brightly, and has stopped being sure which of them really happened. She remembers he called her *koukla*, which is Greek for "doll." She remembers he could whistle through a blade of grass. She remembers he carried her up the cliff path on his shoulders, and that from up there you could see "the whole entire sea," which, when you're four, is the biggest fact in the world.

I remember more. That's the deal *I* got, being older. More to keep. More to lose. Hold that thought, because it turns out to matter quite a lot, and I didn't know it yet.

And finally there's Sputnik.

Sputnik is our dog. He is, as far as anyone can tell, a Staffordshire bull terrier who got into an argument with a Labrador and possibly a draught excluder. He is the colour of a digestive biscuit, he is afraid of the hoover, the postman, and his own reflection in the oven door, and he produces, after tea, smells that could be classified as a chemical weapon under international law. We named him Sputnik because Dad said he had a nose that pointed at the sky and a brain that was permanently in orbit.

Sputnik is important. I'm telling you that now so that later, when a dog seems to be behaving strangely, you'll pay attention, because Sputnik, it turns out, knew about the whole thing before I did. Dogs do. They live a little bit sideways to the rest of us, in a way I now understand better than I'd like to.

So that's us. Number 9 Sefton Terrace. A mam who works nights, a sister who wears wellies to bed, a dog with a war crime for a digestive system, and a dad who's a name on a brass plaque on the seafront and a feeling I get sometimes when the wind comes off the water a certain way.

And me. Nico. The boy who, on a Thursday in May, woke up remembering Friday.

I keep promising you I'm not lying, and I keep wanting to, because I know how it sounds. So let me say it the way my mate Charlie said it to me, later, when I finally told her, because Charlie has a way of cutting through things like a chip-shop fork through a battered sausage:

"Right. So either you can genuinely remember tomorrow," she said, "or you've gone completely radio rental and I'm going to have to be really nice to you, which I'd hate."

And then she made me prove it.

But that's the next chapter, and I've gone on long enough, and Mam says I "don't half go on," which is rich coming from a woman who once spent forty minutes on the phone to the council about a wheelie bin.


## Chapter Three — Dad's Notebook

That night I couldn't sleep, which is funny, given everything, because sleep turned out to be the whole point.

I lay there listening to Saltmere doing its night-time noises — the sea going *shhh, shhh* like it was trying to get a baby off, a gull having an argument with another gull about something a gull would care about, the 11:40 train rattling along the cliff line on its way to somewhere better. And I did the thing you do when your brain has been handed something it can't hold: I went looking for the manual.

I went looking for Dad.

We've still got his things. Mam couldn't get rid of them and I didn't want her to, so they live in a blue holdall in the cupboard on the landing, behind the hoover that Sputnik's frightened of and the Christmas decorations and a fondue set we have used precisely once. I'd been in the holdall before — everyone whose dad has died has been in the blue holdall, or whatever their family's version of the blue holdall is. You go in there at 2am when you can't remember what his voice sounded like and you need to hold something he held.

There's a jumper that doesn't smell of him any more, which is its own small heartbreak. There's his RNLI pager, dead as a stone. There's a watch that stopped at a time that means nothing. And there's a notebook.

I'd seen the notebook before. I'd never really *read* it, because the first few pages are just boring — tide times, the price of diesel, a phone number for someone called Big Col, a shopping list with *FETA (the proper one not the supermarket rubbish)* underlined twice. Dad-stuff. The archaeology of an ordinary man.

But that night I read past the boring bit.

And about a third of the way in, the handwriting changed. It got smaller. Tighter. The kind of writing you do when you don't want anyone to read it, including, maybe, yourself.

It said:

*It happened again. Thursday I knew Friday. The Henderson boy and the dinghy — I knew before the shout came. Knew the channel marker too. Used it. Three lives. Worth it.*

And underneath, smaller still:

*But I am losing things. Cannot find Mama's face this morning. Had it last week. Now a blur where she should be. The more I look forward the more falls off the back. Have to be careful what I spend.*

I read it about nine times.

*Thursday I knew Friday.*

I'm not going to pretend I understood it all, then, at midnight, with my heart going. But I understood enough to feel the hairs go up on my arms in a wave, like when you walk into the cold part of the sea.

My dad had it too.

Whatever *it* was, the maths-test thing, the remembering-tomorrow thing — my dad had had it. He'd used it on the lifeboat. *Three lives. Worth it.* He'd known where the dinghy would be, where the channel marker was, before the call even came in. He'd been doing it for years, quietly, the way he did everything, and he'd written it down in his small frightened handwriting in a notebook behind a shopping list, so that nobody would find it until they were ready.

And there was that other thing. The thing I read and didn't understand and filed away to worry about later, the way you do, the way you always do, right up until later arrives.

*The more I look forward the more falls off the back.*

I didn't know what it meant.

I would.

---

There was one more entry that mattered, near the end, and I want to tell you about it now even though I didn't properly take it in until much later, because it's the kind of thing that's only frightening once you know what it's pointing at.

The last few pages were dated. My dad never dated anything — he was a man who genuinely could not tell you what day it was unless it was bins — so the dates jumped out. They were from the August he died. From the last week.

The very last entry was three words and a number, and the writing was so steady it was almost worse than if it had been shaky.

*Saturday. The Nab. —*

That's it. The Nab is Gallows Nab, the black headland, where the yacht got into trouble, where the wave took him.

He knew.

I think he knew. I think he woke up one morning that August and remembered a Saturday he hadn't lived yet, and in it he was at Gallows Nab in a sea like a grey animal, and it didn't end the way he wanted.

And he went anyway.

That's the bit I couldn't get my head round, then. I get it now. But then, at twelve, at midnight, all I could think was: *if you knew, why didn't you stay home?* Why didn't you ring in sick, fake a bad back, get someone else to go, do literally anything other than walk down to the boathouse and out into the thing you'd seen was going to kill you?

I was angry at him for about a week, on and off, in the privacy of my own head, where you're allowed to be angry at dead people, which is one of the few good things about them being dead.

It took me the whole of the rest of this story to understand his answer.

His answer was *three people came home that night who wouldn't have otherwise.* His answer was that knowing tomorrow doesn't mean you get to dodge it. It means you get to choose how you walk into it.

But like I say. I didn't get that yet. At twelve, with a notebook in my hands and a dead dad's small handwriting telling me he'd had the exact same impossible thing I had, all I felt was the floor of the world tilt very slightly, the way a boat does when something big moves underneath it.

I put the notebook under my mattress, which is the international twelve-year-old's equivalent of a bank vault.

Then I lay back down, and — because I was twelve, and because the human brain is a magnificent idiot — instead of being terrified, I started thinking about sports day.

Sports day was on Friday.

I was in the 100 metres.

And I had just realised that if I went to sleep, I might wake up knowing exactly how the race ended.


## Chapter Four — The Hundred Metres

You need to know two things about me and running.

One: I am not fast. I am the kind of fast where I come fourth in a race of five and the fifth person has a verruca. I'm wiry and I've got long legs that I haven't fully learned to drive yet, like a learner with a van, and on a good day, downhill, with a following wind and a dog chasing me, I am *adequate*.

Two: Macca Dodds is fast.

Mackenzie Dodds is in Year 8, a year above me, and he is built like a fridge that's decided to take up athletics. He's got a buzz cut and a way of looking at you that makes you check whether you've done something, even when you haven't, *especially* when you haven't. He has been, since the second week of September, my own personal weather system — you never know exactly when the storm's coming, but you learn to read the sky.

Macca's whole thing with me started because of my name. "Kostas." He decided, early on, that this was hilarious, and that I was therefore to be called "Kebab," which doesn't even make sense, kebabs being Turkish, not Greek, a distinction Macca would not care about even if he could find either country on a map, which I would put actual money on him not being able to do. He'd say it in the corridor — "Alright, Kebab" — and his mates would laugh, and it was never quite enough to report and always exactly enough to ruin a Tuesday.

So when I tell you that on the morning of sports day I woke up remembering the 100 metres, you have to understand the full picture: Macca Dodds was in my heat.

And in the version I woke up remembering, I lost.

Of course I lost. I always lost. In the memory I came third, behind Macca and behind a Year 7 called Dougie Trewin who has legs like a whippet, and afterwards Macca leaned down on the grass next to where I was getting my breath back and said, quietly, just for me, "Slow *and* a kebab," and walked off, and I lay there on the grass with the whole sky pressing down on me and hated him with a purity that frightened me a bit.

I woke up in my own bed with that hate still warm in my chest like a hot water bottle, and one extremely clear thought:

*Not this time.*

Now, here's the thing I have to confess, because if I'm going to tell you this story I have to tell you the bad bits too, or what's the point. I didn't lie awake agonising about the ethics of using a supernatural gift to win a school sports day. I didn't think about my dad and three lives and *worth it.* I thought: *I am going to make Macca Dodds eat the grass.* And I went down to breakfast practically singing.

Because here's what remembering tomorrow gives you, in a race. It doesn't make you faster. I want to be really honest about the rules, because the rules are the whole game, and a story about someone who can do *anything* is a boring story — Dad knew that, Hal knew that, and I learned it. Remembering tomorrow doesn't make you faster.

It tells you *exactly* how the race goes.

In the memory, I'd gone off too slow. I'd been so worried about Macca on my left that I'd hesitated on the gun — a tiny flinch, a half a heartbeat — and you don't get that half-heartbeat back, not over a hundred metres. In the memory, Dougie Trewin had faded at sixty, which meant if I was still with him at sixty I had him. And in the memory, Macca — and this was the gold, this was the diamond in the dirt — Macca always, *always* looked across at whoever was second around the eighty mark. Couldn't help it. Had to check. And when he did, he lost about a tenth of a second of pure forward Macca.

I didn't need to be faster than Macca. I just needed to be there at eighty metres, on his shoulder, so that when he looked across — and he would look across — I'd be the thing he saw.

---

I told Charlie about the gift before the race. I had to tell someone or I'd burst, and Charlie Begg is the someone you tell things to.

Charlie lives two doors down at number 5, she's got a fringe she cuts herself with kitchen scissors and a complete refusal to be impressed by anything, which is the single most impressive thing about her. We've been best mates since we were four, when she pushed me off the climbing frame for saying girls couldn't be astronauts and then, while I was crying, explained to me in great detail about Valentina Tereshkova, which is the most Charlie thing that has ever happened.

I told her by the long-jump pit, before my heat, in a fast whisper.

She listened with her arms folded and her head on one side, and when I'd finished she said, "Right. So either you can genuinely remember tomorrow, or you've gone completely radio rental and I'm going to have to be really nice to you, which I'd hate."

"I can prove it," I said. "Watch the race. Macca's going to look across at eighty metres."

"Everyone knows Macca looks across at eighty metres," said Charlie. "Macca'd look across at his own funeral. That proves nothing."

"Fine," I said. "Then watch this. After my heat, Miss Okonkwo's going to drop the starting pistol in the long grass and spend five minutes looking for it, and Dougie Trewin's going to be sick behind the equipment shed, and a dog's going to get onto the track."

Charlie raised one eyebrow. She has a very good eyebrow.

"A dog," she said.

"A dog," I said. "I remember it being a brown one."

The gun went.

I'd love to tell you it felt amazing. The truth is it felt *horrible*, in the way that finally getting the thing you've wanted often does. I went off on the gun — no flinch this time, because I'd already lived the flinch and I wasn't going to live it twice — and I drove those daft long legs the way I'd never managed to drive them before, because for once I wasn't running blind. I was running a course I'd already walked. Dougie faded at sixty. I was on Macca's shoulder at eighty.

And Macca looked across.

I will remember the look on his face for the rest of my life, and I have, as you know, more life to remember than most. It was *surprise*. Pure, undiluted surprise, the surprise of a fridge that has just been overtaken by a kebab. And in that tenth of a second where he was surprised instead of running, I put my head down and I went past him, and I crossed the line first, and the whole of Year 7 made a noise I'd never heard them make for me before.

I won the hundred metres.

Behind me, exactly on schedule, Miss Okonkwo dropped the starting pistol in the long grass. Dougie Trewin was sick behind the equipment shed. And a dog — a brown one, a daft springer belonging to someone's nan — got onto the track and would not be caught for the better part of ten minutes.

Charlie found me by the fence afterwards. She wasn't smiling. That's how I knew she believed me. Charlie always stops smiling when something gets real.

"Nico," she said, very quietly, looking at me like she'd never quite seen me before. "What *are* you?"

And over her shoulder, on the grass, I could see Macca Dodds getting to his feet, and I could see him find me with his eyes, and I knew — without remembering anything, just knowing, the ordinary way — that I'd just made a mistake I was going to pay for.

Winning felt brilliant for about four minutes.

That's the other rule nobody tells you about tomorrow. It always, always sends you the bill.


## Chapter Five — The Rules of the Game

Charlie believed me by Friday teatime, which for Charlie is the equivalent of anyone else converting to a major religion. And once Charlie believes a thing, she doesn't sit around going *gosh*. She gets a notebook.

She turned up at our back door on Saturday morning with an actual lab book — a proper hardback one with squared paper that her dad had nicked from work, he's a marine engineer at the dock — and a biro behind each ear, and a face like a scientist who's just been given a grant.

"If this is real," she announced, stepping over Sputnik, who was lying in the doorway being a draught excluder, "then it's got rules. Everything's got rules. Gravity's got rules. My nan's got rules and she's basically lawless. So we're going to find yours."

I made us toast. Pip was at her friend Maisie's, Mam was asleep upstairs after a night shift, and so for one Saturday morning the kitchen at number 9 became the world's most important laboratory, which mostly smelled of dog.

This is the chapter where I tell you the rules, because you need them. Not because they're interesting — although they are, a bit — but because everything that goes wrong later goes wrong *because of* these rules, and I want it to be fair. My dad had a phrase he used about the lifeboat: he said you can survive the sea if you respect it, and you drown if you think you've found a loophole. Turns out the same is true of tomorrow.

So. Here's what me and Charlie worked out, over toast, that Saturday, and over the next two weeks, with experiments, like proper scientists, except our lab was a kitchen and our test subject was me falling asleep on the settee at weird times.

**Rule One: It only happens when I'm asleep.**

Not daydreaming. Not meditating, which Charlie made me try, and which made me feel like a wally. Properly asleep. I wake up with a *memory* of the next day, the way you wake up with a memory of the day before — except it's pointing the wrong way through time. When I'm awake, no amount of squinting at the future does anything except give me a headache and make Charlie write "subject squinting, no effect" in the lab book.

**Rule Two: It's only ever *tomorrow*.**

About a day. From when I wake up to when I next fall asleep, roughly. I can't remember next week. I can't remember next Christmas, which is a shame, because I'd quite like to know what I'm getting. Just the next day. One turn of the world.

**Rule Three: It's not the future. It's *a* future.**

This is the big one. This is the one that nearly killed us, later — and I mean that almost literally. When I sleep, I don't remember what *will* happen. I remember what *would* happen — what's coming if nothing changes, if everybody, including me, just carries on exactly as we are. The default. The track the world is already on.

Which means the second I *do* something with what I know — the second I change anything — I've changed the future. And then my memory of it is wrong. Out of date. A map of a road they've since dug up.

I can't get an updated map until I sleep again.

You see the problem? You don't, yet, not fully. Neither did we. We thought it was brilliant. We thought it meant I had a do-over button on the entire world. We were idiots. We were happy idiots, eating toast, and I miss those two idiots quite a lot.

**Rule Four — and Charlie wrote this one in capitals and underlined it — IT COSTS.**

I'd told her about Dad's notebook. About *the more I look forward the more falls off the back.* She went quiet for a long time, which she does when something frightens her, and then she said, "We need to measure that. We need a baseline."

So she tested my memory. Old stuff. Dad-stuff. She made me write down everything I could remember about my dad — properly, in detail — and seal it in an envelope, and date it, and we hid it in the back of the airing cupboard.

"Why?" I said.

"Because if your dad was right," said Charlie, not looking at me, fiddling with the biro, "then the more you use it, the more you'll lose. And the only way you'll know what you've lost is if you wrote it down before you lost it. You can't notice a gap if you can't remember there was ever anything there."

I didn't like that. I didn't like it the way you don't like the dentist saying "this might be a bit sensitive." But I did it. I wrote three pages about my dad and sealed them up, and I'm so glad I did, for reasons that still make my throat go tight.

Because here's the thing.

That same week — the week of the maths test, and the race, two big uses, two nights of remembering — I lost something.

I went to picture Dad whistling through a blade of grass, the way Pip's always going on about, the thing she remembers, and I realised I couldn't hear it. I could *see* him doing it. But the sound was gone. Just a gap where the sound should be. A blur, where there used to be a noise I'd heard a hundred times on the cliff top.

I sat very still in my bedroom and tried to get it back, the way you chase a word that's on the tip of your tongue, and it wasn't there, and it wasn't there, and it wasn't *anywhere*, and I understood, in my stomach before my head, exactly what my dad's small frightened handwriting had been trying to tell me.

The future isn't free.

You buy tomorrow with yesterday.

And the first instalment I'd paid, without even knowing I was paying it, was the sound of my dad whistling through a blade of grass on a cliff top in the sun.

I didn't tell Charlie that bit. Not for ages. I'm not proud of it. But I'd just found this incredible thing, this superpower, this way to never lose again — and I wasn't ready, yet, to admit what it was quietly taking out of the till.

I told myself it was a one-off. I told myself I'd be careful. I told myself I'd only use it when it really mattered.

You can probably hear how that worked out.


## Chapter Six — The Woman at the Roost

There was a name in Dad's notebook.

I'd skimmed past it the first night, because I was twelve and it was midnight and I was busy having my whole understanding of reality rearranged. But when I went back, looking for the rules, looking for anything, I found it tucked between *tide tables* and *the more I look forward the more falls off the back.*

It said: *Went to see the Quirk woman at the Roost again. She is the only one who doesn't think I'm soft in the head. She says it has a name. She says I'm not the first.*

I showed Charlie.

"The Roost," said Charlie, "is that mad house up on Coble Lane. The one that looks like it's about to fall in the sea. My nan says a witch lives there."

"Your nan thinks the woman in the post office is a witch."

"My nan," Charlie conceded, "thinks a lot of people are witches. But the Roost one she's sure about."

The Roost is the last house on Coble Lane, right out on the edge where the town gives up and the cliff begins. It's tall and thin and leans very slightly seaward, like it's trying to hear something out on the water, and it's the colour of a wet seagull. Everyone in Saltmere knows it and nobody knows who lives there, which in a town this size takes real effort.

We went on the Sunday. I made Charlie come because I am, despite the running and the maths, a coward, and because the gate had a sign on it that said NO HAWKERS, NO COLD CALLERS, NO BOTHER, which is the most North East sign I have ever read.

We knocked. The whole house seemed to tick. Honestly — there was a sound coming from inside it like a thousand tiny machines disagreeing, a soft mad ticking, out of time with itself.

The door opened, and there was Halina Quirk.

She was small and round and old, with white hair pinned up in a way that had clearly given up halfway, and a cardigan that had pockets sewn onto the pockets, and reading glasses pushed up on her head and a *second* pair round her neck on a chain and, I would later learn, a third pair lost somewhere permanently in the cardigan. She smelled of toast and machine oil. She looked at me for about two seconds, and then her whole face did something complicated, and she said:

"You're Stav's lad."

Not a question. I went a bit cold. Nobody had called my dad Stav in two years. To everyone now he's "your father" or "the lifeboatman" or, on the brass plaque, KOSTAS, S.

"Yeah," I managed.

"Of course you are. You've got his trouble round the eyes." She looked at Charlie. "And who are you, the muscle?"

"I'm the scientist," said Charlie, which I think genuinely delighted Hal, because she snorted, and stood back, and said, "Then you'd both better come in. Mind the clocks. And don't let the cat out, he thinks he's allowed but he isn't."

---

Inside, the Roost was the ticking.

Clocks. Everywhere. On every wall, every shelf, the mantelpiece, the stairs. Grandfather clocks and carriage clocks and little tin alarm clocks and a cuckoo clock with the cuckoo hanging out at a sad angle and a wall of cheap kitchen clocks from the pound shop. And not one of them — *not one* — said the same time. They all said something slightly different. The ticking wasn't a clock. It was a hundred clocks, all disagreeing, all racing or dragging, a great soft argument about what time it actually was.

"You're wondering about the clocks," said Hal, lowering herself into an armchair and pointing us at a settee with a one-eyed black cat asleep on it. "Everyone wonders about the clocks. Move Schrödinger, he won't mind, he's dead and alive until you sit on him."

I moved Schrödinger. He minded.

"I used to be a scientist," said Hal. "A proper one, with a lab and a salary and an enemy on the committee. Sleep. I studied sleep — the sleeping brain, what it does in there when the lights are off. Dreams. Memory. Why you can lie down knowing nothing and wake up having sorted something out. The brain does its filing at night, you see. Takes the day's mess and decides what to keep." She tapped her temple. "And every now and then it files something that hasn't been delivered yet."

The clocks ticked.

"The reason they all tell different times," she went on, "is that I got it into my head, after a while, that time isn't the tidy single thing they teach you. We act like there's one clock and we're all on it, marching along into a future that hasn't been written. But the brain doesn't work like that. The brain is a prediction machine. It's running ahead of you all the time, guessing what comes next — that's all seeing *is*, really, your brain guessing and your eyes correcting it. Most people's guessing engine only runs a half-second ahead. Enough to catch a ball. Enough to flinch."

She leaned forward.

"But every so often, one comes along whose engine runs further. A whole day further. And when they sleep, and the filing happens, those predictions get filed *as memories*. The brain can't tell the difference between a thing it's worked out is coming and a thing that's already been. To you, tomorrow feels exactly like yesterday. Doesn't it."

It wasn't a question. Again.

"Yeah," I said, and my voice came out smaller than I wanted. "That's exactly it."

"Your dad sat where you're sitting," said Hal, and her voice changed, went gentler, "and told me the same thing. He was the third one I ever met. You'll be the fourth. It runs in families, sometimes — it ran in his." She looked at me very steadily. "Which means it might run on. Has anyone else in your house ever told you about a dream that came true?"

And I don't know why, but the first thing I thought of, sitting there in that ticking house with a one-eyed cat on my lap, was Pip.

Pip, two weeks before, at breakfast, putting down her spoon and saying, matter-of-fact, the way she says everything: *"I dreamed the bell rang. The one on the Brigg. The one that doesn't ring. It rang and rang and I had to go."*

And Mam had said *eat your Weetabix, Pip,* and I'd thought nothing of it, because six-year-olds say mad things, that's their whole job.

I didn't say any of that to Hal. Not then.

I wish I had. I've thought about that a lot since — what would have changed if I'd said it right then, at the start, in the ticking room, while there was still loads of time. Maybe nothing. Maybe everything. That's the thing about tomorrow. You only ever find out the one way it actually went.

"No," I lied. "Just me."

Hal looked at me for a moment longer than was comfortable. I think she knew. I think she knew most things, in that house, with all those clocks telling her all those different times.

"Well," she said at last, getting up, joints clicking like another small clock. "Then you'd better come back, hadn't you, Stav's lad. Because there are things your father learned the hard way, and there's no sense in you learning them the same way twice." She paused at the door, one hand on the frame. "Did he tell you what it costs?"

"He wrote it down," I said. "The more you look forward —"

"— the more falls off the back," she finished, nodding slowly. "Aye. He found that out late. I hope you find it out early." She fixed me with those clever old eyes. "What have you lost already?"

The grass. The whistle. My dad's mouth pursing on a cliff top, and silence where a tune should be.

"Nothing," I said.

It was the second lie I'd told her in ten minutes, and we'd only just met, and I think that's the moment I should have understood what kind of boy this gift was going to turn me into, if I let it.

I didn't understand it. I went home and slept, on purpose, to find out what Monday held.

Idiots, the pair of us. Happy idiots.

## Chapter Seven — A Short and Slightly Shameful Montage

I'm going to be honest with you about the next two weeks, because if I tidy them up you won't trust me about the rest.

The next two weeks I was, quite frankly, a menace.

Not a big menace. I didn't rob a bank, or fix the lottery, or do any of the things you're probably thinking I should have done, because — and this is the boring truth about remembering tomorrow — you can only remember the tomorrow you're actually *in*. I don't go to the bank. I don't buy lottery tickets. I'm twelve. My tomorrows are made of school, and home, and the bit of beach between them. So my menacing was strictly small-time.

Here is a non-exhaustive list of the things I did with my godlike power over time:

I knew when Mr Pegg was going to do a "no homework if you can answer this" question, and I had the answer ready, and the look on his face was worth a week of running.

I knew that on Wednesday the canteen was going to run out of the good cookies by second lunch, so I got to first lunch early and bought four, two of which I sold to a Year 9 for an outrageous profit and one of which I gave to Charlie and one of which I ate while looking at the Year 9 in a way I hoped was mysterious.

I knew that Pip was going to be inconsolable on Thursday because her favourite programme had been bumped for the snooker, so on Wednesday I quietly recorded the episodes she'd missed, and when Thursday came and she went into the full six-year-old apocalypse — flat on the floor, wailing like the lifeboat siren — I just pressed play, like a magician, and she looked at me as if I'd hung the moon, and I felt about ten feet tall.

That last one. I want you to hold onto that last one. Because that's the thing I told myself, every time the cost nagged at me: *I'm using it for good. I'm using it for Pip.* That's the lie you tell yourself right up to the edge of the thing you shouldn't do. *I'm only doing it for the people I love.* My dad used it to pull strangers out of the sea, and even he ended up writing scared little notes at midnight. What chance did I have, a twelve-year-old with a sister to dazzle and a bully to beat?

Mam noticed I was different, the way mams do — not the facts of it, just the shape. "You've got a look on you," she said one morning, pouring tea, three hours' sleep behind her eyes. "Like the cat that got the cream and then found out where they keep the rest of it."

"I'm just happy," I said, which was true, and which made her stop with the teapot halfway, because I don't think either of us had said that word in our kitchen for a while.

"Good," she said, after a moment, and her voice went a bit wobbly, and she covered it by telling me to take the bins out even though it wasn't bin day. "It's nice. You looking happy. Your dad had that look. Drove me mad." She smiled at her tea. "Couldn't tell him anything. Always three steps ahead."

I went very still over my Weetabix.

*Always three steps ahead.*

I'd never thought about it before. But of course. Of course he was. The man who always knew where you'd left your keys. Who'd start cooking for visitors who hadn't rung yet. Who took an umbrella on the one cloudless morning of the summer and got laughed at and then stood there, dry and unbearable, when the freak shower came in off the sea. We'd all just thought he was lucky. Or clever. Or *Dad.*

He'd been three steps ahead his whole life, quietly, never telling us, paying for it in little pieces of his own past, and we never knew, and now he was a name on a plaque and I had three pages of him sealed in the airing cupboard and a growing suspicion I should go and read them.

So I did. That night. I got the envelope down and broke the seal and read what I'd written about my dad three weeks before.

It said he could whistle through a blade of grass.

I read that line about the grass, and I knew, as a fact, that it was true — that I had written it, that it had happened, that there had been a sound — and I could not, for the life of me, *hear it.* It was like reading about a stranger. Like reading the caption under a photograph of a place you've never been.

Three weeks. Three weeks and a handful of stupid little uses — cookies, the snooker, a maths question — and I'd already worn a hole in my own dad.

I put the envelope back. I didn't sleep much. And in the morning, for the first time, I made myself a promise: *no more small stuff. Only if it matters. Only ever if it really, really matters.*

I meant it, too.

I want that on the record. I meant it.

The trouble is, the very next week, something started to matter more than anything ever had.

But first — Macca.

## Chapter Eight — Macca Dodds Settles Up

You didn't think Macca was going to let the race go, did you?

Of course he didn't. Lads like Macca don't let things go; they file them, and they wait, and they pick the moment, and the moment Macca picked was a wet Tuesday when I'd stayed late for the science club I'd joined entirely because Charlie was in it and because Mr Adesina lets you blow things up in a controlled fashion.

I came out of the science block into the rain and Macca was there, with Bez Hartley and Curly Robson, in the place where the CCTV doesn't reach, which every kid in every school in the country knows the exact coordinates of.

"Alright, Kebab," said Macca.

I'd love to tell you I was brave. I'd love to tell you I'd dreamed it the night before and I had a brilliant plan. But I hadn't slept on it, because I'd been at science club, and so for once I was exactly where you are when something bad happens: in the present, blind, with no idea what came next.

It's a horrible feeling, by the way. I'd got so used, in just two weeks, to walking through my days like a corridor I'd already been down, that being properly *in* a moment — not knowing, heart going, no map — felt like the floor had gone. I think that's worth saying. The gift didn't just show me things. It took away the ordinary, everyday, completely normal experience of *not knowing what happens next* — which, it turns out, is most of what being alive actually is.

Anyway. Macca took my bag. He tipped it out in the puddle — books, my good pen, the cheese sandwich I hadn't eaten — and he stood on the science folder, grinding it under his trainer, and the worst part wasn't even that. The worst part was that he wasn't angry. He was *bored.* He was doing it the way you'd swat a fly, like it was just a thing that needed doing, and that's when I understood that to Macca I wasn't even a person he hated. I was just weather. Something to push through on his way somewhere.

"That's for sports day," he said, like a man balancing the books. And then he and Bez and Curly walked off into the rain, and I knelt down and picked my swollen, ruined books out of the puddle, and I made a decision that I am not proud of.

I decided to use it on him.

I went home, and I fed Sputnik, and I told Pip her drawing of a dinosaur was excellent (it was a sofa with more legs), and I lay down on my bed at half six in the evening, on purpose, and I made myself sleep, so that I would wake up knowing every single thing Macca Dodds was going to do tomorrow — every word, every move, every weakness — and I would use it to take him apart in front of the whole school.

I dreamed.

But I didn't dream Macca winning, or me winning. I dreamed something I wasn't supposed to see.

I dreamed tomorrow, the ordinary track of it, and in it I was crossing the precinct by the bus stops after school, and there was Macca — but small, somehow, smaller than school-Macca — holding the hand of a little lad of about five, a little lad with the same buzz cut and a coat too big for him, and the little lad was crying because he'd dropped his ice cream, and Macca was crouched down in front of him in the rain saying, in a voice I'd never heard him use, "It's alright, Dunny, man, it's alright, I'll get you another, don't tell Mam, she's got enough on, eh? Just our secret. Stop crying now. I've got you."

*I've got you.*

I woke up, and I lay there in the dark, and I did not feel triumphant. I felt like I'd opened a door I had no right to open and seen something private, something that wasn't mine to know.

I knew now that Macca had a little brother called Dunny. I knew that their mam had "enough on." I knew that the great immovable fridge of Year 8, the weather system, the boy who ground my folder into a puddle because he was *bored* — I knew that he crouched in the rain and gave away his own money and told a little crying lad *I've got you,* the exact three words I'd have given anything to hear from someone, in the worst weeks of my life, two years ago.

I didn't take Macca apart the next day. I couldn't. I'd seen behind him, and you can't un-see behind someone, and that's another cost of the gift that nobody warns you about: it's very, very hard to keep hating people once you can see their tomorrows. Everybody's just trying to get a crying kid another ice cream before their mam finds out. Even the fridge.

I'd love to say that was the end of me and Macca. It wasn't. We'll come back to him, and when we do, it'll be in a storm, on a strip of rock, with the sea coming in — and I'll be very, very glad I didn't take him apart when I had the chance.

But that was all later.

The night after I saw Macca and Dunny in the rain was the night everything changed.

That was the night I dreamed about the red wellies.

## Chapter Nine — The Red Wellies

I need to tell you how the bad ones feel, before I tell you the bad one, or it won't land right.

The ordinary rememberings — the maths test, the cookies, the race — they came to me like ordinary memories. Flat, calm, true. The way you remember brushing your teeth. No drama. Just *information,* sitting there in the morning, waiting to be useful.

The bad one didn't come like that.

The bad one came the way your worst real memories come — the way I remember the night two policemen and a woman from the RNLI stood on our doorstep in the rain and Mam made a sound I have never heard a person make before or since. It came not as information but as a *wound.* It came with the volume turned all the way up and then ripped out of the wall.

I woke at 4am. Not gently. I came up out of sleep like a diver who's run out of air, gasping, the duvet on the floor, my heart going so hard I could feel it in my teeth, and for a second I didn't know where I was or when I was, and then it all arrived at once, in pieces, out of order, the way the worst things do.

Cold. I remembered cold. Not cold like a cold day. Cold like a hand round your whole body. Cold like the sea in February closing over your head.

The Brigg. I remembered the Brigg — the causeway out to the little island, the wet black rocks, but wrong, half-gone, the tide higher than it should be, white water breaking across the path where the path should have been dry.

Wind. A wind that wasn't a wind, that was a wall, shoving, screaming, full of spray, the kind of wind my dad used to read like a book and that I had only ever stood inside twice in my life and both times someone had had me firmly by the hand.

The bell. The old bell on the Brigg, the one that doesn't ring, the one that hasn't rung in my lifetime — I remembered it *ringing.* A flat, cracked, frightened *clang, clang, clang,* on and on, getting slower.

And the wellies.

Pip's red wellies. The ones she wears in all weathers. I remembered them in the water. Just the wellies, bobbing, turning, one of them, on its side, in the white churn at the foot of the causeway where the sea comes round both sides at once and meets itself, and the worst, the very worst thing, the thing that brought me up out of sleep with a noise in my throat, was that there was nobody in them.

An empty welly, turning in the water.

And underneath all of it, the way you know things in dreams that aren't dreams — the way I'd known the maths questions, flat and true — I knew the date. I knew the *when.* It sat in me like a stone.

It was going to happen on the sixteenth of August.

The sixteenth of August is two years to the day since my dad went out to Gallows Nab and didn't come back.

And it was, when I sat there shaking in the dark at 4am, exactly twenty-four days away.

---

I didn't sleep again. Obviously. I sat with my back against the radiator, which had gone cold hours ago, and I held my own knees, and I went over it and over it, trying to make it be something else. A nightmare. A normal, ordinary nightmare, the kind anyone might have, the kind that means nothing.

But I knew the difference now. I'd learned the difference. Charlie and me, with our lab book and our toast, we'd proven the difference six ways. Dreams slither away. This one didn't slither. This one sat on my chest with all its cold weight and would not move, the way the test had sat there, the way the race had, the way every single true thing had.

This was a memory.

It was just a memory of something that hadn't happened to my sister yet.

And here's the part that I have to make you understand, because everything I did next — all of it, the right bits and the catastrophically wrong bits — came out of this one fact, this one rule we'd written in Charlie's lab book in capital letters:

*It's not the future. It's a future. It's what would happen if nobody changes anything.*

Which meant — and I clung to this, in the dark, with my heart going — it could be changed.

It *had* to be changed.

I had twenty-four days, and a memory of an empty red welly turning in white water, and a rule that said the future was a thing you could reach into and rearrange, and I was twelve years old and completely, utterly certain that I could save her.

I want to go back and shake that boy by the radiator. Not because he was wrong to try. Because he thought it was going to be simple.

It was the least simple thing I have ever done.

When the light finally came up grey over the rooftops, I went downstairs. Pip was already at the table in her pyjamas, because Pip gets up with the gulls, drawing something with her tongue between her teeth in concentration.

"What you drawing?" I said, and my voice came out strange.

She held it up. It was the Brigg. The little island, the rocks, the broken chapel. And a small stick figure standing on it, and a wavy blue line that was the sea, coming in.

"It's me," she said happily, "going to ring the bell for Daddy."

I had to turn round and pretend to get something out of the cupboard so she wouldn't see my face.

"The bell doesn't ring, Pip," I said, to the cupboard.

"It will," said Pip, with total six-year-old certainty, going back to her colouring. "I dreamed it. It rang and rang and I had to go."

## Chapter Ten — What I Did First (and Why It Was Wrong)

The first thing I did was the most obvious thing, which is usually how you can tell it's going to be wrong.

I decided to make sure Pip never went to the Brigg.

Simple, right? The danger is the Brigg. Keep her off the Brigg. Keep her off it on the sixteenth of August and every day around it, glue myself to her, never let her near the causeway, and the empty welly turning in the water simply never happens. You can't drown on a rock you never stand on.

I didn't tell anyone. That was my first mistake, and I made it for the oldest, stupidest reason there is: I thought I could fix it myself. I thought it was *mine.* My gift, my sister, my job. I'd spent two years being the man of the house in a house that didn't need a man so much as it needed its dad back, and here, at last, was something only I could do.

So I became Pip's shadow.

It was the school holidays now — that's important, the long empty North East summer where the days go on forever and the sea fog rolls in at teatime and Mam's at work and it's just us. Normally I'd have my freedom and Pip would be at Mrs Okafor's or playing out front with Maisie under the loose supervision of whichever adult was about. Now I volunteered. "I'll watch Pip," I said, and Mam looked at me like I'd offered to eat a wasp, because I had spent the previous summer treating watching Pip as roughly equal to a prison sentence.

"You'll watch Pip," she repeated.

"I'll take her to the park. The good park. The one inland."

"The one inland," said Mam slowly. "Away from the beach."

"She likes the swings," I said.

Mam came over and put the back of her hand on my forehead, the nurse in her checking for fever, and I let her, and for a second I nearly told her everything, I nearly said *Mam, sit down, I have to tell you something mad and true,* and the moment came and went and I let it go, because I was twelve and certain and the certainty was like a drug.

So for a week I kept Pip inland.

Swings. The library. The little soft-play place that smells of feet and despair. The big Tesco, where she rode in the trolley and we played a game where she had to find things beginning with each letter. Anywhere, *anywhere,* that wasn't the sea.

And it worked. I want you to know it worked, that week, because the failure of it is the whole point.

On the seventh night I let myself sleep properly for the first time, on purpose, because I needed to *check.* I needed to look down the track again and see the future I'd built. I'd kept her off the Brigg for a week. I'd reshaped the days. Surely now, when I slept, I'd remember a tomorrow with no white water, no empty welly, no cracked bell. Surely now I'd remember an ordinary day.

I lay down, and I made myself sleep, and I dreamed.

And the cold was still there.

The cold was still there, and the wind that was a wall, and the bell — the cracked, frightened bell, *clang, clang* — but it was different now. It had *moved.* It wasn't the Brigg any more.

It was the harbour.

I remembered the old harbour wall, the high one by the lighthouse, slick with rain, and a small figure on the wrong side of the railings, and the sea below not even waiting, just *there,* grey and hungry, and the bell — there's a bell-buoy out past the harbour mouth, I'd forgotten, a navigation buoy that clangs when the swell lifts it — *clang, clang,* the same cracked note, the same slowing rhythm.

And red. A flash of red, going over.

I woke up with the noise in my throat again, and I understood something that turned my stomach to water, something that no twelve-year-old should have to understand at 4am alone in the dark:

I hadn't saved her.

I'd just moved it.

I'd kept her off the Brigg, and the danger hadn't gone anywhere. It had slid off the Brigg like water off a roof and found the next low place, the next way down to the same terrible thing. The harbour. The sea, which is all one sea, which goes all the way round our whole town, which had taken my dad and was, I now understood with absolute clarity, coming for my sister, and which did not care in the slightest *where* I refused to let her stand.

You can't dam a river by blocking one stream. The water just goes round.

I'd learned the first real rule of saving someone from tomorrow, and it was the cruellest one, and Charlie wrote it in the lab book the next day in letters so hard she nearly went through the page:

*YOU CAN'T DELETE IT. YOU CAN ONLY MOVE IT. AND IT WANTS TO HAPPEN.*

I needed help. Proper help. I needed the one person in Saltmere Bay who'd been down this road before and come back to tell about it.

The next morning I left Pip with Mrs Okafor — "I've got a thing," I said, "a science thing, with Charlie" — and I ran all the way up Coble Lane to the leaning house full of arguing clocks, and I hammered on the door of the Roost until the one-eyed cat appeared at the window looking disgusted, and when Hal opened it I didn't even say hello.

I said, "It's my sister. I've seen it. And every time I move it, it moves too."

And Halina Quirk, who I had lied to twice, looked at my face for one long second, and all the brightness went out of hers, and she said, very quietly, "Oh, lad. Oh, no. Not the way it was with your dad."

She stood back to let me in.

"You'd better tell me all of it," she said. "And this time, the truth. We haven't got the time for anything else."

## Chapter Eleven — What Hal Lost

I told her everything. The red wellies. The cold. The bell that doesn't ring, ringing. The sixteenth of August. Pip's drawing, Pip's own dream, *it rang and rang and I had to go.* And then, because she'd said *the truth,* and because I was so tired of carrying it on my own, I told her the other thing.

I told her about the whistle. The grass. The envelope in the airing cupboard, and the line I could read but not hear. I told her I was losing my dad, one piece at a time, and that I'd lied to her about it because I hadn't wanted it to be real.

Hal listened to all of it without moving, in her armchair, with Schrödinger the one-eyed cat pouring himself into her lap like a jug of black water. The clocks ticked their hundred different times around us. And when I finished, she was quiet for so long I thought maybe she'd dropped off, the way old people do.

Then she said, "Let me tell you about my Tom."

---

Hal had been married. I hadn't known that — there was no sign of a husband in the leaning house, no photographs, which I'd thought was just an old-woman thing, but it wasn't. His name was Tom, and he'd been, she said, "a great daft lighthouse of a man, six foot four, frightened of nothing, except the one thing."

Tom had the gift. Not like my dad, who'd come to it grown-up and used it on the lifeboat. Tom had had it all his life, since he was a boy, the same as me. It was why Hal had become a sleep scientist in the first place — she'd married a man who remembered tomorrow and she'd wanted, with the whole of her clever stubborn brain, to *understand* him.

"And we were happy," she said. "We were happy for a long time. He used it the way you'd think — small and kind and clever. Never greedy. He knew the cost. He'd lost things — his own childhood, mostly; he could barely remember being a boy, he'd spent it. But he said it was a fair trade, for a life three steps ahead. He said it was like paying rent." She stroked the cat. "And then we had Michael."

She didn't look at me when she said the name. She looked at the clocks.

"Michael was poorly. From very small. His heart — there was something wrong with the way it was built, and back then they couldn't do what they can do now, and when he was four the doctors told us, in a quiet room, that there was a day coming. They couldn't say when. But it was coming."

The clocks ticked.

"And Tom couldn't bear it. Of course he couldn't, what father could. So he did what you did, lad. Exactly what you did. He thought: *I can see tomorrow. I'll see the bad day coming, and when I see it, I'll change it. I'll keep my boy off the Brigg.*" She smiled, but it was a terrible smile. "So every night he went looking. Every single night, for two years, he made himself sleep and he went down the track looking for the day he'd lose Michael, so that he could be ready to move it, change it, dam the stream."

"Did it work?" I asked, though I already knew, the way you know the end of a story that's only got one way to end.

"You can't dam death, pet," said Hal softly. "It's the sea. It comes round. He'd see it one way, change everything, and it would come round another. A fever instead of a faint. A Tuesday instead of a Friday. And every night he looked, he spent. And do you know what he spent?"

I shook my head.

"Michael," said Hal. "He spent Michael. Every memory he had of that little boy. Looking forward so hard to save him that he burned up every yesterday he had with him. By the end — and the end came, pet, it always comes — Tom was sitting by a hospital bed holding the hand of a child he loved more than his own life, and he could not remember teaching him to ride a bike. Could not remember the sound of him laughing. Could not, at the very end, remember his face from one minute to the next, so that every time he looked away and looked back it was like meeting him again, and losing him again, over and over." She closed her eyes. "He saved nothing. He changed nothing. And he arrived at the worst day of his life with empty hands, having paid for the future with the only thing that ever made the present worth a damn."

The Roost was very quiet, under the ticking.

"He died two years after Michael," said Hal. "Of nothing the doctors could name. I think he'd just spent himself. There was nothing left in the till." She opened her eyes and looked at me, and they were wet, and fierce. "So now you know why an old woman lives alone in a falling-down house full of clocks that won't agree. Because I have spent the rest of my life trying to understand the one thing that took everything from me. And I will tell you what I learned, Nico Kostas, and you will listen, because your father didn't listen soon enough and I will not bury another one."

---

She made me tea I didn't drink, and she told me the rules underneath the rules — the things she'd worked out over forty years that my dad had only half-grasped and poor Tom had died not knowing. I'm going to give them to you straight, because you've earned them, and because Pip's life ended up hanging on every one.

"One," said Hal. "You cannot make a thing *not happen.* You've found that out. The future isn't a single thread you can snip. It's water. Block it here, it rises there. The harder and the more often you push, the more ways it finds to come round, and the more it costs you each time. Every look forward burns a yesterday. You've maybe got — I don't know. A handful left, before it starts taking the big ones. Your mam's face. Your sister's. Yourself."

I went cold. "Then how do I save her? If I can't change it —"

"I didn't say you can't change it," said Hal sharply. "I said you can't make it *not happen.* Those are different things, and the difference is the only door we've got." She leaned forward, and the cat slid off her lap with an offended noise. "You can't stop the day coming, Nico. But you can decide *who is standing there when it does.*"

I stared at her.

"That's the thing your dad learned at the end. It's in his notebook, if you read it right. He stopped trying to dodge his shouts, near the finish. He stopped looking forward to find the calls he could *avoid.* Because he worked out that the sea was going to come for somebody, with him or without him — and the only thing his gift was actually good for, the only honest thing, was making sure that when it came, *he was in the right place.* Not absent. Not hiding inland with the swings. *There.* In the boat. On the rock. With his hands out." Her voice cracked. "Three lives, the night he died. He knew. He went anyway. Not because he couldn't dodge it. Because he finally understood that the gift was never for getting *out* of tomorrow."

She took my hand, and her old fingers were surprisingly strong.

"It was for getting *into* it. At the right moment. On purpose. With your eyes open."

I didn't fully understand her. Not that day. I was twelve, and I'd just heard about a man who forgot his dying son, and all I could think, on a loop, was *a handful left, a handful left, before it takes the big ones.*

But I understood enough to know that everything I'd done so far — the hiding, the swings, the inland park, the keeping-her-off-the-Brigg — was not just useless.

It was the exact wrong shape of the answer.

And I had twenty-three days to work out the right one.

## Chapter Twelve — The Things You Can't Throw Away

I should have gone straight home from the Roost and told my mam everything, the way Hal said. *Don't carry it alone.* She said it three times. She practically tattooed it on me.

I didn't, of course. I was twelve, and I'd just been told by a wise old woman that the thing I most wanted to do — make the bad day not happen — was impossible, and that the best I could hope for was to *be there when it did,* which sounded, to a twelve-year-old, like being told you can't put out the fire but you're very welcome to stand in it. I wasn't ready to hear that. I wasn't ready to give up the idea that I could fix it cleanly, from a distance, without anyone having to be brave or scared or wet.

So instead of telling Mam, I had what I now recognise as a bad idea. I had several bad ideas in a row, actually, each one dafter than the last, and I'm going to tell you about them because they're the proof of Hal's whole lesson, written in my own stupidity.

My first bad idea was the wellies.

The danger, in every version I'd dreamed, came with red wellies. Pip's red wellies, in the water. So — and I genuinely thought this was clever, this was the kind of thinking that wins maths tests — I decided that if there were no red wellies, there'd be no drowning. Cut the thread. Snip the future. No wellies, no welly in the water, no Pip in the welly.

So I took them.

I waited till Pip was asleep, and I crept into her room past Sputnik, who was lying across her doorway looking at me with one disappointed eye, and I lifted the red wellies off the mat where they sit pointing at the door, and I took them downstairs, and I — I'm not proud of this — I put them in a bin bag and I walked down to the big bins behind Codd's amusements at eleven o'clock at night and I threw my little sister's wellies in a skip.

I felt brilliant for about four minutes. You'll remember that's exactly how long winning the hundred metres felt brilliant for. Four minutes is, I have come to understand, the standard warranty on a bad decision dressed up as a clever one.

Then I went home, and I made myself sleep, because I had to know — had to check the future, see the thread snipped, see Pip safe and dry and welly-less.

And I dreamed the cold, and the bell, and the white water. Same as ever. Except now my sister was in the sea in her socks. Bare feet, red socks, where the wellies had been, because of course — *of course* — a six-year-old who's decided she has to go and ring a bell for her dead dad is not going to be stopped by not having any wellies. She'll go in her socks. She'll go barefoot. She'll go in her pyjamas. The wellies were never the danger. The wellies were just what the danger was *wearing.*

I woke up and I understood, with a sick lurch, that I'd thrown away my sister's favourite wellies for nothing, and worse — I'd have to explain where they'd gone, and Pip would cry, and Mam would be baffled, and I'd have made everyone's life a small misery and changed precisely nothing about the big one.

I went and got them back out of the skip at six in the morning. They smelled of chip fat for a week. Pip thought it was hilarious. "My wellies went on an adventure," she told everyone, delightedly, for days. "To the bins. And back." If only she'd known.

My second bad idea was the bell.

If the danger was the *bell* — if Pip was going to the Brigg to ring the bell — then no bell, no reason to go. So one grey afternoon I walked out along the causeway at dead low water, on my own, which was its own kind of stupid, and I climbed up to the little stone arch where the bell hangs green-black with age, and I tried to make sure it could never ring.

I needn't have bothered. It was rusted solid. Seized to the frame, clapper jammed, two hundred years stuck. It couldn't ring if you paid it. I stood there on the chapel wall with the wind off the sea pulling at me and I actually laughed, a horrible little laugh, because the bell was *already* broken, already impossible to ring, and in my dreams it rang anyway. *The bell that doesn't ring, ringing.* I'd been hearing it for weeks and never once asked myself the obvious question: if the bell can't ring, and in the future it rings — then something happens, between now and then, to make it ring. Someone *frees* it. And whatever I did to break it further, the future just routed around, because the future had already accounted for a bell that couldn't ring and found a way to ring it anyway.

You can't snip the thread. I'd tried to snip it twice now — wellies, bell — and both times the future had simply grown a new one, the way a starfish grows a new arm, the way the sea comes round.

And both times, I'd paid.

That's the part I hadn't told you yet, and it's the part that frightens me most, looking back. The looking. All this looking — sleeping on the wellies, sleeping on the bell, sleeping again and again to check my clever snips had worked — every single look cost me, and I was spending like a boy who'd found his dad's wallet.

The morning after the bell, I went to picture my dad's hands. Just his hands — I'd been doing this, checking, the way you check a sore tooth with your tongue, ever since I lost the whistle. His hands on the winch. His hands gutting a fish in three quick movements. His hands doing the thing where he'd catch a wasp out of the air and let it go out the window, completely calm, *it is only a small angry thing, it doesn't want to be in here any more than you want it.*

Gone. The hands were a blur. I knew the facts — big, brown, a white scar across the left palm from the winch, a wedding ring he never took off — but I couldn't *see* them any more, couldn't feel them ruffle my hair, and I sat on the edge of my bed with chip-fat wellies drying on the radiator and understood that I had just spent my father's hands on a rusted bell that was never going to be the problem.

I was doing exactly what Tom did. Exactly. Burning up my dad to save my sister, snip by useless snip, and saving nothing, and changing nothing, and arriving — I could feel it coming — at the worst day of my life with my hands as empty as his.

I should have stopped then. I should have gone to Mam. I should have done a hundred things.

Instead I did the worst one. I decided that if I couldn't snip the thread with one clever cut, I'd find the safe version by brute force. I'd look, and look, and look, until somewhere in all the possible tomorrows I found one where Pip lived — and then I'd just *make that one happen.*

I'm shaking a bit, writing this, even now. Because that was the run-up to the night I nearly lost everything. Not Pip.

Me.

## Chapter Thirteen — Too Many Tomorrows

Here is what nobody tells you about a thing that costs you a little each time you use it: there is no alarm. There's no warning light, no fuel gauge, no kindly voice that says *careful now, you're getting low.* There's just you, and the thing you want, and the quiet, steady, invisible draining of everything you are.

I spent three days looking.

I'm not proud of any of it, and Charlie has made me promise to write it down honestly so that if Pip ever does this — if Pip ever gets it into her head to brute-force the future the way I did — there'll be a record of exactly where it leads, in her own brother's handwriting.

It started sensibly enough, if you can call any of it sensible. I'd sleep at night and check tomorrow. Then I thought: if I nap in the afternoon, I get another look — a fresh one, updated for whatever I'd changed. So I started napping. Then I thought: if I sleep in the morning too, three looks a day, I can run through versions faster, try things, see what shifts the white water and what doesn't. I told Mam I was tired — which by then was the truest thing I'd ever said — and she let me, because I'd "been doing so much with Pip," and she put it down to a growth spurt, and she had no idea that her son was lying in a darkened room forcing himself to sleep four, five times a day to ransack the future for a version where his sister didn't drown.

And the versions came. God, they came. Dozens of them, jumbled, overlapping, because I was changing things faster than I could track — I'd see the harbour, change my plan, nap, see the pool, change again, nap, see the Brigg, the buoy, the beck behind the school that floods, the boating lake, on and on, the danger sliding from place to place like mercury under a thumb, never gone, never *once* gone, just *moved,* exactly as Hal had said, exactly as I refused to accept.

And somewhere in the third day, the looking started to go wrong.

The memories stopped being clear. They came scrambled, out of order, bits of tomorrow stitched to bits of yesterday so I couldn't tell which was which — was that Pip at the harbour tomorrow, or Pip at the harbour last summer with Dad? Were those my own memories I was rifling through, or futures, or had I worn the join between them so thin that they'd started leaking into each other? I'd surface from a nap not knowing what day it was, what was real, whether a thing I was certain of had happened or was going to or never would at all.

Then the nosebleeds started.

The first one I barely noticed — a few drops on my pillow, I thought I'd knocked it. The second one wouldn't stop for twenty minutes and I knelt over the bathroom sink watching my own blood spiral down the plughole and felt, for the first time, properly, mortally frightened — not of the future, but of *me,* of what I was doing to myself, of the thin cold feeling that had got into me, the feeling Hal had named without my understanding it: *gone thin behind the eyes.* I caught sight of myself in the bathroom mirror and there was a grey boy in there I half didn't recognise, and I thought, with a calm that was the scariest part of all: *this is what it looked like for Tom. At the end. This is the till running empty.*

And then Charlie came round.

She took one look at me — grey, blood on my collar, three days unwashed, eyes like a boy who hadn't been properly awake or properly asleep in seventy-two hours — and she went very still, the way she does when something's real, and she said, "Nico. What have you done."

And I tried to explain about the versions, the brute force, the safe tomorrow I was *so close* to finding, I could feel it, just a few more looks — and I heard my own voice as I said it, fast and cracked and certain, the voice of someone who has stopped being able to tell the difference between determination and falling apart, and I saw Charlie's face, and Charlie's face was frightened, properly frightened, of *me.*

"Do you know what today is?" she said quietly.

"It's — " And I didn't. I genuinely didn't. I had to work it out, and I got it wrong, by two days.

"Do you remember," said Charlie, and her voice wobbled, which Charlie's voice does not do, "what we did for my birthday? Last month? You were there the whole day. Do you remember?"

And I reached for it — Charlie's birthday, a month ago, a whole ordinary happy day I knew I'd been at — and there was a blur. A smooth blank. I knew the facts: it was her birthday, I'd been there, there'd been a cake, her nan had said something about a witch. But the day itself — the actual day, my best friend's birthday, one of the good ones, one of the ones you're supposed to keep — I'd spent it. Worn it through. Paid it out, without even knowing, somewhere in the frenzy of looking, for a tomorrow I couldn't even hold onto.

I'd started spending the people I was trying to save. Then I'd started spending the people I wasn't even trying to save. I'd spent a piece of *Charlie.*

I sat down on the floor of my room, very suddenly, and Charlie sat down with me, and I told her — finally, fully, the way I should have told someone weeks before — all of it. The cost. The whistle, the laugh, the hands, the day at the seaside I couldn't find any more, my dad's face going soft and far. And now her birthday. And the nosebleeds, and the grey boy in the mirror, and how frightened I was, and how I couldn't stop, because stopping meant accepting Hal was right, meant accepting I couldn't snip the thread, meant accepting that the only thing left was to *be there,* scared and wet and possibly too late.

Charlie listened to all of it. And then she did the thing that, looking back, actually saved my life — not the rope on the rock, that came later, that was the brave-and-daft thing. This was the hard thing. The real one.

She took my face in her two hands — the hands that would hold the rope, a month from then — and she said, fiercely, furiously, with tears on her face:

"Then stop. *Stop looking.* Right now. You're not finding a safe tomorrow, Nico, there isn't one, you're just killing yourself trying — and even if you found one, what good's a you that can't remember your own dad, or your own best mate's birthday? What use are you to Pip then? You'll be like — like the man in the clock house. You'll be standing on that rock not even remembering why you came." She gripped harder. "We do this *my* way now. The slow way. The boring way. With a notebook, and a map, and other people, and a plan. No more brute-forcing it. No more lying in a dark room five times a day ransacking the future till your nose bleeds. If we ever truly need to know what tomorrow holds, we decide it *together,* you take *one* look, on purpose, and you write it straight down so it's worth what it cost — and that's the most you ever spend again. Never on your own. Never in secret. You hear me? The looking belongs to both of us now. Not you, asleep, alone, bleeding."

And I was so tired, and so frightened, and so glad, finally, to be told what to do by someone who loved me, that I just nodded, and cried, and handed it over.

That's the night I stopped trying to brute-force tomorrow.

That's the night the real plan started — not with a dream, but with two kids on a bedroom floor, one of them grey and bleeding and the other one holding his face and refusing to let him drown in the future the way Pip would nearly drown in the sea.

The looking had nearly finished me. Charlie finished the looking.

And the very next thing we did — the first thing of the slow way, the boring way, the way that actually worked — was the thing Hal had told me to do at the very start, and Charlie had been telling me to do all along, and I'd been too proud and too frightened to do.

We decided to tell a grown-up.

We decided to tell my mam.

## Chapter Fourteen — I Tell My Mam (More or Less)

I decided to tell Mam.

Hal had said *don't carry it alone* — practically the last thing she said as she stood at the door of the Roost with the cat winding round her ankles. "Whatever you do, Nico, don't be your dad. Don't be my Tom. Don't be a man on his own with a secret too big for one person. That's how it eats you." And she'd looked so sad and so serious that I'd promised, and meant it.

So that night, when Mam wasn't on shift, I waited until Pip was in bed and the telly was on low and Mam had her feet up with the cup of tea she lets go cold, and I sat down next to her, and I said, "Mam. I need to tell you something and I need you to not say anything till I've finished."

And she muted the telly, which is how I knew she'd heard the thing under my voice, and she turned to face me, and I told her.

I told her well, actually. I'd practised. I started with the maths test, because it was provable, and I told her about the race, and I showed her Charlie's lab book with the rules in it. And then, gently, because there's no gentle way, I told her I'd dreamed something about Pip. The sea. The sixteenth of August. I didn't say *empty welly.* I couldn't make my mouth do it. I said *I dreamed Pip gets hurt by the sea, and I think it's real, the way the test was real, and I think we have to keep her safe.*

Mam listened to all of it.

And here's the thing about my mam, and I love her, and I need you to not be too hard on her: she's a nurse. She has spent twenty years being the calm one in rooms full of people losing their minds with fear. She deals in the real. Blood pressure. Stitches. The actual, measurable world. And her husband — the love of her life, the chip-shop-queue man — had died two years ago this very month, dragged off a boat by a sea that took no notice of how much we needed him.

So when her grieving twelve-year-old son sat her down and told her, with a homemade lab book, that he could see the future and that the sea was coming for her other child on the anniversary of the day it took her husband —

She didn't hear a superpower.

She heard a boy who wasn't coping.

"Oh, Nico," she said, and her face just — folded, and she pulled me into her, and I could feel her heart going, and she said into my hair, "Oh, love. Oh, my love. Of course you're frightened of the sea. Of course you are. In August. Of course." And she rocked me a bit, the way you rock a much younger child, and she said, "We should have got you someone to talk to. I should have seen it. I've been working too much, I've left you carrying — oh, love, the *test,* you probably did hear about the test, your brain's just —"

"Mam," I said, pulling back. "Mam, no. It's real. I can prove it. Ask me what happens tomorrow, I'll —"

"Nico." And her voice had changed. It had gone careful, and that careful voice frightened me more than shouting would have. "I think we need to get you some help. Proper help. Someone to talk to about your dad. This — sweetheart, this isn't — people can't —"

And I saw it then, the whole shape of my mistake. I'd thought telling her would mean I wasn't alone. Instead I'd just shown her one more thing to be frightened about — me — when she already had Pip and the bills and the night shifts and a dead husband and not enough sleep. I'd added to the pile. And she didn't believe me, and worse, she'd *file* it, the way Macca filed things, and watch me now, and worry, and there'd be a doctor, and a leaflet, and a kind woman with a clipboard asking me how I really felt.

But here's what actually mattered, the thing I didn't clock until two nights later when I slept and looked down the track again:

She believed me about *one* thing. Not the future. But she believed her boy was frightened of the sea in August. And being my mam, being a nurse, being a woman who fixes things with her hands, she *acted* on it.

She cancelled the beach.

We'd had a thing booked — a proper Saltmere August thing, the carnival on the prom, the sandcastle competition, fish and chips on the front, the whole town down by the water on the big weekend. The weekend of the sixteenth. And quietly, the next morning, without telling me she was doing it for me, Mam cancelled it. Booked us a caravan. Inland. A site near Hexham with a swimming pool and a clubhouse and not one single inch of sea, forty miles from the nearest wave, for the whole of that fortnight.

"Change of scene," she said breezily over breakfast. "Do us good. Bit of green instead of all that grey."

She thought she was protecting me from my fear.

She had no idea she'd just picked up the danger, like you'd pick up a sleeping cat, and carried it somewhere new.

Two nights later — the one look Charlie and I had agreed I was allowed, deliberate, just the once, to see whether the caravan had changed anything — I slept, and I looked, and the cold was waiting for me again, and this time there was no Brigg, and no harbour, and no bell-buoy.

This time there was a swimming pool. A clubhouse. And a flash of red, going under, in forty miles of nowhere near the sea.

It had come with us.

It always, always came with us.

## Part Three — The Moving Target

## Chapter Fifteen — Forty Miles from the Sea

The caravan was called "Sunny Glade," which I mention only because there was no glade and there was certainly no sun. There was a field, and a clubhouse with a carpet that had clearly been chosen by someone who hated carpets, and a swimming pool with a flume and a sign that said NO PETTING, which made Pip ask what petting was for the entire fortnight, usually loudly, usually near strangers.

Mam was so pleased with herself. That's the bit that gets me, looking back. She thought she'd cracked it. She thought she'd found the one thing she could do — take her frightened lad somewhere green and quiet, away from the grey water and the brass plaque and the month that had ruined our lives — and she'd done it, and she kept catching my eye and smiling this hopeful, careful smile, like *see? See, love? We're alright. We're miles from it.*

And I had to smile back, because what else do you do, and the whole time my insides were a clenched fist, because I knew. I'd seen it. The pool. The flume. A flash of red going under in the deep end while a bored teenager in a Sunny Glade polo shirt looked at his phone.

So I did the thing again. The watching. The shadowing. Forty miles from the sea and I was *more* on edge, not less, because at least at home I knew the water — I'd grown up reading our sea, the way Dad taught me, the rips and the tides and the times. Here I knew nothing. Here the danger could come from a swimming pool, a duck pond, a water feature outside the clubhouse, a bath. Mam had taken Pip somewhere I couldn't read at all, and called it safety.

Three days in, I cracked.

Pip wanted to do the flume. She'd been on about it since we arrived. And I'd kept her off it — "it's too big for you, Pip," "you have to be this tall, Pip," lying, because she was easily this tall — and on the third day she finally lost it, the full apocalypse, flat on the wet tiles by the pool, howling, and Mam came over and crouched down and then looked up at me with an expression I'd never had aimed at me before.

"Nico," she said quietly, over Pip's wailing. "What is going on with you? You've been on her like a shadow. She can't *breathe.* This was supposed to be a *holiday.*"

And the fist in my chest just — opened.

"She can't go on the flume," I said. "She can't go in the deep end. Mam, please, I just need her to not —"

"Nico." Mam stood up. She had her arms folded and her jaw set and underneath the cross there was something worse, something frightened, the careful nurse-voice cracking. "Listen to me. I brought us here to get you *away* from being scared of the water. And you've brought it with you. You've made it *worse.* You're frightening your sister. You're frightening *me.*"

*You've brought it with you.*

She didn't know what she'd said. She thought she was talking about my fear. But it went through me like the bell-note, like the cracked clang of the buoy, because she was *right,* she was completely right, she just had the wrong reason — I had brought it with us, it had come in the boot of the car with the bags and the dog, it was always going to be wherever Pip was, and there was no green field in England far enough from the sea to leave it behind.

I think that was the moment I properly understood Hal. Not in my head — I'd understood it in my head days ago. I understood it in my stomach, on the wet tiles, with my sister howling and my mam frightened and forty useless miles between us and home. You cannot run from tomorrow. Tomorrow has your address. It came on holiday. It does the flume.

So I stopped fighting the wrong fight.

"Sorry," I said. "Sorry, Mam. You're right. I've been daft." And I crouched down by Pip and I said, "Hey. Hey, koukla. You want to do the flume? I'll come with you. I'll go first and catch you at the bottom. Deal?"

And Pip's wailing switched off like a tap, the way only a six-year-old's can, and she said, "*With* you?" like it was Christmas, and I said yeah, with me, I'll be right there, I'm not going anywhere, and I caught her at the bottom of the flume eleven times that afternoon, soaked, my eyes never once off her, and she screamed with joy every single time, and Mam watched from a lounger with the careful smile back on her face, thinking I was getting better.

I wasn't getting better. I was learning to stand in the water with her instead of dragging her out of it.

It's a small difference. It turned out to be the whole thing.

That night I rang Charlie from the freezing payphone-feeling reception of the clubhouse, on Mam's mobile, and I told her everything — the pool, the deep end, the flash of red, Mam, all of it. And Charlie, two hundred miles of phone line away, was quiet for a bit, and then she said the most Charlie thing she has ever said.

"Right," she said. "We've been doing this all wrong. We keep trying to work out *where.* But where keeps changing. So where's not the answer. We need to find the thing that *doesn't* change. Every version you've seen — what's the same? Not the place. What's the *same*, Nico?"

I closed my eyes in the horrible clubhouse, and I lined them up, the versions, like Charlie'd taught me, like a proper experiment.

The Brigg. The harbour. The pool.

Different places. Different every time.

But —

"The bell," I said slowly. "There's always a bell. The Brigg bell. The buoy out the harbour. And here — there's a bell on the clubhouse door, it rings when you go in, I heard it in the —" My mouth went dry. "There's always a bell. And there's always red. And it's always the sixteenth. And —"

I stopped.

Because the last thing on the list, the thing that was the same in every single version, was the thing I'd been refusing to look at, the way you refuse to look at a cut until you've decided how bad it is.

"And it's always Pip," I said. "Choosing to go. Nobody takes her, Charlie. Nobody pushes her. In every single one, she *goes.* She walks into it. Like she's — like she's going *to* something, not running from anything."

Down the phone, very quietly, Charlie said: "Then we've been asking the wrong question this whole time. It's not *where* is she going to be."

"It's *why* is she going," I said.

"It's why is she going," said Charlie. "Find out why, and you've got the only thing that doesn't move."

I hung up. I went back to the caravan. Pip was asleep in the top bunk with her red wellies on the floor below her, lined up neat, pointing at the door, ready, and her drawing of the Brigg was Blu-tacked to the wall above her head where she could see it last thing at night.

*Going to ring the bell for Daddy.*

I lay in the dark and I thought: I have been trying to lock the door. And all this time, on the other side of it, my little sister has been trying to get out — not because something's chasing her, but because she thinks our dad is on the other side, waiting to be found.

You can't lock a door against love. It just waits, and tries the window.

## Chapter Sixteen — Charlie's Map of Everything

We came home four days early. I engineered it, a bit — I "felt poorly," which wasn't even a lie, I felt poisoned with worry — and Mam, who could see the holiday wasn't working its magic, packed us up without much of a fight. The relief I felt turning back onto the coast road, seeing our grey sea come up over the hill like an old enemy I at least *knew,* I can't describe it. The sea had taken my dad. But it was *our* sea. I could read it. Better the devil you can read.

Charlie was waiting on our front step when we pulled up, lab book under her arm, and the second Mam had gone in to put the kettle on, Charlie grabbed me and marched me up to my room and shut the door and said, "I've made a map."

It wasn't a map of places. It was a map of *versions.*

She'd ruled the squared paper into columns. Down the side, every time I'd looked forward and seen the bad thing. Across the top: the things about each one. PLACE. TIME. WEATHER. WHO'S THERE. WHAT PIP'S DOING. And in each box, what I'd told her.

And when you saw it laid out like that — when you saw it the way Charlie saw it, cold and clear, the way a scientist sees a thing instead of the way a frightened brother feels it — the pattern jumped off the page and grabbed you by the throat.

PLACE changed every time. Brigg. Harbour. Pool.

TIME never changed. The sixteenth. Always the sixteenth. Always around dusk.

WEATHER never changed. Wind. Rain. A storm coming in off the sea. (Even the swimming pool version — there'd been rain hammering the clubhouse roof, I remembered now, a storm even forty miles inland.)

WHO'S THERE: this one made my skin crawl. In the early versions, when I'd been planning to keep her off the Brigg, *I wasn't there.* I was somewhere else, having safely separated myself from the danger. In the later ones, after I started shadowing her, I was closer — but always *too late,* always arriving, always reaching, never quite getting there in time. And in *none* of them — Charlie underlined this twice — in none of them was anyone else there at all. No Mam. No Hal. No crowd. Just Pip, and the water, and me, too far away, every time.

WHAT PIP'S DOING: the same words, every single box, in Charlie's neat capitals.

GOING TO THE BELL. GOING TO DADDY.

Charlie put her pen down.

"So," she said. "Here's what your map says, Nico, and you're not going to like it." She counted on her fingers. "One. You can't change the day. It's the sixteenth, it's the anniversary, it's locked, it's the most locked thing on here. Two. You can't change the weather. There's a storm coming on the sixteenth whatever you do — that's just *weather,* that was always coming, it's got nothing to do with Pip. Three. You can't change *why.* She's going to the bell because she thinks she can reach your dad. That's not going to stop because you hide her wellies. That's *grief,* Nico. You can't ground grief."

"Then what *can* I change?" I said, and my voice did a horrible cracking thing.

Charlie looked at me, and for once she didn't have a smart answer ready, and that frightened me more than anything.

"Two things," she said quietly. "Look at the map. Two columns are the only ones that move. *Where* — and you've proved you can shove that around, but it doesn't help, because she just goes to the water wherever the water is. And *who's there.*" She tapped that column. "That's the only one. That's the only thing on this whole page that you can actually change *and* that might actually matter. Who is standing there. When she goes."

And I heard Hal's voice, in the ticking room, plain as if she was sitting on the end of my bed:

*You can't stop the day coming. But you can decide who is standing there when it does.*

"I have to be there," I said. "Properly there. Not too late. Not arriving. *There.*"

"You," said Charlie. "And not just you, because look — every version where it's just you, you don't make it. You reach and you miss. Every single time." She closed the lab book. Her hand was shaking a little, I noticed, and Charlie's hand never shakes. "If you go alone, Nico, the map says you lose. Your dad went alone, near enough, didn't he? Three lives, but not his own. You can't be your dad. You heard the clock lady. You can't go out there on your own and be a hero, because the map says heroes who go on their own come back as brass plaques."

The room was very quiet. Downstairs the kettle clicked off and I heard Mam getting the cups down, two cups, the ordinary kindest sound in the world.

"So I don't go alone," I said.

"So you don't go alone," said Charlie. "So we build the one thing your stupid magic has never once shown you. We build a version where you're *not* on your own out there. Where there's a whole team standing on that rock. Because tomorrow's never seen that, Nico. It's only ever seen you, too late, by yourself. It doesn't know what happens when you bring people. Nobody's ever shown it."

I stared at her.

"That's the gap," I whispered. "That's the door. It can't show me a future I've never set up. If I get other people there — people it's never counted on —"

"Then for once," said Charlie, "you'll walk into a tomorrow nobody's remembered yet. Including you."

It was, I realised, the most hopeful and the most terrifying thing anyone had ever said to me.

We had eleven days.

And first, before anything else, I had to find out the truth about my little sister and the bell — because you can't bring people to the right place at the right moment if you don't understand, all the way down, what's actually pulling her there.

I had to find out what Pip knew about our dad that I didn't.

## Chapter Seventeen — What My Sister Knew

You'd think the easiest thing in the world would be to ask a six-year-old why she does something.

You'd be wrong. Six-year-olds are like witnesses in cop shows. They know everything and they tell you it in the wrong order, with crucial bits left out, and if you push too hard they go quiet and decide you're cross with them and then you get nothing but a wobbly lip and a demand for a biscuit.

So I didn't push. I did it the slow way, over a couple of days, the way Dad would have. I made myself Pip's favourite person. I did jigsaws. I let her win at snap, which is harder than it sounds because Pip cheats. I took her and Sputnik down to the inland park — *inland,* still, I wasn't a complete idiot — and I pushed her on the swings until my arms ached, and I waited.

And on the second evening, on the swings, with the light going gold and long the way it does in August, she told me.

"Daddy can't find his way home," said Pip, out of nowhere, the way she says the biggest things, swinging, looking at the sky. "Because nobody rings the bell."

I kept pushing. Easy. Don't stop. Don't make it a Thing.

"What bell, Pip?"

"The Brigg bell." She said it like I was being slow. "The lost-sailor bell. Daddy told me. When a sailor's lost at sea, you ring the bell on the Brigg, and the sound goes out over the water, and the sailor hears it, and he follows it home. Daddy *told* me, Nico. He told me when I was little." She kicked her legs. "But the bell doesn't ring any more because it's all rusty and stuck and nobody rings it. So Daddy's lost. He's been lost for ages and ages because nobody rings the bell so he can hear which way is home."

I had to keep pushing the swing. I had to keep my arms moving and my breath going, because if I stopped, if I let the swing slow down, she'd turn round and see my face, and my face was doing something I couldn't control.

Because I remembered now. Dimly — it was one of the things I'd half spent, half worn away, but it was still there, like a photograph left in the sun. Dad, on the Brigg, years ago, before Pip could even really walk, telling us about the bell. The old story. The lost-sailor's bell, that you rang in a storm so the ones still out there could find the harbour mouth in the dark. He'd told it like all his stories, half a true thing and half a thing he'd made better, and Pip — Pip had been so small — Pip had taken it and kept it, whole and bright, the way you keep the things from when you're tiny, because she had so few of him that every single one had to do the work of a hundred.

She thought it was instructions.

She thought our dad had told her, personally, before he died, exactly how to bring him home. Ring the bell on the Brigg, and the lost sailor hears it, and follows the sound back. And she'd been carrying that around for two years, this enormous secret job, this thing only she could do for him, waiting, I now understood with absolute horror, for the right moment.

"When were you going to ring it, Pip?" I asked, very gently, still pushing.

"On the special day," said Pip. "The day he went. Because that's when he'll be listening hardest." She said it so simply. So reasonably. The internal logic of it was *perfect,* that was the dreadful thing — it was completely mad and it hung together like a proof. "I dreamed it, Nico. I dreamed I rang the bell and it went *bong, bong, bong* all over the sea and Daddy heard it and he wasn't lost any more. So I have to. On the special day. I have to go and ring it so he can come home."

The swing slowed. I let it. I came round the front and I crouched down in the woodchips so I was looking up at her, my little sister, six years old, gap-toothed, hair everywhere, holding the whole impossible weight of bringing our dad back from the sea, all by herself, in red wellies.

"Does anyone else know?" I asked. "That you're going to do this?"

She shook her head, solemn. "It's a secret. If you tell, it doesn't work. Like a wish." Then her face crumpled with sudden worry. "You won't tell, will you, Nico? You won't tell Mam? She'll be sad. She gets sad about Daddy." Pip, six years old, keeping the secret to protect our mam, the exact same instinct that had me lying to everyone I loved. We were so alike it broke my heart. "It has to be a secret or it won't work."

And here's where, two weeks earlier, I'd have made my big mistake. Two weeks earlier I'd have said *Pip, no, the bell story isn't real, Dad's not lost, Dad's not coming back, you can't ring the bell, it's dangerous, you're never going near the Brigg.* I'd have tried to lock the door. And she'd have nodded, and said okay, and kept the secret deeper, and on the sixteenth of August she'd have slipped out anyway, because you cannot argue a six-year-old out of the one job her dead dad gave her. You can only make her hide it better.

But I'd learned. By God, I'd learned, the hard way, forty miles from the sea on wet tiles. You don't lock the door against love. You don't ground grief.

So instead I said, "No, koukla. I won't tell. It's our secret."

Her whole face lit up with relief.

"But," I said, "if it's so important — if it's for Dad — then I think it's too important for you to do on your own. I think your big brother should help. I think we should do it together. Properly. So it really works."

Pip considered this with the seriousness of a tiny judge.

"Together," she said, trying it out. "You and me."

"Me and you," I said. "And we'll make sure we do it right. Because if we're going to ring Dad home, Pip, we are not going to mess it up."

She flung her arms round my neck, off the swing, nearly knocking me into the woodchips, and she said into my ear, fierce and whispering, "I knew you'd help. I dreamed you helped. I dreamed you came."

And the back of my neck went cold, because there it was again — *I dreamed it* — and I thought of Hal saying *it runs in families, it might run on,* and I thought, my little sister has been dreaming the sixteenth of August too, all this time, from the other side. She's been remembering it forward, just like me. She's just been too small to know that's strange.

We were both remembering the same tomorrow.

We just remembered completely different things about it.

## Chapter Eighteen — The Lost-Sailor's Bell

I went to Hal that evening. I had to know about the bell — the real bell, the actual history, because Pip's version was a four-year-old's version filtered through two years of missing, and I needed the truth under it if I was going to build a plan I could trust.

Hal was in her kitchen, mending a clock. She mended clocks the way other people do crosswords — not to make them right, she said, because she didn't believe in right when it came to time, but "to keep my hands busy and my ghosts at arm's length." There were three little screws in a saucer and a spring on a tea towel and the one-eyed cat supervising from the bread bin.

"The Brigg bell," she said, when I asked, not looking up from the tiny brass guts of the clock. "Aye. Your dad loved that story. Used to tell it on the boat, the lads said, to the green ones, the new crew, to settle them in a swell." She fitted a screw. "It's true, mostly, which is the best kind of story. There was a bell. Still is, what's left of it. They put it on the Brigg two hundred years ago, before the lighthouse, before any of the modern gear. A fog bell. A storm bell. When the weather came down and a boat was out and couldn't see the harbour mouth, someone — usually some poor soul who'd drawn the short straw, or a wife who wouldn't sleep — would go out along the causeway at low water and ring it. And the sound carries over water in a way it never does over land. The men out in it would hear the bell and they'd know — *that way's home. That way's the rock. Steer for the bell.*"

She set the clock down.

"They reckon it brought a fair few home, over the years. They also reckon," and here she looked at me, "it cost a few their lives, the ringers, the ones who went out and misjudged the tide and got cut off, or got taken off the causeway by a sea. Because here's the thing about the Brigg bell, Nico, the thing the pretty story leaves out. You only ever ring it in the worst weather. That's the whole point of it. You only need it when the sea's at its most dangerous. So the very people who went to ring it for the lost ones — they went out in exactly the conditions most likely to make *them* lost." She shook her head slowly. "It's a kind thing and a cruel thing, both at once. Like most things worth doing."

A kind thing and a cruel thing, both at once.

I thought about my dad telling that story to a frightened four-year-old on a calm summer day, never dreaming she'd file it away as a set of instructions. I thought about Pip, planning to go out and ring it in a storm, on the worst day of the year, alone, in red wellies, to bring her lost sailor home — doing the most dangerous thing there is for the most loving reason there is.

A kind thing and a cruel thing, both at once.

"Does it still work?" I asked. "The bell. Could you even ring it?"

"It's rusted to the frame, last I knew," said Hal. "Hasn't rung in — oh, donkey's years. Stuck solid. You'd have to want it very badly to make that bell ring." She picked up the next clock. "Why d'you ask, lad? What's your sister got to do with a bell?"

So I told her. The lost-sailor story. Pip's secret. *Going to ring the bell for Daddy.* The plan to do it together. And Charlie's map, and the only column that moved, and the gap — the future nobody had ever shown me, the one where I wasn't alone on the rock.

Hal listened with her clever old hands gone still on the broken clock. And when I finished she was quiet for a long time, and then she said something that I have never forgotten and never will, even with everything else that fell out of my head that summer.

"You know what you're describing, don't you, Nico?" she said softly. "You. Your friend. Whoever else you can muster. Going out onto that rock in a storm, on the worst day of the year, to bring a lost one home." She smiled, and it was the saddest, proudest smile I've ever seen on a human face. "You're going to ring the bell. The whole lot of you. You're going to be the ones who go out into the worst of it so somebody else can find their way home. That's the oldest job there is in a town like this. Your dad did it. His crew did it. And now —" she reached over and put her oil-stained hand flat against the side of my face, warm and dry as old paper — "now it's you. The lost-sailor's bell. Except the sailor you're ringing home isn't your dad, pet."

"Then who?" I said.

"Your sister," said Hal. "She's the one who's lost. She has been for two years, out on the water in her own head, looking for him. You can't bring your dad home, Nico. Nobody can. But you might just bring *her* home. If you've got the nerve. And the people. And the sense not to go out there on your own like a hero in a story, because heroes in stories ring the bell and then the sea takes *them,* and I have buried enough heroes."

The clocks ticked, all disagreeing, all telling their hundred different times.

"Build your team," said Hal. "Build it well. And come and tell me, because there's an old woman who knows the tide tables off Saltmere better than the coastguard, and I'll not sit in this house full of stopped time while Stav's bairns go out on that rock alone. Not again. Not this family. Not on my watch."

It was the first time anyone bigger than me had said *we.*

I went home and I slept, and for once I didn't go looking for tomorrow. I just slept, like an ordinary boy, and it was the best sleep I'd had since May.

In the morning I started building the team.

## Chapter Nineteen — Asking Macca Dodds for Help

Of all the things I did that summer, walking up to Macca Dodds and asking him for help was, on paper, the maddest.

But Charlie's map didn't lie. Every version where it was just me, I lost. I needed people. And not just any people — I needed someone who knew the Brigg, who knew the tides, who could read that strip of rock and the water round it the way my dad used to. Charlie was clever and brave but she got seasick on the ferry to the island and she'd tell you that herself. Hal could do tide tables but she was seventy-odd and her hip was, in her words, "a traitor." I needed someone young, and strong, and at home on the water.

And the thing was — I knew, because I'd accidentally seen behind him, because I'd dreamed him in the rain with Dunny — I knew something about Macca Dodds that the rest of the school didn't.

I knew the Dodds family had a boat.

A coble — one of those open fishing boats, high at the front, that they've used on this coast for about a thousand years. It had been Macca's grandad's, and then his dad's, and his dad was — well. The whole school sort of half-knew the dad was "away," and the half-knowing was the kind that meant prison, and nobody said it to Macca's face because Macca's face was not a face you said things to. And since the dad went away, it was Macca, fourteen years old, who took the coble out before school in the season and ran a few crab pots off the Brigg, quietly, and sold the catch to the man at the fish shack on the front, quietly, to put a bit extra in the tin at home. Quietly. Everything about it quiet, because Macca would rather have died than have anyone at Brindley High know that the great immovable fridge of Year 8 got up at five in the morning to haul crab pots so his little brother could have school shoes that fit.

I knew all that. And I knew Macca would hate, more than anything in the world, that I knew it.

Which is exactly why I had to do it right.

I found him at the fish shack on the front, on a Tuesday morning, unloading a crate. No Bez, no Curly — they didn't get up for this, this wasn't a thing you did for an audience. Just Macca, in oilskins too big for him that had been his dad's, smelling of bait and salt, looking about ten years older than he did in the corridor.

He saw me coming and his face shut like a door. "What do *you* want, Kebab."

I'd thought about how to do this. I'd thought about it a lot. And I'd decided the only way through Macca Dodds was straight through the middle, no cleverness, no foresight, no advantage. Just the truth, handed over flat, the way you'd hand over a tool.

"I need your help," I said. "And your boat. And I'll pay you for the morning's catch you'll miss, I've got it saved. And I need you because you know the Brigg and the tides better than anyone our age in this town, and I'm not being soft, I mean it. My little sister's going to be in trouble out there on the sixteenth and I can't get to her on my own."

Macca stared at me. Whatever he'd expected me to say, it wasn't that.

"The sixteenth," he said slowly. "That's spring tides. Big tides. The causeway floods like — " he stopped. "Why would your sister be on the Brigg at spring tides? She daft?"

"No," I said. "She's six. And she misses our dad. And she's got it into her head she has to ring the old bell out there to bring him home." My voice did the cracking thing. I let it. I'd decided not to hide it. "Our dad was on the lifeboat. He drowned two years ago. The sixteenth's the day. And my sister thinks if she rings the bell on the worst day in the worst weather, he'll hear it and come home. And she's going to try, Macca. I know she is. And I can't stop her, I can only be there when she does it, and I can't do it by myself."

The front went quiet except for the gulls and the slap of the water against the wall.

Macca looked at me for a long, long time. And something moved behind his face — something I recognised, because I'd seen it in the mirror for two years. The thing that happens to your face when somebody says a true thing about a parent who's gone and not coming back.

"Your dad was Kostas," he said finally. "The lifeboatman. Off the Nab." It wasn't a question. Everyone in Saltmere knew the lifeboatman off the Nab.

"Yeah."

Macca looked out at the water. When he spoke again his voice was different — lower, rougher, the corridor gone out of it entirely.

"My grandad went off the lifeboat," he said. "Nineteen-eighty-whatever. Before me. Different sea, same rock, near enough." He spat into the harbour, not nasty, just a fisherman's punctuation. "It's why my mam can't stand me taking the coble out. Does her head in. But somebody's got to, eh." He looked back at me, and the look had something new in it, something almost like recognition, one survivor clocking another. "You should've just asked, man. You didn't have to do the whole speech. We don't leave bairns on the Brigg in a spring tide. Nobody does. That's not a favour. That's just — that's the rule."

I hadn't expected that. I'd expected to have to bargain, threaten, beg. I had not expected Macca Dodds to look at me like I was being thick for thinking I'd had to ask at all.

"So you'll help?"

"'Course I'll help, you melt," said Macca. And then, because he was still Macca, and a leopard doesn't change all its spots in one morning: "Doesn't mean we're mates. I still think you're a weird little gadgie and your running style's a disgrace."

"Fair enough," I said.

"And I'm keeping the money."

"Fair enough."

He hauled the crate up onto his shoulder like it weighed nothing. Then he stopped, and without looking at me, in a voice so quiet I almost missed it under the gulls, he said: "How'd you know I had a boat, anyway? Nobody knows I do the pots."

And I had a choice. I could've lied. I'm good at lying, by now you know that about me. But I was so tired of it, the lying, it had cost me so much, and Macca had just handed me the truth, flat, like a tool, so I handed it back.

"I dream things," I said. "Properly. Things that are going to happen, or things that are true. I can't really explain it. And a few weeks ago I dreamed you in the rain by the bus stops, getting your little brother another ice cream because he'd dropped his. Telling him you'd got him. *I've got you.*" Macca had gone very still. "I wasn't snooping. It just — came to me. And I never told anyone, and I never will, and I'm only telling you now so you know I'm not having you on. That's how I know about the boat. And that's how I know about the sixteenth. I dream it, Macca. And in every single dream where I go out there on my own, I don't make it. That's why I need you."

Macca Dodds, fourteen years old, the weather system, the fridge, stood on the front with his dad's oilskins flapping and a crate of crab on his shoulder, and looked at me for a long moment.

"You're not having me on," he said. It wasn't a question either. "Anyone else'd be having me on. You're not."

"No."

"Right," said Macca. He turned to carry the crate up to the shack. "Then we'd best get the tides sorted. Bring your clock woman. And that gobby lass that follows you about — Begg. She's not scared of owt, that one, I've seen her tell Mr Pegg his test was unfair to his face." Almost — almost — the ghost of something like respect. "Aye. Bring the lot. We'll get your sister off that rock."

I walked home along the front with the gulls screaming and the sea going *shhh, shhh,* and for the first time since the night of the red wellies, I wasn't carrying it on my own.

I had a clock woman who knew the tides, a gobby lass with a map of every tomorrow, a fisherman with a boat and a grandad off the same rock, and a daft dog who, I was about to discover, knew more than any of us.

I had a crew.

## Chapter Twenty — The Crew

We met at the Roost.

It is, looking back, one of the strangest gatherings ever held in Saltmere Bay: an old retired sleep scientist, a thirteen-year-old who could remember tomorrow, a girl with a homemade lab book, a fourteen-year-old fisherman in his dead grandad's oilskins, and a Staffordshire bull terrier crossed with a draught excluder, all crammed into a leaning house full of clocks that wouldn't agree, planning to save a six-year-old from the sea.

Hal had the tide table out on the kitchen table, weighed down at the corners with clocks. Schrödinger sat on the bit she needed and had to be repeatedly relocated. Charlie had her map. Macca had a chart of the Brigg he'd drawn from memory — and it was *good,* properly good, every gully and gutter and the deep channel on the north side where the two tides meet and fight, which is the bit, he said grimly, that kills people. And Sputnik had a biscuit, which he'd located with the unerring instinct of his orbiting brain.

We laid it all out. And here is the plan we built, in that ticking kitchen, eleven days before the sixteenth — and I'm telling you all of it now, properly, because it matters that you know what we *meant* to happen, so that later, when almost none of it happens the way we planned, you understand that this is the whole, terrible, beautiful point of the thing I've been trying to tell you about tomorrow.

We meant to do it in daylight.

That was the centre of it. Charlie's whole insight, Hal's whole lesson. Pip was going to go to the bell on the sixteenth no matter what — you can't ground grief. Fine. So we wouldn't stop her. We'd take her. *We'd do it first.* On the morning of the sixteenth, low water, calm, before the storm came in off the sea in the evening — the whole crew, all of us, would walk Pip out along the dry causeway to the Brigg, in the safe part of the day, and we'd help her ring the bell for Dad. Properly. Together. With Macca to free the rusted clapper and Hal to time the tide and Charlie to hold her hand and me right beside her.

Because here was the thing we'd worked out, the thing that cracked it open: if Pip rang the bell *with us, in daylight, safe* — then she wouldn't need to sneak out and do it *alone, at dusk, in the storm.* The whole reason every tomorrow ended in white water was that Pip went secretly, at the worst time, because she thought it had to be a secret or it wouldn't work. *If you tell, it doesn't work, like a wish.* So we'd make a new version. One where it wasn't a secret. One where the people who loved her helped her do the loving, dangerous, necessary thing — safely, in the light, with her hand held.

We'd ring the bell before the sea ever got its chance.

"It's good," said Hal, when we'd laid it all out, and she said it seriously, weighing it like a clock spring. "It's clever and it's kind and it's got the shape of the right answer." She looked round at us all. "But you understand it might not be enough. You understand that the sea's been planning this longer than you have, and it doesn't read your plans, and the one thing your gift has told you, Nico, over and over, is that the water finds a way round. So we don't just have the good plan. We have the bad plan as well. The if-it-all-goes-wrong plan. Because in a town like this, the people who survive the sea are the ones who planned for it being worse than they hoped."

So we built that too. The if-it-all-goes-wrong plan. Macca's coble, fuelled and ready and tied up close, not out at his pots. Hal with the coastguard's number and a reason ready, because "an old woman ringing about a bad feeling gets nowhere, but an old woman ringing to say there's a child cut off on the Brigg in a rising tide gets a helicopter." Charlie with the torches and the throw-line Macca brought, because Macca, it turned out, had done his RNLI youth sessions and knew how to throw a line and what a rip looked like and a hundred other things that fishing families know in their bones. Me, with Pip, never more than an arm's length, on the sixteenth, all day, whatever happened.

And the dog.

"Why's the dog here?" said Macca, not unkindly, looking at Sputnik, who had fallen asleep upside down against a grandfather clock with his legs in the air.

"He's not here for a reason," I started to say.

But Hal was watching Sputnik with her clever old eyes. "Dogs know," she said. "They live a little sideways to us. Tom's old dog used to bark at the door a full minute before Tom woke from a bad night. Knew before *Tom* knew." She nodded at Sputnik. "If anything starts to go wrong on the sixteenth — watch that animal. He'll feel it before any of you do."

I didn't think much of it at the time.

I think about it all the time now. Because when it all went wrong — and it did, it went wrong in the one way none of us had planned for — it was Sputnik, our daft orbiting draught-excluder of a dog, who knew first.

We went over it until we all had it cold. Where everyone stood. Who did what. The signal. The fall-backs. Charlie wrote it all in the lab book in her hard capital letters. And when we were done, when everyone had gone and it was just me and Hal and the ticking, she put her hand on my shoulder and said:

"You've done the one thing your dad never managed, you know. And my Tom never managed. You've stopped trying to do it alone." She looked very tired, suddenly, and very old, and very kind. "Now do me one last thing, Nico, before the day. The most important thing of all. And it'll be the hardest."

"What?"

"Stop looking," said Hal. "No more dreaming it forward. You've got the plan. You've got the crew. From here to the sixteenth, you let tomorrow be a surprise like it is for the rest of us. Because every time you look now, you spend, and you haven't got much left — I can see it, lad, you've gone thin behind the eyes, the way Tom did at the end. And worse than that: every time you look, you make a *new* tomorrow, and you'll panic, and you'll change the plan, and a good plan changed in a panic is no plan at all." Her grip tightened. "Promise me. No more looking. Walk into the sixteenth blind, like a normal lad, holding your sister's hand and trusting your crew. Can you do that?"

And I said yes.

And I meant it.

And I broke it.

I broke it on the fifteenth of August, the night before, when I couldn't sleep and couldn't bear not knowing and told myself just once, just one last look, just to be sure — and it cost me more than everything Hal had warned me about put together.

But I'm getting ahead of myself again.

That's still my whole problem.

## Chapter Twenty-One — The Last Good Days

The days before the sixteenth were, strangely, some of the best of that whole summer.

I know how that sounds. But it's true, and Hal said later it's always true — that the calm before a thing you're dreading can be the sweetest time there is, because you've stopped pretending the thing isn't coming, and once you stop pretending, you can actually *be* there, in the days you've got, instead of fretting them away.

So I was there. For once in my life, I was just *there.*

Me and Pip built a city out of cardboard boxes in the back yard and Sputnik destroyed it and we built it again. We went to the inland park and I taught her to pump her own legs on the swing so she didn't need pushing, which she found insulting and then, once she'd got it, magnificent. Charlie came round and the three of us watched terrible films and ate toast, and Charlie, who is not soft, let Pip do her hair into about forty tiny plaits and sat there looking like an exploded cushion and never said a word about it, which is the kindest thing I've ever seen Charlie do.

And Mam.

Mam was working too much, as ever, but one evening — the thirteenth, I think — she came off an early and we had tea together, the three of us, and afterwards she didn't put the telly on. She sat at the table with her cold cup of tea and she watched me and Pip do a jigsaw, and she had a look on her face I hadn't seen for two years.

"You've been brilliant with her," she said quietly, when Pip had gone to find a missing piece. "This summer. I know I — I've not been here as much as I should. The shifts. I know it falls on you." She turned her cup round and round. "Your dad would be — " she stopped, and pressed her lips together, and started again. "He'd be that proud of you, Nico. The way you've looked after her. The man you're turning into."

And I nearly told her. Right there. The whole thing — the test, the race, the red wellies, the crew, the bell, all of it. The moment opened up like a door, the way it had before, and this time I actually got as far as "Mam, there's something —"

And then her phone went.

It was the hospital. Could she possibly, they were short, two off sick, the sixteenth, the night shift, they wouldn't ask but —

And I watched my mam close her eyes for a second, the way she does, and then say, "Yeah. Yeah, course. I'll be in. No, it's fine." Because that's my mam. Because the bills don't care what month it is, and somebody else's family was going to need her on the sixteenth, and that's just what she does, the way Dad went out into the sea, the way Macca hauls the pots — the people in my town just *go,* when they're needed, even on the worst days, especially on the worst days.

She put the phone down and rubbed her face. "Sorry, love. What were you going to say?"

And the door had shut again. Because now I knew Mam would be at the hospital on the sixteenth, forty minutes away, all night. Which meant the night of the sixteenth would be me and Pip and Mrs Okafor next door with her key. Which meant the crew was more important than ever. Which meant this was not the moment to hand my exhausted mother one more impossible thing to carry into a night shift.

"Nothing," I said. "Just — love you, Mam."

She blinked. We don't say it much, our family. We're more of a *put-the-kettle-on* family than a *say-it-out-loud* family.

"Love you too, you daft article," she said, and her eyes went bright, and she got up quick and busied herself with the cups so I wouldn't see, and I let her, because that's the deal, the one we made without words, the only way our family knows how.

I've gone over that evening a thousand times. The jigsaw. The cold tea. *He'd be that proud of you.* The phone going. The door shutting. If the hospital hadn't rung — if I'd had thirty more seconds — would I have told her? Would everything have gone differently?

Maybe. Maybe not. That's the thing about tomorrow that took me the longest to learn, and that I'd give a lot to un-learn: you only ever get the one version that actually happens. All the others — the ones where the phone doesn't ring, where you say the thing, where the door stays open — they're not real. They were never real. They're just the ghosts you live with afterwards.

The next two days were ordinary and golden. And then it was the fifteenth of August, the night before, and I couldn't sleep, and I made the worst decision of the whole story.

## Chapter Twenty-Two — One Last Look

I want to explain why I did it, even though there's no good explanation, because I think you deserve to understand it even if you can't forgive it. I'm not sure I forgive it, and I was there.

It was half past eleven on the night of the fifteenth. Pip was asleep. Mam was on a late, not back till one. The house ticked and settled. And I lay in the dark with the plan going round and round in my head — the daylight, the crew, the bell, the if-it-all-goes-wrong, Macca's coble, Hal's coastguard, Charlie's torches — and I could not, for the life of me, be sure it would work.

And I had this thing. This gift. This terrible, brilliant thing that could tell me, right now, tonight, whether tomorrow ended with my sister safe or with an empty red welly turning in the water.

Hal had made me promise. *No more looking.* And I understood why. I understood every word of it. *Every time you look you spend, and you haven't got much left.* I understood that looking might panic me into wrecking a good plan. I understood all of it.

But understanding a thing and being able to bear it are two different animals. And I could not bear, lying there in the dark, the not-knowing. After a whole summer of walking through my days like corridors I'd already been down, the ordinary human darkness of *not knowing what happens tomorrow* had become unbearable to me — the very thing everyone else lives inside every minute of their lives, I'd lost the knack of it, like a muscle gone soft.

So I told myself the oldest lie. *Just once. Just one last look. Just to be sure she's safe, and then I'll never do it again.*

I made myself sleep.

And I dreamed the sixteenth.

I dreamed the morning — the crew, the dry causeway, the walk out to the Brigg in a flat calm with the storm still hours off. I dreamed Macca freeing the rusted clapper with a marlinspike and a swear word, and Pip in her red wellies reaching up, and the old bell going *bong* — cracked and flat and not very loud, but ringing, ringing for the first time in my whole life — and Pip's face, lit up like the sun had come out inside her. I dreamed us walking back along the causeway, all of us, Sputnik wet to the belly, Hal with her hand on Pip's head, Charlie grinning, Macca pretending he wasn't. I dreamed *relief.* I dreamed it had worked.

And I woke up at one in the morning crying with relief, actually crying, because I'd seen it, I'd seen tomorrow and tomorrow was *safe,* Pip rang the bell and nobody went in the water and it was all going to be alright —

And then I went to picture my dad's face, the way you do when you're flooded with feeling and you want to share it with him even though he's gone — I went to picture my dad's face —

And it wasn't there.

I want you to understand what I mean. I don't mean it was blurry. I don't mean I had to concentrate. I mean there was *nothing.* Where my dad's face had been, my whole life, there was a smooth blank, like a photograph wiped clean, like a name you've definitely known your whole life and cannot, suddenly, dredge up no matter how hard you claw at it. I knew facts *about* his face — dark eyes, a beard going grey at the chin, a nose Pip has — I could have described him to a police artist from the facts. But I couldn't *see* him. The actual face, the one that used to lean over my bed and say *kalinychta, agori mou,* goodnight, my boy — it was gone. Spent. Paid out, in the dark, at one in the morning, for one last look I'd promised not to take, to buy a tomorrow I could have just *waited for like everybody else.*

I scrambled out of bed. I got the photo — the one on the landing, Dad on the boat, squinting, laughing at whoever held the camera. I held it under the landing light and I stared at it and I thought, *there, there he is, get him back, put him back in,* and I could see the photograph perfectly well, I just couldn't feel that it was *him,* couldn't connect the man in the picture to the warm gap where my dad used to live in me. It was like meeting a stranger who happened to look like your father. Hal had told me about this. *Every time he looked back it was like meeting him again, and losing him again.* Poor Tom. Poor, poor Tom, by the hospital bed. I understood him now. I understood him completely, and the understanding cost me the last warm living memory of my own dad's face, and I sat down on the top stair at one in the morning and I cried like Pip cries, the full apocalypse, silent so as not to wake her, with the photograph of a stranger in my hands.

That's what one last look cost me.

But here's the thing — and it's the thing that nearly killed us all, so pay attention, because it's the cruellest trick in the whole story:

I'd seen tomorrow.

And tomorrow was safe.

I'd seen the bell ring in daylight and everyone come home dry, and I believed it, the way I'd believed the maths test, the way I'd believed the race, flat and true and certain.

So even as I sat there hollowed out and weeping on the stairs, some part of me was saying: *but it was worth it, look, she's safe, you saw it, the plan works, you can relax now, it's done.*

And that — that false, fatal, dead-certain relief — was the trap.

Because I'd forgotten the first rule. The oldest one. The one Charlie wrote in capitals on the very first page.

*It's not the future. It's a future. It's what would happen if nobody changes anything.*

And by *looking* — by sleeping, by dreaming it, by seeing it — I'd already changed something. I'd changed *me.* I'd walk into the sixteenth certain and relaxed and off my guard, instead of frightened and sharp and watching. And a tomorrow you've been shown is a tomorrow you stop fighting for.

I'd seen the bell ring at dawn.

I had not seen what my sister would do at dusk, when the bell at dawn didn't bring her daddy home — because that tomorrow hadn't been built yet, and I'd used up my last look on the wrong one.

I'd spent my father's face to buy myself a lie.

And the storm was already out there, somewhere past Gallows Nab, turning towards us in the dark.

## Chapter Twenty-Three — The Bell at Dawn

The morning of the sixteenth came up flat and grey and calm, exactly the way I'd dreamed it, which should have been a comfort and instead made my skin crawl, because I knew what it had cost me to know.

I hadn't told anyone about the last look. Not Charlie, not Hal. I was too ashamed. I'd had one job in those last days — *stop looking* — and I'd failed it the very first night, and paid for it with my dad's face, and I couldn't bear to say it out loud and watch Hal's eyes go sad and knowing. So I carried it. On the worst possible day to be carrying a secret, I added one more to the pile, the way my whole family always does.

But the morning, at least, went like a dream. Like *the* dream.

We met at the boathouse end of the prom at half six, while the town was still asleep and the tide was right out, the causeway to the Brigg lying bare and black and glistening like the spine of some enormous animal. The sky was the colour of a held breath. Out past Gallows Nab there was a smudge on the horizon, low and bruised, that Macca looked at for a long time without saying anything, and then said, "That's coming in tonight. Met Office had it for eight, but that's coming quicker. We do this now and we're off this rock by nine, all of us, no messing."

Pip was beside herself. She'd been up since five. She had her red wellies on and a posy of flowers from the front garden clutched in her fist — sea-pinks and a dandelion and one of Mam's good roses she'd definitely not been allowed to pick — and she held my hand all the way out along the causeway, hopping the pools, telling everyone who'd listen that we were going to ring the bell and bring Daddy home.

Nobody corrected her. That was the deal we'd made, all of us, without saying it. You don't correct a six-year-old's love. You just hold her hand on the slippery bits.

The Brigg, up close, is sadder and smaller than it looks from the front. The chapel is just three broken walls and a doorway with no door, and grass growing where the floor was, and the bell — the bell hangs in a little stone arch at the gable end, green-black with age, streaked with two hundred years of gull mess and salt, and when Macca got up to it on the wall, balanced like a cat in his grandad's oilskins, he said, "She's rusted solid. Clapper's seized right up. This is going to take some doing."

It took twenty minutes. Macca worked at it with the marlinspike and a tin of his uncle's penetrating oil and a steady stream of muttered swearing that Hal pretended not to hear and Pip drank in like lemonade. Charlie held the torch. I held Pip. Hal stood at the seaward edge with her eye on the smudge and her watch in her hand, timing the turn of the tide, because Hal trusted the sea about as far as she could throw it and she could not throw it at all.

And then there was a grinding shriek of metal giving way, and Macca said "*Got* you," in a voice of pure triumph, and he reached up and took hold of the rope that hung from the clapper — frayed, ancient, but there — and he looked down at Pip, and his great immovable fridge of a face did something I'd never seen it do.

It went gentle.

"Howay then, bonny lass," said Macca Dodds. "It's your bell. You ring it."

And he lifted Pip up — my little sister, six years old, red wellies, gap teeth, a stolen rose in her fist — and she took the rope in both hands, and she looked out over the grey water towards the horizon where she thought our dad was lost and listening, and she pulled.

*Bong.*

It wasn't loud. It wasn't beautiful. It was a cracked, flat, broken old note, two hundred years rusty, and it didn't ring out clear and golden over the water the way it does in the story.

But it rang.

For the first time in my whole life, the Brigg bell rang. And the sound went out low over the flat grey sea, *bong... bong... bong,* as Pip hauled on the rope with her whole tiny body, her face fierce with concentration and hope, and Sputnik threw back his head and *howled* along with it, and even Macca had to look out to sea very hard all of a sudden, and Charlie's torch hand was not quite steady, and Hal had her own hand pressed flat to her chest, and I stood there with the cold morning air going into my lungs and tears running down my face for a dad whose face I could no longer see, and I thought: *there. There. We did it. She's safe. The dream was true. It worked.*

That's what I thought.

Pip rang the bell eleven times — one for every year she might have had him, Charlie said afterwards, though Pip just said it felt like the right number — and then Macca lifted her down, and she stood very still, looking out at the horizon, the flowers held out in front of her.

And the horizon stayed empty.

The smudge of storm got a little closer. A gull went over. The sea did its *shhh, shhh.* And nobody came home out of it, because nobody ever comes home out of it, because that is the one thing about the sea that no bell and no gift and no amount of love has ever once changed.

I watched my sister's face. I watched her wait. I watched the hope hang there, and hang there, and then, slowly, fold.

"He didn't hear it," said Pip.

"Pip — " I started.

"He didn't *come.*" Her voice wobbled. "I rang it and he didn't come."

And Hal was beside her in a moment, crouched down despite the traitor hip, and she said, gently, "Sometimes, pet, it takes a while. Sound travels slow over a big sea. He'll be on his way." Which was a kind lie, the kindest, and Pip wanted to believe it, I could see her wanting to, and we gathered her up and walked her back along the causeway while the tide began, far out, to turn — and I thought it was over, I thought we'd done it, I thought the danger was behind us.

I should have watched her face more carefully on that walk back.

Because Pip wasn't sad, underneath. I worked it out later, far too late. Pip was *thinking.* And what my fierce, stubborn, six-year-old sister was thinking, as she walked back across the spine of the Brigg with her unwanted flowers, was the most dangerous thought in the entire world.

She was thinking: *it didn't work because I did it wrong.*

*It wasn't a secret. Everyone was watching. It has to be a secret, like a wish, or it doesn't count.*

*I'll have to do it again. Properly. On my own. At the special time — when it's dark, when he's listening hardest.*

*And I won't tell anyone this time.*

## Chapter Twenty-Four — The Calm Before

By midday the smudge had become a sky.

It came in off the sea the way the bad ones do here — not in a rush, but in a slow, total takeover, the blue going out of the day inch by inch, the light turning that strange yellow-grey that makes everything look like an old photograph. The wind got up by two. By four it was flinging the first fat rain against the windows and the forecast had gone from "unsettled" to actual warnings, the shipping kind, the kind my dad used to listen to on the radio with his head cocked like Sputnik's.

And we stood down.

That was the thing. That was the mistake, and it was all of ours, not just mine. We'd done the plan. The daylight plan. The bell had rung. Pip had had her moment, safe, in the calm, with her hand held. And now it was teatime and a storm was coming and every grown-up instinct in everyone said the same sensible thing: *get the children indoors, off the front, away from the water, and batten down.*

Macca rang to say he'd got the coble up the slipway and double-tied and he was keeping Dunny in. Charlie's mam wanted her home before the worst of it. Hal said she'd ring me at seven to check in, and to "keep that bairn away from windows and keep a torch handy in case the power goes." Everyone, sensibly, went home.

And me — I had the one thing none of them had. I had a dream of tomorrow, dreamed the night before, in which the bell rang at dawn and everyone came home dry and safe.

I'd already seen the happy ending. So I relaxed.

Do you see it now? Do you see the shape of the trap? Hal had warned me, in those exact words: *a tomorrow you've been shown is a tomorrow you stop fighting for.* I'd looked, and I'd seen the morning, and the morning had been good, and so the watchful, frightened, sharp-edged version of me — the version that had shadowed Pip for weeks, that had read every flicker of her face — that version had gone off duty. I'd seen the storm in the dream and it hadn't mattered, because in the dream we were home before it. I had stopped, somewhere deep down where I didn't even notice, *watching my sister.*

Mam left for her shift at six, in her uniform under her big coat, kissing the tops of our heads, fretting about the drive in the rain. "Mrs Okafor's got her phone on. You ring me if you need owt, you hear? And keep away from the front, the both of you, I mean it, it's wild out there." And she went out into the rain to her little car, and I watched her tail lights go up Sefton Terrace and turn, and that was the last ordinary moment.

Mrs Okafor came round at half six with a pan of jollof rice that could have fed the street and strict instructions about vegetables. She settled in front of the telly with Pip to watch something with singing animals. I went up to my room to — I don't even know. To not-watch. To feel relieved. To run my thumb over a photograph of a man I couldn't feel any more and tell myself it had all worked out.

I had stopped watching my sister for about forty minutes.

It only takes one.

The first sign was the dog.

I became aware, slowly, of Sputnik. He'd been off all evening — pacing, his claws going *tick tick tick* on the kitchen lino, that orbiting brain of his picking up something the rest of us couldn't. Dogs know. *They live a little sideways to us.* And now, downstairs, Sputnik started to bark. Not his postman bark, not his hoover bark. A bark I'd never heard from him — high, and frantic, and aimed at the back door, on and on, scrabbling at the wood.

"Sputnik, *shush,*" I heard Mrs Okafor say.

And then I heard her say, in a completely different voice, a voice that brought me up off my bed and out onto the landing in one movement:

"Nico. Nico, pet — where is Philippa? She was just here. Where's Pip?"

The telly was singing to an empty cushion.

The back door was open, banging in the wind.

And on the mat, where her red wellies always sat, pointing at the door, ready —

— there was nothing.

## Chapter Twenty-Five — Gone

I have never moved so fast in my life, and I've won the hundred metres remembering the future, so that's saying something.

I was down the stairs before Mrs Okafor finished the sentence. I knew. I knew with my whole body, the way you know a thing you've been dreading for a month the instant it finally arrives — there's almost relief in it, a horrible relief, the *here it is, it's here, it came* — and I knew exactly where my sister had gone, because I'd known for weeks, I'd seen it a dozen ways, I'd just let myself believe a dawn bell had bought it off.

The Brigg. The bell. The special time. Properly, this time. On her own. In secret. In the dark.

In the storm.

"She's gone to the Brigg," I said, and my voice came out flat and strange and far too calm, the calm you get when the thing is finally real. "Mrs Okafor — ring 999, ring the coastguard, tell them there's a six-year-old on the Brigg causeway and the tide's coming in. Then ring my mam. I have to go *now.*"

"Nico, you are not going out in — " but she had her phone out already, the nurse in her, like Mam, doing three things while she argued, "— Nico, you wait for —"

"I can't wait," I said. "By the time anyone comes she'll be cut off. I have to *go.*"

And Sputnik was already at the open door, looking back at me, every muscle in his daft body pointed at the front like a compass needle, and he barked once, sharp, *come ON,* and then he was gone into the rain, and I went after him, into the worst night of my life, in my trainers and a hoodie, with no plan and no torch and no dream to walk me through it.

For the first time in months I was running into a tomorrow I had not seen.

And do you know — and this is a strange thing to tell you, in the middle of the worst part — it was the most alive I had ever felt. Because it was *real.* Because I didn't know how it ended. Because for once I wasn't walking down a corridor I'd already been down; I was out in the actual howling unknowable dark with my heart going and my sister somewhere ahead and nothing, *nothing*, decided yet. Hal was right. The gift was never for getting out of tomorrow. This — this blind, terrified, sprinting *not-knowing* — this was being alive, and I'd nearly forgotten how.

The town was battened down and streaming. The rain came sideways. The streetlights had those huge haloes they get, and the wind down Sefton Terrace shoved me from behind like a hand, hurrying me towards the front, towards the sea, and I let it, I ran with it, Sputnik a pale shape ahead splashing through the running gutters, glancing back, *come on, come on, this way.*

As I ran I got my phone out and I did the only sensible thing I managed that whole night. I rang the crew.

Charlie first. She picked up on the second ring — Charlie always picks up — and I gasped out "Pip's gone to the Brigg, tide's coming, I'm running, get to the front," and Charlie didn't waste one single word on *are you sure* or *oh my god,* she just said "ON IT" and hung up, and I found out later she was out the door and on her bike inside a minute, into a storm her mother had forbidden her to go out in, because that is who Charlie Begg is.

Macca next. "It's Pip — she's gone to the Brigg — the bell —" and there was a half-second of silence and then Macca said one word, "*Tide,*" in a voice that had gone very fast and very cold, and then: "Spring tide tonight, top of it's — Nico, the causeway floods two hours before high water on a spring, it's not — what time did she go?" and I said I didn't know, twenty minutes, half an hour, and Macca made a sound I'll never forget and said "I'm getting the boat. Don't you go out on that causeway when it's covered, d'you hear me, you wait for the boat, I'm coming, I'm coming *now*—" and the line went dead.

And Hal. Hal answered and I couldn't even get the words out properly but she understood in three syllables, and Halina Quirk, seventy-odd, with a traitor hip, in a house full of clocks that wouldn't agree, became in one instant the calmest and most terrifying person I have ever heard.

"Listen to me," she said. "Are you running? Keep running, but listen. The coastguard will be coming — Mrs Okafor's call will reach them quicker than mine. I'll ring them too and tell them it's the Kostas girl and they'll launch the inshore boat from the next station, but that's twenty minutes, Nico, twenty at best in this. You'll be there first. So you listen to me now and you do exactly what I say, because this is the bit your father would tell you if he could." Her voice didn't shake. "You do not be a hero. You do not go out onto a covered causeway. The water'll have you off it in a heartbeat and then there's two bairns in the sea instead of one and I have to make two phone calls instead of none. Do you hear me?"

"But she's —"

"*Do you hear me, Nico.*"

"I hear you," I said, running, sobbing, the rain in my mouth.

"If she's still on the dry — you go and you get her and you walk her back, calm, no panic, hold her hand. If the causeway's already gone under — you *stop.* You stop at the edge and you keep her in your sight and you keep her talking and you wait for Macca's boat or the lifeboat, and you do *not* go in after her, because you going in doesn't save her, it just gives the sea two. That's the whole lesson, pet. That's the thing it took your father a lifetime to learn and it's the thing that'll save your sister tonight if you can learn it in the next four minutes. *Being there is not the same as going in.* Be there. Don't go in. Let the crew do the going in. Promise me."

I came over the rise at the top of the front, and the whole bay opened up below me, black and white and roaring, the sea where the sea should never be, the prom awash, the waves coming over the rail and across the road, and out — out past the churning white at the head of the causeway —

— there was a tiny figure. Halfway out along the Brigg. A small figure in red wellies, picking her way out along a strip of black rock that was getting thinner and thinner as the white water came round it from both sides.

"I promise," I said, and I'm not proud of this, but I want to tell you the truth, the whole truth, like I said at the start: I said *I promise,* and even as I said it, looking down at that tiny figure on the disappearing rock, I knew, the way you know the answers to a test you've already taken, that it was the third lie I'd told someone who loved me.

Because there are some promises a brother can't keep.

## Chapter Twenty-Six — The Causeway

I went down onto the front and the noise hit me like a wall.

You don't know what the sea is, not really, until you've stood next to it when it's angry. On a calm day it's the thing that goes *shhh, shhh.* On the sixteenth of August it was a living thing the size of the sky, throwing itself at the town, and the noise of it wasn't a sound any more, it was a pressure, a great grinding roar with the bell-buoy clanging somewhere out in it — *clang, clang,* the cracked note from my dreams, the one that had been trying to warn me for a month — and under it, impossibly, thin and high and almost lost, a child's voice.

Pip was singing.

I could hear it in snatches when the wind turned. She was out on the rock, picking her way towards the chapel, and she was *singing* — that little tuneless six-year-old chant kids do when they're concentrating, keeping themselves company — and it was the most terrible sound I have ever heard, because it meant she had no idea. No idea how fast the water was coming. No idea she was in any danger at all. She was just a little girl going to ring a bell for her daddy, on the special day, at the special time, in secret, like a wish, and the sea was closing round behind her like a trap and she was *singing.*

The causeway was still — just — crossable. The tide hadn't fully covered it. But it was going under in patches now, white water sluicing across the low parts, and every wave that came round Gallows Nab sent the sea surging up both sides at once, deeper each time, and I could see, with the horrible clarity the gift had trained into me, exactly how it was going to go: in a few minutes the low part — the saddle, the dip in the middle of the causeway — would be under for good, and then the rock she was standing on would be an island, and then it wouldn't be anything at all.

Charlie arrived. She skidded her bike into the railings and was off it and beside me, soaked to the bone, hair plastered flat, and she took one look out at the rock and her face did the thing it does, went still, went serious, the smile gone.

"Macca?" she shouted over the roar.

"Getting the boat!"

"Lifeboat?"

"Coming! Twenty minutes!"

Charlie looked at the rock, at Pip, at the white water eating the saddle, and she did the maths the way Charlie does, fast and cold and right, and I watched her arrive at the same answer I'd already arrived at, the answer Hal had begged me not to reach.

"She doesn't have twenty minutes," said Charlie.

"No."

"Macca said don't go on the causeway when it's covered."

"It's not covered yet," I said. "Not all of it. Not the bit she's on. Not — not quite."

Charlie grabbed my arm. Hard. Her fingers dug in. "Nico. Listen to me. Your map. *Your own map.* Every version where you go out there on your own, you don't come back. *Every single one.* You showed me. We wrote it in capitals." Her voice cracked, and Charlie's voice never cracks. "If you go out there alone you die. That's not me being dramatic, that's *data,* that's your own data, you told me the future and the future says if you go alone you *die,* Nico —"

And here is the thing. Here is the whole thing, the centre of everything, the thing I'd been running towards for the entire summer without knowing it.

She was right.

Every version I'd ever seen, I went alone, and I was too late, and I didn't make it. *Every single one.* The map said: a boy goes out onto the Brigg alone to save his sister, and the sea takes him, the way it took his father. Brass plaque. Anomalous wave. The end.

But.

But I wasn't alone.

That was the gap. That was the door. That was the one tomorrow the gift had never been able to show me, the future nobody had ever set up for it to remember — because in every version I'd dreamed, it had only ever been *me,* solitary, heroic, doomed, my father's son walking out into the thing that killed him.

It had never once seen me out there with a crew.

"I'm not going alone," I said.

I turned to Charlie and I grabbed her shoulders and I shouted into the storm, into her frightened furious wonderful face:

"I'm not going alone, Charlie. That's the whole point. That's the door. Every future where I go *alone* I die — but the gift never saw *this.* It never saw you. It never saw Macca's boat coming, or Hal's coastguard, or the lifeboat, or the dog. It only ever showed me the lonely version because that's the only version I ever let it see. We're going to do the one thing tomorrow has never watched me do."

And I took the throw-line off my shoulder — the coil of bright orange rope Macca had pressed on me at the Roost, *just in case, you melt, take it, you never know* — and I pushed one end into Charlie's hands.

"You're my crew," I shouted. "You hold this. You dig in behind the rail. I'm going to go to the low bit — *only* the low bit, only while I can still touch rock — and I'm going to get her across the saddle before it goes under for good, and you are going to be the reason the sea doesn't take me when it tries. Because it's going to try. And it's never, ever had to get past *you.*"

Charlie looked at the rope in her hands. She looked at me. And then Charlie Begg, who pushed me off a climbing frame when we were four and told me about Valentina Tereshkova while I cried, who has never once in her life been impressed by anything, looked at me with something on her face that I would spend a memory on to keep, if I had any left to spend.

"Tie it off properly, then," she said, blinking hard, wrapping the orange rope twice round her fists and once round the iron railing and bracing her trainers against the base of it. "Round your chest, under your arms, not your waist, I read the book. And Nico —"

"Yeah?"

"If you die out there I will never, ever forgive you, and I'll tell everyone you ran like an idiot."

"Deal," I said.

And I tied the bright orange rope around my chest, under my arms, the way the book says, and I checked the knot the way Macca's chart had taught me, and I stepped down off the prom onto the streaming black rock of the causeway, into the white water and the roaring dark, towards the small singing shape of my sister —

— not alone.

For the first time, not alone.

Behind me, somewhere up the front, I heard the snarl of an outboard engine catching, and catching, and *roaring* into life — Macca, and the coble, coming. And further out, faint under everything, a different note in the storm — the deeper drum of the lifeboat's engines, launched from the next station up the coast, my dad's old crew, the orange and blue, coming round Gallows Nab into the teeth of it, the way they always come, on the worst nights, for somebody else's family.

The whole town was coming.

I was the first one there. But I was not, this time, the only one.

And out on the rock, the sea came round the Brigg from both sides at once, and met itself in the middle, and swallowed the saddle whole.

## Chapter Twenty-Seven — The Bell at Dark

The saddle went under just as I reached it.

One moment there was a strip of black rock, ankle-deep, sluicing; the next a wave came round the Nab and the two seas met across it and it was gone, a torrent of white water knee-deep, then thigh-deep, pouring across the gap between me and the chapel rock where Pip stood. Where my sister stood, on the high part, still dry, still — God help me — still *singing,* reaching up on her toes for the bell rope in the dark.

She'd done it. She'd climbed up onto the chapel wall the way she'd watched Macca do it that morning, fearless, certain, and she had the frayed rope in both small fists, and she was ringing the bell. In secret. At the special time. *Bong... bong...* the cracked flat note going out into the storm, swallowed almost at once by the roar, but ringing, ringing, my little sister calling her daddy home through a hurricane on the anniversary of the day it took him.

"PIP!" I screamed. The wind took it and threw it away.

She didn't hear me. She was somewhere else, somewhere only the bell and the dark and her enormous six-year-old love could reach. *Bong... bong...*

I looked at the water across the saddle. Thigh-deep and rising and white and fast, and beyond it, the rock, and Pip, and the bell. And behind me, the orange rope ran back to Charlie, braced against the railing, both fists wrapped, her whole small furious body a single anchor, her trainers skidding on the wet and digging in again.

Hal's voice was in my head. *Being there is not the same as going in. Don't go in. Let the crew do the going in.*

And she was right. She was right about everything. But Hal had never stood where I was standing, with the saddle gone under and her own sister ringing a bell on a drowning rock and no boat yet and no lifeboat yet and the water coming up, up, up — and I understood, in that moment, with total and final clarity, the thing my father had understood on his last night, the thing I'd been so angry at him for.

*If you knew, why didn't you stay home?*

Because three people came home who wouldn't have otherwise.

Because some moments you don't get to dodge. You only get to choose how you walk into them.

And because being there — really being there, all the way there — sometimes *does* mean going in.

But here's what I'd learned that he hadn't, what Hal had drilled into me, what Charlie had screamed at me by the railing, the one thing that made it different this time, the one thing that meant I wasn't just my father walking out into the wave that would take him:

I had a rope. And someone I trusted holding the other end. And a boat coming. And a lifeboat coming. And a dog raising the dead with his barking. I wasn't going in *alone.* I was going in *connected* — tied to the shore, tied to Charlie, tied to the whole town that was, even now, roaring through the dark to get to us.

I wasn't breaking Hal's rule.

I was finishing it.

"CHARLIE," I bellowed back over my shoulder, "TAKE IN THE SLACK — DON'T LET GO —"

And I went into the water.

---

It was so cold it wasn't even cold. It was a fist. It was the fist from my dream, the one that closed round my whole body, and it knocked the breath clean out of me and tried to take my legs out from under me, and it would have, except for the rope, except for Charlie, except that every time the surge tried to sweep me off the saddle and out into the dark the rope went tight and held and I stayed, I stayed, I clawed my way across the drowned rock one freezing handhold at a time with the orange line singing taut behind me.

Halfway across, a swell lifted me right off my feet and I went under, and the world went black and roaring and turning, no up, no down, just cold and noise — and then the rope hauled, Charlie hauled, my crew hauled, and my head broke the surface and I got a breath, half water, and I kept going, because the alternative was Pip alone out there, and there was no version of me, in any tomorrow ever dreamed, that let that happen.

I reached the chapel rock. I dragged myself up out of the water onto it, retching, and I got to my feet, and Pip — Pip finally saw me.

Her face. I'll keep her face from that night for the rest of my life, even if I have to give up every other memory I own to do it. Because it wasn't frightened. That's the thing. She wasn't frightened at all. She was *delighted.*

"NICO!" she shouted, beaming, gap-toothed, soaked, triumphant, still holding the bell rope. "Nico, you came! I rang it and it WORKED — it brought you — I told you it would work if I did it on my own — I rang the bell and it brought somebody home!"

And I didn't have the breath or the time to explain that I wasn't the one she'd rung for, that her daddy wasn't coming, that nobody comes home out of that sea — and anyway, looking back, I think she was more right than I was. Because something *had* come, hadn't it. Something always comes, in a town like ours, when the bell rings on the worst night. It just doesn't always wear the face you wanted.

I grabbed her. I got her off the wall and into my arms and I felt how small she was, how cold, how light, my whole entire family that I could carry in two arms, and I turned to look back the way I'd come —

— and there was no way back.

The saddle was gone. Not knee-deep now, not thigh-deep — gone, a churning channel of black and white water twenty feet across between us and the causeway, far too deep and far too fast to wade, even on the rope. While I'd been crossing, the tide had taken the last of it. We were on an island the size of a kitchen, in the dark, in a storm, and the island was getting smaller.

The rope still ran back to Charlie. I could see her, tiny and far, still braced at the rail, and I saw her understand at the same moment I did — that the rope wasn't a path any more, that I couldn't walk Pip back across that, that all the rope could do now was *not let the sea take us out to where it took my dad.*

And then I heard it. Over the roar, getting louder. An engine, snarling, climbing, fighting the swell.

And out of the dark to seaward, pitching and slamming and absolutely should-not-have-been-out-there, came a small open fishing boat with a single figure at the tiller in oilskins three sizes too big, and a brilliant white searchlight that swung across the water and found us — two soaked children on a shrinking rock — and held us, steady, in a circle of light.

Macca Dodds had brought the coble round the Brigg.

"STAY THERE!" Macca's voice came thin across the water, and even now, even *there,* there was something so completely Macca about being ordered to stay on a rock the size of a kitchen as if I had loads of other options. "STAY ON THE ROCK — DON'T GO IN THE WATER — I CAN'T COME ALONGSIDE, IT'S TOO SHALLOW, THE BOAT'LL BREAK UP — WAIT FOR THE BIG BOAT — THEY'RE COMING —"

And behind him, further out, rounding Gallows Nab with its blue light flashing through the spray, low and orange and absolutely unstoppable, came the Saltmere lifeboat. My dad's crew. The orange and blue. Coming round the Nab into the teeth of it, the way they always come, on the worst nights, for somebody else's family.

For us.

We just had to stay on the rock.

And the rock was going under.

## Chapter Twenty-Eight — Anomalous

There's a word in the report about my dad. I told you about it right at the start, hundreds of pages ago, when I was a different boy who'd just won a maths test. The wave that took him, the inquiry called *anomalous.* Which is a long, cold, scientific word that means: bigger than it should have been. Out of pattern. A wave that the sea had no business making, that came from nowhere, that nobody could have predicted.

Not even, it turns out, a man who could remember tomorrow.

I have thought about that a lot since. My dad could see the future. And the sea killed him with the one thing the future can't show you — the anomaly, the freak, the wave out of pattern. Maybe that's what *anomalous* really means. Maybe it means *the thing tomorrow doesn't know about.* The gap. The door. The wave nobody saw coming.

I'd spent the whole summer terrified of the anomaly. And then, on the rock, with my sister in my arms and the lifeboat still two minutes out and the water coming up over my trainers, I finally understood that the anomaly cuts both ways.

Yes — the sea can make a wave that nobody predicts, and take a good man on a calm crossing home.

But you can make one too.

You can build a future so far off the pattern that tomorrow has never seen it and can't see it and doesn't know it's coming. A boy who always goes out alone, bringing a crew. A bell that never rings, ringing. A bully with a boat. An old woman with the coastguard's number. A dog who knew first. A best friend with a rope, who reads the book, who ties the knot under the arms not the waist.

I was the anomaly now. *We* were. The wave the sea didn't see coming.

The big wave — the real one, the anomalous one, the one out of pattern — it came when the lifeboat was sixty feet away and slowing, when a crewman in orange was already leaning out over the bow with a line, when I'd actually started to believe we'd done it. Of course it did. That's when they come. It reared up black behind the chapel rock, twice the height of the others, silent the way the worst ones are silent, and I saw it in the searchlight and I had about one second, and in that one second I did the only thing there was to do.

I didn't try to outrun it. You can't outrun the sea; I'd learned that in a caravan forty miles inland.

I turned my back on it, and I wrapped my whole body round Pip, and I jammed us both into the angle of the old chapel wall — the broken corner that had stood there two hundred years, that had stood through every storm since before my dad's grandad's grandad — and I wedged us in and I held the rope and I held my sister and I held on, and I shouted into her ear, over the roar, the only thing I had left to give her, the thing she'd taught me, the thing that turned out to be true:

"HOLD ON, KOUKLA — SOMEBODY'S COMING — THE BELL WORKED — SOMEBODY'S COMING HOME —"

And the wave hit.

It took us off the rock. Of course it did. The rope went tight — Charlie, oh, *Charlie,* bracing her whole eleven stone of self against the railing two hundred feet away, taking the full weight of the sea through her wrapped and bleeding fists and *not letting go* — and the rope held, and it didn't let the sea carry us out to where it carried my dad, it held us in the churn, tumbling, and I kept my arms locked round Pip and my back to the rock and we went under and came up and went under, and the cold tried to make me let go, the cold always tries to make you let go, and I did not let go, because in that black roaring nothing, with my memory of my dad's face entirely gone, spent, paid out for a useless look the night before — in that moment I discovered that you do not actually need to remember someone's face to be made of the things they taught you.

Time went strange in the water. I've thought about it a lot since, sitting in Hal's house with the hundred clocks all disagreeing — about how, in the worst ten seconds of my life, time stopped being one thing marching along, tick tick tick, and came apart into a hundred different speeds at once. The wave was instant and the wave lasted an hour. The cold was a single shock and the cold was forever. I had time, in there, in the black churn, to think whole thoughts — *keep her head up, find the rope, don't breathe in, don't fight it, let it take you and the rope will stop it* — and no time at all to do any of them, and both of those were true, both at once, the way the bell is.

I got an arm bar across Pip's chest, the way the men on the boat would have done it, the way some part of me knew without being told, and I felt her cough and buck and grab at me — alive, she was alive, she was fighting, *good girl, fight, don't go soft on me, koukla* — and I jammed my other fist into the loop of orange rope at my chest and I made myself into a single stupid stubborn knot of a boy around my sister, and I let the sea do what it wanted to the rest of me. The rope sang. I could feel it through my whole body, that high taut hum, the weight of the entire North Sea trying to take us out past Gallows Nab to the deep water where it took my dad, and the rope saying *no.* Charlie saying no. Two hundred feet away, a girl with the skin coming off her hands, saying *no, not this one, not today, you can have the sea but you can't have these two.*

And then there was a different shape in the water beside me. Pale. Daft. Paddling like a furious draught excluder, eyes rolling, absolutely where no sensible animal should ever be — Sputnik, who is frightened of the hoover and the postman and his own reflection in the oven door, had come off the prom and into a spring sea in a storm, because his small one was in it, and dogs do not do the maths the way we do, dogs just go. He couldn't help. He was no use at all, in the practical sense; if anything I now had a dog to worry about as well. But I will tell you that the sight of that ridiculous biscuit-coloured face, fierce and frightened and *there,* in the black water at the worst moment of my life, put something back into me that the cold had nearly taken out, and I got Pip's head another inch higher, and I held on. You're not allowed in the sea either, Sputnik. You're allowed in the sea.

Macca's searchlight found us and held us, a hard white circle in all that roaring dark, and over the wind I heard the snarl of the coble's engine rising and falling as he fought to hold her off the rock without piling her onto it — close enough to light us, close enough to throw a line, not so close the swell would smash the boat to splinters on the Brigg and put a third child in the water. He told me afterwards it was the hardest thing he's ever done, holding that boat exactly there, in that, doing nothing, *waiting,* when every bone in him wanted to drive in and grab us — because he knew, the way the fishing families know it in their blood, that *being there is not the same as going in,* that his job in that minute was to be the light and the second rope and the thing the lifeboat steered for, not the hero who drowns three instead of saving two. A fourteen-year-old learned that. Held the line of it with his hands shaking on the throttle. My dad would have known him for crew on sight.

I couldn't see my dad. But I was him. For about ten seconds in that water I was every lesson he ever gave me — *respect the sea, don't fight the rip, hold the small one high, you can survive anything if you don't panic and you don't let go* — and I held my sister's head above the water and I did not let go, and the lifeboat came.

They came the way Dad's crew always came. Out of the worst of it, into the worst of it, hands reaching down. I felt a grab on the back of my hoodie, a fist like iron, and a voice — a grown man's voice, calm in a way that no voice has any right to be calm in a sea like that — saying "I've got you, son, I've got you, let me have the bairn, that's it, that's it, I've got her, you're alright, you're both alright, I've got you" —

*I've got you.*

The same three words Macca said to Dunny in the rain. The same three words I'd have given anything to hear two years ago. The oldest words in my town, the ones we say when we go out into the worst of it for each other.

They got Pip up first. Then me. They wrapped us in foil blankets that did nothing and meant everything, and the coxswain put the boat about and we came in round the Brigg with the bell — I swear this is true, Macca heard it too, and Macca doesn't make things up — with the old bell still ringing behind us in the storm, *bong... bong...,* not because anyone was pulling the rope now but because the wind had got into it, the wind was ringing it, the storm itself was tolling the lost-sailor's bell over the black water, on and on, for everyone who never came home, and for the two who, this time, against every tomorrow I had ever dreamed, did.

On the prom, in the rain and the blue flashing lights, a small soaked daft digestive-biscuit-coloured dog was barking himself hoarse at the sea, and beside him a girl with bleeding hands was being prised off a railing she would not, even now, fully let go of, and a fourteen-year-old fisherman was bringing a battered coble up the slipway with his jaw set and his grandad's oilskins streaming, and somewhere an old woman was on the phone telling the coastguard, in a voice that did not shake, that they could stand the helicopter down, that the children were safe, that the crew had brought them home.

The crew.

We were the crew.

And the sea, for once, for the first time in my whole remembered and un-remembered life, did not get what it came for.

## Chapter Twenty-Nine — The Quiet After

There's a particular quiet that comes after the worst thing nearly happens. It's not peaceful. It's the quiet of a room where everyone has just been screaming and has run out of breath at the same moment. It rings, that quiet. Like the inside of a bell after the bell's stopped.

I remember it in pieces, that night, the way you remember the bad ones — and it strikes me now that for once I remember it the ordinary way, forwards, like everybody else, because I hadn't dreamed it. I walked into that night blind, with no map, and so I get to keep it the way you keep real things, jumbled and bright and out of order. There's a strange gift in that. The nights I saw coming, I half-remember. The night I didn't, I'll never forget.

I remember the foil blankets, and how they crackled, and how useless they were against a cold that was in my bones not my skin.

I remember the lifeboat station, the bright hard fluorescent light of it, and a woman from the ambulance shining a torch in my eyes and asking me my name and what day it was, and me saying "the sixteenth of August" and starting to laugh in a way that frightened her, because of course it was, of course it was the sixteenth, it was always going to be the sixteenth, and I'd known for a month, and here we all were, alive, on the wrong side of it.

I remember Pip wrapped in about nine blankets on a plastic chair, drinking hot Ribena, absolutely fine, absolutely delighted, telling a large bemused lifeboatman the entire story of the lost-sailor's bell and how she'd rung it and it had worked because it brought her brother and then the big orange boat, so really it had worked *twice,* and the lifeboatman nodding along going "is that right, pet, well I never," with his eyes a bit too bright.

I remember Charlie's hands. They'd had to be bandaged — the rope had taken the skin off both her palms where she'd wrapped it round her fists and refused, through everything, to let go. She held them up to show me, mittened in white gauze, and she said, "Look. I've got boxing gloves. I'm going to tell everyone I won a fight," and then her face went and she sort of fell into me and we held onto each other and neither of us said anything because there wasn't anything that would fit.

I remember Macca, in a corner, still in his grandad's oilskins, being talked to quietly and seriously by the coxswain — a proper telling-off and a proper thank-you, both at once, the way men like that do it, *what the hell were you thinking taking the coble out in that, and also, son, that was the bravest daft thing I've seen in thirty years on this crew* — and Macca staring at the floor and going red and saying nothing, and then looking up and across the room and catching my eye, and giving me the smallest nod in the history of nods. We don't leave bairns on the Brigg. That's not a favour. That's just the rule.

And I remember Hal arriving, somehow, having got a taxi up from the Roost, standing in the doorway of the lifeboat station with rain dripping off her, counting us — one, two, the gobby lass, the fisher lad, the dog — counting us all with her eyes, twice, making sure, and then sitting down very suddenly on a plastic chair as if her legs had stopped agreeing to the job, and putting her face in her oil-stained hands.

But mostly I remember Mam.

Somebody had rung the hospital. Of course they had. And my mam, forty minutes away on a night shift, had got the call that every parent in a town like ours dreads on a night like that — *your children, the sea, the Brigg* — the exact call, near enough, that she'd got two years before about her husband, and she had got in her little car and driven forty minutes of flooded coast road in a storm in a way I don't like to think about, and she came through the doors of the lifeboat station still in her nurse's uniform under her coat, white as the foil blankets, and she stopped dead in the middle of the room and looked at us — me and Pip, both wrapped up, both *there,* both alive — and she made a sound.

It was the same sound. The one from two years ago, on the doorstep, with the two policemen. I'd thought I'd never hear it again and I'd hoped I never would. But it came out of her again, except this time — this time it bent, halfway through, from the sound of the worst thing into the sound of the best thing, the worst and the best riding the same single note, and she crossed the room and got both of us at once, one in each arm, Pip and her Ribena and me and my useless blanket, and she held on so hard it hurt and I let it hurt, I wanted it to hurt, and she didn't say anything for the longest time and then she said, into our wet hair, fierce and broken:

"I've got you. I've got you. I've got you both. I've got you."

The oldest words in my town.

They took us to North Tyne General to be checked over — the same hospital where Mam works, which meant that for once she got to sit on the wrong side of it, holding a polystyrene cup of machine tea she didn't drink, while other nurses, her own friends, fussed over her children and pretended they weren't crying. They warmed us up slowly, which is apparently the right way; you don't fling a cold person at a radiator, you bring them back gently, by degrees, the way you bring back anything that's been too far out. I lay under a heated blanket with my teeth chattering like Charlie's lab-book biro on a cold morning, and I watched my little sister, three beds down, hold absolute court.

Because Pip — and I want this on the record, because it tells you everything about her — Pip was having the time of her life. She had a hospital bracelet, which she considered jewellery. She had a nurse who'd brought her a second hot chocolate against medical advice. And she had an audience, and Pip in front of an audience is a force of nature, and she told the story of the night — *her* version of the night, in which she had rung the bell, and the bell had worked, and it had brought first her brother and then a magnificent orange boat — to anyone who came within range, embellishing freely, until in the final version I'm fairly sure she had personally steered the lifeboat. Nobody corrected her. You don't correct a six-year-old who's just been pulled out of the sea and decided the whole thing was basically a success.

A doctor with a kind tired face shone a torch in my eyes and asked me, gently, if I knew what day it was, and I started to laugh again — that frightening laugh, the one that worried the ambulance woman — because of *course* I knew what day it was, I'd known what day it was for a month, it was the seventeenth of August now, we'd actually crossed over, we were on the far side of the date that had been sitting in me like a stone since the night of the red wellies. "The seventeenth," I said. "It's the seventeenth." And the doctor said, "That's right, well done," not understanding at all why a soaked, shivering boy would say it like the best news in the history of the world.

Somebody had smuggled Sputnik in. This is technically a serious breach of hospital policy and I will not say who did it, except that she has a traitor hip and a one-eyed cat at home and a complete disregard for any rule she considers daft. A grey-muzzled dog, towelled into a state of mild electrocution, was produced from inside a very large coat at Pip's bedside for approximately ninety seconds, which is roughly how long it took for the most senior nurse on the ward to spot him and for the entire situation to be resolved with a great deal of fuss and absolutely no actual consequences, because it turned out the most senior nurse on the ward had been at school with my dad and had been crying behind the desk for an hour and was in no mood to be the villain of anybody's night. Sputnik, for his part, located and ate half a ham sandwich during the ninety seconds. Of course he did. Some things the sea cannot change.

And the crew were there. Not the lifeboat crew — *my* crew, the daft impossible one we'd built in a ticking kitchen. Charlie, with her hands going into proper bandages now, refusing to lie down, sitting bolt upright in a plastic chair like a sentry, as if the sea might come up the corridor and have another go and she'd have to grab a rope. Macca, soaked and grey and saying nothing, being made to sit and have his own hands looked at — rope-burned too, I noticed, from holding the coble's lines — and Eddie Solomon the coxswain with one enormous hand on Macca's shoulder, not telling him off, not yet, that came later, just *anchoring* him, the way you anchor a lad who's done a brave and terrifying thing and hasn't come down from it. And Hal, who'd counted us all twice and then sat down very hard, the way she does, the going-out-into-the-worst-of-it having cost her something too.

We were all there. Every one of us. The whole anomaly. The wave the sea didn't see coming, dripping on the floor of a hospital ward at four in the morning, alive.

I don't know how long we stood like that. Long enough. Outside the storm was already easing — the bad ones blow themselves out, Dad always said, they spend everything they've got in a few hours and then there's nothing left — and through the window I could see the first grey of the morning of the seventeenth of August coming up over a sea that was, hour by hour, going back to just being water.

We'd made it to the other side of the sixteenth.

I hadn't, until that moment, properly believed we would.

## Chapter Thirty — What My Mam Found Out

I told her everything two days later.

I'd promised Hal, weeks before, that I wouldn't carry it alone — and I'd broken that promise the way I'd broken all of them, quietly, telling myself it was kinder. But you can't keep a thing like the sixteenth of August secret from your mother. Not after. Not when she'd driven forty minutes through a storm to a lifeboat station. She deserved the truth, all of it, even the mad parts, *especially* the mad parts, and I was done — completely, finally done — with being a person who lied to the people who loved him to protect them. That kind of protecting, I'd learned, just means they're alone with their worry instead of you being alone with yours. It doesn't divide the weight. It just hides it.

So when Pip was at Maisie's and the house was quiet, I sat my mam down at the kitchen table, and I got Dad's notebook out of where I'd hidden it under my mattress, and I put it in front of her.

"I need to tell you something mad and true," I said. "And this time I need you to let me prove it before you decide I need a doctor."

And I told her. The maths test. The race. The rules, in Charlie's lab book. The red wellies and the dream of the sixteenth. The caravan, and how I'd brought it with us. Pip and the bell and *going to ring Daddy home.* The crew. The plan. And then the notebook — Dad's small frightened handwriting — *Thursday I knew Friday. Three lives. Worth it. The more I look forward the more falls off the back.* And the last entry. *Saturday. The Nab.*

I watched my mam read her dead husband's handwriting and understand, slowly, the thing I'd understood at midnight a month before. That he'd known. That on his last morning he'd woken up remembering a Saturday he hadn't lived yet, and seen how it ended, and kissed her, and gone anyway.

She put the notebook down very gently, the way you put down something that might break, and she sat for a long time with her hand flat on the cover. And when she finally spoke, it wasn't what I expected. I'd expected *I don't believe you,* or *we need to get you help,* or even *prove it.* I'd been ready to prove it; I'd memorised the next day's headlines off the late news, I had it all loaded.

Instead she said, very quietly, not looking up:

"He took an umbrella once. The one cloudless morning of the whole summer. Bone dry, not a cloud, and he stood at the door putting his big coat on, and I laughed at him, I said you daft article, look at it, it's glorious. And he just smiled at me. That smile. And at half eleven the sky came over black from nowhere and it absolutely chucked it down, and he came home dry as a bone and never said one word about it, just hung the umbrella up." Her voice wobbled. "I thought he was lucky. I spent fourteen years thinking I'd married the luckiest man in the North East." She finally looked up, and her eyes were full. "He wasn't lucky, was he. He just — knew. He always knew. And he never told me. All those years. He carried it on his own, like you've been carrying it on your own, the pair of you, you and your father, and I never —"

She stopped. She pressed her knuckles to her mouth.

"That's why I get scared," I said, "of telling people things. I think I learned it off him without either of us knowing. Carrying stuff. Thinking it's kinder."

"It's not kinder," said Mam.

"I know that now."

"It's not *kinder,* Nico." And there was something fierce in it, something that had been building for a long time, maybe two years. "It nearly cost me both of you. The pair of you out on that rock and me forty miles away with no idea, because everybody in this family is so busy *protecting* each other that nobody ever just *tells* each other anything." She wiped her eyes hard with the heel of her hand. "Your father did it. You did it. Even Pip did it — kept her secret to protect *me,* a six-year-old, deciding her own mother couldn't handle —" She laughed, wet and helpless. "We're ridiculous. We're a ridiculous family. We'd rather drown than worry each other."

And that — more than the maths test, more than the umbrella, more than the notebook — that was the moment my mam believed me. Not because I proved the future. Because she recognised the *shape* of us in it. She'd lived eighteen years inside a family of people who'd rather carry the sea on their own than hand anyone else a corner of it, and here was the proof, in her dead husband's handwriting and her living son's mad true story, of exactly what it costs.

"No more," she said. She reached across the table and took both my hands, the way Hal had, except these were my mam's hands, and they smelled of the hospital and of home. "Whatever this is — this thing, this gift, this curse, whatever your father had and you've got and God help us maybe your sister's got — we do it *together* from now on. All of it. You don't get to be the man of the house, Nico. You're twelve. You get to be my boy, and you get to tell me things, even the mad ones, *especially* the mad ones. That's the new rule. That's the only rule. Do you hear me?"

"I hear you," I said.

And for once, when I said it to someone I loved, it wasn't a lie.

She made tea. She let it go cold. We sat in the quiet for a bit, the good quiet this time, and then she said, in a completely different voice, a careful nurse-voice with something frightened underneath it:

"This thing it costs. The — *the more falls off the back.* What you've been spending." She turned her cup round and round. "How bad is it? What have you lost?"

And I had to tell her the last hard thing. The thing I'd been dreading more than the storm.

"Mam," I said, and my voice went, completely, the way it does. "I can't see his face any more. Dad's face. I spent it. The night before the sixteenth, I looked one more time when I'd promised not to, and it — it took his face. I know all the bits. I just can't — I can't *see* him. I can't hear him. He's going, Mam. I'm losing him. I saved Pip and I think I paid for it with Dad and I can't get him back and I don't know how to —"

And I couldn't finish, and my mam came round the table and held me while I cried like Pip cries, the full apocalypse, no holding it in, and she didn't tell me it was alright, because it wasn't, and she didn't tell me he'd come back, because he couldn't.

She said: "Then we'll get him back for you. Together. I've still got him. Pip's still got her six things. Mrs Okafor's got him, and the crew's got him, and that mad clock woman's got him, and there's a whole town full of him, Nico, a whole town. You haven't lost your dad. You've just run your own copy thin. And the thing about your dad — " she pulled back and held my face in her two hands, the way he used to — "the thing about a man like your dad is he left copies of himself in *everyone.* You can't lose him. There's too much of him about."

I didn't fully believe her. Not yet.

But it turned out she was right.

And the first person to prove it to me was the last person on earth I'd have expected.

It was an old woman in a falling-down house full of clocks that wouldn't agree — and she had something of my dad's that nobody else in the world had. Something I could not just be told.

Something I could *hear.*

## Chapter Thirty-One — The Tape

Hal sent word, a few days after, through Charlie, that I was to come up to the Roost, and to bring nobody, and to be prepared to stay a while.

I went up Coble Lane on a soft grey afternoon, the sea back to its old *shhh, shhh,* completely innocent, butter wouldn't melt, as if it hadn't tried to eat my entire family the week before. The Roost ticked its hundred different times. Schrödinger the one-eyed cat regarded me from the windowsill with the deep contempt he reserves for people he has decided to tolerate.

Hal made tea I actually drank this time, and she didn't say much at first. She'd aged, I thought, in the week — or maybe I was only just seeing it, the way you suddenly see how old someone is when they've frightened you by nearly not being there. She'd sat down very hard in that lifeboat station. I think going out into the worst of it, even by taxi, even by telephone, had cost her something too.

"You found out what it takes," she said at last. Not a question. Never a question, with Hal. "Your father's face."

"How did you —"

"Because it's the one I'd take, if I were the sort of thief your gift is. The one you look at most. The one you can least afford to lose." She set down her cup. "It went the same way for Tom, at the end. Michael's face, first. Then mine. He used to look at me across the breakfast table like a polite stranger and ask, very kindly, if I'd been married long." She said it lightly, the way you can only say the very worst things once they're old enough. "So I know what you've come up here carrying, Nico Kostas, and I'll tell you straight: I can't give you his face back. Nobody can. What's spent is spent. That's the one rule with no door in it."

My heart, which had stupidly hoped, went down.

"But," said Hal, and she got up, joints clicking, and went to a cupboard under the stairs that I'd never seen open, and from it she took a box. An old shoebox, the cardboard gone soft with age, and on the lid, in faded marker, a date from before I was born, and three words: *KOSTAS — SESSION TAPES.*

"When your dad first came to me," she said, sitting back down with the box on her knees, "twenty-odd years ago, before you, before Pip, when he was just a young lad off the boats who'd worked out he could remember tomorrow and thought he was losing his mind — I studied him. Properly. The way I'd studied Tom. I recorded our sessions. Hours of them. Him describing his dreams, his rememberings, what it felt like, what it cost. Trying to map it, the way your friend Charlie's been mapping yours. We never cracked it. But we talked. For years, on and off. And I kept the tapes."

She lifted the lid. Inside were cassette tapes — actual cassettes, the little plastic kind, dozens of them, each labelled in Hal's neat hand with a date.

"I've got your father's voice," said Halina Quirk gently, "in this box. Twenty hours of it. Younger than you'll remember him, mind — this is the lad, not the dad — but it's *him.* And there's a machine in the corner that still plays them, because I never throw a working machine away, as you may have noticed." A small dry smile. "You can't see his face, pet. But you don't have to lose his *voice.* Not while there's an old woman who kept the tapes."

I couldn't speak.

She put a tape in the machine — she'd already chosen which one, I realised, she'd thought about this — and she pressed a button with one oil-stained finger, and there was a hiss, and a click, and then, into the ticking room, out of twenty years ago, came my father's voice.

I'd thought it was gone. I'd been so sure it was gone. The whistle had gone first, weeks before, and then the laugh, and then on the night of the fifteenth the whole of him, face and voice and all. And here it was. Younger, yes — lighter, an accent stronger than I remembered, the Cyprus still wet in it, before twenty more years of the North East sanded it down — but unmistakably, undeniably, *him.* My dad. Saying, to a younger Hal, with a laugh in it:

*"You want to know what it feels like? It feels like being the only one awake at a party. Everybody's having a lovely time and nobody knows it's nearly over but you. And you can't tell them, because who'd believe you, and anyway — why spoil it? Let them dance. That's what I think now. Let them dance, and you stand by the door, and when it's time, you're the one who knows where everybody left their coats."*

And then — and Hal must have known, must have chosen this exact tape for this exact reason, because her own eyes were closed and her lips were moving along with it — then my father, twenty years ago, in a falling-down house full of clocks, did a thing I had spent and lost and grieved.

He whistled.

Just a few bars. Some old tune, something Greek, something his own mother probably whistled in a warm village near Paphos that I have never been to. A few bars of whistling, bright and clear, on a hissing twenty-year-old tape — and the sound went into me and found the exact shape of the hole it had left, the hole I thought was permanent, and filled it, not with a memory, but with the *thing itself,* the actual sound, kept safe all this time by an old woman in a box under the stairs.

I put my head down on Halina Quirk's kitchen table, next to her hundred disagreeing clocks, and I cried, and this time it wasn't the apocalypse. This time it was the other kind. The kind that lets something out instead of holding something in. The kind you can only do when you've finally got something back.

And Hal put her old hand on the back of my head, the way you'd gentle a frightened animal, and she let the tape run, and my dad whistled on, twenty years away and right there in the room, and outside the sea went *shhh, shhh,* and for the first time since the night of the red wellies I felt, somewhere down underneath everything, that it was going to be alright. Not fixed. Not back to before. But alright.

"You keep the box," said Hal, when the tape clicked off. "It's no good to me. I kept it for him, and now I'm keeping it for you, and that's the whole of what an old woman's good for in the end — keeping things, for the ones who come after, until they're ready." She pressed the soft old shoebox into my arms. "Your father stood by the door at the party, pet, and he knew where everybody left their coats. And he left *you* his." She tapped the box. "Don't you spend any more of him chasing tomorrow. You've no need to. Tomorrow's just going to come anyway, like it always does, whether you look or not. You've found that out the dear way." She held my eyes. "Let it be a surprise now. Like the rest of us poor mortals have to. There's a freedom in it you've not tasted yet. Your dad found it, right at the end. *Let them dance.*"

I carried the box home in both arms like it was made of glass, which in a way it was.

And that evening I started getting my dad back — not from inside my own worn-thin head, but from everywhere else. From everyone. From a whole town full of him.

## Chapter Thirty-Two — A Whole Town Full of Him

Here is a thing I have learned, and I am still only thirteen now, so again, take it or leave it: you do not keep a person by holding them in your own head. That's the mistake I made. That's the mistake my dad made, and poor Tom, and maybe everyone who's ever been frightened of forgetting someone they love.

You keep them by *spreading* them out. By saying their name. By telling their stories until everyone you know is carrying a bit, so that even if your own copy wears thin — even if some thief in the night spends your dad's face to buy a useless look at tomorrow — there are a hundred other copies out there, in a hundred other people, and you can go and visit him in any of them.

I found this out by accident, the week after the tape, because once Mam and I made the new rule — *tell each other things, even the mad ones* — our whole house changed, and the change spread out of the house and into the town like a tide coming in.

It started with Mam. She'd been so careful, for two years, not to talk about Dad, because she thought it would hurt us, and we'd been so careful not to talk about Dad, because we thought it would hurt her, and so a man who'd been the loudest, warmest, most everywhere presence in all our lives had gone almost completely silent in his own house, locked in a blue holdall behind a frightened hoover. And now, suddenly, we let him out.

Mam started telling me things. Properly. At the kitchen table, over tea she let go cold. The chip-shop queue. The umbrella. The time he tried to teach her to swim and they both nearly drowned laughing. The night I was born — "he was useless, your father, completely useless, he stood in the corner of the delivery room going *she's so cross, why's she so cross,* and the midwife threatened to send him for chips." She did his voice. She wasn't very good at it, but she did it, and it was *something,* it was a bit of him, and I took it and I kept it.

Pip had her six bright things and she gave me all of them, gladly, again and again, because Pip's whole approach to grief is to talk about it constantly and loudly in public, which I used to find mortifying and now think might be the healthiest thing in our entire family. *Daddy called me koukla. Daddy could whistle through a blade of grass. Daddy carried me up the cliff and you could see the whole entire sea.* She didn't have many, but the ones she had she'd kept perfect, like pressed flowers, because they'd had to do the work of a hundred.

Mrs Okafor, it turned out, had Dad too. He'd fixed her fence, twice, and never let her pay, and argued with her about football, and once, the winter before he died, dug her out and three other houses on the terrace when the snow came down and the council didn't, and she'd never told me any of it because why would she, until I asked, and then she sat me down with jollof rice and gave me an hour of my father I'd never had.

The crew had him. I went down to the lifeboat station — they'd invited me, all of us, the whole crew, there was a thing, a thank-you, and I'll tell you about it — and the men and women who'd gone out on his last shout, and on a hundred shouts before, told me Dad-stories I'd never heard, the kind you only tell once a lad's old enough, the funny ones and the frightening ones and the one about the dinghy and the Henderson boy and the channel marker that I'd read about in his notebook — *I knew before the shout came* — except they didn't know *how* he'd known, they just knew that Stav Kostas had a feel for the water that bordered on uncanny, that he'd say "go left, go further left, it'll be by the green can" when there was no reason on earth to think so, and he'd be right, he was always right, "spooky, your dad, God rest him, best pair of eyes on the crew and half the time they were shut."

And Macca, of all people, gave me a bit of him too. We were on the front, a couple of weeks on, and Macca was mending a pot, and out of nowhere he said, not looking up, "My grandad knew your dad. Before either of us. Off the boats." He worked at the netting. "My grandad reckoned your dad was the only one who ever came to see my mam, after. When my grandad went off the lifeboat. Everyone else sort of — couldn't, you know, didn't know what to say, kept away. Your dad came every week for a year. Fixed stuff. Brought fish. Never made it a big thing." Macca shrugged, fierce and awkward. "I never knew that till my mam told us, after your sister and the bell and all that. So. Aye. Your dad was alright. For a melt." Which, from Macca, was practically a hymn.

Bit by bit, person by person, I got my dad back. Not the way I'd had him — I'll never have his face the way I had it, that's gone, that's the price, and some nights it still catches me out and I have to get the tape, or the photo, and do the work. But I got *more* of him, in a way, than I'd had before. Because the dad I'd carried alone in my head had only ever been *my* dad — the one who tucked *me* in, taught *me* to read the water. And the dad the town gave back was bigger than that. He was the man who came to a grieving woman every week for a year and never made it a big thing. He was the umbrella, and the snow, and the green can, and the chip-shop queue. He was, it turned out, *everywhere,* exactly like Mam said. There was too much of him about to ever really lose.

You keep people by spreading them out.

It's the opposite of what fear tells you. Fear tells you to hold tight, to clutch, to keep it all in your own two hands where you can watch it. And you can wear a person right through, holding them like that. The way you wear a photograph through, taking it out too often.

Let them out. Say their names. Tell the stories until everyone's carrying a bit.

That's the only way I know to keep someone forever.

My dad stood by the door of the party, and he knew where everybody left their coats.

And it turned out he'd left a bit of himself in every single one of them.

## Chapter Thirty-Three — The Thank-You

The lifeboat station did a thing for us, the second Saturday after.

I'd been dreading it, a bit. I'm not good at being looked at — I spent the whole summer trying to win a race and then spent the rest of it wishing I hadn't, because it put Macca onto me — and the idea of standing up in front of the whole crew and being *thanked* made me want to climb into the blue holdall and pull the hoover in after me.

But it wasn't like I'd feared. It wasn't a stage and a microphone and everybody clapping. It was the crew room, with the big urn of tea and the plate of biscuits that appears at every single event in the North East regardless of the occasion — christenings, funerals, the end of the world, there will be a plate of biscuits — and it was the crew, in their jumpers, not their kit, just people, and it was us, and Hal, and Mrs Okafor, and Charlie with her hands still bandaged, and Macca, scrubbed up and miserable in a shirt his mam had clearly made him wear, with a small buzz-cut boy of about five glued to his leg, staring at everything with enormous eyes.

Dunny. In real life. Off the tape of my own dream and into the world, holding his big brother's hand exactly the way I'd seen him do it in the rain.

The coxswain — his name's Eddie Solomon, he's about a hundred and he's been on the boat since before my dad, and he has a face like a cliff that's decided to be kind — Eddie said a few words. He didn't make a speech. He just said that in thirty years he'd seen a lot of brave things and a lot of daft things and that the previous fortnight had featured an unusual amount of both, and that the daft things and the brave things had been, as they so often are, *exactly the same things.* He said that Macca Dodds had taken a coble out in conditions that no fourteen-year-old had any business taking a coble out in, and that he was never, ever to do it again, and that it had quite possibly saved two lives, and that both of those things were true at once and he wasn't going to pretend they weren't.

He said that Charlie Begg had held a rope, on a railing, with her bare hands, against the full weight of a spring sea, and not let go, and that there were grown men on the crew who'd have let go, and that he'd be proud to have her on the boat in five years if she fancied it, and Charlie went the colour of a postbox and stared at her bandages and said, "I read the book," very quietly, which made Eddie Solomon laugh a great cliff-falling-into-the-sea laugh.

He said that Halina Quirk had kept her head and made the right calls in the right order and that the coastguard had her down as "informed and credible," which Hal said afterwards was the nicest thing anyone had said about her since 1987.

And then Eddie Solomon looked at me, and the room went a bit quiet, and he said:

"And this is Stav's lad."

And there it was again. *Stav's lad.* Except this time it didn't make me feel like a name on a plaque. This time, in that room full of people who'd known him, who'd gone out into the worst of it beside him, who carried bits of him I'd never even met — this time it felt like being handed something. A name to grow into. A coat that was a bit too big, that I'd get the wear out of eventually.

"Your dad," said Eddie, "would have done exactly what you did. Tied himself on, and gone for the bairn, and trusted his crew to hold the line." He paused. "He'd also have told you off something rotten for not waiting one more minute for the boat. Both of those, at once. You understand me, son?"

"Yeah," I said. "Both at once."

"Both at once," said Eddie Solomon, nodding, like I'd passed a test. "That's the job. That's the whole job, in a town like this. You'll do."

And then somebody put the kettle on again, and Dunny discovered the biscuits, and it stopped being a thank-you and turned into the thing it was always going to turn into, which is to say a lot of people from the same small town standing about in a warm room drinking tea and being quietly, fiercely glad that for once the sea hadn't won.

I ended up next to Macca by the window, the two of us watching Dunny systematically work his way through the entire biscuit plate.

"He likes you," said Macca, of his brother. "He keeps asking about the lad who went in the water. You're a hero to him. It's pathetic."

"I'm a hero to a five-year-old," I said. "It's the best I've ever done."

Macca almost smiled. Then he said, not looking at me, in the low corridor-gone-out-of-it voice, "I'm not going to bully you any more. Just so you know. The Kebab thing. All that." He scratched the back of his neck. "It was — I dunno. You have a go at people when things are bad at home, sometimes. Easier than — whatever. It's not an excuse. I'm just saying. I'm not going to do it any more."

It was, I'm fairly sure, the longest sentence Macca Dodds had ever said to me that wasn't a threat, and the closest a lad like Macca gets to *sorry,* and I knew better than to make a thing of it.

"Okay," I said.

"Doesn't mean we're mates," said Macca.

"Doesn't mean we're mates," I agreed.

Dunny, having finished the biscuits, came and leaned against my leg and looked up at me with his enormous eyes and said, "Did you really go in the *sea*? In the *storm*?" and I said yeah, and he said "Were you *scared*?" and I crouched down so I was looking at him level, the way I'd crouched in front of Pip on the swing, and I told him the truth, because I was done lying to people, even small ones, *especially* small ones.

"Yeah," I said. "I was really scared. That's allowed. Being scared isn't the opposite of being brave. Being scared is sort of — the main *ingredient* of being brave. You can't be brave if you're not scared first. You'd just be showing off."

Dunny considered this with the total seriousness of the very young.

"My brother's brave," he said, finally, decisively, and went back to check whether more biscuits had arrived.

And Macca, by the window, went very red, and looked out at the sea very hard, and didn't say anything at all.

We're not mates. We never did become mates, not exactly. But there's a thing between us now that's maybe better than mates, or at least more useful in a town like ours. We're *crew.* And in Saltmere Bay, when the bell rings on the worst night, crew is the thing that comes.

## Chapter Thirty-Four — Charlie Begg

I should tell you properly about Charlie, because she's the reason any of us are still here, and she'd never tell you that herself, and she'd hate this chapter, which is partly why I'm writing it.

The bandages came off her hands after about ten days. Underneath, the rope had left marks — pale lines across both palms, where the skin had healed funny, that the doctor said would fade and mostly did, except for one, a thin white line across her right palm that never went, and that Charlie, to this day, refers to as her "lifeline," and shows people, and makes up increasingly outrageous stories about. Shark. Industrial accident. Sword fight. The truth — *I held a rope so my best mate's sister wouldn't drown* — she only tells when it actually matters, which is the most Charlie thing about her: she'll lie for fun, all day long, but the true things she keeps for when they count.

The school did an assembly about it. Of course they did. Brindley High loves an assembly. "Local Heroes," they called it, and they had a photo on the screen of the lifeboat and a man from the RNLI came and talked about water safety, and they read out our names, and the whole of Year 7 and Year 8 turned round to look at us, and I wanted to die, and Charlie sat next to me with her chin up and her marked hands folded in her lap, daring anyone to say a word.

Afterwards, in the corridor, a girl from our year — Yasmin Tench, who I'd had a small and entirely hopeless crush on since about October, the kind where you go a bit stupid and drop things — Yasmin Tench came up to me and said, "That was so brave, what you did," and looked at me with actual real interest for the first time in recorded history.

And I'm not proud of this, but a month before, that one sentence from Yasmin Tench would have been the entire highlight of my year. I'd have replayed it for a week. I might have used the gift on it, God help me — slept on it, found out what to say back.

Instead I said, "Thanks, but honestly it was mostly Charlie," and I meant it, and Charlie, beside me, made a noise like a kettle and went red and said "shut up, Nico," and Yasmin laughed, and the three of us walked to French together, and somewhere in there the crush on Yasmin Tench just — quietly evaporated, the way they do, and turned into something better and more ordinary, which is to say I stopped going stupid and dropping things and we just got on, the three of us, which is worth a hundred times more than a crush and lasts a great deal longer.

But here's the thing I really want to say about Charlie, the thing the assembly didn't, the thing the "Local Heroes" slide left off.

It wasn't the rope. The rope was the brave-and-daft thing, the thing that made the slide. But the rope was the *easy* bit, for Charlie. Charlie's never been frightened of doing a thing. Charlie pushed me off a climbing frame when we were four.

The hard thing Charlie did was the lab book.

The hard thing Charlie did was, way back in May, when I told her I could remember tomorrow, refuse to just believe me and refuse to just laugh at me, and instead get a notebook, and squared paper, and *test* it. Build the rules. Make me prove it, and prove it, and prove it again. Because that's what a real friend does with your impossible thing — not swallow it whole, and not spit it out, but take it seriously enough to find out if it's true. And then, when it turned out to be true and terrible, it was Charlie's cold clear scientist's eye, not my panicking brother's heart, that found the one thing on the whole map that I could actually change. *Who's there.* It was Charlie who worked out that I'd been seeing only the lonely version because I'd only ever let the gift see a lonely boy. It was Charlie who said *bring people, build a future it's never watched.*

I had the gift. But Charlie had the thing that's actually rare, the thing that's worth more than remembering tomorrow: she could look at a frightening, impossible mess and *think,* clearly, kindly, without flinching, and find the door.

I told her all this once, badly, sitting on the sea wall eating chips out of the paper a few weeks after. I made a hash of it, because I'm better at writing things down than saying them — clearly — but I got the gist out. That she'd saved Pip more than I had. That the gift was nothing without her. That I'd be a brass plaque without her.

Charlie ate a chip. She looked out at the sea. And then she said, in the flat Charlie way that means she's actually quite moved:

"Well. Yeah. Obviously. You'd be useless on your own. You're all heart and no method. I'm all method." She handed me the chip paper. "That's why it works. Don't make it weird."

"I'm not making it weird."

"You're making it a bit weird."

"Charlie. I'm trying to say thank you."

"I know," said Charlie. She bumped her shoulder against mine, hard, which is the most affection Charlie has ever shown a human being and probably ever will. "You're welcome. Now give us the last chip, you've had loads."

I gave her the last chip.

She's going to be on the lifeboat one day. Eddie Solomon was right. She read the book. She'll read all the books. And one night, years from now, when the bell rings, Charlie Begg will go out into the worst of it with a clear cold head and a thin white line across her right palm, and she'll bring somebody home, and she'll never once make it weird.

She's the best person I know. Don't tell her I said that.

She'd hate it.

## Chapter Thirty-Five — The Bell, Properly

We went back to the Brigg at the end of August, on the kind of day that the North East coast does about four times a year to apologise for all the others — flat calm, the sea like a sheet of beaten pewter, the sky high and pale and enormous, the tide right out and the causeway lying dry and safe and innocent in the sun.

It was Pip's idea, in a way. She'd gone quiet about the bell, after the sixteenth — quiet in a worrying way, the way she'd gone quiet on the walk back that morning, and I'd learned by then to pay attention to Pip's quiets. So one evening I sat with her on the end of her bed, under her drawing of the Brigg that was still Blu-tacked to the wall, and I asked her, straight out, because we were a family that told each other things now.

"You alright, koukla? About the bell. About Dad."

Pip thought about it for a long time, swinging her legs.

"He didn't come," she said finally. "I rang it twice and he didn't come. So I think — " and her little face did a brave, grown-up thing that broke my heart — "I think he can't. I think being lost at sea isn't the kind of lost where you can find your way back. I think it's the other kind." She looked at me. "Is it the other kind, Nico?"

And there it was. The question. The one Mam and I had spent two years tiptoeing round, the one we'd protected each other from so carefully that we'd left a six-year-old to work it out alone, on a rock, in a storm.

"Yeah," I said. "It's the other kind, Pip. He's not coming back. I'm sorry. He's really not."

Pip nodded slowly, like she'd known, like she'd been waiting for someone to be honest enough to confirm it.

"But," I said, and I told her the thing I'd learned, the thing the whole town had taught me, "here's the thing about the bell. You were nearly right. You just had the wrong idea about who it brings home." And I told her what Hal had told me, on the rock, weeks before. That the lost-sailor's bell was never really for the dead. The dead don't hear bells. The bell was for the *living* — for the ones still out on the water, in the dark, who'd lost their way and needed to know which direction was home. "And you've been lost, Pip. For two years. Out on the water in your own head, looking for him. And the bell — the bell brought *you* home. That's what it's for. It's to bring home the ones who can still be brought."

Pip considered this. And then she said the wisest thing anyone said in the whole of this story, wiser than Hal, wiser than the notebook, wiser than me:

"Then we should ring it for *everyone* who's lost. Not just Daddy. All of them. So they all come home."

So that's what we did.

The whole crew came. Me and Pip and Mam — Mam, who hadn't set foot past the prom in two years, who walked out along that causeway in the sunshine holding both our hands so tight it hurt, and stood on the Brigg where the sea took her husband, and breathed, and breathed, and let it be just a rock again, just a beautiful rock in the sun. Charlie came, with her marked hands. Macca came, and brought Dunny, and brought his mam, who I'd never met, a small tired woman with Macca's exact jaw who cried the whole time and said it was the wind. Hal came — Eddie Solomon sent two of the crew down to half-carry her along the causeway over the slippery bits, traitor hip and all, and she sat on a folding chair somebody'd thought to bring, with Schrödinger the cat improbably present in a basket, surveying the sea like a small fierce admiral. Mrs Okafor came with, inevitably, food. And Sputnik came, and immediately found something dead to roll in, because some things the sea cannot change and the moral compass of a Staffordshire bull terrier is one of them.

Macca had brought tools, and his uncle, and they'd freed the bell properly this time — oiled it, eased the seized clapper, mended the frayed rope — so that when it rang now it rang *clear,* not the cracked flat note of the storm, but a real bell-note, bright and round and carrying, out over the calm water and back off the cliffs of Gallows Nab.

And one by one, we rang it.

Pip went first, and she rang it for Daddy, and this time she didn't wait for him to come, because she understood now, and she rang it just to say *I know where home is, and I'm here, and I'm alright.* Mam rang it next, and didn't say who for, and didn't need to. Macca rang it for his grandad, off the same rock, a different sea. His mam couldn't, she just put her hand on the rope and bowed her head. Hal rang it from her chair, with help, for Tom, and for Michael, and the bright note went out over the water and an old woman who'd spent forty years studying time closed her eyes and, for a moment, let it be.

And I rang it for my dad.

I'd thought I'd have something to say, in my head, when I did. Some speech. I'm a words person; I'd half-written it. But when I took the rope in both hands and looked out at the sea that took him, all the words went, and what came instead, completely without me deciding it, was a tune.

I whistled. The few bars off the tape, the old Greek tune, the one his mother probably whistled in a warm village I've never been to. I couldn't whistle it well — I'm rubbish at whistling, it's one more thing he'd have taught me if he'd had the time — but I got it out, the shape of it, and then I rang the bell, eleven times, one for every year I'd had him, and the bright note and the broken whistle went out together over the pewter sea, and somewhere up the coast, I like to think, a man standing by the door of the party heard where I'd left his coat.

We didn't fix everything that day. You don't. Grief isn't a maths test; there's no full marks, no done, no over. But we turned the worst place in our lives back into a beautiful rock in the sun, and we gave the bell back to the living, and we came home along the dry causeway in the late light, all of us, every one, nobody left out on the water.

The RNLI rings it now. Once a year, on a Sunday in August, for everyone the sea has taken from Saltmere Bay — a proper service, with the crew, and the families, and the whole town walking out along the causeway at low water. They call it the Ringing. They reckon it's been going for years, some half-remembered old tradition they've revived.

It hasn't been going for years. It's been going since the summer my sister tried to ring our dad home in a storm and a daft dog and a gobby lass and a fridge with a boat and an old woman with the coastguard's number wouldn't let the sea have her.

But I don't correct them.

Some traditions are better with a bit of mystery in them. My dad taught me that. Half a true thing, and half a thing you make better. That's the best kind of story.

## Chapter Thirty-Six — Pip

I need to tell you about Pip, and the dreams, because if I don't you'll worry about it the way I worried about it, and because it's the part of this story that isn't finished, and maybe never will be.

You'll remember — I told you right at the start, and then again in the ticking room at the Roost — that Pip had said things. *I dreamed the bell rang. It rang and rang and I had to go.* Weeks before I told her anything. And on the rock, in the storm, with the sea coming up, she'd shouted it at me, delighted: *I dreamed you came. I dreamed you helped.*

It runs in families, Hal said. It ran in your dad's. It might run on.

It's running on.

I didn't want to believe it. For a while after the sixteenth I told myself Pip was just a little kid who'd said some spooky things that happened to line up, the way little kids do, the way I'd told myself the maths test was a coincidence right up until 2:15. But I know the difference now. I've learned the difference the dear way, as Hal would say. And the truth is my little sister remembers tomorrow.

Not like me. Not yet, anyway — she's six, and it comes to her sideways, in proper dreams, all tangled up with the ordinary mad dreams six-year-olds have, so that she can't tell the difference between a dream where she remembers the future and a dream where she's a horse who lives in the freezer. To her it's all just *dreams.* She doesn't know that some of them are memories pointing the wrong way through time. She doesn't know it's strange. She's too little to know it's strange, exactly like Hal said, and I am absolutely terrified of the day she gets big enough to find out.

Because I know what's coming for her. I know about the maths tests and the races and the brilliant easy wins, and I know about the cost, the worn-thin places, the faces that go. I know about the boy on the stairs at one in the morning with a photograph of a stranger. I had to find all of it out alone, with only a dead man's frightened notebook for a map.

Pip won't have to.

That's the one thing I've decided, the one promise I've made that I intend to keep, having broken so many. When Pip's old enough — when it starts coming to her clear, the flat true memories, the *Thursday I knew Friday* — I'll be there. I'll have the notebook, and Charlie's lab book, and Dad's tapes, and everything Hal taught me, and everything I learned out on that rock. I'll know the rules. I'll know the cost. I'll know about the door, and the crew, and *being there is not the same as going in,* and *you can't dam a river by blocking one stream,* and *let them dance.* And I'll sit my sister down, the way I should have been sat down, the way my dad never got sat down, and I'll say:

*Pip. There's something about our family. It's a gift and it's a curse and it's the same thing as both, like most things worth having. And you are not going to carry it alone. Not for one single day. Because I tried that, and Dad tried that, and an old woman's husband tried that, and it nearly took everything from all of us. We don't do that any more, in this family. We tell each other things. Even the mad ones. Especially the mad ones.*

I talked to Hal about it. Of course I did. I tell Hal everything now; she's the nearest thing I've got to Dad's map. She listened, and she stroked the cat, and the clocks ticked their hundred different times, and then she said:

"You're frightened for her. Good. That's right. But don't be *only* frightened, Nico. Don't make the mistake of thinking it's only a curse, because that's its own kind of lie." She looked at me. "Your dad pulled people out of the sea with it. You saved your sister with it. It's a terrible thing and a wonderful thing, both at once, and the trick — the only trick there's ever been — is the people you share it with. Tom had it and it destroyed him because he hid it. You had it and it nearly destroyed you for the same reason. But you stopped hiding, in the end, and look what it did. A bell rung. A girl brought home. A whole town, out on a causeway in the sun, ringing for their lost." She nodded slowly. "Pip won't be alone. She's got something you and your dad and my Tom never had at her age. She's got *you,* who knows. That changes everything. That's the only thing that ever changes anything."

So I'm not as frightened as I was.

I watch her, though. I won't pretend I don't. When Pip comes down to breakfast and puts her spoon down and gets that particular faraway look and says "I had a funny dream" — I go still, just for a second, and I listen. Most of the time it's the freezer horse. But every now and then it's something else. Every now and then my little sister tells me, matter-of-fact, mouth full of Weetabix, a small ordinary fact about a day that hasn't happened yet, and she's always right, and she has no idea, and I write it down in the back of Charlie's lab book and I don't say anything.

Not yet.

She's six. She gets to be six.

There'll be time for the rest of it. There's always time. That's the one thing I know about the future that nobody else does: it keeps coming, whether you look or not.

I'm just going to make sure that when it comes for Pip, she's not standing on the rock alone.

## Chapter Thirty-Seven — Letting Them Dance

People always ask me — well, the people who know, which is a small careful circle: Mam, Charlie, Hal, and now you — they always ask the same thing in the end.

*Do you still do it?*

And it's a fair question, and the honest answer is complicated, so let me give it to you properly, because it's the last real thing I've got to teach you about tomorrow, and it's the thing it took me longest and cost me most to learn.

I *can* still do it. The gift didn't go away. I half-hoped it would — there'd be a kind of relief in waking up one morning ordinary, the choice taken out of my hands. But it didn't go. It's still there, the way it was there for my dad his whole life, the way it's coming for Pip. If I sleep on something, I'll wake up remembering tomorrow. That's just how I'm built. You can't un-build a person.

But here's the thing. The till's nearly empty. I spent so much, that summer — the test, the race, the cookies, the snooker, the caravan, all the desperate looking at the sixteenth, and then, worst of all, the one last look that cost me my father's face. I ran my own past so thin that there are holes in me now, soft places, things I'll never get back. And I've learned — Hal taught me, Tom taught me without ever meaning to — that I haven't got much left to spend. A handful. Enough for an emergency. Not enough to waste.

So I made a rule. I wrote it down. Not in Charlie's lab book — that's for the science. I wrote it in *his* notebook, Dad's notebook, on the first empty page after his last entry, after *Saturday. The Nab.* I thought he'd want me to. I thought it was a thing you handed down, a thing you added to, like a lighthouse-keeper's log.

I wrote:

*I will not look forward to win things. I will not look forward to dodge things. I will not look forward to know what people think of me, or what to say to them, or how a thing turns out, because those are the best bits of being alive and I am not giving them away again for anything as cheap as not being scared.*

*I will only look forward if someone I love is in mortal trouble and there is no other way to reach them. And even then, never alone. Never again alone.*

*The gift is not for getting out of tomorrow. It's for getting into it, at the right moment, on purpose, with your eyes open, with your crew.*

*The rest of the time — let them dance.*

And then, because it felt right, because he'd started this and I was finishing it, I added one more line, the line he never got to write because the sea took him before he learned it:

*Tomorrow comes anyway. So live today like it's the only one you're sure of. Because it is. That's the one thing even I don't get to know.*

I closed the notebook. I put it in the soft old shoebox with the tapes, *KOSTAS — SESSION TAPES,* and I put the box on the shelf in my room where I can see it, not hidden, not under the mattress, not behind a frightened hoover. Out where it lives. Where Pip can find it one day, when she's ready, and read both our handwritings, her dad's and her brother's, two frightened boys who learned the same hard lesson twenty years apart.

So no. I don't really do it any more.

I let tomorrow be a surprise.

And I want to tell you — because nobody ever told *me,* and I went looking for the gift like it was the best thing in the world — I want to tell you what an absolute astonishment it is, after a year of walking down corridors I'd already been down, to live the ordinary way. To not know. To get up in the morning and have *no idea* what the day holds. To sit a test and not know the questions. To run a race and not know who wins. To say something to a girl and not know if she'll laugh.

It's *terrifying.*

It's the best thing there is.

The not-knowing — the thing every single person reading this has, for free, every minute of their lives, the thing you probably never think about because you've never had it taken away — the not-knowing is where everything lives. Hope lives there. Surprise lives there. The whole point of being alive lives in the gap between now and a tomorrow you can't see. My dad knew that. *Let them dance.* He stood by the door at the party, the only one who knew it was nearly over, and instead of spoiling it he let everyone dance, and he loved them while they did, and when it was time he was the one who knew where they'd left their coats.

I used to think the saddest thing in the world was not being able to see the future.

Now I think the saddest thing in the world would be being able to.

I had it. I had the thing everyone thinks they want. And I gave it back — mostly, carefully, keeping just enough for an emergency — and the thing I got in exchange was *Tuesday.* An ordinary Tuesday, that I couldn't see coming, full of things I didn't know would happen.

I wouldn't trade it back for anything.

Well. Almost anything. There's still a soft place where my dad's face used to be. Some nights I'd trade a lot to have that back.

But I get up anyway. That's the dedication, at the front of this book, if you read it — *for the ones who get up anyway.* That's all bravery ever was. Being scared, and not knowing, and getting up into the morning regardless.

My dad got up into a morning he knew would kill him, because three other people would get to keep getting up.

The least I can do is get up into a morning I can't see at all.

## Chapter Thirty-Eight — The Slow Way Round

I went back to school in September as a minor celebrity, which lasted, mercifully, about a week and a half, until Dale Ferris brought a stick insect in for the form room and it escaped into the heating system and that became the only news anyone cared about for the rest of the term. Schools are like that. The sea nearly takes you, and then a stick insect gets in the radiators, and the universe rebalances.

Year 8 is much like Year 7, only the trousers are longer and the teachers have given up pretending they don't have favourites. Mr Pegg still teaches maths in his flat weather-warning voice. I still get the cylinder mixed up with the circle, and now I do it the honest way, by genuinely not knowing, which is somehow more satisfying than getting it wrong on purpose because you remembered getting it wrong. I came nineteenth out of thirty in the autumn maths assessment, completely fair and square, no looking, and I was prouder of that nineteenth than I'd ever been of the full marks, because it was *mine,* I'd earned every wrong answer with my own actual brain.

We had a cross-country in October. I ran it without looking. I had no idea, the whole way round, whether I was doing well or badly — which, it turns out, is what a race actually feels like, and it's *brilliant,* your lungs on fire and your legs going and the not-knowing driving you on. I came eighth. Macca came second; he's properly fast, Macca, when he's not being a fridge about it. He waited for me at the finish, bent over with his hands on his knees, and when I crossed the line he said, "Eighth. Not bad. Better than that disgrace at sports day," and I said, "I came *first* at sports day," and Macca said, "Aye, and we both know how," and grinned at me, an actual grin, and that was that. We're not mates. We just both know how, now. Which is its own thing.

Dunny waits for me at the school gates sometimes, when Macca's got the pots, to walk up the bank together. He's decided I'm his second-favourite person after his brother, and he keeps me updated, in enormous detail, on the lives of various small plastic dinosaurs. I don't mind. After the summer I had, a five-year-old's entirely fictional dinosaur soap opera is precisely the correct amount of stakes for a Tuesday.

The thing nobody tells you about almost losing everything is how *ordinary* afterwards is. You think there'll be a glow. You think you'll walk around lit up, grateful, profound, noticing every sunset. And you do, for about a fortnight, and then the dishwasher breaks and your sister uses your good headphones to listen to a song about a frog four hundred times and you're back to being a normal slightly annoyed thirteen-year-old, and the strange thing, the lovely thing, is that *that's the gift.* The ordinary is the gift. Being slightly annoyed about the frog song is the whole entire prize. There are people, in a town like mine, who'd give anything — anything — to be slightly annoyed about a frog song with someone who's not coming back.

So I try to notice it. Not in a wet, sunset-counting way. Just — I notice, sometimes, in the middle of an ordinary fed-up Tuesday, that everyone's here. That the kitchen smells of toast and dog. That Mam's home tonight and not on a night shift. That Pip's drawing something that is either a horse or a sofa. That I don't know what happens tomorrow.

And I think: this. This is the good bit. This right here. Let them dance.

Charlie says I've gone soft. Charlie says a near-death experience has turned me into "a fridge magnet with a quote on it." Charlie is probably right. But Charlie also, when she thinks I'm not looking, touches the white line on her right palm sometimes, the lifeline, the rope-mark, and I've seen her face when she does it, and I know that under all the method there's a Charlie who knows exactly what I mean about the frog song.

We don't talk about it. We're not that kind of friends. We're the better kind.

Mam went part-time at the hospital in the new year. Days only, no more nights. We can't really afford it and we're managing anyway, the way our family manages, with Mrs Okafor's vegetables and Macca's mam's mate's fish at trade price and a tin on the mantelpiece that everyone pretends not to top up. Mam being home at bedtime is worth more than the money. She reads to Pip. She lets her tea go cold at *our* table now, with us, instead of alone after a shift. Some nights she gets the tapes out — Dad's tapes, the young-lad-not-the-dad ones — and we listen to him together, his accent thick, laughing at something twenty years ago, and Mam closes her eyes, and Pip falls asleep, and I sit there in a house full of the people I didn't lose, listening to the one I did, and it is sad and it is happy, both at once, like the bell, like everything worth having.

Both at once.

That's the job. That's the whole job, in a town like this.

## Chapter Thirty-Nine — Sputnik Knew First

I promised you, right at the beginning, that you should pay attention to the dog. I said that Sputnik knew about the whole thing before I did. I'd better make good on that, because he's earned a chapter, and because if I don't give him one he'll know — he always knows — and he'll do the thing he does on the good rug.

Here's what I've worked out about Sputnik, and about dogs in general, and Hal backed me up on this, so it's practically science.

Dogs live a little bit sideways to us.

We live in a line — yesterday behind, today here, tomorrow ahead, marching along, tick tick tick. But a dog doesn't do the line. A dog lives in a sort of *now* that's wider than ours, a now with a bit of soon already in it, the way the smell of a thing arrives before the thing does. That's why your dog's at the door before you've got your key out. That's why Sputnik starts circling the kitchen a full ten minutes before anyone's even *thought* about tea. He's not psychic. He's just standing a little further round the corner of time than we are, sniffing.

And that summer, when the corner of time had something terrible round it, Sputnik knew.

I see it now, looking back. He'd been off for weeks. Pacing. Whining at the back door for no reason. Sleeping pressed right up against Pip's bedroom door instead of in his basket, which I'd put down to him being a daft needy article and which was, I now understand, a dog standing guard over the thing he could smell coming. The night of the sixteenth, when the barometer dropped and the storm turned towards us and my sister got that quiet plotting look that I missed and he didn't — Sputnik was the one who knew. Sputnik was the one barking at the back door while I sat upstairs being relieved about a future I'd seen. Sputnik raised the alarm. Sputnik led me down Sefton Terrace into the storm, a pale shape ahead, glancing back, *this way, this way, come ON.* If that dog hadn't barked — if Mrs Okafor hadn't looked up and said *where's Pip* — we'd have lost ten more minutes, and ten minutes, on a spring tide, is the whole of everything.

A daft Staffie crossed with a draught excluder, frightened of the hoover and the postman and his own reflection in the oven door, saved my sister's life by being slightly further round the corner of time than the rest of us.

So he gets a chapter. And he gets, every single day for the rest of his orbiting life, the crusts off my toast, which he considers no more than his due.

He's getting on now, Sputnik. Grey round the muzzle. He sleeps more. He's given up on the postman as a lost cause and reached a sort of cold peace with the hoover. But he still sleeps against Pip's door most nights, and I've stopped thinking that's daft. I think he's on watch. I think he can smell, a little way round the corner, the thing that's coming for her one day — the gift, the dreams, the lot — and I think, in his own way, he's decided the same thing I've decided: that she's not going to face it alone.

There's the two of us on watch now. Me and the dog.

It's a good team. He's all nose and no method. I'm all heart and no method. Between us, somehow, it works.

I'll tell you one more thing about Sputnik, and then I'll let him get back to the rug.

A few weeks after the sixteenth, I had a bad night. They still come, the bad nights, less often now but they come — the soft place where Dad's face used to be aches like a tooth, and the storm replays itself behind my eyes, and I can't sleep, and I lie there in the dark frightened of a tomorrow I've promised not to look at. And on this one bad night, the door of my room nudged open, and there was a click of claws on the floorboards, and Sputnik — who is not allowed upstairs, who has never once in his life been allowed on a bed — heaved his ridiculous biscuit-coloured self up onto my bed, turned round three times, and collapsed against me with a sigh like a deflating lilo, and put his daft heavy head on my chest, and looked at me with his sad orbiting eyes, and stayed.

He's not allowed on the bed.

He's allowed on the bed.

Some rules you keep, and some rules you find out were never the real rules at all. Dad taught me that, near enough, and so did Hal, and so, it turns out, did a dog.

I slept.

In the morning he was gone, back on the rug, all innocence, as if a thirteen-year-old boy would ever make up something so soft.

Good lad, Sputnik. Good lad. Best nose in the North East, and half the time it's pointed at the sky.

## Chapter Forty — What I Know About Tomorrow

I've told you nearly the whole of it now, and before I take you to the last two bits — the house of clocks, and the day a year on — I want to stop and give you the thing I actually learned. The useful thing. Because you might be reading this lying awake at night, frightened of the morning, the way the dedication says, and if you are, then this chapter's for you, and the rest is just a story.

I can't give you the gift. Nobody can; you've either got it or you haven't, and mostly, blessedly, you haven't. But I spent a whole year being one of the only people alive who could actually see tomorrow, and I worked some things out, and they turn out to be true *whether you can see tomorrow or not.* So here they are. The rules of tomorrow, from someone who's been there and back.

**One. You can't make a bad thing not happen by hiding from it.** I tried. I kept my sister inland, off the Brigg, away from the harbour, forty miles from the sea, and the danger came on holiday with us and did the flume. The thing you're frightened of doesn't go away because you've left the room. It just waits. The only way past a thing is *through* it — eyes open, on purpose, at the right moment. You can't dodge tomorrow. You can only choose how you walk into it.

**Two. Trying to control the future costs you the past.** Every hour I spent looking forward, frightened, planning, scrying, I spent an hour I should have been spending *being there* — and worse, I spent actual memories, real ones, of the people I was so busy trying to protect. You can love someone so hard, so frightenedly, so much in the *future tense* — what if, what if, what if — that you forget to be with the actual them, in the actual room, today. Don't do that. I did that. It cost me my dad's face. Be here. They're here now. Now is the only time you're ever actually with anyone.

**Three. The thing that saves you is almost never you, alone.** Every version of the future where I went out onto that rock by myself, I died. *Every single one.* The only future that worked — the only one tomorrow had never even seen, because I'd never let it — was the one with a crew. A friend with a rope. A bully with a boat. An old woman with a phone. A dog. My whole town, roaring through the dark. Whatever you're facing, the single most powerful thing you can do, the thing that changes outcomes that nothing else can change, is *tell someone, and let them stand with you.* Hiding it to protect them doesn't protect them. It just leaves you alone on the rock.

**Four. Being scared is the main ingredient of being brave.** I told Dunny this, crouched down in the lifeboat station, and I'll tell you. People think brave means not scared. It doesn't. Brave means scared *and you get up anyway.* My dad was scared the morning he went to Gallows Nab; I'm certain of it now; he'd seen how it ended. He went anyway, and three people came home. Charlie was scared on that railing; her hands were shaking before she wrapped the rope. She held on anyway. If you're scared of the morning, you're not weak, and you're not broken. You're just standing right at the start of being brave. The next bit — the getting up — that's the bit that's yours to do.

**Five, and last. Tomorrow comes anyway.** This is the big one. This is the one my dad learned right at the end and I learned out on a rock and Pip's going to learn one day with me sitting beside her so she doesn't have to learn it alone. You can see it coming or you can't; you can fret it or you can't; it makes no difference at all. Tomorrow comes anyway. The sun comes up on the just and the unjust and the people who lay awake all night and the people who slept like logs, exactly the same. So you might as well sleep. You might as well let it be a surprise. You might as well spend tonight being slightly annoyed about the frog song with the people who are still here, because that — that ordinary, unremarkable, un-looked-at *tonight* — is the only part of any of this that was ever really yours.

That's it. That's everything I know.

I had to nearly drown my whole family to learn it. You're getting it for the price of a book.

You're welcome.

Now — the clocks.

## Chapter Forty-One — The House of Clocks

I go up to the Roost most weeks now. It started as me needing things from Hal — answers, the map, the science. It turned into something else. It turned into me just going to see an old woman, because I like her, and because she's lonely in a way she'd never admit, and because she's the last person alive who knew my dad the way I'm only now getting to know him, off the tapes and out of other people's stories.

I bring her things. Toast, which she lives on. Batteries for the clocks, because a house of a hundred clocks gets through batteries like Pip gets through felt-tips. Schrödinger the cat tolerates me now, which from Schrödinger is a knighthood.

And we talk. About time, mostly, because that's Hal's subject and always will be, and because once you've talked to Hal about time for an afternoon you understand why she lives the way she does, in a leaning house full of clocks that won't agree.

"People want time to be one thing," she told me once, mending a carriage clock with the little screws in a saucer. "One clock, one line, everybody on it together, tick tick tick. It's tidy. It's a comfort. And it's a lie." She held the clock up to the light. "There's no one clock, pet. There never was. There's your time and my time and the dog's time and the time the sea keeps, and the time of a man twenty years dead on a cassette tape, all of them running at once, all of them a little bit different, all of them *true.*" She set it down, still wrong, still arguing with its ninety-nine neighbours. "That's why I keep them like this. To remind myself. Anyone who tells you they know what time it is, exactly, for certain, for everyone — they're selling something. The honest answer is *it depends who's asking, and where they're standing, and how fast they're going, and whether they're asleep.*"

It should be a maddening way to live, in a house where you can never tell what time it is. It isn't. It's the most peaceful house I've ever been in. Because once you stop needing all the clocks to agree — once you let them each be a little bit wrong, a little bit their own — the ticking stops being an argument and turns into something else. A sort of soft mad music. A hundred small voices, none of them quite right, all of them keeping going anyway.

A bit like a town, when you think about it. A bit like a family.

There's one clock in the Roost that's different from all the others, though, and I didn't notice it for the longest time, and when I finally asked about it Hal went quiet in a way she doesn't often.

It's a little clock on the mantelpiece. Plain. Old. And it's stopped. Not running-wrong like the others — *stopped,* its hands frozen, dead as my dad's watch in the blue holdall.

"That was Tom's," said Hal. "It stopped the day he died. I never wound it again." She looked at it for a long moment. "Forty years I've kept this house full of clocks running, every one of them, wrong but running, because running's the thing, running's being alive — and that one I let stop, and kept stopped, because I couldn't bear to start his time again without him in it." She turned the screwdriver over in her old fingers. "Daft, really. Sentimental. I'm a scientist. I should know better."

I didn't say anything. I've learned, from Hal, that sometimes the kindest thing is to let a silence be, the way you let a clock be wrong.

But the next time I came — and I'd thought about it all week — I brought something. A blade of grass. A fat one, the kind you can whistle through, from the bank by the cliff path. And I gave it to Hal, and I told her about Dad teaching me, on the cliff top, before I spent it; and how Pip kept that memory when I lost it; and how I got the *sound* back off her tape, the whistle, but never the lesson, never the *how.* "I can't whistle through grass," I said. "Dad never finished teaching me. And you knew him longer than I did, near enough. I thought — I dunno. I thought you might know how. So you could finish it. For him."

Hal looked at the blade of grass in her hand for a very long time.

And then this seventy-odd-year-old retired sleep scientist, in a leaning house full of clocks that wouldn't agree, stretched the grass between her two thumbs, and put it to her lips, and *blew,* and produced the most appalling, magnificent, ear-splitting squawk I have ever heard a human being make, like a duck being sat on by another duck, and Schrödinger shot off the bread bin in horror, and we both laughed until we couldn't breathe, until we cried, until the laughing turned into the other thing and back again, both at once, the way it does.

She taught me. It took all afternoon. I can do it now — badly, a thin reedy parp, nothing like the bright tune on the tape, but it's a sound, it's *the* sound, and I made it with my own breath, and it's mine, and nobody can spend it but me.

When I left that day, Hal stopped me at the door, the way she does, one hand on the frame.

"I'll not be here forever, you know," she said. Plainly. No drama. The way she says everything. "I'm old, and I'm tired, and I went out into the worst of it last summer same as the rest of you, and it took something out of me it's not giving back." She held up a hand before I could say anything. "I'm not telling you to be sad. I'm telling you because you're the keeper now, whether you like it or not. The tapes. The notebook. What I know about the thing your family carries. Somebody has to hold it for Pip, and for whoever comes after Pip, and I'll not be here, and your mam doesn't have it in her bones the way you do." Her clever old eyes held mine. "You're the one who keeps the things now, Nico. For the ones who come after. Until they're ready. That's the whole of what we're good for, in the end, the keepers. You understand me?"

"I understand," I said.

"Good lad." She patted my cheek, dry and warm as old paper. "Now go home. Your mam'll have the tea on. And Nico — " I turned back. "Wind Tom's clock for me. Next time you come. I think I'd like it running again. I think I'm ready. I think it's time his time started up again, even without him in it. Because that's the thing, isn't it. That's the thing you taught *me,* you and your sister and that bell." She smiled, and it wasn't the terrible smile from the first day, the one full of Tom and Michael. It was a new one. "Running's the thing. Running's being alive. Even when you're running wrong. Even when you're running alone. You keep going anyway."

I wound Tom's clock the next week. It still keeps terrible time. It's a little bit wrong, like all the others.

It's running, though.

It's running.

## Chapter Forty-Two — A Christmas, Let Out

The first Christmas after the sixteenth was the first Christmas in three that we let my dad come.

I don't mean a ghost. I mean we said his name. For two years Christmas had been a careful, tiptoeing thing, the three of us moving round a Dad-shaped hole in the middle of the room, all pretending not to see it, all exhausted from the pretending. Mam would put a brave face on and cook too much and we'd watch telly and go to bed early and be quietly, separately, sadly relieved when it was over.

Not this one.

This one, Mam got the box of his decorations down — he had his own, my dad, daft ones, a plastic Father Christmas on a motorbike, a string of lights shaped like little boats that half worked — and instead of leaving them in the loft like the last two years, we put them up. The boat lights along the mantelpiece, over Tom's clock's cousin, the carriage clock Hal gave Pip for her birthday "to start her collection." The motorbike Santa in the window. And Mam told the story of each one as she unwrapped it, where he got it, why it was so terrible, why he loved it, and we laughed, and it was sad, and it was happy, both at once.

We had people. That was new too. We'd been a closed-up little island of three for two years, and that Christmas we opened the door. Mrs Okafor came, and brought enough food to withstand a siege. Charlie came, and her mam and dad. And — this is the part nobody saw coming, least of all me — Macca came, and Dunny, and their mam, because their Christmas was going to be a thin and difficult one with the dad still away, and Mam had heard, the way mams hear things, and had simply rung Macca's mam and said *you're coming to us, no arguments, there's far too much food and somebody's got to eat it,* in the exact voice my dad used to use when he'd decided to be kind to you and wasn't going to take no for an answer.

So our small house was full. Stuffed. Loud. Dunny under the table organising a war between his dinosaurs and Pip's. Macca and me doing the washing-up because the grown-ups had taken over the front room, the two of us up to our elbows in suds, not mates, just crew, just two lads from the same town who both know how. Charlie refereeing a board game with the ruthlessness of a tournament official. Mrs Okafor and Macca's mam and my mam at the table with the good biscuits and a bottle of something, the three of them, who'd all lost somebody to that coast in one way or another, laughing in the particular way that people laugh when they've cried enough to earn it.

And Sputnik, who had positioned himself with strategic genius beneath Dunny's chair, doing very well indeed out of the dinosaur wars.

At one point — late, the little ones flagging, the boat lights doing their half-working best — Mam put the tape on. Dad's tape. The young-lad one. And his voice came into the full warm crowded room, twenty years young, thick with the Cyprus still in it, laughing at something Hal said in 1999, and the room went quiet to listen, all of them, even Macca, even Dunny, and then — *let them dance* — Mam turned it up instead of down, and said "right, that's enough sitting about," and put on actual music, the daft old stuff Dad liked, and made us all dance, in the front room, in the crowded house, on the first Christmas we let him out.

I'm rubbish at dancing. So was my dad, apparently — Mam says he danced "like a man fighting off bees." I must get it from him. I danced like a man fighting off bees, in a front room full of people who were mostly there because the sea had taken something from them too, and I caught sight of myself in the dark window, the motorbike Santa glowing beside my reflection, and for a second — just a second — I didn't flinch at my own face the way I sometimes do, looking for his in it and not finding it.

For a second I just thought: there's a lot of him in this room.

There's too much of him about to ever really lose.

Let them dance.

Pip fell asleep on the sofa at half nine, mid-sentence, the way she does, a dinosaur still in one hand. I carried her up. She's getting big — she's seven now, she was seven by then — and heavier, and one day soon I won't be able to do this, carry my sister up to bed, and I know it, so I notice it, the weight of her, the warmth, the way she fits her head into my neck even asleep.

I put her down, and pulled the duvet up, and her red wellies were on the floor by the bed, lined up neat, pointing at the door, ready, the way they always are.

She murmured something, not awake, not asleep.

"I dreamed it snows tomorrow," she said. "And we make a snowman with a carrot."

And then she was properly under, breathing slow.

I stood in the doorway for a moment, looking at her. At the wellies. At the drawing of the Brigg, still on the wall, that she won't take down.

Then I went to the window and looked out at the clear cold sky over Saltmere Bay, not a cloud in it, the stars hard and bright over a flat black innocent sea, the kind of sky that has no business turning to snow.

I didn't sleep on it. I didn't need to. I'd given that up. I let it be a surprise, like the rest of you poor mortals have to.

But I'll tell you this, and it's the last ordinary miracle in this book, and it's true, Charlie can vouch for it, ask anyone in Saltmere:

In the morning, it snowed.

And we made a snowman, with a carrot.

## Chapter Forty-Three — The Day Before the Day

The second sixteenth of August came round the way they all come round, whether you look or not — slow at first, the long light evenings of July, and then all at once, the calendar turning over and there it was, tomorrow, the day, the special day, the worst day, coming.

It's different now, the run-up. The first one — the one this whole book has been about — I faced alone and frightened and lying to everyone. This one we faced the way we face things now, in our ridiculous tell-each-other-everything family: out loud, at the kitchen table, with too much tea.

Mam had the day booked off months in advance. No night shift this year, no hospital ringing at six to beg her in; she'd told them in January, *not that day, never that day again, ask anyone else.* She'd learned too. We'd all learned. You don't leave the people you love alone on the days the sea is closest.

Pip was alright. That's the thing that amazed me most. Pip, who a year before had been planning in secret to ring our dad home through a hurricane, was — fine. Sad, in the ordinary way, the way you're allowed to be sad about a dad who's gone. But not lost. Not out on the water in her head. She'd been brought home, by a bell and a rope and a fisherman and a dog, and she'd stayed home, and now she talked about Dad the way you should be able to talk about someone you've lost — easily, often, with the sadness and the funny bits all mixed in together, both at once.

The night before the day, I sat up late. Old habit. The house ticked and settled — we've got a few clocks of our own now, Hal keeps giving them to Pip, "for the collection," and they don't agree, and I've grown to love the sound. Mam was asleep. Pip was asleep, wellies by the bed, drawing of the Brigg on the wall. Sputnik was on watch outside her door, grey muzzle on his paws.

And I felt it. The old pull.

*Just one look. Just to be sure tomorrow's alright. Just to know nothing's coming. You've got a little left in the till. One small look, just for peace of mind, and then —*

It was so strong, that pull, on that night of all nights, the anniversary of the worst thing, with my whole family asleep and breakable around me. The fear wanted me to look. Fear always wants you to look. That's the whole trick of it.

I got the notebook out. Dad's notebook, with his frightened handwriting and his last entry and my rules written after. I read my own rules. *I will only look forward if someone I love is in mortal trouble and there is no other way to reach them.* I sat there and I asked myself, honestly, the way Charlie taught me, like a scientist: is anyone in mortal trouble? Is there no other way? Or are you just frightened, and tired, and wanting the one thing that's ever made you feel safe, which is knowing?

Just frightened. Just tired. Just wanting to know.

Not a good enough reason. Not nearly.

So I didn't look.

I want you to understand what that cost, because it's the bravest thing I did in the whole of this story, braver than the rock, braver than the water — because the rock I did in a rush, with no time to think, and this I did slow, alone, in the quiet, with all the time in the world to give in. I closed the notebook. I put it back in the soft old shoebox with the tapes, on the shelf where it lives. And I went to bed not knowing what tomorrow held, on the one night of the year I most wanted to know, and I lay in the dark and let the not-knowing be there, huge and dark and ordinary, the same not-knowing every single person on earth lies down inside every single night, and I breathed through it, and eventually — the way it does, the way it does for everyone — sleep came, plain and dreamless and merciful.

I didn't see the morning coming.

I just trusted it would come, the way it always does, and that we'd meet it together, the way we do now.

And in the morning Mam was making bacon, badly, the way she does, and Pip was drawing, and the boat lights were still up from Christmas because we never quite take them down, and Sputnik was angling for bacon, and it was the sixteenth of August, two years to the day since the sea took my dad, and we were all of us there, every one, in the warm bacon-smelling kitchen, alive.

You don't get to know that's how the morning will go. Not really. Not even me, not any more.

You just get up, and find out, and it turns out to be bacon.

Most mornings, it turns out to be bacon. That's the thing nobody tells you when you're lying awake at night frightened of the morning. The vast, overwhelming majority of mornings, in the end, turn out to be bacon.

We ate it. We got our coats. And we walked down to the front, the four of us — me, Mam, Pip, and a grey-muzzled dog who was absolutely not invited to a memorial service and absolutely came anyway — to ring the bell.

## Chapter Forty-Four — The Ringing

The whole town comes to the Ringing now.

That second year, the first proper one, the one they'd organised and printed an order of service for and everything, half of Saltmere Bay walked out along the causeway at low water on the morning of the sixteenth of August, under a high pale sky, on a sea so calm and so innocent you'd never believe what it was capable of, and that's the point of the calm, I think — the calm is the sea saying *look, I can be like this too,* and you have to hold both, the calm and the storm, both at once, that's the only honest way to live beside it.

They'd done the Brigg up. Not much — you can't tame a ruin and you shouldn't — but they'd cleared the worst of the rubble from the chapel, and laid a path of boards across the rough bit, and fixed a proper new rope to the old bell, the bell Macca and his uncle had freed and oiled and brought back to life. The vicar from St Hild's said some words. The RNLI were there in force, the crew in their kit, Eddie Solomon like a kind cliff at the front. There were a lot of families. That's the thing you forget, growing up in a place like this — how many families. The sea's been taking people from Saltmere Bay for a thousand years. We're all of us, every family in that town, downstream of some storm.

They read the names. That's the heart of it, the Ringing. They read out the names of everyone the sea has taken from Saltmere Bay — as many as they've got, going back generations, off the brass plaques and the church records and the memories of the oldest people in the town — and as each name is read, the bell is rung, once, clear, out over the water.

It takes a long time. It should. There are a lot of names.

When they got to KOSTAS, STAVROS, my mam rang the bell.

She'd said she wasn't sure she could. She'd said it that morning, over the bad bacon, her hands not quite steady. But when the name came — *Stavros Kostas, lifeboat crew, lost off Gallows Nab* — she walked up, and she took the rope, and she rang it, once, clear and round and carrying, and the note went out over the flat pewter sea and came back off the cliffs, and my mam stood there with her hand on the rope and her eyes closed and her face turned up to the high pale sky, and she didn't cry, she just breathed, and let it be, and that was braver than crying.

Pip rang it for him too, when Mam stepped back — they let her, the crew, they understand about Pip and that bell, the whole town does now, the story's got about, half true and half made better, the way the best ones do. She rang it once, clear, and she didn't wait for him to come, because she's seven now and she knows it's the other kind of lost, and she rang it just to say *I know where home is, and I'm here, and I'm alright, and I'm not lost any more.*

And then she looked at me, and stepped back, and it was my turn.

I took the rope. I looked out at the sea that took my dad, the calm innocent pewter sea, and I did what I did the first time, the only thing that's ever come when I've reached for words and found a tune instead.

I whistled.

Better than last year. Still not well — I'll never whistle like him, the bright tune on the tape, that went into the sea with him — but better. The few bars of the old Greek song, his mother's song, from a warm village I've still never been to but mean to, one day, when I'm bigger. I whistled it badly and bravely into the calm bright air over Saltmere Bay, and then I rang the bell, once, clear, for my dad, who stood by the door of the party and knew where everybody left their coats.

And here's the thing. Here's the last true thing about the bell, the thing Pip was nearly right about all along, the thing I finally understood standing there with the rope in my hand and my whole family and half my town around me on the rock where the worst thing happened:

The bell doesn't bring the dead home. The dead are home already; that's what dead means; they've gone on ahead to wherever the coats are kept.

The bell brings the *living* home. It always did. That's what it's for. It's the sound that says, across the dark water, to everyone still out there, lost, grieving, frightened of the morning, lying awake at night: *this way. This way is home. There are people here. We left a light on. We kept your coat. Come in out of the worst of it. You don't have to be out there alone.*

I rang it for my dad.

But really, the way Hal taught me, the way I'll teach Pip, the way you ring a lost-sailor's bell —

I rang it for all of us still here. For Mam, home from the water in her head at last. For Pip, brought in off the rock. For Macca and Dunny and their mam, downstream of a different storm. For Charlie, who'll go out into the worst of it one day with a clear cold head and bring somebody home. For Hal, in her folding chair with the cat, who'd kept the tapes and the time and the things, for the ones who come after, until they were ready. For Sputnik, grey-muzzled, on watch. For a whole town of people who go out, on the worst nights, for each other, and who come home, when the bell rings, to a light left on.

The note went out over the water.

And every one of us, lost or found, living or remembered, was, for one clear ringing moment, home.

## Chapter Forty-Five — Where Everyone Is Now

I should tell you where everyone is now, because I've made you care about them — that was the whole idea — and it's only fair, before I let you go, to leave you knowing where they left their coats.

It's the spring now, as I write this. I'm thirteen and a half, which Auntie Sandra still says is a difficult age, and I've decided she's just going to say that about every age until I'm forty, at which point she'll start on "you're not getting any younger." There is no easy age, in Auntie Sandra's universe. There's a kind of comfort in that.

Mam's good. Properly good, not brave-face good. Days only at the hospital, home for tea, home for bedtime. She laughs more. She's started seeing a friend — a man, Dave, who runs the chandlery on the front and is kind and dull in exactly the right proportions and who Pip has decided is "alright but not as funny as Daddy," which is the highest mark Dave is ever going to get and which Dave, to his credit, seems entirely at peace with. I was braced to hate Dave. I don't. He doesn't try to be my dad, which is the only thing he could have done to make me hate him, and he didn't do it, so I don't. He fixed my bike and didn't make it a big thing. My dad would have approved of a man who fixes things and doesn't make them a big thing.

Pip's eight soon. She's still got the wellies, a new pair, the old ones finally died. She's still got the gift, coming clearer all the time, sideways, in dreams she can't yet tell apart from the freezer-horse ones. I'm still on watch. So's the dog. When she's ready — when it starts coming flat and true, the *Thursday I knew Friday* — I'll be there with the box and the notebook and everything Hal taught me and everything I learned the dear way, and she will not carry it one single day alone. That's the promise. That's the whole reason I'm writing this down, if you want the truth, which you've earned by now.

Charlie's Charlie. She's started a science blog that four people read, three of whom are me logging in on different devices to make her feel better, which she found out about and was furious about and secretly, I think, quite touched by. She's signed up for the RNLI youth scheme the day she's old enough, which is in two years, and she's already read every book about it twice. The white line's still on her right palm. She still lies about how she got it. She still keeps the true version for when it counts.

Macca's getting his GCSEs out the way and reckons he'll go to sea proper, like his grandad, like his dad before it all went wrong — the merchant ships, deep water, away for months. I think he'll be good at it. The fridge thing, the not-being-bothered thing, turns out to be exactly what you want in a man on a big ship in a big sea. His dad's out now, by the way, and home, and it's complicated, the way these things are, and Macca doesn't talk about it and I don't ask, because that's the deal, that's the crew deal, you don't ask, you're just *there.* Dunny's six and still updates me on his dinosaurs. The wars rage on. There have been several ceasefires. None have held.

Hal's slowing. I won't pretend otherwise; we don't pretend, in my family, not any more, and Hal's family now, near enough. She doesn't get out to the front much, the traitor hip, but I go up most weeks, and I bring the toast and the batteries, and I wind Tom's clock, and we talk about time. She gave Pip another clock last month. The collection's getting silly. Pip's room ticks now, a hundred small disagreements, and she sleeps right through it, the way you sleep through the things that love you.

Tom's clock keeps terrible time. It's running, though.

And Sputnik — Sputnik is grey all over the muzzle now, and sleeps eighteen hours a day, and has reached a philosophical acceptance of the hoover that borders on enlightenment, and still, every single night, sleeps against Pip's door, on watch, a daft biscuit-coloured dog standing a little way round the corner of time, guarding the small one against a thing only he can smell coming.

He's allowed on the bed now. We stopped pretending about that too.

As for me — I'm the keeper now. That's the job Hal handed me at her door, and I've taken it, and it turns out the way you do the job of keeping things for the ones who come after is, partly, you write them down. So that's what this is. This whole book. I'm writing it for Pip, mostly, to go in the box with the tapes and the notebook, so that when her tomorrow comes for her she'll have her brother's whole stupid story to hold, and she'll know she's not the first, and she'll know she's not alone, and she'll know the rules, and the cost, and the door, and the crew, and *let them dance,* and *being scared is the main ingredient of being brave,* and *most mornings, in the end, turn out to be bacon.*

But I'm writing it for you, too, whoever you are, lying awake wherever you are, frightened of whatever morning's coming for you.

Because you might not have a gift. You almost certainly don't; hardly anyone does; it's mostly a curse anyway, take it from me. But you've got the thing that actually matters, the thing that's actually rare, the thing I had to nearly drown my whole family to find:

You've got people. Or you can get them. You can tell someone the mad true thing, and let them stand on the rock with you, and hold the other end of the rope.

You don't have to be out there alone.

Nobody does.

That's the whole of it. That's everything I've got. One more chapter and I'll let you go, and you can get back to your own tomorrow, the one you can't see, the one that's coming anyway, the one that's going to be — statistically, overwhelmingly, against all your three-in-the-morning fears —

bacon.

## Chapter Forty-Six — The Boy Who Remembered Tomorrow

I started this book by telling you that the worst day of your life almost never tells you it's coming. And that's true. It's the truest thing I know.

But I told you mine did. Mine sent a memory ahead of itself — the cold, the bell, the empty red welly turning in the white water — the way a wave sends that thin cold lick of water up the beach a full second before the rest of it arrives and soaks your socks.

And here's the thing I understand now, that I didn't understand then, sitting on my bed at twelve looking at my hands, terrified and thrilled and certain I could fix the whole world if I just slept on it:

The memory that came ahead of the worst day wasn't a curse. It wasn't even really a warning. Warnings tell you to run, and you can't run from the sea, I tried, it does the flume.

The memory that came ahead was a *gift.* Not the remembering. The remembering nearly killed us. The gift was the *time.* It gave me time. Time to be frightened, and time to get it wrong, and time to learn that getting it wrong alone was the whole mistake — and then, just barely, just in time, time to get it right. Time to build a crew. Time to find a fisherman with a boat and a girl with a rope and an old woman with a phone and a dog with a nose pointed round the corner of time. Time to learn the only lesson that's ever mattered, the one my dad died still learning, the one Hal's husband died never learning at all:

You meet the worst day with people. Or you don't meet it at all — it meets you, on a rock, alone, and the sea wins.

We didn't let the sea win. Not that time. Not my family. Not on Hal's watch, not on the crew's watch, not on the watch of a daft grey-muzzled dog.

I gave the gift back, mostly. I told you that. I keep just enough for an emergency, and I haven't had one, and I hope I never do, and if I do I'll meet it the way we met the last one — not alone, never again alone. The rest of the time I live forwards, blind, like you, into a tomorrow I can't see, and it is the most frightening and the most wonderful way there is to be alive, and I wouldn't swap it back for anything.

Well. Almost anything.

I still can't see his face. That never came back. Some nights it aches, the soft worn place where my dad's face used to be, and on those nights I get the box down, and I play the tape, and I listen to a young man twenty years gone laughing in a house full of clocks, and I whistle along, badly, the way he taught me through an old woman through a blade of grass, and I let it be sad and happy, both at once, the way the bell is, the way everything worth having is.

I have made my peace with the cost. He'd have wanted me to. *Three lives. Worth it.* He spent himself for strangers; I spent his face for my sister; and if I had it all to do again, every night, every look, every memory worn through — I'd do it again, exactly the same, for the eight-year-old asleep in the next room with the wellies by her bed and a hundred clocks ticking her their hundred different wrong times and a dog on the door keeping watch.

Worth it. Worth all of it.

So. That's my story. The boy who remembered tomorrow, who learned that the only good thing tomorrow's ever been for is showing you who to bring with you when you finally have to walk into it.

It's getting late, as I write this. The house is doing its night-time noises — the sea going *shhh, shhh* like it's trying to get a baby off, our own small clocks disagreeing in the dark, Sputnik snoring against Pip's door. Mam's reading in the front room. Pip's asleep. Tomorrow's out there somewhere, past Gallows Nab, turning towards us in the dark, and I have absolutely no idea what's in it, and that's exactly how it should be.

In a minute, my mam's going to call up the stairs.

I don't know what she's going to say. I genuinely don't — I haven't looked, I never look any more. Maybe it's the bins. Maybe it's tea. Maybe it's just my name, in the voice that used to make my full name sound like a swear word and now mostly just sounds like *come down, you're missed, there's people here, the light's on.*

I don't know what she's going to say.

I'm going to find out the same way you would. The same way everyone does. The slow way. The good way. The only honest way there's ever been.

I'm going to wait.

And — there. There it is. Right on time, on no time I could ever have predicted:

"NICO! Tea's ready! And bring that dog down, he's not fooling anyone!"

Coming, Mam.

I'm coming.

I don't know what happens next, and I've never, in my whole strange remembered and un-remembered life, been so glad of anything.

Let them dance.

*THE END*

## A Note on Remembering Tomorrow

*(in which the author owns up)*

Right. Before the grown-ups write in.

People sometimes ask me whether the science in my books is *real,* and I always feel a bit like Nico being asked whether he's lying, because the honest answer is *yes, mostly, sort of, up to a point, and then I made the rest up and hoped you wouldn't notice.* So in the interest of fair play — which, as Hal would tell you, is the only kind of play worth having — here's what's true and what isn't.

The bit about your brain being a *prediction machine* is true. Genuinely, properly true, and stranger than anything I invented. Your brain does not sit there politely waiting for the world to happen to it. It is constantly, frantically, guessing what comes next — half a second ahead, all the time — and then nudging the guess when the world disagrees. That's why you can catch a ball, and why a magician can fool you, and why, when you miss the last step on the stairs in the dark, your whole body lurches: your brain had already *remembered* a step that wasn't there. Look up "predictive processing" if you want to feel peculiar for an afternoon.

The bit about your brain doing its *filing at night* is true, too. While you're asleep, your brain sorts through the day, decides what to keep, throws a lot of it out, and files the rest — which is why a problem you couldn't crack at bedtime is sometimes just *solved* when you wake up, as if pixies did it. They didn't. You did. Asleep. Show-off.

*Déjà vu* — that creepy feeling that a brand-new moment has happened before — is real, and nobody is completely sure why it happens, which means I'm allowed to have my own theory, and my theory is in this book.

The bit where Nico remembers actual specific facts about an actual specific tomorrow — the maths questions, the empty welly — is, I'm sorry to report, almost certainly *not* real. Almost. *Probably.* I have never met anyone who could genuinely do it. But I've met a *lot* of people who were absolutely certain they'd dreamed something before it happened, and I've noticed that we are all very good at forgetting the nine hundred dreams that *didn't* come true and remembering, forever, the one that did. Which is its own kind of magic, if you think about it. The human brain: rubbish at statistics, brilliant at a good story.

And the bit about memory being a thing you can *spend* — about losing the past if you live too much in the future — that one isn't science at all. That one's just true. You won't find it in a textbook. You'll find it in anyone who's ever been so frightened of what's coming that they forgot to be where they actually were, with the people who were actually there. I've done it. You'll do it, if you haven't. Try not to do it too much. That's the whole moral, and I've dressed it up in a storm and a dog and a bell so it goes down easier, the way you hide a tablet in a bit of cheese.

The tides are real, by the way. Tidal causeways really do flood faster than you'd ever believe, every single year, all round the coast of these islands, and every single year they catch people out — grown, sensible people, who only meant to pop across for a minute. If you are ever near one: check the times, believe the times, and get off the rock. The sea does not care how good your reasons are. Ask the RNLI. Better still, support them. They're volunteers. They go out into the worst of it for strangers, for nothing, on the worst nights, and they always have, in towns exactly like Saltmere Bay, which doesn't exist, except that it's everywhere.

There. That's the science. Now stop reading the notes and go and be slightly annoyed about a frog song with someone you love.

Let them dance.

## Acknowledgements

This is the bit where the author pretends he did it all on his own for fifty thousand words and then, on the very last page, frantically admits he didn't. Fair enough. Here goes.

Nobody writes a book by themselves, the same way nobody gets off the rock by themselves, and if there's one thing this whole story has been banging on about, it's that, so it would be a bit rich of me to stand here and take the credit. Consider this my rope-and-crew page.

Thank you to my editor, who read an early draft in which the dog died, gently put it down, and said, "Absolutely not, bring the dog back, what is *wrong* with you." Sputnik lives because of her. She also removed approximately four thousand of my dashes, of which I have, as you may have noticed, smuggled most back in.

Thank you to my agent, who answers panicked emails sent at hours when only Hal's clocks are awake, and who has never once told me an idea was too daft, which is either great faith or a clever filing system; I've decided not to ask which.

Thank you to the real lifeboat crews, the volunteers of the RNLI, who do for actual living people what Nico's dad does in these pages, and who let me ask a great many stupid questions about tides and shouts and channel markers and answered every one as if it weren't stupid at all. Any mistakes about the sea are mine. They'd never make them.

Thank you to the teachers and librarians who put my books into the hands of children who insist they "don't really read," and who then have to deal with those same children turning up the next week demanding the sequel. You are the bell on the Brigg. You are the sound that says *this way, there's a light on, come in.* I don't think you always know that. You are.

Thank you to the young readers who wrote to me about my other books — including the ones who wrote to point out a thing on page 112 that didn't add up, with a thoroughness that would make Charlie proud. You keep me honest. Fair-play sci-fi has a fearsome quality-control department, and it is mostly eleven.

Thank you to my own family, who put up with a man who goes very quiet at dinner and is clearly somewhere out on a fictional headland in his head, and who have learned to recognise the particular look that means *don't ask him what day it is, he's plotting a storm.*

And thank you, finally, to you. The person holding this. Whether you're reading it under the covers with a torch when you're meant to be asleep, or being made to read it for school and resenting every minute, or lying awake at the difficult age, or any age — frightened of a morning you can't see coming.

Here is the only thing I really know, and you can take it or leave it, because I'm a grown-up and we're often wrong:

The morning will come anyway. It always does. And it will, far more often than your three-o'clock-in-the-morning brain will ever admit, turn out to be bacon.

Get up into it anyway.

You don't have to do it alone.

Nobody does.

*— written in a house that is not nearly leaning enough, within the sound of a sea that is not nearly as dramatic as the one in this book, and at a desk surrounded by clocks that, I am pleased to report, do not agree.*
