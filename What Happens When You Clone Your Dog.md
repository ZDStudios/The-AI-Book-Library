# PART ONE: THREE WEEKS

---

## Chapter One — The Worst Word in the World

There are some words you shouldn't have to learn when you are twelve, and on a wet Tuesday in March I learned the worst one.

The word was *inoperable*.

I learned it in the small back room of the Kelsey Bay Veterinary Surgery, which smells of disinfectant and wet towel and, underneath that, very faintly, of fear — although I might be making the fear part up, because I was the one who was frightened, and when you are frightened you tend to think everything around you is frightened too, even the walls.

The vet was called Mr Pringle, which is a ridiculous name for a person and an even more ridiculous name for the person who is about to tell you your dog is going to die. He had a kind face and very clean hands and a way of crouching down to your level that I would normally have found nice. He crouched down to my level now.

"Bonnie," he said. "Do you understand what I'm telling you?"

I did understand. I just didn't want to, which is a completely different thing, and I think Mr Pringle knew that, because he waited.

On the steel table between us, lying on a green towel with his long grey nose hanging over the edge, was Scratch.

I should tell you about Scratch, because everything that happened — the breaking in, the police, the man with the briefcase, the thing on the doorstep, all of it — happened because of Scratch, and you won't understand any of it unless you understand him first.

Scratch is a greyhound. Was a greyhound. *Is.* I'm going to say *is*, and you'll just have to keep up, because the whole point of this story is that the question of whether Scratch *is* or *was* turns out to be a lot more complicated than it sounds, and I spent three months not knowing the answer, so you can manage a few hundred pages.

Scratch is a greyhound, the colour of a wet pavement with darker stripes running through it, which is a pattern called brindle, although when I was little I thought it was called "bindle" and nobody corrected me for about four years. He is thirteen years old, which for a greyhound is properly ancient. He has a notch torn out of his left ear from before we got him, one white toe on his back foot like he stepped in paint, and a way of leaning his entire body weight against your legs when he wants something, which greyhound people call "the lean" and which everyone else calls "why is your dog falling over."

He is afraid of the following things, in no particular order: the wheelie bin, shiny floors, the vacuum cleaner, men in flat caps, the sea, fireworks, and a particular advert for car insurance that has a meerkat in it. He is not afraid of actual danger, ever, which Mum says is "very on brand."

He sleeps about twenty hours a day. The other four hours he spends eating, being taken to the toilet, and lying in a patch of sun in the front room with his legs sticking straight out like he's been dropped from a height. People think greyhounds must need loads of exercise because they're racing dogs. They don't. A greyhound is basically a very fast settee. You give him one mad sprint round the field, where for nine seconds he becomes the most beautiful and terrifying thing you have ever seen, a grey streak, a bend of pure speed — and then he comes back and sleeps for the rest of the day like he's done a full shift down a mine.

And he had been mine since I was five years old. Which, if you're keeping track, is seven years. Which is more than half my life. Which is *all* of the part of my life I can actually remember.

"It's his heart, mostly," Mr Pringle was saying, in his careful voice. "It's very enlarged, and it's having to work too hard. And there's a mass — a growth — near it that we simply can't operate on without ..." He stopped. "Without the operation itself being the thing that — " He stopped again.

"Killing him," I said. I find it's better to say the bad word yourself than to watch a grown-up try to find a soft way round it. The soft way round takes longer and it's worse.

Mr Pringle looked relieved and ashamed of being relieved. "Yes," he said.

"How long," I said. Not even a question really. I was twelve, but I'd watched enough films to know the script.

"It's very hard to say."

"How long though."

He glanced at Mum, who was standing behind me with her arms folded, the way she stands when she's holding herself together by force, like if she let her arms go her whole self might come apart. Mum's name is Donna. She works two jobs and she is tired in a way that has soaked all the way into her bones, and she did not have the money for this vet's appointment, and she'd brought me anyway, and she did not cry, because Mum does her crying somewhere I'm not allowed to see, which I used to think was unfair and now think is probably the bravest thing about her.

"If we keep him comfortable," said Mr Pringle, "and we can, there's medication, it's not expensive — I'd say you're looking at ... weeks rather than months. Possibly three. Maybe a little more, maybe a little less. I'm sorry, Bonnie. I really am. He's had a wonderful long life for a greyhound. That's down to you."

Three weeks.

I want to tell you that I was brave and dignified and said something wise. What actually happened is that I put my face down next to Scratch's face, against the green towel, so close that his whisker tickled my cheek, and he opened one eye and looked at me — just looked, the way he always looked, like he was mildly surprised and pleased that I existed — and I felt his breath, which smelled of dog and of the chicken Mum had poached for him that morning because he'd gone off his usual food, and I did not cry either.

I am Donna Maguire's daughter. I know how to do my crying where nobody can see.

What I did instead, with my face an inch from my dying dog's face, was make a decision.

I didn't know yet that it was a decision. It felt more like a thought arriving from somewhere outside me, the way thoughts sometimes do when they're too big to have made yourself. The thought was very simple and completely mad and I want you to remember that I was twelve and my heart was breaking, because otherwise the next bit makes no sense at all.

The thought was: *No.*

Just that. *No. Not him. I'm not allowed to lose him as well. I'll find a way. There has to be a way. People are clever now. Somebody, somewhere, must have worked out how to keep a dog.*

And the awful, brilliant, ruinous thing — the thing that turned a sad story about a girl and her old dog into the strangest few months of anyone's life — is that I was right.

Somebody had.

---

Mum paid the bill at the front desk, which came to forty-one pounds, and I watched her not look at the number when she tapped her card, and I memorised the receptionist saying "and we'll pop the heart tablets in a little bag for you, pet," and I memorised the bell over the door, and the rain, and the way Scratch refused to walk across the shiny floor of the waiting room so I had to half-carry him, all forty kilos of trembling dog, and the way an old man in the corner with a cat box on his knee said, "He's a canny age, that one," in a soft voice, and I memorised all of it because I had three weeks and I had decided I was going to memorise *everything.*

What I didn't know — what I couldn't have known, standing in the doorway of the vet's with the rain coming in sideways off the North Sea and my dead dad's dog leaning his whole weight against my legs because he was scared of the wet pavement — was that in eleven weeks' time I would be standing in almost exactly the same spot, holding almost exactly the same dog, and the question I'd be trying to answer would not be *how do I keep him.*

It would be *what on earth have I done.*

But I'm getting ahead of myself. Mum says I always get ahead of myself. She says it like it's a fault. I think it's just that when you've spent your whole life waiting for somebody to come back, you get into the habit of skipping forward to the end to check whether it's safe.

Let me go back to the beginning. Let me tell you about the day Scratch arrived, because that's the day my dad left, and you can't understand one without the other.

---

## Chapter Two — A Short History of My Dog (and, Accidentally, My Dad)

We live at number 9 Ewesley Terrace, in Kelsey Bay, which is a town on the north-east coast of England that you have never heard of and never will, because nothing happens here, except that one time something did, and you're reading about it.

Kelsey Bay is the kind of place where there's a Greggs, an amusement arcade that's been "closing down" for nine years, a beach that's lovely for about four days in July, and a wind that comes straight off the North Sea like it's got a personal grudge. There's a Metro station, so you can get into Newcastle in forty minutes if you want bright lights and to feel poor. There's the front, where old people sit in their cars to look at the waves without getting out. And up the bank behind the town, past the last houses, there are the allotments, where my grandad grows leeks the size of toddlers and keeps secrets the size of my whole life, but I didn't know about the secrets yet either.

Our house is a terrace, which means it's stuck to the house next door, which is stuck to the one next to that, all the way down, like the houses are holding hands so they don't blow into the sea. Out the back there's a yard and then a back lane, which is just a strip of cracked concrete that runs behind all the houses where the bins go and the cats fight and, once, where everything in my whole life turned out to have started. But again. Getting ahead of myself.

The point is, it was in that back lane, on a freezing morning seven years ago, that I first saw Scratch.

I was five. I remember it the way you remember things from when you were five, which is to say I remember it in flashes, like photos with the edges gone soft, and I've stared at those photos so many times since that I'm honestly not sure any more which bits I remember and which bits I've built. But I remember this:

I remember my dad.

I remember he was a big warm shape, and he smelled of cigarettes and Lynx Africa and the cold, and he had a way of crouching down so his face was level with mine — funny, isn't it, all the men in my life crouch down to my level to tell me things, and never one of them has had good news — and he said, "Bonnie. Howay and look what I've got."

*Howay* is a word from up here. It means "come on." It's the most important word my dad ever taught me, although I didn't find that out for years and years, and I'm not going to tell you why yet because it'll ruin it.

And there, on a bit of frayed rope, shaking with cold and fear and looking at me like I was the single most dangerous thing he had ever encountered, was a greyhound. He was young then, but he didn't seem young — greyhounds never look young, they look like they were born already worried — and he was so thin I could see every rib, and he had a notch torn out of his ear, and a sore on his side, and he wouldn't come near me.

"He's a rescue," Dad said. "Off the track. They were going to —" and he didn't finish that sentence, and I know now what the end of it was, because I know now what happens to a lot of greyhounds when they stop being fast enough to make money for people. "Anyway," Dad said. "He needs us. What do you reckon? Can you be dead gentle?"

I could be dead gentle. I sat down on the cold concrete of the back lane, because I'd read in a book that you should get small for a scared animal, and I held out my hand and I didn't move and I waited, and it took about a thousand years, and then the greyhound stretched out his long grey nose and sniffed my fingers, and then — very carefully, like he was apologising — he licked me once.

"There," said my dad, very quietly, behind me. "There you are. He likes you, Bon. He's chosen you."

I named him Scratch because of the scratchy sore on his side, which is not a good reason to name a dog, but I was five. Mum wanted to call him something normal like Rex or Buddy, and Dad backed me up, and so Scratch he was, and Scratch he stayed, for thirteen years.

What I did not understand at the age of five — what took me until the age of twelve and a half and a great deal of trouble to understand — was that my dad did not bring that dog home because the dog needed us.

He brought the dog home because *he* was leaving, and he couldn't bear to leave me with nothing.

Because here is the photo I remember most clearly of all, and I've never told anyone this, not Mum, not Lola, not anyone, until now:

I remember being five years old, kneeling on the cold concrete with my arms around a shaking grey dog, and looking up to say something to my dad — *thank you,* probably, or *can he sleep in my room* — and my dad wasn't crouching down any more. He was standing up. And he was already looking down the back lane, towards the end of it, towards the gap where the lane meets the road and the road meets everywhere else in the entire world.

He was looking at the way out.

And eleven days later, he was gone.

---

I'm not going to make a big thing of it. That's the first rule of being me: you do not make a big thing of the dad thing. Loads of kids' dads have gone. Lola's dad lives in Manchester with a woman called Sharon and Lola sees him every other Christmas and she's fine, she's the most fine person I know. It's not a big deal. It is the most ordinary thing in the world. Dads go.

It's just that most kids at least *know.* Most kids get a reason, even a rubbish one. Lola got "your dad and I want different things." My friend Marek got "your dad's gone to live in Poland with your nana." Even a lie would be something to hold.

I got nothing. I got a dad who put a frightened dog into my arms on a Tuesday and was gone the following Saturday week, and a mum who, whenever I asked — and I stopped asking when I was about eight, because of the look on her face — would only ever say one of two things.

She'd say: "He went, Bonnie. That's all there is to it."

Or, if I pushed, if it was late and she was tired and I was being cruel the way you're cruel to the people you're safest with, she'd say: "Some people aren't built to stay, love. It's not about you. It was never about you."

Which is the kind of thing grown-ups say when they think they're protecting you, and which actually just teaches you that you are the kind of thing people leave.

So I stopped asking. And I had Scratch.

That's what nobody understood, and what I couldn't explain, and what made the three weeks the worst three weeks of my life: Scratch wasn't just my dog. Scratch was the last thing my dad ever gave me. Scratch was the only living creature left in the entire world who had actually *been there.* Who had been in that back lane. Who had seen my dad's face on the morning he chose to give something instead of just taking himself away.

Scratch was the only witness I had left to the fact that, once, for at least one Tuesday morning, my dad had loved me enough to want me not to be alone.

And he had three weeks to live.

You can see, can't you, why I lost my mind a little bit. You can see why a sensible, reasonably clever, not-at-all criminal twelve-year-old girl ended up doing the things I'm about to tell you about.

I wasn't trying to clone my dog.

I was trying not to be left again.

## Chapter Three — Things That Are Free and Things That Are Not

Here is a list of free things: the sea, the wind, the rain, walking, looking at things, being sad.

Here is a list of things that are not free: heart tablets for an old dog (£8.50 a fortnight, which doesn't sound like much until it's coming out of a budget that's already been counted three times), the bus, the Metro, school trips, new shoes when your feet have grown again, the heating, and — as I found out that very week — keeping a dog alive past the point where dogs are supposed to be kept alive.

We are not poor. I want to be clear about that, because Mum would be mortified, and because it's not even true. We are the other thing, the thing that doesn't have a good word: we are *careful.* We are the family who knows exactly how much is in the account, who turns the immersion heater on for forty minutes and not a minute more, who has chips on a Friday as a treat and means it. Mum does mornings as a care worker — going round to old people's houses, getting them up, washed, fed, making them laugh, she's brilliant at it, they love her — and then three evenings a week she does the tills at the big Asda out by the bypass. She is fifty-one kilos of pure stubbornness and she has kept us afloat for seven years on her own and I would, genuinely, set fire to anything for her.

The trouble with being careful is that there's no slack in it. There's no spare. So when life hands you a bill you can't plan for — a dog with three weeks to live, say, and a daughter who has decided, in the secret furnace of her own chest, that three weeks is not going to be allowed — there is simply nowhere for the money to come from. There's no rich uncle. There's no savings jar marked *In Case Of Miracle.* There's just the number in the account, and the number is the number.

I knew all this. I'd known it my whole life. It's why, when I started — that very night, sitting on my bed with Scratch's heart tablets in their little paper bag on my knee and my phone glowing in the dark — when I started typing things into my phone, I already knew I couldn't have what I was looking for.

I typed: *can you stop a dog dying of a heart problem*

The internet said no, basically, in nine hundred different ways.

I typed: *how to keep an old dog alive longer*

The internet said: love them, keep them warm, soft food, no stress. The internet did not understand. The internet thought I was asking about *time.* I wasn't asking about time. I was asking about *forever.*

And then, at twenty to midnight, with Scratch snoring on the floor beside my bed like a faulty radiator, I typed the maddest thing I could think of, the thing you only type when you've given up on sensible, the thing you type as a kind of joke to yourself in the dark:

*can you bring a dog back*

And the internet — and this is the moment, this is where the whole thing starts, so picture it, picture a girl in a cold bedroom in a town you've never heard of, with an old dog dying at her feet and the North Sea wind rattling the window — the internet said:

*Yes.*

---

It was a news article. It was three years old. It had a photograph of a woman in America holding two identical fluffy white dogs, one slightly bigger than the other, and the headline said: **"I Cloned My Best Friend": Pet Owners Paying £40,000 to Bring Back Their Dogs.**

I read it four times.

I'm going to tell you the science properly later, because it matters, and because I promised at the start that everything in this book that *is* possible, I'd explain so you could understand it and even predict it — that's the deal, that's the difference between this and a story about magic. But for now, here is what that article told a twelve-year-old at midnight, and here is roughly how much of it was true:

It said that cloning animals is real, and has been real since 1996, when scientists in Scotland made a sheep called Dolly out of a single cell from another sheep's udder, which I think is a hilarious place to make a sheep from. *(True. All true. Dolly was real. You can go and see her — she's stuffed, in a museum in Edinburgh, looking faintly embarrassed about the whole thing.)*

It said that the first ever cloned pet was a cat, in Texas, in 2001, called CC, which stood for "Copy Cat," which I thought was the best joke a scientist has ever made. *(Also true.)*

It said that nowadays, if you have forty or fifty thousand pounds, there are companies — real ones, with websites — who will take a cell from your beloved pet, even after it's died, and make you a new puppy or kitten with exactly the same DNA. An identical twin, just born years later. *(True. Genuinely, properly true. I'll wait while you go and look it up. See? Told you.)*

And then it said one more thing, near the bottom, almost as a joke, a quote from some scientist warning people not to expect too much. The scientist said: *"A clone is not a resurrection. You are not getting your friend back. You are getting their twin — same body, perhaps, but a blank mind. None of the memories. None of the bond. It will not know you. People need to understand: you cannot clone a soul."*

I read that last bit, *you cannot clone a soul,* and something in me, some small hard stubborn thing that I think I must have got from Mum, said: *We'll see.*

I didn't have forty thousand pounds. I didn't have forty pounds. I had three weeks and an old dog and a missing dad and a furnace in my chest, and somewhere out there, real and true and impossible, was a thing that could bring a dog back.

The next morning I told Lola.

That was my first mistake, or possibly the best decision I ever made, and to this day I genuinely could not tell you which.

## Chapter Four — Lola Okafor Is Not Frightened of Anything

Lola Okafor lives four doors down at number 1 Ewesley Terrace, which means she gets the end house with the extra window, which she has never once let me forget.

We have been best friends since we were six, when she moved up from London and turned up at Kelsey Bay Primary in the middle of a snowy January wearing a coat so puffy she could barely put her arms down, and announced to the entire class, on her first morning, that she had once seen a fox eat an entire chicken in her old back garden "including the bones," and that she was not afraid of the dark, of needles, of heights, or of "any of you." She was six. She has not changed.

The thing you need to understand about Lola is that where most people have a sensible voice in their head that says *that's a bad idea, don't,* Lola has a voice that says *that's a bad idea — how soon can we do it.* Her mum, who is called Bisi and is a veterinary nurse at Mr Pringle's surgery and is the single calmest human being I have ever met, says that when Lola was born the midwife held her up and Lola "looked round the room like she was deciding whether to buy it." This is exactly right.

I found Lola at the bus stop on Sea Road the next morning, and I told her about Scratch — she already knew he was poorly, she'd been round, she'd lain on the floor next to him and let him put his head on her leg, because Lola understands dogs in a way she does not always understand people — and I told her three weeks, and I watched her face do the thing where she goes very still and her jaw sets, which is Lola not crying, which is the closest Lola gets to crying.

And then I told her about the cloning.

I expected her to laugh. I'd half come to laugh at it myself, in the cold light of the bus stop. It seemed mad now, in daylight, the way mad midnight thoughts do.

Lola did not laugh. Lola got the look. The buying-the-room look.

"How much," she said.

"Forty thousand pounds. We can't —"

"How much without the company. How much is it to actually *do* it. The science bit."

"What do you mean?"

"Bonnie." She said my name the way she does when she thinks I'm being slow, which is often. "The forty grand isn't the science. The forty grand is the *company.* The fancy website and the waiting list and the bloke in America who flies the puppy to you in a little jet. The actual science — taking the cells, growing the thing — somebody *does* that. In a building. With machines. Around here, probably." She narrowed her eyes. "My mum says there's a whole department at the university in town that does animal genetics. Dogs especially. There was a thing in the paper. They're always begging the vet's for samples and stuff."

"The university," I said.

"Eslington," said Lola. "The big science block down by the river. Mum's been there. They've got the machines, Bonnie. The actual machines. That do the actual thing."

The bus came. We got on. We went up top, to the front seats, which is the law. And as the bus pulled out along the front, past the grey heaving sea and the old people in their cars, Lola said the sentence that started everything — that put us, eventually, on the wrong side of a locked door at one o'clock in the morning with an alarm going off and a man shouting; the sentence I have thought about every single day since:

"So we don't *buy* it," said Lola Okafor, eating a Babybel she'd produced from somewhere, looking out at the sea like she was deciding whether to buy that too. "We just need a bit of Scratch — a proper bit, with the DNA in, my mum'll know how — and a way into that building. And then, Bonnie —"

She turned and looked at me, and she was grinning, and underneath the grin she was as serious as a heart attack, because she loved that daft old dog nearly as much as I did, and because Lola Okafor genuinely, truly, is not frightened of anything.

"— and then we *bring him back ourselves.*"

I should have said no. A sensible girl would have said no.

I had three weeks. I said, "How."

## Chapter Five — My Grandad Grows Leeks the Size of Toddlers

Before I tell you how, I have to tell you about my grandad, because he's stitched through all of this, even though for most of it he didn't say a word, which is the most grandad thing about him.

My grandad is called Wattie. It's short for Walter, but nobody has called him Walter since before I was born, possibly not since before *Mum* was born, possibly not ever. He is my dad's dad — which I want you to hold on to, because it matters more than anything else in this whole story, and I didn't understand how much for a long time. My dad left. My dad's dad stayed. Work that one out. I couldn't, for years.

Grandad lives in a flat on the Whitethorn estate but he doesn't really live there, he just sleeps there. He *lives* up the allotments, on a patch of ground the size of two buses behind a fence made of old doors, where he grows vegetables with a seriousness that other people reserve for religion. Leeks, mainly. Show leeks. Around here, growing a giant leek for the September show is a proper thing, an old men's war fought in sheds, and Grandad has won the Kelsey Bay & District Leek Show four times and come second the year a man called Eric Stamp "watered his pot at midnight, the cheating get," a crime Grandad has been describing to me, in full, approximately twice a year, since I was small.

I went up to the allotment the day after the bus, the Saturday, because I always go up on a Saturday, and because Scratch likes it there, and because I wanted — I think — to be somewhere my dad had been. My dad grew up on that allotment. There's a photo in Grandad's shed, gone orange with age, of a skinny boy of about ten holding a marrow nearly as big as himself, grinning, gap-toothed, and the boy is my dad, and I have spent a stupid amount of my life looking at that photo trying to find myself in his face.

It was cold and bright. Grandad was in the shed with the little gas heater on, drinking tea the colour of a creosote fence out of a mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST GRANDAD, which I bought him for £3 from the market and which he genuinely loves. Scratch shuffled in and did three slow circles and collapsed onto the old dog bed Grandad keeps just for him, by the heater, and sighed the enormous sigh of a dog who has reached his favourite place in the entire world.

Grandad watched him do it. Something moved in Grandad's face and then went still again.

"Your mam phoned me," he said. He didn't look at me. He looked at the dog. "About the ticker."

"Three weeks," I said. There didn't seem any point dressing it up. Grandad's not a dressing-it-up man.

He nodded slowly. He's got a big square head, my grandad, and hands like bunches of carrots, and he was quiet for a long time, and then he reached down — it cost him something, you could see it cost him, his back's not good — and he put one of those great hands flat on Scratch's side, on the rise and fall of him, and he kept it there.

"I was there, you know," he said. "When your dad got him."

I went very still. Grandad doesn't talk about Dad. The rule in my family about Dad has two halves: Mum says *he went, that's all there is to it,* and Grandad says nothing whatsoever, ever, like the subject is a hot pan he learned long ago not to touch.

"At the track," Grandad went on, still looking at the dog, his hand still on the dog. "Brough Park. Me and your dad used to go, Sat'days. Daft, really. You never win. But we went." A pause. "This one — Scratch — he wasn't fast enough, see. Three years old and washed up. And there's men there, when a dog's no use to them, they don't —" He stopped, the way Mr Pringle had stopped, the way everyone in my family stops at the edge of the bad thing. "Your dad couldn't have it. He'd had a few. He marched straight up to the bloke and he says, *how much,* and the bloke laughs at him, and your dad puts twenty quid in his hand — twenty quid he didn't have, mind, none of us had it — and he walks out of Brough Park with this great daft frightened animal on a bit of rope, and he says to me, he says, *Da, our Bonnie's going to love him.*"

Grandad's voice did something on the word *Bonnie.* I pretended not to hear it. You learn to give people that, in my family. You let them have their wobble without watching.

"That was the kind of thing he'd do," Grandad said. "Your dad. Spend money he didn't have to save something that couldn't save itself." And then he took his hand off the dog and picked his tea back up and the shutters came down behind his eyes, the way they always do, and I knew the door had closed.

But I'd had it open for a second. I'd had Dad, alive, twenty-odd years old, drunk and skint and soft-hearted, marching up to a hard man for the sake of a useless dog and a daughter who'd love him.

*Our Bonnie's going to love him.*

I sat in the warm shed with the giant leeks waiting outside in the cold and my dying dog asleep at my feet, and I thought: *I do. I did. I have, every single day. And I am not going to lose the last thing you gave me without a fight.*

I didn't tell Grandad the plan. Obviously. You don't tell a four-time Leek Show champion that you're planning to break into a university. But I want it on the record that I went up there looking for my dad and I came down with a reason. Grandad gave me that, even though he'd be horrified to know it.

He gave me the picture of a Maguire who spends what he hasn't got to save something that can't save itself.

I was a Maguire. It was, you could say, in my DNA.

---

When I got home there was a white van outside our house with **MICK'S MAN & VAN — NO JOB 2 SMALL** painted on the side, and that meant Mick was in.

Mick is my mum's ... I genuinely don't know the word. Boyfriend sounds wrong for a forty-six-year-old man with a bad knee. Partner sounds like they run a law firm. Mum says "my fella" and goes a bit pink. He's been around about two years. He's a big soft slow-moving man who does removals and house clearances and odd jobs, who smells of WD-40 and Polo mints, who calls me "Bonneh" with no D on the end and "petal" when he forgets I've told him not to. He is not my dad. He has never once tried to be my dad, which is the one genuinely clever thing about him, and which I have never given him credit for, and which I am only now, telling you this, beginning to think might actually be a kind of love.

At the time I just found him annoying, because he was there, and Dad wasn't, and that's not fair on Mick but I was twelve and grief makes you unfair.

"Now then, Bonneh," he said, from the settee, where he was watching the racing with the sound down and a mug balanced on his stomach. "How's the owld feller?" He nodded at Scratch, who'd gone straight to his patch of sun. Mick reached down without even looking and rubbed Scratch's ear in exactly the spot Scratch likes, and Scratch leaned into it, the traitor, and I thought, *even my dog likes you more than I do,* and felt mean, and went upstairs.

Through the floor I could hear them, Mum and Mick, the low rumble of them in the kitchen, the kettle, Mum's laugh — Mum has a proper laugh, a big one, you don't hear it much — and I lay on my bed and got out my phone and I started, properly now, to plan a crime.

I made a list. I'm a list person. I get it from Mum.

**THINGS WE NEED (by Bonnie Maguire, age 12):**
1. A bit of Scratch with the DNA in (ASK LOLA'S MUM — without telling her why)
2. A way into Eslington University science building
3. To find out which machines and what to do (HOW????)
4. To not get caught
5. To not get caught (this is on here twice on purpose)

It was, as plans go, mostly holes. But I had one thing going for me, one stroke of pure dumb luck, and I found out about it on the Monday, when our form tutor handed out a letter, and I read it on the bus with Lola, and Lola went so still and so bright that the woman across the aisle asked her if she was all right.

The letter said that Year 8 would be going, in two weeks' time, on an educational visit.

To Eslington University.

To the Department of Biological Sciences.

For a "hands-on STEM open day."

I looked at Lola. Lola looked at me.

"That's not luck," said Lola, very quietly, folding the letter up small and putting it in her pocket like evidence. "That's *fate.* That's the universe saying *go on then.*"

I didn't believe in fate. I still don't, not really. But I'll tell you this for nothing: if there is a universe, and it does say things, then on that grey Monday on the top deck of the number 19, it definitely, definitely said *go on then.*

## Chapter Six — The Open Day, and the Most Interesting Woman in the World

The Eslington University Department of Biological Sciences is in a building called the Helix, which is a real building made of glass and steel down by the river in Newcastle, and which is shaped — and I promise I'm not making this up, scientists are such show-offs — like a twist of DNA. Two great curving ramps wind up around each other from the ground to the sixth floor, crossing over on little bridges, so the whole thing looks like the thing it studies. I stood in the car park looking up at it and I thought it was the most beautiful and the most terrifying building I had ever seen, and both of those turned out to be correct.

There were sixty of us, in two coaches, plus Mr Dunlop, who teaches us science and has the energy of a man counting down the days to retirement, and Miss Okonkwo, who is twenty-four and keen and was clearly the one who'd organised the trip and kept saying "isn't this *exciting,*" and a teaching assistant called Mrs Frost whose entire job for the day was standing by the toilets.

We got a talk in a lecture theatre from a man about microscopes. We got given goggles. We got split into groups and walked round different labs, and most of it, I'll be honest, washed over me, because I wasn't there to learn. I was there to *look.* I was casing the joint. Lola and I had a system: I memorised doors and signs and which way the corridors went, and Lola — who can talk to absolutely anyone — chatted up the student helpers, the young ones in lab coats with lanyards, asking dead innocent questions. *Do you have to swipe in everywhere? Is it the same card for all the doors? What happens at night, is there security? Do the freezers really keep things frozen if the power goes off?* She is a genius. The students thought she was just keen. By the time we'd done two floors I knew where the fire stairs were, that there was a goods entrance round the back off Pottery Lane that the students used for ciggie breaks because the door "never latches proper," and that there was exactly one security guard on at night, "old Sefton, he's sound, he basically watches telly in the lobby."

And then we went up the last ramp, to the fifth floor, to a lab with a sign on the door that said **CANINE GENOME & DEVELOPMENT GROUP**, and a smaller sign underneath that said, in marker pen on laminated paper, **PLEASE DO NOT LET THE DOGS OUT, NO MATTER WHAT THEY TELL YOU,** and I forgot, completely, for about an hour, that I was there to commit a crime.

Because there were dogs.

Not lab dogs in cages, the way you're imagining, the way I was dreading. There was a big sunny room, more like somebody's enormous living room than a laboratory, with sofas and dog beds and a fenced-off garden bit out the back, and in it were five dogs of various shapes and sizes, lying about, being dogs, and a couple of students sitting on the floor among them with clipboards, and the dogs all came wuffling over to sniff sixty children, and it was, frankly, the best part of any school trip in the history of school trips.

And in the middle of it, sitting cross-legged on the floor in jeans and a jumper with a hole in the elbow, with a spaniel's head in her lap and her glasses pushed up into a mad nest of grey-brown hair, was a woman.

"Right, you horrors," she said, not getting up. "Sit down on the floor, all of you, go on, the dogs prefer you when you're small, they're like cats that way, only cleverer, don't tell the cat people I said that. I'm Dr Lillico. I run this circus. Who can tell me what a clone is?"

I sat down on the floor of the Canine Genome and Development laboratory, with a fox-red Labrador immediately reversing onto my lap as if she'd been waiting all day for somewhere to sit, and I looked at Dr Petra Lillico, and I want to tell you that I knew, right then, that she was going to change my entire life.

I didn't. You never do. But I do remember thinking she was the most interesting-looking grown-up I'd ever seen — interesting the way Grandad's allotment is interesting, like there's a lot more going on round the back than you can see from the path — and I remember that when nobody answered her question, I did.

"It's an identical twin," I said. "Born later. Same DNA. Made from one cell."

Dr Lillico looked at me. Properly looked, over the top of the spaniel, the way grown-ups almost never look at you, like I'd said something that counted.

"Yes," she said. "Exactly that. Not a copy. Not a photocopy, not a resurrection, not magic — a twin. Same recipe. Different cake." She smiled, and it was a crooked, tired, genuinely delighted smile. "What's your name?"

"Bonnie," I said.

"Good name," said Dr Lillico. "There was a Bonnie in the very first litter we ever — " and then she stopped, just for a second, just a flicker, and something crossed her face, something I'd recognise later but didn't then, and she finished, "— a long time ago. Right. Who wants to meet a dog who is, genetically speaking, the exact same animal as that one over there?"

And she pointed to two beagles, in opposite corners of the room, who looked, when you looked, *exactly* the same. Same everything. Born, she told us, four years apart.

I looked at those two identical dogs.

And the furnace in my chest, the one that had been roaring quietly since the green towel and the word *inoperable,* went very, very calm.

Because now I knew it was real. Now I'd seen it with my own eyes. It wasn't a midnight article and a woman in America. It was here. In my city. In this building. Up the second of these two DNA-shaped ramps, behind the door with old Sefton and the latch that doesn't latch, in this sunny room full of dogs.

The thing that could bring Scratch back was real, and it was twenty-five minutes from my house on the Metro.

I just had to take it.

## Chapter Seven — A Plan With Far Too Many Holes In It

We had two weeks. Scratch had — well. We didn't know exactly. Mr Pringle had said three weeks on a Tuesday, which I'd decided meant the twenty-second day was the one I had to fear, and I'd marked it in my head, and I counted down to it every single morning the second I woke up, before I was even properly awake, my brain doing the maths before my eyes were open: *nineteen days. Eighteen. Seventeen.* It's a horrible way to live, counting down to a thing you'd give anything to count up from instead.

So everything had to happen fast, and everything had holes in it, and here is where I have to be honest with you about what kind of person I am, because I think it matters.

I am not brave. That's Lola. I am not clever in the way that wins prizes. What I am — the one thing I am, the thing I got from my mum and her mum before her, a whole line of careful, stubborn, north-east women — is that I do not give up. I am extremely, almost stupidly, hard to put off. You can tell me a thing is impossible and I will simply keep standing in front of it, like the sea in front of Kelsey Bay, going at the same problem over and over with the patience of weather, until something gives. Usually it's me that gives. But not always. Sometimes it's the cliff.

So Lola and I went at the holes in the plan, one at a time, over the next two weeks, in Lola's bedroom, which has a lock on the door and a window onto the back lane and a poster of the bones of a horse that her mum gave her and that Lola thinks is "sick" in the good way.

**Hole 1: A bit of Scratch with the DNA in.**

This one, it turned out, was easy, and it was Lola's mum who solved it without ever knowing she had. Bisi Okafor came home from the vet's one evening and over tea (jollof rice, and I have never told my own mum how much better Bisi's cooking is than ours, I'm not stupid) she was telling Lola about a dog whose owners wanted to "bank its DNA," and how all you need is a cheek swab — a special long cotton bud, rubbed inside the cheek for thirty seconds, "the cells come away, that's all you need, the whole animal's in there, in every cell, it's a miracle if you think about it, eat your rice." And Lola, not looking up from her rice, said, "Have we got those swabs at the vet's, the cheek ones?" and Bisi said, "Box of them in the back, why," and Lola said, "School project," which is the answer to everything, and the next day there were three sterile cheek swabs in foil packets in Lola's blazer pocket.

I want to be clear that I felt bad about this. I felt bad about all of it, the whole way through, in a low constant hum, like the heating. But I'd done the sum, and the sum came out the same every time: *one old dog's life, against a long cotton bud and a bit of trouble.* I'd have done a lot worse than nick a cotton bud. I'd find that out, too.

**Hole 2: A way in.**

The open day had given us the goods door on Pottery Lane that "never latches proper." Lola had clocked it, and on a Saturday we went back into town "to go to Primark" (we did, also, go to Primark, for cover, and I bought a pair of socks I didn't want) and we walked down Pottery Lane and looked at the door, dead casual, two girls passing, and Lola, without breaking stride, reached out and pushed it, and it opened.

It just opened. A heavy grey fire door, into a concrete stairwell, with a sign on it that said FIRE EXIT — KEEP CLEAR, and it opened, because somebody, some student going for a tab break, had wedged a folded-up beer mat into the latch hole so they wouldn't get locked out, and had forgotten to take it out, and so the most important security door in the entire building was being held permanently very slightly open by a beer mat advertising Newcastle Brown Ale.

I have thought about this a lot since. I have thought about how the cleverest, most expensive, most carefully designed systems in the world — a building shaped like DNA, full of machines that can build a living animal from a single cell — can be undone completely by one tired twenty-year-old and a beer mat. I don't fully know what the lesson is. Lola says the lesson is "everything's held together with chewing gum and hope, Bonnie, that's the actual secret of being a grown-up, the sooner you know it the better." She might be right.

**Hole 3: Which machine, and what to do.**

This was the big one. This was the hole the whole plan should have fallen down. Because even if we got in, even if we got to the fifth floor, even if we found Dr Lillico's lab — what then? I didn't know what a cloning machine looked like. I didn't know what to do with a cheek swab. I'd imagined, in the daft way you imagine things, that there'd be a sort of ... slot. That you'd post your dog's DNA into a slot and a light would go green. I knew that was stupid even as I imagined it.

We did everything we could. We watched videos. We read things we three-quarters understood about something called "somatic cell nuclear transfer," which is the actual name for the actual thing, and which means — roughly, and I'll do it properly later — you take an empty egg cell, you put your animal's DNA into it, and you give it a tiny zap to trick it into thinking it's been fertilised and start growing. We learned the words. We did *not* learn how to do it, because you cannot learn how to do it from your friend's bedroom in Kelsey Bay, because it takes people years and a building shaped like DNA.

In the end, three days before, sitting on Lola's floor surrounded by printouts, I said the thing out loud that I think we'd both been avoiding.

"We can't actually do it. Can we. Even if we get in. We can't work the machines. We don't know how. We'll get all the way in there and we'll just be ... two kids holding a cotton bud."

And Lola, who is not frightened of anything, was quiet for a long time. And then she said something that I have never forgotten, and that turned out to be — by complete accident, neither of us could have known — the truest and most important thing anyone said in the whole story.

"Then we don't try to do it ourselves," Lola said slowly. "We get the swab to where the machines are. We get it *into the system,* somehow — into a freezer, onto a bench, into one of their sample trays, whatever — somewhere a scientist will find it. And we leave a note. And we let *them* do it." She looked up. "We can't bring Scratch back, Bonnie. We were never going to be able to. But maybe — maybe we can get him into a building full of people who *can,* and just ... hope one of them is the kind of person who would."

I think about that a lot. *Hope one of them is the kind of person who would.*

Because here is the thing I didn't know, sitting on Lola Okafor's bedroom floor seventy-two hours before we broke the law: there *was* a person, in that building, who would.

I'd already met her. She had a hole in the elbow of her jumper and a spaniel asleep in her lap and a secret of her own that was so much bigger than mine that when I finally found it out, it knocked the wind clean out of me.

But that's later. First, we had to get in. And to get in, we needed a night when the whole town would be making enough noise to cover for us.

We needed the fireworks.

## Chapter Eight — Thirty Seconds Inside a Greyhound's Cheek

You would think the easiest part of stealing your dog's DNA would be the dog. The dog loves you. The dog would, you'd assume, hold still.

The dog did not hold still.

We did it on the Thursday night, in my bedroom, with the door shut and Mum downstairs watching telly with Mick, and I have to tell you it was the closest the entire operation came to total disaster, which when you consider how the rest of it went is really saying something.

The swab is a long thing, like a cotton bud that's been stretched, in a sealed foil packet, and Bisi had said all you do is rub it firmly inside the cheek for thirty seconds, against the inside of the mouth, to gather the cells. Thirty seconds. I have timed thirty seconds since, on purpose, just to remind myself: it's nothing. It's the length of an advert. It is, when you are trying to hold it inside the cheek of a thirteen-year-old greyhound who has decided that the long white thing approaching his face is The Enemy, approximately one hundred years.

Scratch did not bite. He has never bitten anyone in his life; he wouldn't know how; he'd apologise if he did. What Scratch did was the greyhound version of resistance, which is to turn his whole long neck slowly away from you with the unanswerable dignity of an elderly duke declining a second helping of trifle, while making a noise — not a growl, greyhounds don't really growl — a sort of low, mournful, put-upon *grrooo,* the noise of a creature whose trust in the universe has been fundamentally shaken.

"Hold his head," whispered Lola, who had come round under cover of "doing homework."

"I am holding his head!"

"Hold it *more!*"

"He's got a really long head, Lola, there's a lot of it!"

In the end it took all three of us — me, Lola, and a slice of ham — and even then I only got about nineteen of my thirty seconds before he wrenched away and gave me a look of such deep and genuine betrayal that I nearly called the whole thing off then and there. I sat on my bedroom floor with the swab in my hand, and Scratch retreated to the far corner and lay down with his back to me, the way he does, a great grey crescent of disappointment, and I felt like the worst person who had ever lived.

"Bonnie," said Lola gently. "It's nineteen seconds. Mum said thirty. It might not be enough."

So I had to do the other one. I had to crawl across my own bedroom floor and apologise to my dog, which I did, out loud, in a whisper, with my face in his neck — "I'm sorry, I'm sorry, I'm trying to *save* you, I know you don't understand, I'm sorry" — and bribe him with the rest of the ham, and do it again, and that time I got my thirty seconds, all thirty, rubbing gently now, talking the whole time, while my dog forgave me, because that is the entire and complete job description of a dog, the forgiving.

We had our sample. Two swabs of it, sealed back in their packets, with the date written on in biro: cells from inside the cheek of Scratch Maguire, every one of them carrying, folded up impossibly small, the complete instructions for building him again.

I held them in my hand and they weighed nothing at all.

That night I couldn't sleep, and at about one in the morning I got up and wrote the note. I'd been putting it off, because Lola's plan only worked if the note worked — if whoever found our sample read it and felt something — and I didn't trust myself to find the words. I'm not a words person. I'm a list person.

But it turns out that when it's one in the morning and your dog is dying on the floor beside you and you have one shot at the heart of a stranger, the words come.

I wrote it out about nine times. This is the one I kept. I know it off by heart, because I've thought about it every day since, and because — well. You'll see why.

> *To whoever finds this,*
>
> *I'm sorry I came into your lab. I know it's wrong and I'll never do anything like it again, I promise.*
>
> *Inside this packet there are cells from my dog. His name is Scratch. He is a greyhound and he is thirteen and the vet says he has three weeks to live, and by the time you read this he might already be gone, and I couldn't think of anything else to do.*
>
> *I know you can make a dog from cells. I came on the school open day and the lady — Dr Lillico — showed us the two beagles that are the same dog born twice. I know it costs forty thousand pounds and I haven't got forty pounds. I know this is mad.*
>
> *But my dad gave me Scratch on the last week before he left and he is the only thing I have got left from before, and I am not asking you to give me my dad back, I know you can't, I'm only asking about the dog.*
>
> *If you can do anything, anything at all, please. And if you can't, please don't throw him in the bin. Please keep him cold and safe somewhere. I'll find the money one day. I'll spend my whole life finding it.*
>
> *I'm really sorry. Thank you for reading this.*
>
> *Bonnie*

I read it back, and I cried, finally, the first proper crying I'd done since the green towel, alone in the dark at one in the morning with the words *the only thing I have got left from before* sitting on the page in my own rubbish handwriting, and Scratch woke up and came and put his long nose under my arm, the way he does, like he's trying to lift the sad off you with his face, and I let him.

I didn't know, writing it, that I'd just made the single biggest mistake of the entire plan. I'd signed it. I'd written my name. And not just my name — I'd written *the lady, Dr Lillico,* and *the school open day,* which is to say I had, in one short note, handed a complete stranger every single piece of information she would need to find a twelve-year-old girl in a town called Kelsey Bay.

If you're going to commit a crime, don't write the victim a letter explaining exactly who you are and why. It turns out the police find that part quite helpful. So, as it happened, did Dr Petra Lillico.

The funfair came on the Wednesday before Easter, the way it does every year, rolling into town on a convoy of lorries and setting up on the links at the top of the front — the waltzers, the big wheel, a ride called the Oblivion that's basically a way of paying four pounds to be sick, hook-a-duck, the lot — and the whole of Kelsey Bay went a bit electric the way it always does, the air smelling of diesel and candyfloss and the sea, music thudding out over the water till midnight, and a fireworks display promised for the Saturday, the last night, as the grand finale.

Saturday was the night. We'd picked it weeks before, on Lola's bedroom floor, and as it came closer it stopped being a square on a calendar and started being a real night that was actually going to happen, with real doors and real darkness and real being-caught, and I'd be lying if I said I wasn't frightened. I was extremely frightened. I'm not Lola.

The alibi was simple and it was the oldest alibi in the world: I was sleeping over at Lola's. Lola was sleeping over at mine. Each set of mums thought we were four doors away at the other one's house. It works because mums don't ring each other to check; they're too busy and they trust you, and that trust is a terrible, useful thing, and I felt the weight of abusing it like a stone in my shoe the whole way through.

Mum was on a late at Asda till ten. Mick was taking his nephew Kyle to the fair. Grandad was at his shed, where he basically lives. Bisi was on a night shift at the vet's. The town was looking the other way, at the lights and the noise and the sea.

The one part of the plan I hated — the part I still hate, the part I'd undo if I could undo any of it — was Scratch.

Because we couldn't take him, obviously. And he was frightened of fireworks. Properly frightened, the deep old kind of fear that I think went all the way back to the track, to bangs and crowds and bad men. On firework nights he shook. He pressed himself into the gap behind the settee and he shook, and the only thing that helped was me, lying on the floor next to him with my arm over him and my mouth against his ear, telling him it was all right, telling him I was there, telling him I'd always be there.

And on the one night he would need that more than any other night of his last three weeks on earth — I was going to leave him. To shake alone behind the settee. So that I could go and break into a building to try to save him.

I have turned that over so many times. It's the cruellest maths in the whole story: to try to keep him forever, I had to abandon him for one night. I left the radio on for him, tuned to the classical station, turned up loud to cover the bangs. I left my old jumper in his bed so he'd have my smell. I lay with him for an hour before we left, until I had to peel myself away, and his eyes followed me to the door, and I have never in my life wanted so badly to just stay, to forget the whole stupid plan, to lie on the floor of number 9 Ewesley Terrace with my dog and let three weeks be three weeks.

But three weeks wasn't going to be three weeks. Not if I could help it.

"Howay," said Lola softly, at the back door, using my dad's word without knowing it was my dad's word. "Bonnie. We have to go. Last Metro back's twenty to midnight."

I looked at Scratch one more time. He thumped his tail, once, on the floor.

"I'll bring you back," I told him. I meant it as a promise about the night. I had no idea — none — how literally, and how strangely, I was going to end up keeping it.

And we went out into the back lane, into the dark and the diesel and the distant thud of the funfair, two girls in dark coats with a stolen DNA sample and a handwritten note and a beer mat's worth of luck, and we walked down the lane to the gap at the end — the same gap my dad had looked at the morning he decided to leave — and we turned towards the Metro, and the first firework went up over the sea behind us, green, and bloomed, and fell.

## Chapter Nine — The Helix at Night

Newcastle at night, when you're twelve and not supposed to be there, is a different city.

We came up out of the Metro at the Haymarket into cold bright noise — students, mostly, spilling between bars, nobody looking twice at two kids, which was the plan; a city full of young people is a brilliant place to be invisible — and we walked down towards the river the way we'd memorised on the open day, past the cathedral, down the steep streets where the buildings lean in, until the crowds thinned and the bars stopped and there was just us and the streetlights and the great black river at the bottom and, rising up against the orange sky on the far bank, lit from inside, twisting, the Helix.

It looked, at night, exactly like what it was: a thing that should not be possible, made of light, holding the river's reflection in its glass like it had swallowed a second river.

"Right," whispered Lola, even though there was no one to whisper to. "Pottery Lane's round the back. Then up. We've done this in our heads a hundred times. We just do it for real now and we don't stop and we don't talk and if anything goes wrong we *run,* we don't argue, we just go, separate ways, and meet at the Metro. Yeah?"

"Yeah," I said. My voice came out as basically air.

Pottery Lane was a cobbled cut between the Helix and the building next door, dark, smelling of bins and old beer, with one broken streetlight buzzing on and off like it couldn't make its mind up. And there, halfway down, exactly where it had been, was the grey fire door — the goods entrance — and as we got close I could see, even in the dark, the pale corner of the beer mat still wedged in the latch, still holding the most expensive door in the city a quarter of an inch open.

I genuinely thought it wouldn't be there. I'd built a whole second plan in my head for when it wasn't there. It was there.

Lola hooked her fingers round the edge of the door and pulled, slow, and it came open with a soft scrape, and the dark inside was darker than the dark outside, and the cold breathed out at us, lab-cold, chemical, and from somewhere far up inside the building came the faint, enormous hum of a place that never fully sleeps, all those freezers and machines keeping all those frozen, growing, half-finished things at exactly the right temperature, all night, every night, forever.

We went in. Lola took the beer mat out and let the door click properly shut behind us — "so it looks normal," she breathed, "so old Sefton doesn't clock it on his round" — and just like that we were locked inside the Eslington University Helix building at half past nine on the Saturday night of the Easter funfair, and there was no quick way back out, and I have honestly never been so frightened in my entire life, then or since, not even later, not even with the man and the briefcase, because later I at least sort of knew what I was doing.

The stairwell was bare grey concrete, lit by a single emergency light, going up and up, the railings spiralling away above us. We did not take the lift. Obviously you do not take the lift; the lift has cameras and pings when it stops. We took the stairs, five floors, on the balls of our feet, and I counted them, because counting is the thing I do instead of panicking, and there are a hundred and four steps from the goods door to the fifth floor of the Helix, and I will remember that for the rest of my life.

On the fifth floor the stairwell door had a little wired-glass window in it, and through it we could see the corridor — *our* corridor, the one we'd walked on the open day — lit low, nightlights only, stretching away towards the curve of the building, and at the far end, the door we wanted, with its sign: **CANINE GENOME & DEVELOPMENT GROUP.**

And halfway between us and the door, a square of blue-white light spilling out across the floor from a doorway, and the murmur of a television, and the unmistakable, ordinary, terrifying sound of a human being clearing his throat.

Sefton. The night guard. *He basically watches telly in the lobby,* the student had said — but the lobby on the fifth floor, it turned out, had a little security desk in it, and tonight, of all nights, old Sefton wasn't in the main lobby downstairs. He was up here. Right between us and everything.

We stood in the stairwell with our backs flat against the concrete, not breathing, and through the wired glass I watched the blue light flicker, and I heard the tinny voice of a football commentator — *he's watching the match,* I thought, *the late game* — and I thought about the last Metro at twenty to midnight, and I thought about Scratch shaking behind the settee, and I thought about the twenty-second day, and I did the only thing a person like me knows how to do.

I waited.

We waited eleven minutes. I counted those too. And at minute eleven, the football must have gone to half-time, because old Sefton stood up — we heard the chair, the grunt of a big man getting up — and he came out of the lobby into the corridor, and turned the *other* way, away from us, towards the lifts and the toilets at the far north end, walking slow, a big soft shape against the nightlights, a flask in his hand, off for his half-time wee and a top-up of tea, the most human thing in the world, and the second the toilet door swung shut behind him Lola grabbed my sleeve and breathed one word —

"*Now.*"

— and we went through the stairwell door and down that low-lit corridor faster and quieter than I have ever moved in my life, past the spilling blue light of the empty lobby with the football still murmuring to nobody, all the way to the end, to the door that said CANINE GENOME & DEVELOPMENT GROUP, and I grabbed the handle and pushed and my heart fell straight through the floor —

— because of course it was locked.

Of course it was. The goods door was held by a beer mat, but the lab itself, the actual lab, with the actual machines, was locked, with a proper electronic lock, a little black box with a red light, the kind you need a card for, the kind two kids from Kelsey Bay absolutely do not have, and Sefton would be back from the toilet in about ninety seconds, and we were standing in full view in a low-lit corridor next to a locked door with nowhere to hide and a football match ticking down to half-time being over.

"Lola," I whispered. "*Lola.* It's locked. What do we —"

And Lola Okafor, who is not frightened of anything, looked at the little black box with the red light, and then she looked at the door beside it — a second door, a plain one, no sign, that we hadn't even noticed on the open day — and on a hook beside *that* door, on a simple metal hook screwed into the wall where any cleaner or technician could reach it, where it had clearly hung for years because nobody had ever once imagined two children standing in this corridor at half past nine at night, was a lanyard.

And on the lanyard was a white plastic card.

And on the card, under a photo of a woman with mad grey-brown hair and her glasses pushed up into it, were the words:

**P. LILLICO — CANINE GENOME GRP — MASTER ACCESS.**

She'd left her spare card on a hook. The most careful, most expensive system in the city, undone — again — by the most ordinary human thing of all: a tired person, at the end of a long day, who hangs her keys by the door so she won't lose them, and forgets that a door is only as locked as the person who hangs the key beside it.

Lola took the card off the hook. She held it against the little black box.

The red light went green.

And the door to the place where they bring dogs back into the world clicked, softly, open.

## Chapter Ten — One Touch

Inside, the lab was two rooms.

The first was the sunny dog room from the open day, except now it was dark, the dogs all asleep — I could hear them, the soft heavy breathing of sleeping dogs, a snuffle, a leg paddling at a dream — and one of them, a pale shape in a bed by the window, lifted its head and looked at us, and my whole body turned to ice, but the dog just looked, and yawned, this enormous wet yawn, and put its head back down, because it was a lab dog raised by kind people and it had decided we were fine. *Please,* I thought at it. *Please stay asleep. Please decide we're fine.* It did. They all did. I will love those dogs forever for not barking.

The second room was through a glass door, and it was the actual laboratory, and it was where everything happened, and I can still close my eyes and walk round it.

It was small and very clean and very cold, lit by the green and red standby lights of a dozen machines, all of them humming, that hum I'd heard from the stairwell, the sound of a place keeping things alive in the dark. There were benches and microscopes and a row of tall white units along one wall that I knew were freezers — minus eighty degrees, the labels said, the kind that keep cells asleep for years. There were tanks of liquid nitrogen with frost on the lids. There were screens, dark, waiting.

And on the main bench, under a big microscope with two joystick things on either side, there was a setup that was clearly in the middle of something. A tray. A row of tiny clear dishes under the lens. A neat handwritten label, in marker pen, that said: **ENUCLEATED — READY — TRANSFER A.M. DO NOT DISTURB.**

I didn't know what *enucleated* meant. I know now. It means the eggs in those dishes were donor eggs that had had their own DNA carefully sucked out, leaving them empty, leaving them waiting — little blank cells, hollowed out, ready to be given a new set of instructions and tricked into building a whole new animal. Dr Lillico had prepared them that very evening. She'd been planning to come in the next morning, the Sunday, and do a transfer — to put new DNA into those empty eggs and start them growing.

She'd left it all set up. Primed. One step from done.

"Bonnie," whispered Lola, by the door, keeping watch, her voice tight. "Quick. Leave the stuff and let's *go.* He'll be back."

That was the plan. The whole plan. Leave the second swab and the note somewhere a scientist would find them, and run. That's all. I want you to remember that, because everything that went wrong went wrong in the next ninety seconds, and none of it was the plan.

I took out the note and the foil packet with the second swab, and I looked for the right place to leave it. Somewhere obvious. Somewhere Dr Lillico would see it first thing. And the obvious place — the most obvious place in the whole room, lit up, set up, waiting, with a label saying READY — was the bench with the dishes and the microscope.

So I went to it. And as I reached out to lay the note down beside the tray, I saw the screen.

There was a small screen built into the machine beside the microscope — the unit the dishes sat in, a squat grey box with a lid and vents, humming faintly, with a touchscreen on the front glowing soft blue in the dark. And on the screen, in calm white letters, it said:

**PROTOCOL: AUTO-SCNT (the ARK)**
**STATUS: PAUSED — AWAITING DONOR SAMPLE**
**▸ INSERT SAMPLE AND PRESS TO BEGIN**

And underneath, a single button on the screen. Big. Friendly. The kind of button a machine has when its whole reason for existing is that anyone can use it.

I have thought about this moment more than any other moment of my life. I have lain awake and replayed it a hundred, a thousand times, and I want to be completely honest with you about what went through my head, because if I dress it up as an accident — *the machine just went off, it wasn't my fault* — then I'm doing the thing my whole family does, the stopping at the edge of the bad thing, and I promised at the start I'd tell you the truth.

Here is the truth. I read that screen — *insert sample, press to begin* — and a thought arrived, fully formed, in the furnace of my chest, and the thought was not *this is somebody else's experiment* and it was not *I should leave now.* The thought was:

*It's asking for a sample. I have a sample. I came all this way to give a building my dog. Here is the building, asking.*

I didn't think it would work. I want that on the record too. I am not so daft that I thought a twelve-year-old could clone a dog by pressing one button. I thought — if I thought anything — that I'd be loading my dog into the system, properly, into the *real* machine, the actual one, so that when Dr Lillico came in the next morning she wouldn't just find a note, she'd find Scratch already *in there,* already part of it, impossible to ignore, impossible to throw in the bin. I thought I was making my note louder. I thought I was making it so they *couldn't say no.*

I opened the foil packet. I took out the swab — the second one, the good thirty-second one, with Scratch's cheek cells on it. There was a small open port on the front of the unit, below the screen, exactly the size and shape of a swab, with a little arrow, because Dr Lillico had built this machine for tired technicians and she had built it to be *easy.*

I put the swab in the port.

And I pressed the button.

For a second, nothing. A small mechanical click, somewhere inside, like a throat being cleared.

Then the screen changed.

**SAMPLE DETECTED. DONOR DNA LOADING.**
**AUTO-SCNT INITIATED — DO NOT INTERRUPT.**

And the squat grey box came alive.

It didn't roar, like a film. That's the thing nobody tells you about the most important moments — they're quiet. It just ... worked. A soft whir. A series of tiny precise sounds, ticks and hums, the dishes under the microscope sliding fractionally on a moving stage, a faint blue light pulsing inside the unit where, I understood with a horror that started in my feet and went all the way up, where it was, right now, in the dark, automatically, doing the thing. Taking Scratch's DNA off the swab. Putting it into those empty waiting eggs. Getting ready to give them the tiny electric zap that would trick them into thinking they'd been made, into starting to grow, into becoming.

A progress bar appeared. It said it would take fourteen minutes.

"Bonnie." Lola's voice, from the door, very strange. "Bonnie, what did you do. What's it doing. *What did you do.*"

"I don't — I didn't — it said insert the sample, I thought —" I was backing away from the bench now, and my hand had gone to my mouth, and the calm white letters on the blue screen were counting down, *13:58, 13:57,* and I understood, far too late, in one cold rush, that I had not made my note louder.

I had started it. The real thing. By myself. By accident. By choosing, in one stupid hopeful second, to press a button I had absolutely no right to press.

And that's when the dog in the next room — the pale one, the one that had decided we were fine — changed its mind.

Because the machine had woken it, or our voices had, or it had finally smelled that we did not belong, and it stood up in its bed in the dark, and it pointed its nose at the ceiling, and it let out a single, huge, echoing bark — *BOOF* — the deep night-bark of a big dog who has decided the world needs to know something, and the other dogs woke, and joined in, four of them, five, the whole room exploding into barking, and through the chaos of it, faint and getting louder, from down the corridor, from the direction of the toilets, I heard the worst sound in the world:

old Sefton, shouting, "OY! WHO'S THERE? *OY!*" — and the thud of a big man beginning, as fast as a big man can, to run.

## Chapter Eleven — Run, and Don't Argue

*If anything goes wrong we run, we don't argue, we just go.*

We'd said it. We'd said it in the cold by the river not an hour before, and thank God we'd said it, because there was no time to decide anything. There was only the barking and Sefton shouting and the machine counting down behind me — *13:41* — and Lola already wrenching the lab door open, and the two of us bursting out through the dark dog room with five dogs going off like alarms on every side, and out into the corridor.

Sefton was at the far end, by the lobby, a big shape silhouetted against the blue light of his football, and he saw us — two kids, dark coats — and he shouted "*STOP!*" the way they do, the way that has never once in human history made anyone stop, and we ran the other way, towards the stairwell, towards the hundred and four steps, and I have never run like that, my school shoes slapping the floor, Lola ahead of me, the EXIT sign green and blessed at the end of the corridor, and behind us Sefton coming after us, shouting into a radio now — "I've got two intruders, fifth floor, they're heading for the west stairs" — and I thought, with a strange flat clarity, *that's it then, that's the police, we're caught, it's over,* and I kept running anyway, because what else do you do.

We hit the stairwell door and went down those hundred and four steps about six at a time, swinging round the railings, and Sefton — to his eternal credit and our eternal luck — was sixty years old with a flask of tea inside him and he was not coming down a hundred and four steps six at a time for anybody, so his footsteps and his shouting fell away above us, floor by floor, and we hit the bottom and the goods door and Lola hit the bar and we were out — out into Pottery Lane and the cold and the buzzing broken streetlight and the diesel-and-candyfloss smell of the funfair drifting all the way down from the front, all the way across the city, and we ran, separate, like we'd promised, Lola peeling left and me right, and we did not stop and we did not look back and we did not argue.

I ran until I couldn't, until I had a stitch like a knife and my lungs were full of broken glass, and I ended up somewhere down by the river under a bridge, bent double, and I made myself walk, just walk, hood up, ordinary, a girl walking, and I went the long way round to the Metro, the way we'd planned for if it went wrong, in case they were watching the obvious way, and I tapped my pass at the Haymarket — *and yes, I'll come back to the pass, it matters, but I didn't think about it then* — and I got on the second-to-last Metro of the night, and I sat in the bright empty carriage shaking like Scratch in a thunderstorm, and somewhere in the city behind me a squat grey machine was counting down, *11:20, 11:19,* doing the impossible thing in the dark, all on its own, because I had pressed a button.

Lola was on the same train. We'd planned that too — different carriages, meet at our stop. When the doors opened at Kelsey Bay we found each other on the platform and we didn't say anything, we just walked, fast, up Sea Road, past the dark shut shops, towards the front where the funfair was still going, the big wheel turning slow against the black sky, the music thudding, and just as we reached the top of the bank the fireworks started — the grand finale, the last night — the whole sky over the sea going up in green and gold and red and white, *boom, boom, boom-boom-boom,* the bangs rolling across the water and bouncing off the houses, and everyone on the front cheering, hundreds of people, faces turned up and lit up, having the loveliest ordinary night of their lives.

And I stood there with my heart going like the fireworks and one single thought cut clean through everything else, through the panic and the guilt and the disbelief, one thought that was worse than Sefton, worse than the police, worse than the button:

*Scratch.*

The fireworks. The bangs. And my dog, alone, behind the settee, shaking, with no one beside him, on the one night I'd promised — *I'll always be there* — and broken it.

I left Lola at her door. We still hadn't said a proper word; there were no words yet for what we'd done. She just gripped my arm, hard, for a second, her face white and strange in the firework light, and then she went in, and I ran the last four doors to number 9 and let myself in the back, and the radio was still going, classical, turned up loud, and the front room was full of the flicker of the fireworks coming through the curtains, and I dropped down onto my knees by the settee and I looked into the dark gap behind it.

And there he was.

Pressed into the corner, as small as a big dog can make himself, trembling, his eyes huge and white-rimmed, and when he saw me he didn't even get up, he just — he made this sound, this high thin sound I'd never heard him make, half a whine and half a question, and I crawled in there with him, into the dust and the dark behind the settee, in my coat, with the smell of the city and the lab still on me, and I put my whole self around my dog and my mouth against his torn ear and I said, "I'm here, I'm here, I've got you, I'm so sorry, I'm here," over and over, while the sky banged and the radio played and my dog shook against me and slowly, slowly, stopped.

We stayed there a long time. Long after the fireworks finished. Long after the funfair music stopped and the town went quiet and the only sound was the sea and Scratch's breathing going slow and even at last, and his heartbeat — that great tired enlarged heart, working too hard, ticking down its own three weeks — under my hand.

And I lay behind the settee with him in the dark and I thought about what I'd done.

I'd gone to save him. And instead I'd left him to be frightened alone, and I'd committed an actual crime, and I'd been seen, and I'd written my name on a note, and I'd tapped my own pass at the station, and somewhere across the water a machine I had no right to touch was building something out of my dog that I did not understand and could not stop and could not take back.

*What have I done,* I thought. The exact words from the start of this book. *What on earth have I done.*

I didn't know yet that the answer was going to walk up our garden path eleven weeks later on four wobbly legs.

I just knew that I'd broken a promise to the one creature on earth who would never, ever break one to me. And I lay there in the dark and I held him, and for the first time since the green towel, I let myself properly cry, the ugly kind, the kind with no dignity in it at all, with my face in my dog's neck, and Scratch — who was the one who'd been frightened, who was the one who was dying, who had every right to be the one comforted — Scratch turned his long grey head, and he licked the tears off my face, once, twice, patient, gentle, the way he had in the back lane when I was five years old.

Forgiving me.

It's the whole job, the forgiving. He never once stopped doing it. Not even at the very end.

*Especially* not at the very end.

## Chapter Twelve — Pretending to Be Normal Is a Full-Time Job

For four days I waited to be arrested.

I want to tell you what that's like, in case you ever find yourself doing it, although I sincerely hope you don't. It's like having a second person living inside your chest who never sleeps and never shuts up. You can be doing the most ordinary thing — eating Weetabix, sitting in maths, walking the dog slowly to the end of the lane and back because that's as far as he can manage now — and the whole time the second person is going: *they'll have the CCTV by now. They'll have the note. Did you touch anything without gloves? You touched everything without gloves. You tapped your pass. Your pass has your name on it. Donna Maguire's daughter, on camera, on the platform, at half past eleven at night.*

Pretending to be normal, it turns out, is a full-time job. You have to remember to do all the things your face does when it isn't hiding anything, and you don't know what those things are, because you've never had to think about them before, and the more you think about them the weirder your face gets, until you're sitting at the tea table arranging your own eyebrows like a person who has never owned a face.

"You're quiet," said Mum on the Sunday, the day after, ladling out mince. "You sickening for something?"

"Tired," I said. "Stayed up late at Lola's."

Which was almost the truest lie I've ever told.

Mick, bless him, saved me, by being Mick. "She's growing," he announced, to the room, with total confidence, as if growing were a disease he'd had himself. "Our Kyle went dead quiet for about a year and then shot up like a runner bean. Eat your mince, Bonneh, you need it for the growing." And Mum laughed her big laugh, and the moment passed, and I loved Mick, very slightly and very secretly, for the first time, for being too daft to notice I was a criminal.

School was worse, because school is where they teach you about consequences.

On the Monday we had assembly, and Mr Frostick, the head, who is approximately ninety and has a voice like a foghorn with regrets, stood up and said he wanted to talk to us about *making good choices,* and I swear on my life he looked directly at me for the entire eleven minutes, even though there are nine hundred of us and the chances of him knowing my name are roughly the same as the chances of him knowing what a TikTok is. I sat in row G between Lola and a boy called Bashir who eats glue, and I did not breathe. By the time he got to the bit about how "a moment's foolishness can follow you for years," I had basically left my own body and was watching myself from somewhere up near the basketball hoops, thinking, *this is it, this is where two police officers come in and put a hand on my shoulder in front of everyone, including Chloe Aldous, who will tell the whole school.*

Two police officers did not come in. Mr Frostick was, it turned out, talking about chewing gum under the desks. But I had learned something about myself, which is that a guilty conscience will take any ordinary sentence in the English language and turn it into an accusation. For about two weeks, *everything* anyone said was about me. "Has anyone seen my charger?" — they know. "Who left the science lab unlocked?" — *they KNOW.* Miss Okonkwo, who'd organised the open day and was therefore, in my fevered brain, basically Interpol, asked me in the corridor how I'd "found the trip," and I said "I didn't take anything," which is not an answer to that question, and is in fact the single most suspicious sentence a human being can produce, and she just looked at me, puzzled, and said, "...good?" and I went and hid in the library until my heart stopped trying to climb out through my ears.

"You have got to *calm down,*" Lola hissed at me at break, sharing a Twix in the bit behind the science block where the bins are. "You look like a meerkat. You're doing the thing where you go all stiff and your eyes go everywhere. Innocent people don't do that. *I'm* not doing that."

"You broke in too!"

"Yeah, but I'm not the one who left a *signed confession* leaning on a microscope, am I," said Lola, which was fair, and unkind, and exactly what I needed, because it made me laugh, and laughing is the only known cure for the meerkat face. "Listen. If they had us, they'd have us by now. You don't wait four days to nick a kid. The longer it's quiet, the better it is. Eat your half of the Twix and stop looking like you're about to be sentenced. You're doing my head in and you're going to get us *both* caught looking guilty about a thing nobody knows happened."

She was right, of course. She's always right; it's her worst quality. But it wasn't the police I should have been worrying about, and it wasn't a teacher, and it wasn't Chloe Aldous. It was the quiet itself. Because Lola was half right and half completely wrong: the longer it stayed quiet, the *worse* it was, not the better, only neither of us understood that yet.

The thing I couldn't understand was the silence.

Because nothing happened. No police. No phone call. No teacher pulling me out of registration with a strange look. I checked the local news on my phone about forty times a day — the *Evening Chronicle*, the Kelsey Bay community Facebook group, everything — and there was not one single word about a break-in at the university. Not one. A whole night of barking dogs and a shouting security guard and two kids running through the building, and the world said absolutely nothing about it.

"That's weird, right?" I said to Lola, on the Monday, on the top deck of the 19, both of us hollow-eyed. "That's not normal. If you break into a uni, it's in the paper. It's a *thing.*"

Lola had been thinking about it too. Lola is always thinking about it, whatever *it* is. "Unless," she said slowly, watching the sea go by, "somebody doesn't want it in the paper."

"What do you mean?"

"Universities hate bad publicity. My mum says it's all they care about, the league tables and the reputation and the donors. A break-in's embarrassing. Maybe they're keeping it quiet." She frowned. "Or — "

"Or what?"

"Or somebody's keeping it quiet for their *own* reasons," said Lola. "Somebody who maybe shouldn't have left a machine switched on overnight with everything ready to go, set up so any random idiot could press one button and run it. Somebody who, if there was a big investigation, might have some awkward questions to answer about what exactly that machine was for."

I looked at her.

"I've been reading about her," said Lola, and she got out her phone, and she showed me, and there she was: Dr Petra Lillico, on the university website, younger in the photo, with a list of letters after her name as long as your arm. *Reader in Reproductive Genetics. Principal Investigator, Canine Genome & Development Group.* And below it, a line that made the back of my neck go cold: *The CGDG's work is supported by an industrial partnership with Lazarus Biosciences Ltd.*

"Lazarus," I said. "Like in the Bible. The bloke who came back from the dead."

"Yeah," said Lola, and she didn't smile. "I clocked that and all."

We didn't know, then, who Lazarus Biosciences were. We didn't know about Mr Sole, or the launch, or the men in the good suits, or how close all of it was going to come to taking everything away from me. We just knew that the silence was wrong, that the silence was hiding something, and that somewhere out there a woman with a hole in the elbow of her jumper was sitting on a secret as big as ours.

What we didn't know was that she already had our note. That she'd had it since seven o'clock on the Sunday morning, when she'd come in to do her transfer and found her machine had already run, and a packet she didn't recognise in the port, and a piece of paper, in a child's handwriting, leaning against her microscope.

And that she'd been doing exactly what we'd been doing. Sitting very still. Saying nothing. Pretending to be normal. And working out what on earth to do about a twelve-year-old called Bonnie.

She found me on the Thursday.

## Chapter Thirteen — The Woman by the Car

She was waiting outside school.

Not at the main gate, where the mums and the minibuses are, but round the side, on Mortimer Road, by the bit of railing where the Year 11s vape and think nobody can see them, leaning against a small dark-green car that had a dent in the door and a sticker in the back window that said A DOG IS FOR LIFE. She had her hair in the same mad nest and a big coat on and her hands in her pockets, and she was looking right at me, and I knew, the second I saw her, exactly who she was and exactly why she was there, and my legs went to water.

Lola, beside me, went very still. "Is that—"

"Yeah," I said.

"Do we run?"

And I thought about it. I genuinely thought about it. And then I thought about the machine, and the dishes, and the words on the screen — *donor DNA loading* — and I realised that running was over. Running had been Saturday night. This was the morning after running. This was the part where you find out what you've done.

"No," I said. "She's not got the police with her. If she had the police, she'd have brought the police. She came on her own." I took a breath. "I need to know what happened to Scratch. To the — to the sample. She's the only person who knows."

So I walked up to Dr Petra Lillico on Mortimer Road, with Lola two steps behind me because Lola does not let you face things alone, and I stopped in front of her, and I didn't know what to say, so I said the truest thing.

"I'm really sorry," I said. "It was me. Lola didn't press it, that was me, it was all me, she just kept watch. Whatever happens, it was me."

Dr Lillico looked at me for a long moment. Up close she had lines round her eyes from a face that had done a lot of squinting down microscopes and possibly a lot of being tired, and her eyes were a pale tea-coloured brown, and they were not angry, exactly. They were something more complicated than angry. They were *studying* me, the way she'd studied me on the open day when I said *identical twin, born later.*

"You're the one who knew what a clone was," she said. "Bonnie. From the school that came on the eleventh. I remembered the name when I read the note. There was a Bonnie in the first litter we ever made, did I tell you that, on the day? Funny." She shook her head, like clearing it. "I've spent four days deciding whether to ring the police, your school, or your mother. Do you want to know why I haven't done any of them?"

I nodded. I didn't trust my voice.

"Sit in the car," said Dr Lillico. "Both of you. I'm not — God, I'm not abducting you, ring your mother and tell her where you are if you like, you should anyway, you should always — just. I can't have this conversation on a street where your teachers can see me, because this conversation is going to get me sacked, and possibly arrested, and I would quite like to choose the order those things happen in. Get in the car. There's a café on the front at your end. I'll buy you a hot chocolate and tell you what you've done, and then you can tell me why, although I think I already know, because I've read your note about four hundred times." Something moved in her face. "It's a very good note, Bonnie. It's the best argument for doing something stupid I've read in twenty years of being asked to do stupid things."

We rang Mum's number and got her voicemail and left a message saying we were getting a hot chocolate with "a friend's mum," which was the fifth lie of the week and felt like nothing now, and we got in the dented green car, and Dr Lillico drove us along the front to a café called the Beach Hut where she bought three hot chocolates with cream and a flake, and she sat down opposite us with her hands round her mug, and she said:

"Right. The bad news, the worse news, and the impossible news. Which do you want first?"

"Bad," I said.

"The bad news is that what you did is a crime, several crimes, and that you got extraordinarily lucky, because the only camera that was working on the fifth floor that night was a dummy, and because Bill Sefton is sixty-three and saw two kids in coats and could not pick you out of a line-up if his pension depended on it, and because I removed your note and your sample from the lab before anyone official saw them. The university thinks it was a couple of students after laptops. It's been 'dealt with internally.' Nobody is looking for you." She fixed me with the tea-coloured eyes. "Nobody except me. And that's only because you signed your work, you daft girl. Never sign your work."

"What's the worse news," I said. My heart was going.

Dr Lillico turned her mug round on the table, once, twice. "The worse news," she said, "is that I should have wiped that machine and thrown your sample in the incinerator on Sunday morning, and reported the break-in properly, and protected myself. That's what any sensible person would have done. It's what the people I work for would tell me to do — and they would be right, by every rule I'm supposed to follow." She looked out at the grey sea for a second. "I didn't. And now I'm sitting in a café lying to my own employer, with two children, drinking a hot chocolate, and I'm in this with you, whether either of us likes it. So that's the worse news. You've got me. God help you."

There was a silence. A gull screamed outside. Lola, beside me, had not touched her hot chocolate, which for Lola is basically a coma.

"And the impossible news?" I whispered.

Dr Lillico leaned forward. And for the first time, under the tiredness and the worry and the anger, I saw the other thing in her, the thing I'd seen for a flash on the open day — a kind of wild, terrified, helpless delight, the look of someone who has spent their whole life chasing something and has just, against every rule, caught a glimpse of it.

"The impossible news, Bonnie," she said quietly, "is that it worked. The transfer ran. The activation took. As of this morning there are six early embryos in an incubator in my lab, dividing, growing, perfectly healthy — and every single cell of every one of them is, genetically, your dog. Scratch. The machine did exactly what it was built to do, just nine hours earlier than I planned and with rather more drama. You didn't fail, you daft, brilliant, criminal child." She sat back. "You succeeded. That's the impossible news. That's the worst news of all. Because now the two of us have to decide what on earth we're going to do about six tiny copies of a dog who isn't even dead yet."

And outside the window of the Beach Hut café, the sea went on heaving against the wall of Kelsey Bay, grey and patient and old, and I sat there with my hot chocolate going cold and cream sliding down the side of the mug, and I understood that I had not stopped anything on Saturday night.

I'd started it.

It was real. It was growing. It was him.

And he hadn't even died yet.

## Chapter Fourteen — Six Tiny Copies and One Big Lie

Dr Lillico explained the science to us over a second round of hot chocolate, and she did it properly, the way she'd done on the open day, like we were people and not children, and I'm going to give it to you the way she gave it to us, because she made me promise — much later — that if I ever told this story I'd get the science right, "because the science is the only part that's actually true, Bonnie, and people will believe the lies and laugh at the truth, they always do."

So. Here is how you clone a dog. Here is the true bit.

Every cell in Scratch's body — every cell in *your* body — contains a complete set of instructions for building the whole animal. The whole dog, folded up in a single cell from inside his cheek. That's DNA. That part's just true, it's not even clever, it's the most ordinary miracle there is.

To make a clone, you need two things: a cell with the DNA you want — that was my swab — and an empty egg cell to put it in. You take an egg cell from some other dog, and you carefully suck out its own DNA, leaving it hollow. That's what "enucleated" meant on the label. An empty egg. A blank.

Then you put the new DNA inside the empty egg — that's the "nuclear transfer" bit — and you give it a tiny pulse of electricity, a zap, which tricks the egg into thinking it's just been fertilised, into waking up and starting to divide. One cell into two, two into four, four into eight. An embryo. Growing.

"And normally," said Dr Lillico, "that's the part that goes wrong. Over and over. It's fiddly and brutal and it fails ninety-nine times out of a hundred. It took the team in Scotland two hundred and seventy-seven goes to get Dolly. *Two hundred and seventy-seven.* That's why it costs forty thousand pounds — you're paying for all the failures." She looked at her mug. "So I built a machine to do the fiddly bit. To do it the same way every time, perfectly, automatically, no shaking hands, no human error, hundreds of times a night while everyone's asleep. That's what you switched on, Bonnie. That's what made it work first time when it should've failed. You didn't get lucky with a normal machine. You got lucky with mine."

"Is that why the company funds you?" said Lola, who misses nothing. "Lazarus. Because if the fiddly bit's cheap and automatic, then cloning's cheap, and then you can sell it to anyone?"

Dr Lillico looked at Lola for a long moment, and something tired and unhappy crossed her face.

"You're a sharp one," she said. "Yes. That's exactly why. They didn't fund me to bring back one little girl's greyhound. They funded me to build a machine that turns grief into a product. Forty thousand pounds, and a waiting list, and 'bring them back.'" She said the words like they tasted bad. "And I let them, because they paid for the equipment, and the equipment let me do the work I actually care about." She stopped, like she'd said too much, and didn't finish that sentence. I'd find out what she actually cared about soon enough. It turned out to be the most important thing of all.

"So there's six of them," I said. "Six embryos. That are him."

"Six early embryos that are genetically identical to Scratch, yes. Dividing nicely. But Bonnie." She put her mug down and she looked at me, and her voice changed, got gentler and harder at the same time. "I need you to understand something, and I need you to really hear it, because it's the cruellest part and it's the part people refuse to believe even when you tell them straight. An embryo in a dish is not a puppy. To become a puppy it has to grow inside a living mother dog — a surrogate — for two months. I have a dog who can do that; she's an old breeding girl called Maggie, retired, soft as butter, she's carried litters before and she'd love nothing more. That part I can do. If we do nothing, the embryos die in the dish in a few days and that's the end of it. If we go ahead — if I put one or two into Maggie — then in about nine or ten weeks, if everything works, which it very often does not, there would be a puppy."

"A puppy that's Scratch," I said. My heart was up in my throat.

"No," said Dr Lillico.

She said it so flatly, so finally, that the café seemed to go quiet around it.

"That's the part I need you to hear, Bonnie, before you decide anything, because if you go into this thinking you're getting your dog back, it will break your heart worse than losing him, and I will not be part of breaking a child's heart, I've done enough damage in my life. A clone is a twin. Same body, grown again. Same colour, near enough — actually not even exactly the same, coat patterns drift, you'd be surprised — but the same animal, physically. And *nothing else.*" She leaned in. "It will not know you. It will not remember the back lane, or the beach, or your dad, or thirteen years of sleeping by your bed. It will be born brand new, a blank, knowing nothing, the way every puppy is born knowing nothing. It will not be Scratch. It will be a stranger who happens to look like him. The body comes back. The *dog* doesn't. There's a thing people say — *you can't clone a soul.* It's the one bit of cloning that everybody gets right." She sat back. "Do you understand? Truly?"

And I sat there in the Beach Hut café with the cold sea outside, and I thought about it, properly, the way she was asking me to, and I found that I did understand, and that I didn't care, and I'll tell you exactly why, because it's the realest thing about me.

"My dad gave me Scratch," I said. "On the last week before he left. And I don't know why he left, and I'll probably never know, and there's no part of him left in the whole world except that dog. So even if it's a stranger that looks like him — even if it knows nothing — it's still made of him. It's still the last bit of the last thing my dad ever gave me. And I would rather have a blank dog that's made of Scratch than nothing at all. I'd take the body. I'd take any of it." I wiped my eyes, angry at them. "I'm not stupid. I know it won't be him. I just can't — I can't let *all* of him go. Not all at once. Not the dog and the dad and everything. There has to be one thing I get to keep."

There was a silence.

And Dr Petra Lillico looked at me across the table, and her tea-coloured eyes had gone bright and wet, and she did a strange thing. She reached across and put her hand over mine, just for a second, the way you would with a grown-up, an equal, and she said, very quietly:

"All right. Then we'll keep him. The body, anyway. We'll keep what we can." A pause. "I had a dog like that once. Made of someone, I mean. I'll tell you about it one day." And then, brisk again, blinking hard, gathering up the mugs: "But you have to swear to me, both of you, on whatever you love most — and I think I know what that is — that you will tell *no one.* Not your mum. Not your grandad. Not a single soul. Because what we're about to do is against the rules of my work, against the law I think, and absolutely, definitely against the wishes of the people who pay for everything in that building. If Lazarus found out I'd used their machine to clone a pet greyhound for nothing, for a child, for *love* — they wouldn't just sack me. They'd shut down everything I do. And there are reasons — " she stopped, again, that same stopping — "there are reasons I cannot let them shut down everything I do. Not yet. So. Can you keep a secret the size of a dog?"

"Yes," I said.

"Yes," said Lola.

"Then God help all three of us," said Dr Lillico, and she went to pay, and that was the day I stopped being a girl whose dog was dying and became a girl with a secret growing in a lab — and a lie I now had to tell my own mum, every single day, for eleven weeks, while my real dog ran out of time in front of us all.

The lie was the worst part. Worse than the crime. I want you to know that. I've never been so careful with anyone as I was with Mum in those weeks, and every careful word was a brick in a wall I was building between us without meaning to, and it would take the whole rest of this story to knock that wall down.

But first, before any of it, I had to lose him.

The real one. My Scratch. And there was no machine and no science and no secret in the world that could stop that. It came nine days later.

## Chapter Fifteen — The Last Good Day

I didn't know it was the last good day while it was happening. You never do. That's the cruelty and the mercy of it both — that the last good day just looks like a good day, and you spend it ordinarily, and only afterwards do you go back and pick it up and turn it over and over and realise it was the last one, and wish you'd held it differently.

It was a Sunday, the last proper warm Sunday I can remember from that spring. Scratch had been getting slower and slower — the heart tablets bought us comfort but not time, exactly as Mr Pringle had said — and there were days now when he didn't want to get up at all, when he'd lie in his patch of sun and just watch us move around him with his eyebrows, those greyhound eyebrows that move independently like he's conducting a tiny orchestra of worries. But that Sunday he woke up bright. He came and stood by my bed and did the thing he used to do when he was younger, the *pointed-nose-under-the-duvet* thing, the cold wet announcement that the day had started and I was in it whether I liked it or not.

So we had a good day. We gave him a good day. I think, looking back, some part of all of us knew.

Mum made him scrambled eggs, which he was not allowed and which she made anyway, and he ate them off a proper plate on the kitchen floor like a king. Mick drove us — me and Mum and Scratch — out to the long quiet beach past the lighthouse where dogs are allowed off the lead, and Scratch, who hadn't run in months, who I thought would never run again, put his nose up into the wind off the sea and got a look in his eye, the old look, the track look, and he *went.*

Not far. Not fast, not really, not like the grey streak he'd been. But for about nine seconds, on the hard wet sand with the tide a long way out, my old dying dog stretched out his legs and ran, full out, ears flat, body folding and unfolding like the most beautiful machine that was ever built, the thing greyhounds are *for,* and Mum grabbed my hand and we both watched him and we were both crying and both laughing, and Mick stood a little way off with his hands in his pockets pretending to look at the sea so we could have it, and Scratch ran a great wide curve across the empty beach and came back to us with his tongue out and his eyes blazing and pure mad joy coming off him in waves, and he leaned his whole weight against my legs, the lean, the *I'm-here-and-you're-here* lean, and I knelt down in the wet sand in my jeans and put my arms round him and his heart was going like a drum, too hard, far too hard, and I didn't care and neither did he.

It's my favourite memory in the entire world. It's the one I'd save if I could only save one. My dog, running, because he wanted to, one last time, on an empty beach, with everyone who loved him watching.

We went up to Grandad's allotment after, and Grandad had — and I didn't think anything of it at the time, but I think of it constantly now — Grandad had got a bit of frying steak in, "for the dog, like, seeing as," and he cooked it on the little gas ring in the shed, and Scratch lay on his special bed by the heater and ate steak and Grandad sat with his big carrot hand on the dog's side, the way he does, feeling him breathe, and he didn't say anything, and Mum and I sat on upturned buckets in the spring sunshine outside among the leeks, and nobody said the thing, the *this might be the last time* thing, because in my family you don't say the thing.

But on the way out, while Mum and Mick were at the car, Grandad caught my arm. And he held it for a second, and he looked at me with his big sad square face, and he said something I didn't understand then and have never forgotten.

"He's been a good friend to you, that dog," Grandad said. "Better than some. He stayed." And his voice went strange on *he stayed,* and his eyes went somewhere far off, down the years, and I thought he just meant the dog, I thought he just meant Scratch.

He didn't just mean Scratch. He meant my dad. *Better than some. He stayed.* He was telling me something, my grandad, in the only way he knew how, which was sideways, about a dog — he was telling me that he knew, that he'd always known, that the thing that broke our family was a man who didn't stay, and that he was sorry, and that he carried it. I just couldn't hear it yet. I didn't have the key.

The dog had the key. The dog always had the key. I just didn't know it, that last good Sunday, with the steak and the leeks and the sea still in our hair.

We went home. Scratch slept in his patch of evening sun with his legs sticking straight out, worn through, happy. And I lay on the floor beside him, the way I always did, with my hand on his side, feeling the too-hard heart, and I told him about the day even though he'd been there for all of it, because that's what you do with a dog, you tell them the story of their own life, and they listen like it's the best story ever told, because to them it is.

"You ran today," I told him. "You showed them. You showed them all."

He thumped his tail, once, on the carpet.

It was the last time he ever did.

## Chapter Sixteen — Three Weeks Was Always Optimistic

It was the Wednesday. Day nineteen, by my counting, which I never stopped doing, not for one morning.

I came down for breakfast and he didn't get up.

That was all. That was how it started. He was in his bed and he lifted his head when I came in but he didn't get up, and when I knelt down beside him I could feel it before I could see it — something had changed in the night, some tide had gone out — his gums were pale, nearly white, and his breathing had a catch in it, a little hitch at the top, and when he tried to stand to come to me his back legs wouldn't hold and he sat down again, hard, surprised, and looked at me, and I will never as long as I live forget the look on his face, because it wasn't pain, it was *apology.* He was sorry. He was sorry he couldn't get up. He thought he'd let me down.

"Mum," I called, and my voice came out wrong, and she was down the stairs in about four seconds in her dressing gown, and she took one look and she didn't say *he'll be all right.* She didn't lie to me. She is Donna Maguire and she does not lie to me about the things that matter, only about how tired she is and how much is in the account. She knelt down on the other side of him and she felt his gums and his chest and she went very still, and then she looked at me across our dog, and her eyes were full, and she said, "I think it's time, my love. I think he's telling us it's time."

I'm not going to write all of it. Some things are mine. But I'll write the parts you need.

Mr Pringle came to the house, which is a thing they do, the good vets, for the old animals at the end — they come to you, so that the last thing isn't the shiny floor and the disinfectant and the fear, so that it's home, so that it's the patch of sun and the smell of your own people. He came at eleven o'clock with a nurse — and the nurse was Bisi, Lola's mum, calm as still water, and she squeezed my shoulder and didn't say anything daft — and they were so gentle, both of them, so unhurried, and Mr Pringle knelt down on our front room carpet in his good trousers and he explained to me, to *me,* not over my head to Mum but to me, exactly what would happen, and that it wouldn't hurt, that it would be like falling asleep but kinder, that Scratch would just get very heavy and very peaceful and then he'd be gone, and that I could hold him the whole time.

So I held him the whole time.

I got down on the floor and I gathered up as much of my big long dog as I could into my lap, his head, his lovely grey head with the torn ear, and I put my face down next to his face the way I had in the vet's three weeks before, so close his whisker tickled my cheek, and I told him the things. I told him he was the best dog. I told him he was the bravest, fastest, kindest dog, that he ran like the wind on the beach, that he was the best friend I ever had, that I loved him, that it was all right, that he could go, that I'd be all right, which was a lie, the last and kindest lie, the only one I've never once regretted. Mum had her hand on him. Grandad had come — Mick had gone and fetched him — and Grandad knelt there too with his carrot hand on the dog's side, feeling him breathe, the way he always did, right to the end.

And Mr Pringle did the thing, very quietly, and Scratch sighed — one long sigh, the enormous sigh of a dog who has reached his favourite place in the entire world — and got heavy in my arms, and his too-hard heart, that great worn-out heart that had loved me for seven years and run on a beach for nine seconds three days before, went quiet.

And that was it. That was the end of Scratch.

Mr Pringle listened with his stethoscope and nodded, gently, and said, "He's gone, pet. He went easy. He went with you holding him. You couldn't have given him a better end than that," and Bisi was crying, the vet nurse who'd seen it a thousand times was crying, and Mum was crying, and Grandad — my grandad, my four-time Leek Show champion grandad who never shows a thing — Grandad put his face in his hands and made a sound I'd never heard a grown man make, and I understood, even through everything, even then, that he wasn't only crying for the dog.

I didn't cry. Isn't that strange? At the actual moment, I didn't. I was too far inside it. I just held my dog and stroked his torn ear and felt the warmth start, very slowly, to leave him, and felt something in the middle of me close like a door.

And then — and I'm not proud of this, and I've gone round and round on whether to even tell you, but I promised the truth — then, with my dead dog warm and heavy in my arms and my family broken open around me, a part of my brain, a cold clear part, the furnace part, woke up and said one word.

It said: *Now.*

Because Dr Lillico had told me. *RNA is fragile, Bonnie. If there's any chance — any chance at all — of keeping more than the shape, I have to have the samples within an hour or two of him passing, fresh, while everything's still — while it can still be read. Otherwise it's gone, the same way memory's gone, it just dissolves. So if it happens when I'm not there, you ring me, and you don't wait, no matter how much you want to just sit with him. You ring me. You'll have one chance.*

She'd never told me what the samples were for. Not really. She'd said *to keep more than the shape* and she'd looked away when she said it, and I'd been too frightened and too sad to push. I didn't know, kneeling on that carpet, that I was about to make a decision that would change everything — that would mean the puppy who came eleven weeks later was not a blank stranger after all.

I just knew that she'd said *you'll have one chance,* and that the door in the middle of me had closed, and that I am, when it comes down to it, a Maguire — the kind who spends what she hasn't got to save something that can't save itself.

I laid my dog's head down, very gently, on his bed.

And I went into the kitchen, on the worst morning of my life, and I closed the door, and I took out my phone, and I rang Dr Lillico, and when she answered I said the three words that started the second impossible thing, the bigger one, the one I still don't fully understand to this day.

"He's gone," I said. "Come now."

## Chapter Seventeen — What I Let Her Do, and Where We Put Him

She came in twenty-five minutes. She must have driven like a maniac. I've never asked.

The timing worked out the way bad timing sometimes mercifully does. Mr Pringle and Bisi had gone. Mum had gone up to lie down, because she'd been up since six and she was wrung out like a cloth, and because Mick, who is useless at almost everything and quietly brilliant at the one thing that counts, had said, "Howay, Donna, come up and lie down ten minutes, I'll see to things," and steered her gently up the stairs. And Grandad — Grandad had said, in a thick voice, not looking at anyone, "I'll away up the lottie and start a hole. By the shed. He liked it by the shed." And Mick had driven him up to the allotment to dig, because that's what men like my grandad and Mick do with grief, they go and dig a hole, they make their hands do the thing their hearts can't.

So I was alone in the house with my dead dog when Dr Lillico's dented green car pulled up in the back lane.

I let her in the back door. She had a cold box with her, a proper one, the kind with dry ice that smokes when you open it, and she had her work face on, but when she came into the front room and saw him — saw Scratch, in his bed, in the patch of sun, looking for all the world like he was just asleep, like he'd get up in a minute and stick his cold nose under the duvet — she stopped. And the work face came off. And she stood there for a moment looking down at a dog she'd never met, the dog she'd already, secretly, copied six times over, and she said, very quietly, "Oh. Oh, he's a beautiful old fella, isn't he," and her voice cracked right down the middle, and I realised that whatever she was about to do, she didn't take it lightly. That mattered to me. It still does.

"Bonnie," she said gently. "Go and put the kettle on. Make us a tea. You don't need to be in here for this, and you don't want to be, and he isn't in here anymore anyway — whatever made him *him,* it's already gone, it went when his heart stopped, I promise you that's true. This is just — it's just keeping a few pages of a book before the book closes. It won't hurt him. Nothing can hurt him now. Go on. Kettle."

So I went and put the kettle on, and I stood in the kitchen with my back to the door and my hands flat on the cold worktop, and I listened to the kettle roar, and I did not watch, and I will not tell you the details because I don't have them, because I chose not to have them, and that was Dr Lillico's last kindness of a day full of them.

I'll tell you the truth about how I felt, though, because that's the deal.

I felt like a traitor. I felt like the lowest, coldest, most unnatural girl in the world — that on the very morning my dog died in my arms, I had let a stranger into the house to take pieces of him for an experiment, while my mum lay grieving upstairs not knowing, while my grandad dug a grave up the bank not knowing. I felt that I had done something you're not supposed to do, crossed a line that's there for a reason, treated my best friend's body like a *resource.* That feeling has never entirely gone away. I don't think it should. Some lines, even when you have a good reason to cross them, you're supposed to feel for the rest of your life.

But underneath the traitor feeling, down in the furnace, there was the other thing, and I won't pretend there wasn't, because pretending is the family disease and I'm trying to be the one who stops.

Underneath, there was hope. Mad, desperate, shameful hope. *Keeping a few pages of a book before the book closes.* She'd said it to comfort me but it had gone into me like a hook, because — pages of a book. *Pages.* Not just the shape of him. Not just the body. Was it possible — was there the smallest chance — that some page of him, some line, some single sentence of the thirteen-year story that was Scratch, might be kept? Might be read again?

I didn't know. I didn't dare ask. I made the tea.

When I came back in, the cold box was shut and Scratch was just Scratch again, tucked in his bed, and you couldn't see that anything had been done at all, and Dr Lillico had a smudge on her cheek and red eyes and she took the mug of tea from me with both hands like it was the most precious thing she'd ever been given, and she said, "Thank you, Bonnie. For the tea. And for — for trusting me with this. I'll take care of it. Whatever happens, I'll take care of all of it." And then she said the thing that I held on to for the next nine weeks like a person in the sea holds on to a bit of wood: "Don't hope. Promise me you won't hope. It's never worked. It almost certainly won't work. But — " and she paused at the back door with the smoking cold box in her arms, and she looked back at me, and there was that wild helpless terrified delight again, buried right at the bottom of all the sadness — "but I have never, in twenty years, had a sample this good, this fresh, from a dog whose clone was already growing. So. Don't hope. And I'll see you in nine weeks."

And she went.

---

We buried Scratch that evening, up at the allotment, by the shed, where Grandad and Mick had dug a deep neat hole in the soft spring ground between the leek bed and the old apple tree.

It was a beautiful evening, which felt like a cheek. The light went gold and long over the allotments and the sea was a flat silver line beyond the rooftops and the blackbirds were singing their hearts out the way they do at the end of a warm day, like they're being paid by the note. We wrapped Scratch in his favourite blanket, the tartan one, and Mick — gentle, careful Mick — carried him, because none of the rest of us could, and we laid him down in the hole on his blanket with his manky flat tennis ball, Mr Ball, tucked in beside him, because you can't send a dog anywhere without his ball, and his collar, and a bit of the frying steak Grandad had left over and saved, "for the journey, like."

And then we filled it in. We all did a bit. Even Mum, in her work fleece, crying, shovelling soil onto our dog in the gold evening light. And Grandad got a paving slab he'd been saving and he set it over the place so the cats and the foxes couldn't get at it, and we all stood round the slab not knowing what to do with our hands or our faces, and nobody said anything, because in my family we don't say the thing —

— until Grandad, my grandad, cleared his throat, and looked down at the slab, at the new grave of a dog his lost son had carried home on a bit of rope thirteen years before, and said, in a voice like gravel, the only eulogy Scratch ever got, and the best one anybody could have given him:

"He stayed."

Two words. *He stayed.* And Mum let out a sound and put her face in Mick's chest, and I understood, dimly, far off, that those two words were the most enormous thing my grandad had ever said in front of me, that they were about so much more than the dog, that there was a whole drowned country of grief and guilt and a missing man underneath those two small words.

But I couldn't get to it. Not yet. I didn't have the key.

The key was in the ground three feet from where I was standing, where my dad had buried it one frozen dawn six years before — and a few miles away, in an incubator in a lab, the only creature in the world who could find it again was nine weeks from being born.

I just stood there in the gold light with my family, over the grave of my best friend, and I cried at last, properly, finally, the crying I'd held back all day, and Mum came and held me, and we held each other up among the leeks, and the blackbirds sang, and the sea shone, and the worst day of my life ended the way all days end, in the dark, with the stars coming out one by one over Kelsey Bay like they hadn't even noticed.

## Chapter Eighteen — The Longest Nine Weeks, and the Sea Slugs

Here is what grief is actually like, in case nobody's told you, because they didn't tell me and I had to find out.

Grief is not a thing that happens and then is over. Grief is a new lodger who moves into your house without asking and never leaves and just gradually, over a very long time, becomes someone you can live with. The first days it's unbearable — you keep reaching for the dog who isn't there, you keep hearing his nails on the kitchen floor, you put your hand down by the side of the bed in the dark for a head that isn't going to be there, and each time it's like losing him again, fresh, a hundred small new deaths a day. The house was *so quiet.* I never knew how much sound a sleeping dog makes until there was no sleeping dog making it. The patch of sun in the front room was just a patch of empty carpet, and I couldn't look at it, and Mum couldn't either, I'd catch her not-looking at it the exact same way I was not-looking at it, the two of us choreographing our whole lives around an empty bit of floor.

And the whole time — the whole nine weeks — I was carrying the secret.

You have to understand what that was like, because it's the part I'm least proud of and the part that nearly broke me and Mum for good. My mum was grieving our dog, openly, honestly, the way you're supposed to. And I was grieving him too — that part was real, that part was the realest thing in the world — but I was *also* sitting on a secret the size of a dog, a secret that said *he might not be all the way gone,* and I couldn't share it with her, the one person who'd loved him nearly as much as me, because Dr Lillico had said tell no one and because if it didn't work — *don't hope, it almost certainly won't work* — then telling Mum would just be breaking her heart twice.

So I lied. By saying nothing, which is the cleanest kind of lying and the worst. I let my mum think we were grieving the same thing the same way, when really I had a trapdoor of hope under my grief that she didn't know was there, and every day I didn't tell her, the wall between us got one brick higher. She knew something was off. Mums always know. "You'd tell me, wouldn't you, Bonnie," she said one night, out of nowhere, drying a plate, not looking at me, "if there was something. You're not — you've gone somewhere I can't reach, since Scratch, and I know it's grief, love, but it feels like more than grief, and I just — you'd tell me?" And I said, "I'm fine, Mum, it's just grief," which was the worst lie of all of them, worse than the break-in, worse than the note, because it was a lie that pushed away the one person reaching for me. I've apologised for it since. She forgave me, of course. That's the family disease's one good symptom — we forgive like it's going out of fashion. We get it from the dog.

I saw Dr Lillico twice in those nine weeks, in secret, on Saturdays, telling Mum I was at Lola's. And it was on the first of those Saturdays, in her flat — a small flat full of books and dog hair and not a single dog, which I noticed and didn't understand yet — that she finally told me what the samples were for. What I'd let her take. What was, even then, growing inside Maggie the surrogate along with the body of my dog.

"You're owed the truth," she said, "now it's done, and now there's no un-doing it. Sit down. Have you ever heard of a sea slug?"

I had not heard of a sea slug, particularly.

So she told me. And I'm going to tell you, the way she told me, because this is the centre of everything, this is the real science, the bit you won't believe and that is nevertheless true, and I promised at the very start that I'd explain the impossible thing so well that you could understand it and even predict it, and here it is. Here's the deal I made with you. Pay attention.

"Everybody knows," said Dr Lillico, "that memory lives in the brain. And mostly that's true — memory lives in the connections between brain cells, in the wiring, in which cells are joined to which and how strongly. That's why you can't clone a memory. When I clone a dog, I copy the DNA, the recipe — but the wiring of the brain isn't in the recipe. The wiring gets built fresh, by living, by experience. A new brain, blank, waiting to be wired up by a new life. That's why a clone knows nothing. The recipe's the same; the wiring's brand new."

"Right," I said. "You told me. It won't be him."

"That's what I told you. That's what I believe." She leaned forward. "But here's the thing that's kept me up at night for twenty years, Bonnie. There's another idea. An old idea, a heretical idea, an idea that gets scientists laughed at and ruins careers. The idea is: what if *some* memory — not all of it, not your address and your times tables, but the deep stuff, the oldest, strongest, most-repeated, most-emotional memories — what if some of *that* isn't only in the wiring? What if some of it is also stored in *molecules?* In chemicals. In a thing called RNA, which is like a working copy of bits of your DNA that your cells are using right now. What if your deepest memories leave a kind of — a residue. A chemical fingerprint. Down in the cells themselves."

"Is that real?" I said. "Or is that one of the laughed-at ideas?"

"Both," said Dr Lillico, and she almost smiled. "In the 1960s a scientist called James McConnell trained flatworms — little worms — to flinch at a light. Then he ground the trained worms up and fed them to untrained worms. And he said the untrained worms learned to flinch faster — like they'd eaten the memory. Everyone laughed at him. Nobody could repeat it. It became a joke, a thing you tell students about how science goes wrong." She paused. "And then, in 2018, a very serious, very respected scientist at a university in California called David Glanzman took a sea slug — a big sea creature called Aplysia, with simple memories you can measure — and he trained it to do a defensive curl when you tapped it. Then he took the RNA out of the trained slug, and he injected it into a slug that had never been trained. And the untrained slug — " she spread her hands — "did the curl. It behaved as if it remembered something that had only ever happened to a *different animal.* They published it. It's real. You can look it up. It made the news all over the world and then everyone got embarrassed and stopped talking about it, because if it's true — if memory can live in a molecule and be moved from one animal to another — then it breaks something we thought we knew about what a 'self' even is."

I sat very still in her dog-haired flat. "And that's what you took," I whispered. "From Scratch. RNA. From his — "

"From the parts of him where the deepest memories would leave the deepest fingerprint, yes. Fresh, while it could still be read." She looked at me steadily. "And I introduced it into the embryo. Into the one we put into Maggie. At the exact early window when the nervous system is first forming and might — *might* — take an impression. Nobody has ever done this in a mammal. Nobody has ever cloned an animal and then tried to give the clone a residue of the original's mind. It is almost certainly impossible. It will almost certainly do nothing at all, and the puppy will be a blank, like I always told you, and you must hold on to that." She took my hands. "But you called me in time. You gave me the freshest, best sample I have ever had, from a dog whose perfect genetic copy was already growing in the right kind of body. It's the one shot anyone has ever had to find out. And so — God forgive me, because it's *your* dog and *your* grief I'm gambling with — I took it. I tried. Now we wait. And we do not hope. Do you understand the rules?"

"Strong memories," I said slowly, working it out, the way she'd taught me to. "Not everything. The deepest, oldest, most-repeated, most-emotional ones. They might come through. As — what? As feelings? He won't be able to *talk.*"

"As instinct," said Dr Lillico softly. "As behaviour. As fear, and joy, and knowing things he shouldn't know. If it works at all — *if* — it won't be a dog who remembers in words. It'll be a dog who is mysteriously, impossibly drawn to the things the first dog loved most, and frightened of the things the first dog feared most, with no way of explaining why. A dog haunted by the ghost of another dog's strongest feelings. That's the most it could ever be." She let go of my hands and sat back, suddenly looking very tired and very afraid of what she'd done. "And if that happens, Bonnie — if it actually happens — then the deepest, strongest feeling that dog ever had is the one most likely to come through. And we'll both have to ask ourselves what that was. What was the strongest thing in Scratch's whole life. What was the deepest groove in the record."

---

I met the mother on the second Saturday.

I hadn't thought about the mother. That's the thing nobody tells you about cloning, the thing that isn't in the brochure: that an embryo in a dish can't become a puppy by itself, that it needs a living, breathing, ordinary dog to grow it, to carry it for two months, to give birth to it in pain and lick it clean and feed it warm. There's always a mother. There's no machine for that part. There never will be. Dr Lillico drove me, in the dented green car, out past the bypass and up a farm track I'd never been down, to a low building behind the university's vet field station, and she let us in with a different keycard, and there, in a warm pen full of clean straw, was Maggie.

Maggie was a greyhound — of course she was — but an old one, a soft golden-fawn old lady with a grey muzzle and the kindest, daftest face I'd ever seen, who heaved herself up when we came in and came over wagging her whole back end and leaned on me, the lean, *I'm here and you're here,* like she'd known me all her life. She'd been a breeding dog, Dr Lillico said, retired now, "carried five litters of her own and never lost one, soft as butter, the best mother I've ever known, dog or human." And she was pregnant. You could see it, just starting, the swell of her low belly under the fawn fur.

"That's him," I said. I could hardly get the words out. "In there. That's — Scratch. He's in there. In *her.*"

"That's the embryo we made, yes," said Dr Lillico, gently. "Growing. With Scratch's RNA in him, for what it's worth, which may be nothing. Maggie's doing all the work. Maggie does all the real work. I just press the buttons." She crouched down and rubbed the old dog's ears, and Maggie groaned with pleasure and flopped over to have her tummy done, shameless, and we both laughed, and it was the first time I'd laughed in the nine weeks, and it cracked something open in me that needed cracking. "People think the science is the clever bit," Dr Lillico said, doing the old dog's tummy. "The machine, the DNA, the freezing. It isn't. The clever bit is *her.* Forty million years of being a mammal. I can't build that. Nobody can. All I can do is borrow it. We always have to borrow the mother. Don't ever let anyone tell you cloning makes a life, Bonnie. It doesn't. It just — moves one. From a cheek swab into a good old dog who knows how to grow a heart."

I sat down in the straw next to Maggie, and she put her grey muzzle in my lap and sighed, and I thought about this kind old retired dog growing my dead dog inside her, doing the one thing no laboratory on earth could do, and I thought it was the strangest and most ordinary miracle I'd ever heard of, both at once, which is what all the real ones are.

"What happens to her?" I said. "After. When he's born. Do you — does the company — "

"She comes to live with me," said Dr Lillico, and her voice went odd. "I've already decided. They'll want to put her back in the programme — she's a good carrier, they'll want to use her again — and I won't have it. She's done. She's earned her settee. The day that pup is weaned, Maggie retires to my flat and never grows another thing but old and fat and happy, I swear it on — " She stopped. And she looked at the old fawn dog for a long moment, and then she said, quietly, the thing she'd been not-saying for weeks. "I had a dog called Maggie when I was a girl. The first Maggie. My dog. She died when I was twelve, of a thing they couldn't fix, and I have spent thirty years and an entire career trying to find a way to not let that be the end of the sentence. I named this one after her. I don't even know why I'm telling you." She wiped her face, brisk. "Because you're twelve, I suppose, and your dog just died, and I'm looking at you and seeing me, and hoping to God you don't spend the next thirty years the way I have, mistaking a refusal to say goodbye for a kind of love. Come on. Say goodbye to Maggie. We'll come back. And on the way home I'll buy you chips and you can try, just try, not to hope."

I said goodbye to Maggie. I came back four more times over those weeks, in secret, and each time the swell of her was bigger, and each time she leaned on me, and I came to love that daft kind old dog nearly as much as I'd loved my own, which I think is the real reason Dr Lillico kept taking me — not for the science, but because a girl drowning in grief needs somewhere warm to put her hands, and there is nothing warmer in the whole world than the side of a sleeping pregnant dog.

But on the way home from that first visit, eating chips out of paper in the dented green car with the rain coming down, I kept turning Dr Lillico's question over and over — *what was the deepest groove, what was the strongest thing in Scratch's whole life* — because she'd said it would be the thing most likely to come through, and I wanted, more than anything, for it to be me.

I didn't know the answer. I thought, then, that it would be me. I thought, if anything came through, it would be his love for me, because surely I was the strongest thing in his life, surely thirteen years of me was the deepest groove.

I was wrong. It wasn't me.

It was one frozen dawn in a back lane, six years before, when a man Scratch loved more than anything in the world came back, just once, and said goodbye to the dog because he couldn't bear to say it to his little girl.

That was the deepest groove. That was the strongest feeling Scratch ever had.

And nine weeks later, it came home, on four wobbly legs, and it walked straight up our garden path.

## Chapter Nineteen — The Puppy on the Step

It was a Saturday morning in the middle of June, nine and a half weeks after we buried Scratch, and I knew it was coming, because Dr Lillico had texted me the night before — a single message, no name, from a number I'd saved as "Dentist" in case Mum ever looked at my phone:

*Tomorrow morning. Be at home. I can't be seen anywhere near you now — there are people at the lab asking questions I can't answer, and the safest place in the world for him is your house, where he's just a puppy and nobody's looking. I'm sorry it has to be like this. He's healthy. He's perfect. Don't tell a soul where he came from, not even your mum, ESPECIALLY not your mum, for her sake as much as mine. Look after him, Bonnie. And — I broke my own rule. I hoped. You should probably look at his face before you decide whether to forgive me. P.L.*

I didn't sleep. Obviously I didn't sleep. I lay awake all night in the quiet house with the empty patch of carpet and I tried to do what she'd said and not hope, and I failed completely, hope roaring through me like the tide coming in, and at the same time I was sick with dread, because — what if it was a stranger? What if I opened the door and there was a puppy who looked like Scratch and felt like nothing, a blank, a body, and I had to stand there and feel my heart understand that I'd lost him all over again, that the body without the dog was actually *worse,* a kind of haunting?

The doorbell went at twenty past nine.

Mum was in the kitchen. I got to the front door first — I'd been hovering near it for an hour, pretending to tidy the shoe rack — and I opened it, and there was nobody there. The street was empty. The dented green car was already gone, away round the corner, I just caught the sound of it. And on the front step, on our cracked front step at number 9 Ewesley Terrace, was a cardboard box, the medium kind, with the top folded shut, and a piece of paper taped to it, and a small sound coming from inside it. A small, scrabbling, *complaining* sound.

I knelt down. My hands were shaking so hard I could barely work the flaps. The note just said, in Dr Lillico's spiky handwriting: *His name is up to you. Everything else is already decided — you'll see. Be brave.*

I opened the box.

And a puppy looked up at me.

I want to get this exactly right, because it's the most important moment in this whole book, more important than the break-in or the launch or any of it, and I've never managed to say it properly out loud to anyone, so I'm going to try here.

He was a greyhound puppy, eight or nine weeks old, all enormous paws and folded-over ears and loose skin he hadn't grown into, sitting in a nest of an old jumper at the bottom of the box, blinking up at the sudden light. And he was brindle — grey, like a wet pavement, with darker stripes running through it. And he had one white toe on his back foot, like he'd stepped in paint. And he had a face — a long, anxious, hopeful, faintly-surprised-to-exist greyhound face — that I had been looking at every single day for seven years, that I had held in my hands three weeks and nine and a half weeks ago, that I knew better than I knew my own.

It was Scratch's face. Tiny, new, soft, unmarked — but Scratch's. The same face. The face my dad had carried home on a bit of rope.

And the puppy looked up at me out of Scratch's face, and he did a thing that no eight-week-old puppy who had never met me in his life had any business doing, the first of the impossible things, the one that knocked the wind clean out of me right there on the doorstep:

He stopped scrabbling. He went still. He looked at me — really *looked,* the way Scratch looked, mildly surprised and pleased that I existed — and then he stretched out his long soft nose, and he sniffed my fingers, once, and then, very carefully, like he was apologising, he licked me.

Exactly once.

Exactly the way Scratch had, in the back lane, when I was five years old, when my dad said *there you are, he's chosen you.*

I made a sound I've never made before or since. And I picked him up — he weighed almost nothing, he was all warmth and milk-smell and heartbeat — and I held him against my chest, and his heart was going *pip-pip-pip-pip,* fast and tiny and perfect, a brand-new heart with nothing wrong with it at all, a heart that would last, and I stood on the doorstep and cried into the neck of a puppy who had my dead dog's face, and the puppy did not squirm or complain. He pressed himself into me. He leaned. As much as a puppy that small can lean, he leaned his whole self against me, and sighed, and settled, like he'd been looking for me, like he'd finally, after a long cold journey, got home.

"Bonnie?" Mum's voice, behind me, from the hall, sharp with alarm at the sound I'd made. "Bonnie, what — "

And then she stopped.

Because she'd come to the door, and she'd seen what I was holding, and Mum — my mum, who had buried our dog nine weeks before, who not-looked at the empty patch of carpet every single day — Mum put her hand over her mouth and went absolutely white, and said, in a whisper, "Oh my God. Oh my *God,* Bonnie. Where — he's the *image* of him. He's the spit of Scratch as a pup, I — where has he *come* from?"

And there it was. The question. The first telling of the lie I'd have to tell my own mum, on the doorstep, with the truth warm and breathing in my arms.

I had it ready. Lola and I had built it for weeks. "There was a note," I said, which was true. "He was just — somebody left him. On the step. Look." I showed her Dr Lillico's note, which gave nothing away. "I think — Lola said her mum had heard about a greyhound at the rescue that had pups and they were looking for homes, foster homes, and everyone knows we — that we had Scratch, that we're greyhound people — I think somebody must've — " I ran out of road. I looked at her over the puppy's head, and I did the only true thing I could do, which was let her see my whole face, wet and broken and wild with a hope I couldn't explain. "Mum. He licked me. He came out the box and he looked at me and he licked me once and he *leaned,* Mum, he did the lean, and I know it's mad and I know it's too soon and I know we can't afford it but please. *Please.* I can't put him back in the box. I can't."

And my mum stood in the doorway and looked at the puppy, and looked at me, and I watched about six different things fight it out on her face — the grief, still raw; the worry about money; the sense that this was somehow, impossibly, too neat, too soon, too strange; and underneath all of it, plain as day, the exact same helpless leap of the heart that I was feeling, because she'd loved that dog too, and here was his face, new and alive, sniffing the morning, in her daughter's arms.

"We're not — " she started, and her voice wobbled. "We can't just — Bonnie, we've not even — it's only been nine weeks, love, I'm not — " and she reached out, the way you can't not, and the puppy turned his head and sniffed her fingers and then nibbled them with his needle teeth, and Mum made a wet helpless laughing sound, and that was it, that was the moment, I saw her lose.

"*Fostering,*" she said at last, fierce, wiping her eyes, pointing a finger at me like she was laying down the law, like she hadn't already completely surrendered. "That's all. We *foster* him. We see if it's right. We are not — I'm not promising — and you're cleaning up every single mess, Bonnie Maguire, every wee and every chewed shoe, and we'll have to ring this rescue and do it *properly,* with papers — "

"Yes," I said. "Yes. Everything. I promise. Everything."

(There was no rescue. There were no papers. That was a whole other web of lies I'd have to spin in the coming weeks, with a lot of help from Lola and, eventually, from Bisi, who worked it out and — but that's later.)

Mum reached out and took the puppy from me, the way she used to take me when I was small, expert, both hands, and she held him up and looked into his face, his impossible familiar face, and her own face crumpled, and she said, very softly, to the puppy, not to me, "Well, aren't you a thing. Aren't you just the spit of him. Where did you come from, eh? Where on earth did you come from?"

And the puppy looked back at her out of Scratch's eyes and had no way on earth to tell her the truth.

But I knew the truth. And the truth was standing on the doorstep with me, and it was the strangest, most frightening, most wonderful truth in the world, and it had only just begun to show itself.

He hadn't come from a rescue.

He'd come from a box, from a lab, from a machine I'd switched on by accident, from the freshest sample Dr Lillico had ever taken, from the deepest groove in a dead dog's heart.

He'd come home.

## Chapter Twenty — Itch

We couldn't keep calling him Scratch.

I tried, that first day. I'd say "Scratch" and the puppy's ears would do a thing — they'd lift, just slightly, a flicker, like a word half-remembered — but I couldn't make the sound come out of my mouth more than a few times before it broke me, because Scratch was the dog under the paving slab up the allotment, Scratch was the dog who ran on the beach, Scratch was thirteen years and a torn ear and a too-hard heart, and this was — this was something else. Something new wearing his face. To call him Scratch felt like lying to both of them, the dead one and the new one, like trying to staple them together when they weren't the same.

But I couldn't call him a totally new name either, because he wasn't a totally new dog, and pretending he was felt like a different lie.

Lola solved it, the way Lola solves everything, by not overthinking it.

She came round that first afternoon — I'd texted her one word, *HE'S HERE,* and she'd run the four doors in about nine seconds flat — and she sat on our front room floor with the puppy in her lap, and the puppy chewed her thumb, and she looked at him for a long time with her head on one side, the buying-the-room look, and then she said:

"He's Scratch, but he's not Scratch. Right? He's like... Scratch happening again. Scratch, the sequel." She grinned, and the grin had tears behind it, because Lola loved that old dog too. "He's the *itch* you get after the scratch."

And that was it. That was the name. The puppy looked up at the sound of Lola's laugh, and I said, testing it, "Itch?" — and his ears did the lift again, the flicker, and his tail, his ridiculous whippy puppy tail, went *thump* on the carpet, once, and Lola and I looked at each other, because we'd both seen it, that single thump, in the exact spot Scratch used to lie.

Itch. The clone of Scratch was called Itch. I have to tell you, my dead dog would have found that absolutely hilarious. He had a good sense of humour, Scratch. Greyhounds do. People don't know that about them.

Now. Before I tell you the other thing, the not-quite-perfect thing, I have to tell you how nearly the whole business fell apart in the first week over a piece of paper, because it's important, and because it's the bit that turned Lola's mum from somebody we were lying to into somebody on our side, which we needed more than we knew.

The problem was Mum, and the problem with Mum was that Mum is *honest.* She wanted to do it properly. "We're not just keeping a strange dog that turned up in a box, Bonnie," she said, on about day two, while Itch ate a corner of the doormat. "What if he's microchipped? What if somebody's lost him and breaking their heart? We're taking him to the vet's, today, to get him scanned and checked and registered, and if this rescue exists they'll have records, and we'll do it right." Which is exactly the correct, responsible, lovely thing a good person does when a puppy appears on the step. It was also, for me, a catastrophe, because there was no rescue, there were no records, and there was no microchip, and the only person who could make a dog out of a cell was hiding from her own employer in a flat full of dog hair and could not exactly come and vouch for him.

So we went to the vet's. To Mr Pringle's surgery. Where, of all the people in the world who could have been on the desk that morning, was Bisi.

I nearly turned round and walked out. But Mum was already saying, "Bisi! Oh, am I glad it's you, you'll never guess, look — " and lifting Itch up onto the counter, and Bisi Okafor, Lola's mum, calm as still water, looked at the puppy.

And I watched her see it.

I watched her clock the breed, and the brindle, and the one white toe, and the long anxious greyhound face — the exact face of a dog she had helped to die, with her own hands, on our front room carpet nine weeks before, a dog whose passing she had wept over. I watched her go very, very still. And I watched her decide something, in about a second and a half, with her eyes flicking once to me and then back to the puppy, and whatever she decided, she did not say *this is the spit of your dead Scratch* and she did not say *where has this really come from* and she did not say any of the nine hundred true and dangerous things on her face. She picked up the microchip scanner and ran it over the back of Itch's neck, and it beeped its empty no-chip beep, and she said, smoothly, to Mum, "No chip. That's common with the rescue fosters, they chip them when they're rehomed permanently, not before." Which is a real thing, and a true thing, and also, in that moment, a door she was holding open for me, the exact way her daughter had held open a door six months before.

Then she gave Itch his first jabs, and weighed him, and checked his heart — "strong as an ox, this one, nothing wrong with this little ticker" — and she set Mum up with a vaccination card and a foster record under our address, doing it all with her calm vet-nurse hands, and Mum was happy, and the paper problem was solved, and on the way out, while Mum was paying, Bisi crouched down to my level — even Bisi, even her, crouching down to tell me a thing — and she said, very quietly, just for me:

"I don't know what you've done, Bonnie. I've decided I don't want to know, because I think if I knew, I'd have to do something about it, and I don't want to do something about it, because — " she looked at the puppy, and her professional face wobbled, just for a second — "because that is the healthiest, happiest nine-week-old greyhound I have ever scanned, and you clearly love him to bits, and the world has quite enough people doing the right thing and breaking children's hearts. So I'm going to do the wrong thing and keep my mouth shut. But if he's ever poorly, you bring him straight to me, and you don't go to anybody else, because I'm the only vet professional in this town who isn't going to ask the questions, and a dog that came from — wherever he came from — might need somebody who already isn't asking. All right?" She stood up. "And Bonnie. Whatever it was. I'm glad you did it. He's a beautiful dog." And she went back to the desk and called the next appointment like nothing had happened.

That's how Bisi found out. Sort of found out. *Decided not to find out,* which is a very Bisi way of finding out, and which made her, from that day, the third grown-up who quietly held a door, and the first one I hadn't had to confess to — she'd worked it out and chosen me anyway, which is a different and bigger thing than being told.

The rest of that first week was just — a puppy. I'd forgotten, in seven years of an old dog, what a puppy actually *is,* which is a small furry machine for converting your house into damage. Itch ate: a slipper, the corner of the doormat, three pegs, a whole crayon (his next poo was a beautiful and alarming blue), the TV remote, and one of Mick's enormous work boots, which he could fit his entire front half inside, so that for a while we had a dog with a boot for a head, blundering blindly into the furniture, and even Mum, even grief-flattened Mum, laughed until she had to sit down. He weed everywhere. He cried at night until I took him into the bed, where he slept in the exact spot, against my legs, that Scratch had slept, and the weight of him there was so much like and so much not like the old weight that I lay awake half that first night not knowing whether I was the happiest or the saddest I'd ever been, and deciding, in the end, that there isn't always a difference, that some feelings are just too big to have a single name, and that's all right too.

So. Itch. And I have to tell you about the not-quite-perfect part now, because it matters, because it turned out to be the whole point, the thing the entire story was secretly about, even though it felt at the time like a disappointment.

Because here's the thing I noticed, that first day, holding him, that I hadn't seen in the rush on the doorstep: he wasn't an exact copy. Not quite.

His ears were both perfect. No notch. No torn left ear. And of course there wouldn't be — I worked it out slowly, lying awake that night with the puppy asleep in a box by my bed (in *my* room, Mum had given in on that within about four hours) — of course there wouldn't be, because the torn ear wasn't in Scratch's recipe. The torn ear happened *to* Scratch, at the track, before we ever knew him. It was an injury, a scar, a thing life did to him. And you can't clone a scar. You can only clone the recipe, and the recipe said *perfect ear,* the ear Scratch was born with, the ear he had before the world got to him.

And his white toe was on the wrong foot. Scratch's painted toe was back left. Itch's was back right. And there was a little splash of white on Itch's chest that Scratch never had at all.

I didn't understand it, so the next Saturday I asked Dr Lillico — in secret, in her flat — and she nodded like she'd been expecting it, and she explained, and it's a true thing and a strange one and I want you to have it.

"Coat patterns aren't fully in the DNA," she said. "Not the exact placement. The recipe says *brindle, with some white* — but exactly *where* the white goes, which toe, what splash on the chest, that's decided fresh each time, partly at random, while the puppy's growing, by tiny accidents of how the cells happen to move about. The first ever cloned cat — CC, Copy Cat — her donor was a calico, all patches of orange and black and white, and CC came out a completely different pattern. Grey and white. People were furious. They wanted their cat back and they got a cat with the wrong colours. Because the pattern isn't the recipe. The pattern is the *baking.*" She looked at me carefully. "Does it upset you? That he's not identical?"

And I thought about it, and I said the thing that I think is the truest thing I learned out of this entire mad story, and I didn't even know I knew it until I said it:

"No," I said slowly. "Because it means he's not a photocopy. He's not a — a fake Scratch, a forgery. He's his own actual dog. He got made his own way, with his own accidents, his own wrong toe and his own bit of white. He's *real.* He's a real new dog who just happens to be — made of the same recipe." I swallowed. "And his ears aren't torn. Nothing's hurt him yet. He hasn't been to the track. He hasn't lost anybody. He gets to start again, with none of the bad bits, none of the scars. That's — " and I had to stop, because it had got me right in the throat, "— that's the kindest thing, isn't it? That's the kindest thing in the world. He gets to keep the good recipe and skip all the bad that happened to it."

Dr Lillico looked at me for a long moment, and her tea-coloured eyes went bright, and she said, very quietly, "You are an extraordinary child, Bonnie Maguire," which is the only time in my whole life an adult has said that to me, and I've never forgotten it, even though I don't think it's true. I'm not extraordinary. I just spent a lot of time with a dog, and dogs teach you the important things, if you let them.

But here's the thing that the not-quite-perfect-copy business was hiding, the thing that took me another fortnight to see, the thing that turned everything upside down.

It was true that Itch wasn't an exact copy of Scratch's *body.* Wrong toe, perfect ears, extra white. The body had drifted.

But the *other* thing — the impossible thing, the thing Dr Lillico had taken from the deepest groove and gambled everything on — the other thing hadn't drifted at all.

Because three days after we named him, Itch — a nine-week-old puppy who had been alive for nine weeks, who had never been to our house before that Saturday, who had never in his short life laid eyes on a wheelie bin — Itch walked out into our back yard for the first time, on his little wobbling legs, sniffing everything, delighted by everything, the way puppies are.

And then he saw the wheelie bin.

And he stopped dead. And his hackles went up, all the way down his tiny spine. And he backed away from it, trembling, his eyes gone huge and white-rimmed, and he let out a sound — a high, thin, terrified sound, half a whine and half a question — that I had heard exactly once before in my life.

Behind the settee. On the night of the fireworks. From a dog who was already dead.

The body had drifted. But the fear — the deep, old, grooved-in fear — had come through perfectly, untouched, exact.

And I stood in the back yard and looked at my new puppy, frozen in terror in front of a wheelie bin he had every reason in the world to find completely boring, and I felt the whole thing tilt under my feet like the deck of a ship, and I thought:

*It worked. Oh my God. It actually worked. He's in there. Some of him is actually in there.*

And then I thought, with a coldness that started in my feet and went all the way up, the thought that would drive the entire rest of this story, the thought that would lead me to the tin in the ground and the truth about my dad and the worst and best days of my whole life:

*If the fear came through — what else did?*

# PART THREE: WHAT THE DOG REMEMBERED

## Chapter Twenty-One — A Complete List of Things Itch Should Not Have Known

Lola and I started a list. Of course we did. I'm a list person; she's a Lola.

We kept it in a notebook with a dog on the front that we hid under the loose floorboard in my room, and we wrote down every single thing Itch did that an ordinary puppy could not possibly do — every impossible thing, every leak from the dead dog's mind into the live one's body — and we tested them, carefully, like scientists, because Dr Lillico had taught me that you don't just *believe* a thing, you *check* it, you try to prove yourself wrong, and only when you can't is it real.

Here is the list. I've still got the notebook. Some of these will stop you in your tracks the way they stopped us.

**THINGS ITCH SHOULD NOT KNOW (but does):**

*1. The patch of sun.* There's a spot in the front room, by the window, where the sun comes in for about two hours in the afternoon. Scratch lay in it every single day of his life. Itch — at nine weeks old, his first afternoon in the house — toddled across the whole room, past about six more comfortable spots, and lay down in exactly that patch, with his legs sticking straight out, and went to sleep. We moved his bed to the other side of the room. The next day he ignored the bed and went back to the patch. It's not the warmest spot. It's not the comfiest. It was just *Scratch's.*

*2. The lean.* No puppy leans. Puppies fall over, which is different. Itch leans — puts his whole increasing weight against your legs and stands there, the greyhound lean, the *I'm here and you're here.* He did it the first day. You can't teach the lean. Scratch took about a year to start doing it. Itch was born knowing.

*3. Mr Ball.* This is the one that made Lola actually scream. We'd buried Scratch with his manky flat tennis ball, Mr Ball, up at the allotment. But there was an old spare — a second manky flat tennis ball — that had lived, for years, down the side of the radiator in the hall, in a gap you could only see if you lay flat on the floor and looked. We'd forgotten it existed. Itch, on his third day, walked into the hall, lay flat on the floor, stuck his whole front leg down the side of the radiator, and pulled out a tennis ball none of us had thought about in two years. He knew it was there. He'd never been in the hall before. He just *knew.*

*4. "Walkies-this-way."* When Scratch was younger we always turned left out of the back gate, towards the dene, never right towards the main road. Itch, the first time we opened the back gate, hauled on the lead — left. Always left. He's never once tried to go right.

*5. The vacuum, the shiny floor, the meerkat advert.* All of Scratch's fears, exact. The meerkat one is the maddest. There's an advert with a cartoon meerkat in it, and when it comes on the telly Itch growls at the screen, low, the way Scratch used to, and nobody has ever been able to explain why Scratch hated that meerkat, and now there are two dogs, one of them dead, united across the grave by a shared and totally inexplicable hatred of a cartoon meerkat selling car insurance. If that's not a soul I don't know what is.

And then there were the two that didn't fit. The two we couldn't explain even with "it's Scratch's memory," because they weren't about *our* life with Scratch at all. They were about a life before me. A life I wasn't in.

*6. The whistle.*

I found it by accident. I was mucking about, trying to teach Itch to come, doing different whistles, and I did one — two notes, a low one then a high one, *fweet-FWEET,* I don't even know where it came from, my mouth just did it — and Itch went *berserk.*

Not scared. The opposite. He went into a state of total, ecstatic, whole-body joy that I had never seen in him or in Scratch or in any dog, ever. He spun in circles. He cried — actually cried, a high happy keening — and ran to the back door and scrabbled at it and ran back to me and spun again, looking up at me, looking at the door, looking at me, his whole body one enormous question, *where is it, where is it, do it again, where IS it,* and I did it again, *fweet-FWEET,* and he nearly turned himself inside out, and then — and this is the bit that got me — and then, when nothing happened, when the whistle didn't bring whatever it was supposed to bring, he stopped. And he stood in the middle of the floor. And he drooped. The joy went out of him like water out of a bath, and he made a small lost sound, and he went and lay in the patch of sun with his back to me, exactly the way Scratch used to lie when he was disappointed.

I sat on the floor with my heart going. Because that whistle meant something. That whistle meant the *best thing in the world* to whatever was inside Itch. And it wasn't me — I was right there, and the whistle made him look *past* me, towards the door, towards something coming, something that wasn't coming.

I'd never whistled that whistle in my life. I didn't know where my mouth had got it.

But somebody had. Somebody, once, had whistled exactly that — *fweet-FWEET* — and to the dog who was inside Itch, that sound had meant joy was about to walk through the door.

*7. "Howay."*

This one was quieter, and it took me longer to notice, and when I did notice it the bottom dropped out of my stomach.

It's just a word. Everyone here says it. *Howay,* come on, let's go. Mum says it, Mick says it, I say it. But there's a *way* of saying it — soft, two notes almost like the whistle, *how-way,* with a little lift at the end — that, when anyone happens to say it like that, makes Itch's ears do the thing, the lift, the flicker, and makes him turn his head towards the back door. Towards the lane. The same direction as the whistle.

And I knew that voice. I didn't know I knew it, but I knew it, the way you know a smell before you can name it. Somewhere under seven years and a closed door in the middle of me, somebody used to say *howay* exactly like that. Somebody used to stand at the back door and say *howay, Bon, howay and look what I've got,* and put a frightened dog into my five-year-old arms.

I sat on the floor of my own front room, with a puppy who had my dead dog's face and a stranger's joy living inside him, and I understood, all at once, cold from my feet to my scalp, what I was actually looking at.

Itch didn't just have Scratch's memories of *me.* Of *us.* Dr Lillico had taken the deepest grooves, the strongest feelings — and the strongest feeling in Scratch's whole life wasn't me at all. I'd thought it would be me. It wasn't.

It was a person who used to whistle two notes and say *howay* and bring joy through the back door. A person Scratch had loved more than anything in the world. A person who, at some point, had stopped coming — so that the joy turned into waiting, and the waiting turned into the deepest groove of all, the keening at the door, the drooping, the lying down with your back to the room.

It was my dad.

The dog remembered my dad.

The dog remembered my dad better than I did. The dog had loved my dad with a love so huge it had survived death and cloning and a machine in a lab, and it was sitting in my front room, in a puppy's body, crying at a whistle, waiting at a door, for a man I had spent seven years training myself not to think about.

And I had spent those seven years being told *he went, that's all there is to it.* And here, finally, was a witness. The only witness. A witness who couldn't speak — but who could *show* me. Who *was* showing me, every day, in the only language he had.

I just had to learn to read him.

I closed the notebook. I looked at the puppy lying with his back to me in the patch of sun, drooping, mourning a man at a door.

"Okay," I said to him, very quietly. "Okay. You knew him. You're trying to tell me. I'm listening. Show me. Show me what you know."

And the next morning, before it was even light, he did.

## Chapter Twenty-Two — The Back Gate at Dawn

I woke up because he was gone.

He slept in my room, in a box at first and then, within about a week, on the actual bed, against my actual legs, exactly where Scratch used to sleep, a warm heavy comma of dog — and I'd got so used to the weight of him in the night that when it wasn't there, at twenty to five in the morning, I woke up like someone had pulled a plug.

The box was empty. The bed was empty. My door, which I'd shut, was nosed open — he'd learned to do that, push the handle, Scratch could do it too — and the landing was grey with the not-quite-light of a June dawn.

I went downstairs in my pyjamas, cold, my heart going, thinking he'd had an accident or got stuck or chewed something he shouldn't, and the kitchen was empty, and the back door — the back door was open. Not wide. Just off the latch, the way it goes if you don't slam it, and I'd been the last one up and I must not have slammed it, and Itch had pushed through it, out into the yard, in the cold blue dark before dawn.

And I found him at the back gate.

He was sitting bolt upright at the gate to the back lane — the gate my dad walked out of for the last time, the gate I'd carried a frightened greyhound through when I was five — and he was looking down the lane, towards the gap at the end, towards the road and the everywhere-else, with his whole body rigid and electric and *waiting.* His ears were up. His head was high. Every muscle in him was pointed down that lane like a compass needle, like an arrow on a drawn bow, like the most important thing in the world was about to come round that corner, any second now, any second.

He didn't even look at me when I came out. That's how I knew it was real, and deep, and not about me. Itch loves me; in the normal run of things he can't take his eyes off me; I am, in the ordinary daylight world, his entire sun and moon. But not at the gate at dawn. At the gate at dawn I didn't exist. At the gate at dawn there was only the lane, and the gap, and the thing that was coming.

I sat down on the cold step in my pyjamas and I watched him wait.

I watched him wait for a long time, as the sky went from black to blue to grey to the first thin gold, as the blackbirds started up, as a milk float whined past somewhere and a gull went over. He waited the way only a dog can wait, with his whole entire self, with a faith that would break your heart, certain — completely certain — that if he just kept watching that corner, the person would come.

And then, slowly, as the light came up and the lane stayed empty and the town began to wake and it became, even to a dog, undeniable that no one was coming — slowly, he drooped. The ears came down. The high proud head sank. And he turned, at last, and saw me on the step, and he came over, low, his tail doing a small apologetic wag, and he climbed into my lap, all legs and cold fur, and pressed his face into my chest, and made the small lost sound.

And I held him, and I cried, because I understood exactly what I'd just watched, because I had done it myself.

I had *been* him. For about three years, after Dad left, I used to do exactly that. Not at dawn — after school. I'd come home and sit on the front step and watch the end of the street, certain, completely certain, that one day his car would come round the corner. I'd watch until it got dark and Mum called me in. I never told anyone. And I'd stopped, eventually, because you can only sit on a step watching an empty corner for so many years before something in you quietly gives up and closes a door, and that door had stayed closed for half my life.

And here was a dog — a brand-new dog, nine weeks old — sitting at a gate at dawn, doing the exact thing I'd given up doing, watching the exact corner, waiting for the exact same man. Because the dog hadn't given up. The dog couldn't give up, because the dog didn't know the man was never coming. The dog only had the memory, the strongest one, the deepest groove: *he comes round that corner. At dawn. If you wait. He came once. He could come again.*

*He came once.*

I stopped crying. I sat very still on the cold step with the puppy in my lap, and I turned that over, because it was important, it was the most important thing yet.

*He came once.* The dog's memory wasn't of my dad *leaving.* I'd assumed the waiting was about the day he left. But a dog doesn't wait at a gate at dawn because someone left through it years ago. A dog waits at a gate because, at that gate, at that time of day, *the person once came back.* The waiting only makes sense if there was a homecoming to wait for. You don't keep watching a corner because someone left round it. You keep watching it because, one time, against all odds, in the cold and the dark, someone came *round* it. And it was the best moment of your whole life. And you've been waiting for it to happen again ever since.

My dad came back.

Not the day he left. Some other day. Some dawn. After he was already gone — he came *back,* round that corner, into that lane, and the only one who saw him, the only witness in the whole sleeping town, was a grey dog who loved him, who went out of his mind with joy, who never, ever forgot it, who carried it down to his death and beyond his death, into a new body, into a machine and out the other side, all the way to a June morning at a back gate, still waiting, still certain, still keeping watch on the corner where, once, the best thing in the world had come true.

My dad came back. And nobody ever told me.

I looked down at the puppy in my lap, at his anxious hopeful face, my dad's last gift wearing my dead dog's recipe, and I said the thing out loud, the thing that turned the whole rest of the summer into what it became:

"He came back. Didn't he. He came back and nobody told me." My voice didn't even shake. I felt very calm, the cold clear furnace calm. "Okay. Show me the rest. Where did he go? What did he do? You were there. You're the only one who was there." I stood up, holding him, and looked down the empty lane towards the gap where everything in my life had always either left or refused to arrive. "Take me. Whenever you're ready. Take me where he went."

It took him nine days. Nine days of me watching him, following him, reading him, while the threat I didn't know about yet crept closer through phone calls and spreadsheets and a man in a good grey suit who was, even then, sitting in an office across the river, frowning at a machine's log file, and finding, in a list of authorised overnight runs, one run that nobody had authorised at all.

But for nine days I didn't know about him. For nine days there was just me and a puppy and a question, and on the ninth day, up at the allotment, in the soft ground three feet from where we'd buried Scratch, Itch started to dig.

## Chapter Twenty-Three — What Was Under the Apple Tree

I'd taken to bringing Itch up to the allotment most days. Partly because Grandad liked it — though he'd gone strange and quiet around the puppy, Grandad had, he'd look at Itch with the oddest expression, like a man doing a sum that wouldn't come out, and I didn't understand it yet but I will tell you, when I do understand it, it's one of the saddest and best moments of the whole story. And partly because *Itch* liked it — he was drawn to that allotment like iron filings to a magnet, hauling on the lead all the way up Sleekburn Bank, and I'd let him, because I was following him now, reading him, letting him take me.

Grandad wasn't there that day. It was just me and Lola and the puppy, in the long gold evening, among the leeks. And Itch, who'd been pottering about sniffing things, suddenly stopped.

He'd gone to the corner by the old apple tree — a gnarled, half-dead old thing in the corner of the plot that Grandad keeps "for the blossom, and because your dad climbed it," a sentence Grandad had said to me exactly once, years ago, and I'd filed away in the place where I keep the crumbs about my dad. Itch stood under the apple tree, and his nose went down, and his whole body changed — went tight and certain, the dawn-at-the-gate certainty — and then he started to dig.

My first thought was the grave, and my stomach turned over, because Scratch's grave with its paving slab was right there, only a few feet away, and the idea of Itch digging at it — some horrible loop, the dog digging up the dog — I lunged for him. But he wasn't at the grave. He was under the apple tree, a good three feet from the slab, in a patch of bare earth between two roots, and he was digging with a focus and a fury I'd never seen in him, both front paws going like little engines, soil flying out between his back legs, pausing only to shove his nose down into the hole and snuffle and then dig again, faster.

"Bonnie," said Lola, very quietly, kneeling down beside me. "He's not digging *random.* Look at him. He's digging like he's digging *something up.* Like he knows there's something there."

"He does know," I said. My mouth had gone dry. "That's the whole point. He knows."

We let him dig. We even helped — I went and got Grandad's hand fork from the shed and loosened the soil for him, the two of us and the puppy all digging now under the apple tree in the last of the light, and Lola kept watch on the path in case Grandad came back, and the hole got deeper, eight inches, ten, a foot, and I was just starting to think there was nothing there, that I'd read him wrong, that it was just a puppy digging a puppy hole —

— when the fork hit something that wasn't earth and wasn't root and wasn't stone.

Something that went *tonk.* Something metal.

Lola and I looked at each other. Itch had stopped digging. He'd backed off, and he was sitting now, very still, watching the hole, his job done, his ears at half-mast, and there was something in the way he sat — quiet, finished, *delivered* — that made the back of my neck prickle.

I reached into the hole with my bare hands and I scraped the wet soil away, and there it was. The edge of it. The lid of a tin. An old biscuit tin, the round kind, gone rusty at the seams but mostly whole, wrapped — I could see now, as I worked it loose — wrapped round and round in a clear plastic bag, a freezer bag, the way you'd wrap something if you wanted it to survive years in the ground. Somebody had buried this carefully. Somebody had wanted it to last. Somebody had wanted it to be *found.*

I lifted it out. It was heavier than I expected. The plastic was filthy and the tin underneath had a faded picture on the lid, a Christmas scene, robins and holly, the kind of tin that has Christmas biscuits in it and then lives forever holding buttons and string in everybody's nana's house in the whole of England.

My hands were shaking so hard Lola had to help me get the plastic off.

And then I had it on my knees, in the gold light, under the apple tree my dad climbed as a boy, three feet from the grave of the dog he gave me — a rusty Christmas biscuit tin, sealed with a rim of old tape gone yellow and brittle.

"Bonnie," whispered Lola. "Are you — do you want me to — "

"No," I said. "I have to."

I picked at the tape. It came away in dry little flakes. And I worked my fingers under the lid, and it was stuck, years stuck, and then it gave all at once with a soft metal *pock,* the most enormous sound in the world, and I lifted it off.

And I looked inside the tin that my dad had buried under an apple tree six years before, at a dawn that only a dog had seen.

On the top, face up, looking back at me, was a photograph.

It was a Polaroid, the old square kind with the white border, gone a bit orange and soft like all old photos, like the one of Dad with the marrow. And in it — in it was a man, youngish, with my chin, my exact chin, I'd know that chin anywhere because I see it in the mirror, grinning that gap-toothed grin from the marrow photo, sitting on a front step I recognised, *our* front step, number 9. And in the crook of one arm he was holding a baby in a yellow babygro — me, it had to be me, fat-cheeked and outraged-looking, about one year old. And leaning against his other side, thin and young and worried, with a notch already torn out of his left ear, looking up at the man with an expression of such complete and total adoration that it stopped my heart, was a grey dog.

The three of us. Dad, and me, and Scratch. On the step of our house. One photograph, of the one time, maybe the only time, the three of us were ever together and someone thought to take a picture.

And turned over, on the back, in biro, in handwriting I had never seen before in my life but knew instantly, the way Itch knew the whistle, the way you know a thing that's wired into you below where words go — in my dad's handwriting, it said:

*My three best things. Don't forget me. — Dad x*

I sat under the apple tree in the gold evening with the rusty tin on my knees and the photograph in my hand and a puppy made of my dead dog watching me quietly with his ears at half-mast, and I did not cry, and I did not make a sound. I just looked at my dad's face, and my own baby face, and my dog's adoring one, and the four words on the back, *don't forget me,* written by a man who thought he was disappearing forever, to a daughter he was sure would forget him.

I hadn't forgotten him. I'd spent seven years not-forgetting him so hard it had bent my whole life out of shape.

And under the photograph, in the tin, there was more. There was a thick bundle of letters, folded, tied with a bit of garden twine. There was a small package wrapped in a hanky. And there was a cassette tape — an actual cassette, the kind I'd only ever seen in Grandad's shed — with a paper label on it, and on the label, in the same biro, the same hand, it said:

**FOR BONNIE. PLAY WHEN YOU'RE SAD. love DAD**

"Lola," I said. My voice came out very small and very far away. "I think my dad left me a tin. I think he came back, and he couldn't — he couldn't see me, for some reason, he couldn't knock on the door — so he buried me a tin. With the dog watching. And he's been waiting in the ground for six years for somebody to dig him up." I looked at the puppy. "And the only one who knew where he was, was the dog. Who's dead. Except he's not. Except I made him again. By accident. By breaking into a building." I started, finally, to shake. "Lola. I think I cloned a dog so that he could show me where my dad buried himself."

And Lola Okafor, who is not frightened of anything, put both her arms around me and the tin and the photograph all together, and held on, and said the only thing there was to say, which was nothing at all.

## Chapter Twenty-Four — Dear Bonnie

I didn't read the letters at the allotment. I couldn't. I got the tin home — hidden in my coat, my heart going the whole way down the bank — and I waited until that night, until the house was asleep, until it was just me and the puppy and the lamp, and then I untied the garden twine and I read my dad's letters in order, because he'd numbered them, in the corner, 1 to 9, because even falling apart my dad was apparently a list person, and now I knew where I got it.

I'm not going to give you all of them. Some of them are just mine; I've earned at least that. But I'll give you the parts you need to understand, because they're the parts that explain everything — the leaving, and the coming back, and the not knocking, and the tin.

The first one was dated, in the corner, from when I was five. From the week before he left. It started:

*Dear Bonnie,*

*You're asleep upstairs and the new dog is asleep next to you, he won't leave your room, and I've been sat here at the kitchen table for two hours trying to work out how to be a man who doesn't do the thing I'm going to do, and I can't, because I've made it so the only way to keep you safe is to not be here. I'm going to try to explain it and you're five so you won't read this for years and I hope by the time you do it'll all be sorted and you'll laugh at your daft dad. But just in case.*

And then he explained it. And it was so ordinary, and so sad, and so much smaller and more human than the monster-dad or the mystery-dad I'd built in seven years of imagining, that I had to put the letter down and breathe.

He'd lost his job. The yard he worked at had closed, all of it, two hundred men, and there was no other work, not for a scaffolder with no qualifications in a town where everything was shutting. And he'd been ashamed, and frightened, and instead of telling Mum how bad it was, he'd tried to *fix* it, quickly, the stupid way, the way men round here have been ruining themselves for a hundred years: he'd gambled. The dogs, at first — Brough Park, with Grandad, a few quid — and then more, and then online, on his phone, at the kitchen table at night while we slept, chasing it, the way you chase it, certain the big win was one more bet away, that he'd put it all right and Mum would never have to know how close they'd come.

He didn't win. Nobody wins. He'd written that himself, in the letter, and I'd thought of Grandad saying it in the shed — *you never win* — and understood that Grandad had been saying it to a ghost.

He'd ended up owing money to a man. Not a bank. The other kind. The kind that comes to your house. And he was terrified — there was a bit in the second letter, and I could feel his hand shaking through the biro — he was terrified that the man would come to our house, to the house where his wife and his little girl slept, and that we'd get hurt because of what he'd done. And he'd convinced himself — and you have to remember he was drowning, people who are drowning don't think straight — he'd convinced himself that the only way to keep us safe was to *take the danger away with him.* To leave. To draw the trouble off, like a bird with a broken-wing act, leading the fox away from the nest.

*If I'm not there, they've no reason to come to you,* he wrote. *They want me, not you. So I'll go, and I'll lead them off, and I'll get work somewhere far away and pay it down and come back when it's clean and safe and I can look your mam in the eye. That's the plan, Bonnie. I swear to you that's the plan. I'm not leaving because I don't love you. I'm leaving because I love you and I've been a coward and a fool and this is the only brave thing I've got left in me, and it doesn't even look brave, it looks like a man walking out on his family, and I'll have to let everyone think that, your mam and your grandad and you, I'll have to let you all think I'm just a bad man who left, because the truth is more dangerous than the lie. So think it if you have to, Bon. Think your dad was a wrong'un. It's safer. Just — somewhere underneath, if you can — keep a little door open. In case.*

*Keep a little door open. In case.*

I sat in my room in the middle of the night and I thought about the front step, and the empty corner, and the three years I sat watching it, and the door in the middle of me that I'd let close because *he went, that's all there is to it,* and I put my face in the puppy's neck — he'd woken up and come to me, the way he does, the way Scratch did, trying to lift the sad off me with his face — and I cried so hard and so quietly that I thought I might turn inside out, because I *had.* I had kept a little door open. For years. Long past when it made any sense. And I'd hated myself for it, thought it was weakness, thought it was me being pathetic, a little girl who couldn't take a hint.

It wasn't weakness. He'd *asked* me to. Across seven years and a sealed tin, he'd asked me to keep a door open, and some animal part of me, the same part that lived in the dog, had heard him, and obeyed.

And then I got to letter number six, and the writing changed — older, the biro different, the date a year later — and I understood that this was the one from the dawn. From the time he came back. And my hands went cold, because I knew, before I read it, that this was the day the dog never forgot.

But I'd been reading for two hours, and it was nearly three in the morning, and there's only so much of your own father you can take in one night after seven years of nothing.

So I'll tell you about the dawn letter, and the cassette, and what my dad's voice did to me when I finally heard it, in the next chapter. I had to stop. I had to lie down in the dark and hold the dog and let it all be true for a while before I could go on.

That's allowed, by the way. When the truth comes all at once after years of nothing, you're allowed to stop, and lie down, and let it be true a bit at a time. The dog taught me that too. Dogs only ever live one bit at a time. It's the thing they're wisest about.

## Chapter Twenty-Five — A Man's Voice in a Cold Van

It turns out that the hardest part of hearing your dad's voice for the first time in seven years is finding a machine that will play a cassette.

Nobody owns a cassette player. I want you to really sit with how completely the world has decided cassettes are over. We tried everywhere. Lola's house: nothing. My house: nothing, just the old radio that's only a radio. The library: the lady laughed, not unkindly, and said "ooh, you'd want a charity shop, pet," so we tried the three charity shops on Sea Road, and the British Heart Foundation one had a CD player and a record player and a thing for playing minidiscs, whatever they are, but no cassette player, and the man said they "come in and go straight out, the cassette ones, the old fellas buy them."

I was nearly crying with frustration — I had my dad's actual voice in my actual pocket and I could not get at it, which felt like a cruel little joke on top of everything — when Lola, eating a Twix she'd produced from somewhere, said, "What about Mick's van?"

And I stopped dead on the pavement.

Because she was right. Mick's van — Mick's ancient, diesel-smelling, held-together-with-hope white van, MICK'S MAN & VAN, NO JOB 2 SMALL — Mick's van was, as Lola put it, "basically 1994," and it had, in the dashboard, under the radio, a slot. A cassette slot. I'd seen it a hundred times and never once thought about it.

So that evening I asked Mick if I could sit in his van.

I didn't explain. I couldn't explain. I just stood in the kitchen, twelve years old, holding a cassette he couldn't see, and said, "Mick, can I sit in your van for a bit? On my own? I want to — I just want to be somewhere quiet," and I must have had a look on my face, because Mick, who is daft about almost everything, looked at me for a second with his slow kind eyes, and then he didn't ask a single question. He just reached into his pocket and put the van keys on the table and slid them across to me and said, "Aye, course, Bonneh. Heater works if you turn the key one click, don't start the engine but. Take your time. Nobody'll bother you out there." And he went back to the telly.

And I have thought about that a lot, since. Mick gave me his van keys and asked nothing. He's not my dad. He's never tried to be. But on the night I went to hear my dad's voice, the man who actually stayed handed me the keys and the warmth and the quiet and not one single question, and let me go. There's more than one way to be somebody's father. I didn't know that yet. I was starting to.

I sat in the cold van in the dark with Itch on my lap, and I turned the key one click, and the heater wheezed on, and the little orange dashboard lights came up, and I took the cassette — FOR BONNIE. PLAY WHEN YOU'RE SAD. love DAD — out of its case, and I pushed it into the slot.

There was a clunk. A hiss. The sound of tape, the old soft underwater hiss of magnetic tape, the sound of the past.

And then my dad spoke.

"*Is it on? Is the red light — right. Okay. Hello, Bonnie.*"

I can't describe what it did to me. I've tried. There aren't words for hearing a voice you'd forgotten you'd forgotten — a voice that turns out to have been living in you the whole time, under everything, in the basement of you, in the part the dog lived in too. It was a Geordie voice, soft, warm, a bit nervous, a young man's voice, with my grandad's gravel just starting at the bottom of it. It was the voice that said *howay and look what I've got.* It was the voice that named the joy the dog waited for. And the second it came out of the speaker, Itch — who'd been half-asleep on my lap — went rigid, and his head came up, and his ears went up, and he stared at the dashboard, at the speaker, with an intensity that broke my heart all over again, because he knew it. Of course he knew it. He'd been waiting at the gate for it every dawn of his short life.

"*So,*" said my dad's voice, in the cold van, six years late, "*your mam says you can't sleep without a story, and you've gone right off the one about the rabbit, so I'm going to tell you a new one, that I made up, just for you, and it's about a dog. Are you ready? Right.*"

And he told me a story. My dad told me a bedtime story, in the dark, in a van, when I was twelve and he was gone. It was a daft story, a made-up story, about a greyhound called the Fastest Dog in the World who was so fast he could run all the way round the whole earth in one night, faster than the dark, so that "*wherever you are, Bonnie, however far away, the Fastest Dog in the World can run to you before you've even finished being sad, and he'll always, always find you, because you're the thing he runs towards, you're his home, and a dog can always find his way home.*"

I had a puppy on my lap who had run all the way round death itself to find his way home.

I had to stop the tape. I sat in the van and shook and held the dog and the dog shook and stared at the speaker, the two of us, and then I made myself press play again, because there was more, because at the end of the story my dad's voice went quieter and closer, like he'd leaned in to the microphone, like he was tucking me in, and he said:

"*Night night, Bonnie. I love you the whole world. Howay now — eyes shut.*"

*Howay now.* Two notes. The lift at the end.

And then, last of all, after a little pause, after a breath — my dad whistled. Two notes. Low, then high. *Fweet-FWEET.* The whistle. The exact whistle. The one my mouth had found by accident, the one that turned Itch inside out with joy. It was on the tape. It had always been on the tape. It was how my dad called the dog in at night, the last thing, every night — *fweet-FWEET,* and the dog would come trotting in from the yard and curl up by my bed, and my dad would whistle the house to sleep, the little girl and the rescued dog, both of them, every night, *fweet-FWEET, night night, eyes shut.*

And when the whistle came out of the speaker, Itch threw back his head, and he howled.

Not a sad howl. I thought it would be sad. It wasn't. It was the most joyful sound I have ever heard an animal make — a long, rising, singing howl, his whole body in it, his nose to the roof of the van, answering the whistle the way he'd been born wanting to answer it, the way the dog inside him had answered it every night for years and then waited his whole life to answer it again — and I put my arms around my howling dog in my mum's boyfriend's cold van, and I howled too, the pair of us, singing back to a dead man's whistle in the dark, and out in the lit kitchen window of number 9 I saw Mick get up and come to the glass and look out at the van, worried, and then — and I love him for this, I will love him for this forever — and then *not* come out. He stood at the window and watched over us and let us howl. He knew it wasn't his to come into. Daft, kind, watching Mick, guarding two howling creatures in his van and asking nothing.

That's the cassette. I've got it still. I've had it put onto my phone now, properly, so it can't ever be lost or chewed or wear out — but I keep the actual tape in the actual tin, with the photograph, where my dad put it, because some things you should keep in the form the love came in.

I played the rest of the tape eventually. And I read the dawn letter, letter number six, that same night, by the orange dashboard light, with my dad's whistle still ringing in the van. And that's how I found out what happened the morning the dog never forgot — and why my dad came all the way back, to the very gate, and then didn't knock.

It wasn't that he didn't want us.

It was that somebody told him not to.

And I knew exactly who.

## Chapter Twenty-Six — What Grandad Knew

The dawn letter was short. He'd written it after, you could tell — written it sitting somewhere, in a café or a car, with the thing just done and his whole life just decided, the biro pressed hard, some of the words gone wobbly.

*Dear Bonnie,*

*I came back. I want you to know that. Whatever else, I want you to know your dad came back. I got it clean — took two years and it nearly killed me but I paid the man off, every penny, I got free, and the first thing, the only thing I wanted, the thing that kept me going the whole time, was to come home to you and your mam and try to be let back in.*

*I came at dawn because I was a coward, I'll be honest, I wanted to see the house before I had to face anybody, I sat in the lane in the dark working up to it. And the dog found me. Scratch. He got out somehow, the back gate, and he found me in the lane and Bonnie I have never been so glad of anything in my whole life, he went mad, he cried, he climbed right up me, he remembered me after two years, two years and he remembered me, and I thought, if the dog'll have me back like this then maybe — maybe —*

*And then your grandad came up the lane. Off to his lottie, first light, the way he does. And he saw me.*

And then my dad wrote down what Grandad said. And I'm not going to put all of it here, because it's between them, the two of them, father and son in a cold lane at dawn with a crying dog between them, but the shape of it was this: that Grandad was glad — Grandad cried, my dad wrote, *your grandad cried, Bonnie, I've only seen him cry twice and that was one* — but that Grandad was also frightened, and protective, and he told Joe the truth as he saw it, which was that Donna had spent two years putting herself and her little girl back together with her bare hands, that they were finally steady, finally healing, and that if Joe came back now — if he knocked on that door and turned everything upside down again, and then it didn't work, and then he left *again,* because what if he left again — it might break what couldn't be mended twice. *Let them heal, son,* Grandad said. *You've done the hard brave stupid thing and got yourself clean and I'm proud of you and I'll never say different. But the bravest thing now might be to let them be. A clean break heals. It's the breaking and breaking again that cripples a bairn.*

And my dad — who always, always did what the strongest feeling told him, and whose strongest feeling that morning was shame, and love, and the terror of hurting us twice — my dad believed him. Or let himself believe him, because it was the path that asked the least of his courage, and he knew that about himself, he wrote it right there, *I think I let your grandad tell me to go because I was too much of a coward to risk you a second time, and I've dressed it up as love every day since, and maybe it was both, maybe a thing can be cowardice and love at once, I don't know, Bonnie, I'm not a clever man.*

So he buried the tin. He'd brought it ready — the letters, the photo, the tape, his three best things — half hoping he'd never need to bury it, half knowing he would. He took the dog up to the allotment with him, in the grey dawn, and he buried the tin under the apple tree he'd climbed as a boy, *so that one day, when you're grown, when it can't hurt anybody anymore, you'll know the truth. I'll tell your grandad it's there. He'll give it to you one day. When it's safe. When you're ready.*

And then he gave the dog one last cuddle, and whistled him — *fweet-FWEET* — and sent him home down the lane, and the dog went, looking back, looking back, looking back, and my dad watched his daughter's dog trot away into the waking town, and then he turned, and he walked out of the gap at the end of the lane, and he didn't come back again.

Grandad never gave me the tin.

Six years. Grandad knew it was there — *under the apple tree,* my dad had told him, *give it to her when she's ready* — and Grandad never gave it to me. Grandad watched me grow up not-forgetting a dad I'd been told was just a bad man who left, watched me sit on the step and watch the corner, watched me close the door in the middle of myself, and he never dug up the tin, never said the words, never gave me the truth that was sitting in the ground a hundred yards from where we buried my dog.

I went up to the allotment the next day to ask him why.

I want to tell you I was angry. I was, a bit. But mostly, walking up Sleekburn Bank with Itch hauling on the lead ahead of me, straight for the allotment, straight for Grandad, I was something else, something I didn't have a word for, and I think the word might just be *old.* I felt very old. I felt like I'd aged about thirty years in two days. I felt like I finally understood that the grown-ups in my life weren't keeping the truth from me because they were villains. They were keeping it because they were frightened, and ashamed, and trying, in their clumsy broken human way, to protect a child from a thing they thought would hurt her more than the silence did.

They were wrong. The silence hurt more. It nearly always does. But they weren't villains. They were just people, doing their best in the dark, getting it wrong out of love. Which is, I've decided, what most people are, most of the time. It's the most important thing I learned that whole summer, and I learned it walking up a hill behind a dog.

Grandad was in the shed. He had the gas heater on and the WORLD'S OKAYEST GRANDAD mug in his hand, and when Itch and I came in he looked up, and he saw my face, and he saw — I had it with me — he saw the photograph in my hand, the Polaroid, Dad and me and the dog on the step.

And my grandad, my four-time Leek Show champion grandad who never shows a thing, went grey. He put the mug down very carefully, like it had got heavy, and he looked at the photograph in my hand and then at the puppy at my feet — the puppy who was watching him, ears up, with Scratch's exact eyes — and his face just... came apart. Slowly. Like the slab over a grave when the ground shifts.

"You found it," he said. His voice was a wreck. "Under the tree. He told me under the tree. How did you — " and then he looked at Itch again, at the dog, at the *dog,* and something moved behind his eyes, some impossible sum finally coming out, and he said, in a whisper, "...the dog took you to it. Didn't he. Somehow. The dog knew." He sat down heavily on the upturned bucket. "I always said there was too much of your dad in that animal. I never knew how right I—" He stopped. He put his big carrot hands over his face, the way he had at the funeral. And my grandad said, from behind his hands, muffled and broken, the thing he'd been carrying for six years:

"I sent him away, Bonnie. Your dad. He came home and he was clean and he was crying and that dog was climbing up him, and I sent him away. I thought I was protecting you. I thought — I thought he'd only break you again. And I have lain awake every night for six years not knowing if I did the kindest thing a father ever did or the cruellest, and I have never, ever been able to tell." He took his hands down. His eyes were streaming. "Can you ever forgive a daft old man who loved you too much and your dad not enough, all at the same minute, in a cold lane, with the worst decision of his life and about four seconds to make it in?"

And the puppy — Itch, my Itch, my dad's last gift wearing my dead dog's face — got up, and crossed the shed, and leaned his whole growing weight against my crying grandad's legs.

The lean. *I'm here and you're here.*

And I understood that the dog had just answered the question for both of us, in the only language he had, the language my dad and my grandad and the dog all spoke and I was finally learning: the language of *staying.* Of leaning in. Of being here, now, one bit at a time, when it would be so much easier to go.

So I forgave him. Of course I forgave him. We're Maguires. We forgive like it's going out of fashion.

We get it from the dog.

## Chapter Twenty-Seven — What Mum Knew, and What She Didn't

I gave Mum the tin that night.

I'd been dreading it more than anything — more than Grandad, more, in a way, than the break-in. Because Mum was the one who'd held us together. Mum was the one who'd done the mornings and the evenings and the careful sums, who'd never once let me go without, who'd done her crying where I couldn't see. And I was about to drop a six-year-old tin into the middle of all that careful holding-together, and I didn't know what it would do to her.

I waited till Mick had gone home and the house was quiet, and I came down and I put the tin on the kitchen table in front of her, and I said, "Mum. I need you to look at something. And I need you to not be cross about how I got it, not tonight, because it's about Dad, and I think you've been protecting me from stuff, and I think it's time we stopped protecting each other so hard, because it's making us strangers."

She went very still. She looked at the tin — the rusty Christmas tin — and then at me, and something in her face I'd never seen, a kind of bracing, the way you brace before a wave.

"Where did you get that," she said quietly.

"Grandad's allotment. It was buried. Dad buried it." I sat down across from her. "Mum. Dad didn't just leave because he was a bad man. He left because he'd lost his job and he got into gambling and he owed money to a — to a bad man, and he was scared they'd come to the house, to us, and he thought the only way to keep us safe was to take the trouble away with him. It's all in here. He wrote it all down. For me. To find one day."

Mum's face had gone white and then crumpled and then fought its way back to still. "I knew about the gambling," she said. Her voice was flat and careful, the voice she uses for the things that cost the most. "Not all of it. Enough. I knew there was money gone and lies told and that he'd got us in trouble he was too ashamed to name. That part I always knew, Bonnie, and I never told you, and d'you want to know why? Because you worshipped him. You were five and you thought the sun shone out of him, and I was not going to be the one to tell my little girl that her hero gambled the leccy money and brought men to our door. I'd rather you thought he just *left.* I'd rather be the one you were angry at than make you carry that. So I said *he went, that's all there is to it,* and I let you be angry at a ghost, because angry's easier to carry than ashamed." She wiped her eyes, fierce, the way I do. "So if you've come to tell me I kept things from you — aye. I did. Guilty. I kept the ugly bits of your dad so you could have the rest. That's the only thing I've got to say sorry for and I'm not even all the way sorry, because you were *five.*"

"I know," I said. And I did know, now. "I'm not cross about that, Mum. I get it. But — " and here it was, the part that was going to hurt her, the part even she didn't know — "Mum, there's a letter in here from later. From a year after. He came back."

She didn't move. "What?"

"He got clean. It took him two years but he paid it all off and got free, and he came back to ask if he could come home. He came to the lane at dawn. And — " my voice wanted to break and I made it hold, because she needed to hear this clean — "and Grandad found him. And Grandad told him to go. Told him you and me were finally healing and he'd only break us again. And Dad believed him, and he buried this tin so I'd know one day, and he left, and that was the last time. Mum, he *came back.* And Grandad sent him away. And never told you. Either of you. For six years."

I have seen my mum tired and I have seen my mum sad and I have seen my mum cry at a dog's grave. I had never, until that moment, seen my mum *break.* It went through her like a current. She made a sound and put both hands flat on the table like the floor had tilted, and she said, "He came *back?*" in a voice that wasn't really a question, that was just the past seven years rearranging themselves all at once behind her eyes — every story she'd told herself, every *he never even looked back,* every night she'd lain awake building him into a man who didn't care, and all of it suddenly, horribly wrong. "He came back and your grandad — *Wattie* — Wattie *sent him* — and let me think — for six *years* he let me think — "

And then my mum did something that frightened me and then didn't. She laughed. A terrible, wet, broken laugh, with her hand over her mouth. "Oh, the *daft* old — oh, the *state* of us," she said, through the laughing and the crying, which had become the same thing. "The state of this *family,* Bonnie. All of us. Lying to each other out of *love.* Him to me, me to you, your grandad to the both of us, your dad to all of us, everybody so busy protecting everybody that nobody knows a single true thing about anybody, and the only one telling the truth this whole time — the only honest one in the whole rotten lot of us — " and she looked down, and Itch was sitting at her feet looking up at her, ears at half-mast, and her face just dissolved — "was the *dog.*"

And she got down on the kitchen floor, my mum, in her work fleece, at midnight, and she gathered up the puppy who had Scratch's face, and she held him and cried into him, and I got down there too, and we held each other and the dog all together on the lino, the two of us, the last two Maguires in the house, finally — finally — telling each other true things.

I didn't tell her the truest thing of all. Not that night. I didn't tell her where Itch had really come from, that he wasn't a foster pup, that he was Scratch made again in a lab by a machine I'd switched on, that he carried Scratch's memories, that *that* was how I'd found the tin. I couldn't. It was too much, on top of everything; it would have sounded mad, or like a cruelty, like I was promising her the dog could come back too. So I held that one back, one last secret, the biggest, and I told myself it was to protect her, and I knew, even as I told myself that, that I was doing the exact thing my whole family does, the thing that had just cost us six years.

The truth has a way of not waiting for you to be ready, though.

Three days later it came up our path in a good grey suit, carrying a briefcase, and it took the choice clean out of my hands.

## Chapter Twenty-Eight — The Man with the Briefcase

His name was Mr Sole.

I know — I thought it was a joke too, the first time he said it, standing on our front step in a suit that cost more than Mum made in a month, holding out a card that said **DAMIAN SOLE, DIRECTOR OF COMMERCIAL DEVELOPMENT, LAZARUS BIOSCIENCES LTD.** "Like the fish," I said, because I couldn't help it, because when I'm frightened my mouth gets ahead of me, and Mr Sole smiled a smile that didn't go anywhere near his eyes and said, "Like the only part of the foot that touches the ground, actually. The part that takes all the weight and never complains. May I come in?"

He could not come in. Mum was at work; it was the summer holidays; it was meant to be just me and Itch and a quiet Tuesday. But Lola was round, thank God, because Lola is always round, and we stood in the doorway, two twelve-year-olds and a half-grown greyhound puppy, and we did not let the man with the briefcase past the step.

"I'll come to the point," said Mr Sole, when it was clear the step was as far as he was getting. "Because I think you're a clever girl, Bonnie — it is Bonnie, isn't it? — and clever girls prefer the point." He glanced down at Itch, and something moved behind his eyes, something hungry and careful, the look of a man pricing a thing in a shop window. "That is a very unusual dog. We believe — my company believes — that he may have been produced using equipment, processes, and biological material belonging to Lazarus Biosciences. Without authorisation. Which would make him, legally and in every other way that matters, *ours.* An asset. The single most valuable laboratory animal currently alive on the planet, if our suspicions are correct." The dead smile again. "We'd like him back."

The bottom dropped out of me. *He knows.* Of course he knew. The machine had a log. Dr Lillico had warned me, hadn't she, weeks ago — *there are people at the lab asking questions I can't answer.* And now the questions had a face and a briefcase and a card with a fish's name on it, standing on my front step, calling my dog an *asset.*

"He's not a dog to you," I heard myself say. My voice was shaking but the words came out clear. "Is he. He's not even a dog. He's a — he's proof. That's what you want. You want to prove the memory thing worked so you can sell it. 'Bring them back. All of them. Even the part that matters.' You want to take him to pieces to find out how much of the real Scratch is in there, so you can put it in your machine and sell it to every sad person with forty thousand quid." I was crying now, angry crying, but I didn't stop. "He's not an asset. He's not proof. He's *mine.* He's my dog. He's the last thing my dad ever gave me, twice over, and you are not having him."

Mr Sole looked at me for a long moment, and I'll give him this, just this: something flickered. Some last guttering bit of a person, way down under the suit, that heard a child say *the last thing my dad ever gave me* and felt it, just for a second, before the commercial development director switched the light back off.

"That's a very moving speech," he said quietly. "I mean that. We'll be using something like it in the advertising, actually — that's exactly the feeling we're selling. Grief. The refusal to let go. You've just described our entire business plan, Bonnie, very beautifully, and I thank you." He clicked the briefcase open and took out a thick envelope and held it out. "Here is a letter from our lawyers. It explains our legal position regarding the animal, which is extremely strong, and it makes your mother an offer — a generous one, a life-changing one, frankly, for a family in your circumstances — to surrender him quietly and sign something that says none of this ever happened. Talk to her. Talk to your scientist friend, if she's still talking to anyone after this week." He snapped the case shut. "We have a launch in nine days. A very important launch, with very important people, where Lazarus tells the world it can afford to bring back everyone's beloved pet. I would so much rather have the dog by then than a fuss. Fusses are bad for launches." He turned to go, then paused, and looked back, and for one second the smile was gone and there was just the cold flat thing underneath. "Nine days, Bonnie. Don't make us come and take him. Nobody wants that. Least of all him."

And he walked back down our path and got into a car that purred where Mick's van rattles, and he was gone.

Lola and I stood on the step. Itch leaned against my legs, hard, the lean, *I'm here.* The envelope sat in my hand like a brick.

"Bonnie," said Lola, very quietly, and for the first time in the whole entire story, in seven years of knowing her, Lola Okafor sounded frightened. "What do we do?"

I looked down at my dog — my impossible dog, the most valuable animal on the planet, the last thing my dad ever gave me, twice — and I felt the furnace in my chest go cold and clear and steady, the way it had on a green towel a whole lifetime ago.

"We're going to need everybody," I said. "Mum. Grandad. Mick. Bisi. Dr Lillico. All of them. No more secrets. The secrets are how they win." I closed my hand around the envelope. "He wants no fuss before his launch. Fine. Then what we're going to give him, Lola, is the biggest fuss this town has ever seen."

# PART FOUR: BRINGING HIM HOME

## Chapter Twenty-Nine — No More Secrets

I told Mum everything that night. All of it. The break-in, the note, the button, the machine, Dr Lillico, the samples I'd let her take off Scratch's body on the worst morning of our lives, the surrogate, the cassette of sea slugs and RNA and the deepest groove — all of it, the whole mad true story, sitting at the kitchen table with the lawyers' envelope between us and the puppy asleep on my feet.

I thought she'd be furious. I thought she'd cry, or shout, or send me to my room until I was forty. I'd braced for all of it.

What she actually did was go very quiet, and very still, for a long, long time, while I talked. And when I finished — when I got to the part about *the most valuable animal on the planet* and *don't make us come and take him* — Mum didn't look at the envelope, and she didn't look at me. She looked at the dog. At Itch, asleep on my feet, in the patch of streetlight that comes through the kitchen at night.

And she said, in a whisper, "So when you said he licked you on the step. And did the lean." She swallowed. "That wasn't a new dog being friendly. That was — that was *him.* Remembering you. Scratch. Coming back to you."

"Some of him," I said. "The strongest bits. Mum, that's how I found the tin. Itch took me to it. He remembered where Dad buried it because Scratch was *there.* Scratch is — bits of Scratch are in there, in his head, and they led me to Dad. The dog brought Dad back to us. The dog you've been grieving. He's not all the way gone, Mum. He never finished going."

And my mum, Donna Maguire, fifty-one kilos of pure stubbornness who does her crying where no one can see — my mum got up from the table and came round and knelt down on the kitchen floor and very, very gently, so as not to wake him, put her hand on the sleeping puppy's side. On the rise and fall of him. The way Grandad always did. Feeling him breathe.

"Scratch," she whispered to the dog. Testing it. The name we hadn't been able to say.

And Itch's ear flickered in his sleep. And his tail went *thump,* once, on the lino. And he sighed the enormous sigh of a dog who has reached his favourite place in the entire world.

Mum put her face down against him and stayed there a long time. And when she lifted it up she wasn't grieving anymore, exactly. She was something fiercer. She looked at the lawyers' envelope on the table like it was a slug she'd found in the salad, and she stood up, and she wiped her face, and she said the most Mum thing she has ever said, the thing I will have carved on something one day:

"Right. Well. They're not having him. I have buried that dog *once,* Bonnie, and I'll not have some man in a suit dig him back up to take him to *bits* in a *laboratory* for *money.*" She picked up the envelope, walked to the bin, pedal-bin lid up, and — then stopped, and put it back down, and almost smiled, a hard little smile. "No. We keep it. We keep everything now. Evidence." She looked at me, and there was something in her face I'd been waiting my whole life to see her turn on the world instead of just enduring it. "You said we need everybody. Ring them. Ring all of them. Get them here tomorrow. If that company wants a fuss, they've just picked one with the wrong family."

We had the meeting the next night, in our front room, and I want you to picture it, because it's my favourite scene in the whole story after the beach, and because it's the moment a girl with a secret became a town with a plan.

There was me and Mum and Lola. There was Grandad, who'd come down off the allotment in his good cap, who couldn't stop staring at Itch with that come-out-at-last sum on his face, who at one point reached down and touched the dog's head and said, thickly, "Bit of our Joe in there, then. After all," and had to go and stand by the window for a minute. There was Mick, vast and quiet on the arm of the settee, who when he understood the whole story said only, "So the company's bullying a bairn over a dog," and went a colour I'd never seen him go, a slow dangerous red, Mick who I'd thought was too daft to notice anything. There was Bisi, Lola's mum, calm as still water, who'd worked most of it out already ("a greyhound pup with no microchip, no breeder, no papers, the spit of your Scratch, turning up in a box — Bonnie, love, I'm a veterinary nurse, I'm not *daft,* I just decided some questions weren't mine to ask").

And there was Dr Petra Lillico.

She arrived last, in the dented green car, and she looked terrible — grey, hollow, like she hadn't slept in a week — and she stood in our doorway and looked round at all of us, this whole crowded front room full of people she'd been hiding from for months, and she said, "They sacked me on Monday. Walked me out of the building with my things in a box. Twenty years." She gave a wrecked little laugh. "Sole found the log. He's known for weeks. He let me twist." She looked at me, and at the dog, and her tea-coloured eyes filled. "I'm so sorry, Bonnie. I've brought all this down on you. I should never have — "

"Sit down, pet," said my mum, who had never met her, in the voice she uses for the old people on her rounds, the voice that has gentled a thousand frightened mornings. "You brought my daughter's dog back to her, and you brought her dad back an' all, by accident, which is the only good accident in this whole business. Sit *down.* Mick'll get you a tea. And then you're going to tell us everything you know about that company, because I can see it in your face that you know something, and we're going to use it to make them leave my family alone."

And Dr Lillico sat down. And she got her tea. And she told us how to win.

## Chapter Thirty — The Thing in the Freezer Nobody Was Meant to See

Here is what Dr Lillico knew, and it was the key to everything.

"They sell a miracle," she said, sitting in our front room with the tea going cold in her hands. "'Bring them back.' Forty thousand pounds and you get your dog, your cat, your soulmate, returned to you good as new. That's the advert. That's the launch. That's what all the important people are flying in to invest in next week — *affordable* resurrection, my machine, the Ark, cloning made cheap enough to sell to anyone with a broken heart and a credit card." She paused. "And here is what they do not put in the advert. Cloning is brutal, and it fails, and it fails *most* of the time. To get one healthy puppy that looks like your dead friend, you start dozens of pregnancies. Surrogate dogs — living, breathing dogs, like Maggie, who carried Itch — kept pregnant over and over, miscarrying, having Caesareans, used up. Puppies born wrong — too big, hearts not finished, things I won't describe in front of you, that don't live, that they 'discontinue.' For every miracle on the advert there's a freezer full of the ones that didn't work, and a kennel full of worn-out mothers, and Lazarus has spent a great deal of money making very sure that the people writing the cheques never, ever see inside that freezer or that kennel." She set the tea down. Her hand was shaking. "I kept records. Quietly. For years. Photographs. Numbers. The real success rate, not the one in the brochure. I told myself I was keeping it to fix it from the inside one day. The truth is I was keeping it because I'm a coward who couldn't bring herself to either blow the whistle or look away, so I just — collected the evidence of my own complicity, and hated myself, and carried on." She looked up, and there was something new in her face, something that had decided. "But I've nothing left to lose now, have I. They've taken the job. So they can't take *that* from me anymore. The fear of losing everything is the only thing they ever had."

The room was very quiet. Itch had gone and put his head on Dr Lillico's knee, the way he does, the way Scratch did, lifting the sad off her with his face. She stroked his ear without seeming to know she was doing it.

"So here's the shape of it," said Lola, who had been scribbling in the dog notebook the whole time, because Lola thinks with a pen. She looked up, and the buying-the-room look was back, and it had never been fiercer. "Sole wants two things. He wants Itch — because if the memory thing *worked,* Itch is worth a billion pounds, the holy grail, proof you can bring back not just the body but the actual *mind.* And he wants *no fuss before the launch,* because the launch is where all the money comes from." She tapped the pen. "But those two things fight each other. Because the only way to *prove* Itch is special is to admit how he was made — by breaking the rules, with no permission, illegally, on a kid's pet, with a secret experiment the company's own ethics people never approved. The second they reach for Itch in public, the whole illegal mess comes out. And *we've* got the other thing — " she nodded at Dr Lillico " — the freezer. The mothers. The real numbers. The ugly truth behind 'bring them back.'"

She put the pen down.

"So we don't hide from the launch," said Lola Okafor. "We *go* to the launch. And we make Mr Sole a very simple offer, in front of exactly the people he can't afford to have hear it. He leaves Itch alone — forever, in writing — and he stands up and tells the world that the dog is nothing special, just an ordinary pet, no memories, no miracle, a failed experiment that came to nothing. He *disclaims* him. And in return, the freezer stays shut, and the illegal experiment on the little girl's greyhound stays a secret, and his precious launch goes ahead. Or — " and she smiled, and it was a smile with a town behind it — "or we open the freezer. On launch day. In front of the cameras and the investors and the whole world. And Lazarus Biosciences becomes the company that tortures mother dogs and steals children's pets, on the single most important morning of its life."

Nobody said anything for a moment.

Then Mick, from the arm of the settee, said slowly, "So we make it so the only way he gets to keep his money... is to give up the dog and say the dog's worthless." He nodded, working it through, his big honest face creasing up. "We make his greed and his lying point the *other* way. At protecting Bonnie instead of robbing her."

"Yes," said Dr Lillico, staring at Mick like he'd just solved a problem she'd been stuck on for twenty years. "Yes. Exactly that. We don't beat his self-interest. We *turn it round.*"

"And how," said my mum, practical as ever, the only one not carried away, "do a scientist with no job, a care worker, a man with a van, two bairns and a dog actually *get into* a launch full of millionaires and reporters, pet? Because I can't see them letting us in the front door."

And there was a silence.

And then Grandad — my grandad, who'd said almost nothing all night, who'd been standing by the window with his back to us looking out at the dark, carrying his six years of guilt and his bit of our Joe in the dog — my grandad turned round.

"The launch is at the Helix," he said. "Aye? That glass building, down the river. I read it in the envelope." He almost smiled, and for a second he looked exactly like the boy with the marrow. "I've been on the committee of the Kelsey Bay and District Leek Show for thirty-one year, and I'm telling you now, there is nothing on God's earth — no university, no laboratory, no man in a suit — that has a back door an old allotment lad and a man with a van can't find his way through, if there's a dog that needs saving on the other side of it." He put his good cap back on. "Your dad used to get us into the dogs at Brough Park for nothing every Saturday of his life, Bonnie. Through the gap by the bins. He had a gift for it." His voice went thick again, but he was smiling now, properly. "Reckon it's about time the gift came back round."

And that's how it came to be that on the morning of the most important launch in the history of Lazarus Biosciences Ltd, a scientist, a care worker, a man with a van, a vet nurse, an old man in his good cap, two twelve-year-old girls, and the most valuable animal on the planet, were all hiding in a delivery bay off Pottery Lane, behind a fire door held very slightly open by a folded-up beer mat.

Some things, it turns out, you can rely on.

## Chapter Thirty-One — The Helix, in Daylight, with Everyone Watching

The second time I broke into the Helix building it was ten o'clock in the morning, and I had my mum with me, and a vet nurse, and an old man in his good cap, and a sacked scientist, and a man named after a fish's offer in a briefcase to outwit, and it was, somehow, more terrifying than the first time, not less, because the first time if it all went wrong I'd only lose myself, and this time I could lose everyone.

The beer mat was a different beer mat — six months on, somebody else's tab break, the same eternal human carelessness — but it did the same job, and the goods door opened, and we went up the bare grey stairwell, all of us, the whole daft brave crowd of us, Itch carried in Mick's arms so his claws wouldn't tick on the concrete, and somewhere above us, through the building, we could hear it: a great warm roar of money and importance, the sound of three hundred people in a glass atrium being told that death was now optional.

The launch was in the big atrium on the ground floor, the one with the two DNA ramps spiralling up out of it, and Dr Lillico had drawn us a map on the back of an envelope, and the plan had three parts and about ninety things that could go wrong.

Part one was Lola and Bisi, who peeled off on the second floor towards the AV room — the little booth where they run the screens and the sound, because Lola, who is not frightened of anything and who had spent two days watching online tutorials, was going to get us control of the big screens, and Bisi was going with her because, as Bisi put it, "I'm not having my twelve-year-old commit her first proper crime without her mother present, it's just not how we do things." (Bisi and my mum had become, over the planning week, a force of nature I would not wish on any enemy. They'd taken to finishing each other's sentences. Sole never stood a chance, honestly, the moment those two joined up.)

Part two was Dr Lillico, who was going to walk out into that atrium in front of three hundred people who all knew her, because the one thing Lazarus could not do, the one thing that would look worst of all, was throw out their own celebrated, recently and quietly sacked, lead scientist in front of the world's press.

And part three was me. And Itch. And the truth.

I stood in the stairwell doorway and looked out at it: the glittering crowd, the champagne at ten in the morning, the enormous screens, the press pen with its cameras, and the stage, white and lit, with the Ark on it — *my* machine, the one I'd switched on by accident in the dark, gleaming now under spotlights like a holy relic — and behind a lectern, in a suit that probably had its own postcode, mid-flow, golden, terrible, was Mr Damian Sole.

" — because grief," Sole was saying, in a voice like warm honey poured over a knife, "grief is the one thing every single human being on earth has in common. We have all lost someone. We have all stood at a graveside and thought: *if only.* If only I could have one more day. One more walk. One more time feeling them rest their head on my knee." A picture came up on the screens behind him: a golden retriever, soft-focus, gazing. The crowd made a soft *aww.* "Ladies and gentlemen, investors, friends — Lazarus Biosciences is here to turn *if only* into *once more.* We are here to announce that, as of today, with the Ark system you see behind me, the cost of bringing home your beloved companion will fall from forty thousand pounds — to under four." The crowd gasped, applauded. "We are going to make love affordable. We are going to make *goodbye* — optional." He spread his arms. "And I have one more thing to show you today. Something that, until this morning, I wasn't sure we'd be able to share. Something that proves we can bring back not just the *body* of your friend — but, perhaps, *more.* Something we've been calling — internally — Project Lazarus *Plus.*"

And the screens changed.

And I felt Mick go rigid beside me, and Mum grab my arm, because there, on the giant screens, in front of three hundred people and the world's press, was a photograph of a brindle greyhound puppy with one white toe and an anxious hopeful face.

Itch.

"This," said Sole, and his voice had gone soft and triumphant, "is a dog who should not be possible. A clone — yes — but a clone who, our data suggests, *remembers* a life he never lived. Who knows things he was never taught. Who carries within him the actual memories of the animal he was copied from." He smiled out at the crowd, and I knew, with a sick lurch, that he'd never meant to make us a quiet offer at all — that the briefcase and the nine days had been a bluff, a way to keep us still, while all along he'd planned *this,* to unveil Itch to the world as his miracle whether we agreed or not. "We are still — ah — *finalising acquisition* of the animal itself. But I can tell you that when we do, Lazarus will not merely sell you back your pet's body. We will sell you back their *soul.*"

The crowd went up like a firework. Applause, cheering, a forest of phones. And up on the stage, golden and glowing, Mr Sole basked in it, the man who was going to take my dog to bits and sell the pieces, and I felt the furnace in my chest go past cold, past clear, all the way to something I'd never felt before, something white.

I looked at Mum. Mum looked at me. And my mum, my careful, stubborn, never-makes-a-fuss mum, lifted her chin and said the second thing I'll have carved on something one day:

"Well, go on then, Bonnie. He's *your* dog. Go and tell them whose he is."

And I picked up Itch out of Mick's arms — he was heavy now, half-grown, all legs, my dad's gift and my dead dog's recipe and my own whole heart — and I held him against my chest, and I felt him lean into me, *I'm here and you're here,* and I walked out of the stairwell, past a security guard too astonished to stop a child with a dog, out into the glittering atrium, towards the stage, towards the man named after the only part of a foot that touches the ground.

And I'd got about halfway, with the crowd just starting to turn and murmur and point — *is that — there's a child — is that the dog?* — when two things happened at once.

The first was that every enormous screen in the atrium went black, and then lit up again — and Lola had done it, Lola Okafor had actually done it — with something that was not a soft-focus golden retriever.

It was the inside of a freezer.

The second was that Mr Sole stopped smiling, and leaned down to the man beside the stage, the one with the earpiece, and pointed — straight at me, at the dog in my arms — and said something I couldn't hear but could read on his suddenly ugly face, three words, very clear:

"*Get the dog.*"

## Chapter Thirty-Two — The Biggest Fuss This Town Has Ever Seen

What happened next took about four minutes, and I've watched it back since — all of it got filmed, every second, that's rather the point — so I can tell you exactly, even the bits I was too frightened to take in at the time.

The screens showed the freezer. Then they showed the next photograph, and the next — Dr Lillico's photographs, the ones she'd been collecting for years and hating herself for keeping, the real cost of *bring them back,* the part that never makes the advert. I won't describe them. The three hundred people in that atrium didn't need them described either; you could hear it go through the crowd like a cold wind, the *aww* curdling into something else, the champagne lowering, the phones turning from the stage to the screens. And a voice came over the speakers — Bisi's voice, steady as still water, reading the numbers Lillico had given her, the real success rate, the worn-out mothers, the ones they "discontinued" — while Lola kept the pictures coming.

And Mr Sole shouted, "Turn that *off* — that's — that footage is *stolen,* that's a *security breach* — TURN IT OFF — " and the launch began, very fast, to come apart.

And in the middle of it, a man with an earpiece came for my dog.

He was big and he was quick and he came through the confused crowd straight at me, and I turned, holding Itch, and there was nowhere to go, and I thought *no, no, not after everything, not when we're so close,* and the man's hand closed on Itch's collar —

— and several things made of love arrived all at once.

Mick arrived. Big, daft, gentle Mick, who does removals and house clearances, who can carry a wardrobe up three flights on his own, simply put himself between the man and the dog, a wall of WD-40 and Polo mints, and said, "I wouldn't, marra," in a voice I'd never heard him use, and the man bounced off him like a bird off a window. Grandad arrived, swinging his good cap, shouting something about Brough Park that didn't entirely make sense but was clearly very rude. Mum arrived, and Mum — I have never been prouder — Mum got right up in the face of a security guard twice her size and said, "That is a *child* and her *dog* and if you lay one finger on either of them in front of all these cameras I will end this company's *entire morning,*" and the guard, who had a mother of his own somewhere, took a step back.

And then Dr Petra Lillico walked out into the middle of the atrium, in her good jumper with the hole in the elbow that she hadn't mended for the occasion, and the crowd recognised her — *that's Lillico, that's the lead scientist, the one who built the Ark* — and a hush spread out from her like ripples, and she said, not shouting, not needing to, in the clear carrying voice she'd used to teach sixty children what a clone was:

"My name is Doctor Petra Lillico. I built that machine. I ran the cloning programme here for six years. Everything on those screens is real, and I have a great deal more of it, and I will give every last page of it to every reporter in this room — " she nodded at the press pen, where the cameras had swung round and were drinking her in — "unless this company does three things, right now, in front of all of you, so that it can never quietly un-do them later."

The atrium had gone completely silent. Even Sole had stopped shouting. He stood at his lectern, golden no longer, grey now, the colour of a man watching the single most important morning of his life turn to ash, and doing the only thing men like Sole ever really do, which is sums. Fast, desperate sums, about what costs him least.

"One," said Dr Lillico. "Lazarus Biosciences will stop the Ark programme until it can be done without that — " she pointed at the screens — "without the freezer. Independent inspectors. Real numbers. Or no programme at all."

"Two. This company will state, publicly, now, on the record, that it has *no claim whatsoever* on a certain greyhound puppy currently in the arms of a young lady over there — that he is an ordinary pet, of no scientific value, that any suggestion otherwise was — " and here she looked straight at Sole, and her voice could have cut glass — "*marketing.* A made-up story. A lie told to sell a product. Say it, Damian. Tell them the dog is nothing special. Tell the whole world there's no miracle in that animal at all." 

And I understood, then, the whole terrible cleverness of it, the trap with no way out, and I almost felt sorry for him. Almost. Because Lola had been exactly right, weeks before, in our front room with her pen: the only way Sole could prove Itch was a miracle was to admit the crime that made him. And the only way to make the scandal go away was to swear, in front of the world, that there was no miracle at all. He could have the dog, or he could have his company. He could not have both. We'd built him a door that only opened one way, and the way it opened was *us.*

"And three," said Dr Lillico, and her voice changed, went quieter, went down to the bottom of everything, and she looked round the whole silent glittering ruined atrium, at the investors and the reporters and the three hundred people who'd come to be sold the end of goodbye. "Three. I'd like everyone here to listen to something. It's short. It was written by a twelve-year-old girl, at one o'clock in the morning, beside a dog she loved who was dying, and it is the truest thing about grief I have ever read, and it is the exact opposite of everything this company tried to sell you this morning. Because Lazarus told you that you can buy your way out of losing things. And you can't. You never could. But you can do the other thing. The thing this girl did. You can love something so much that a little bit of it — the realest little bit — finds its way home anyway, on its own, for free, the way love does." 

And she took out a piece of paper, soft and worn from being read four hundred times, and she read my note. My actual note. *To whoever finds this. Inside this packet there are cells from my dog. His name is Scratch.* All of it, to the whole silent room, all the way down to *I'll find the money one day. I'll spend my whole life finding it. I'm really sorry. Thank you for reading this. Bonnie.*

And when she finished, you could have heard a pin drop in that atrium, except for one sound, one small sound, that I will hear for the rest of my life: the sound of grown men and women, hard-nosed investors and cynical reporters, three hundred people who'd come for champagne and miracles, quietly, helplessly, beginning to cry.

Mr Sole gave us all three things. Of course he did. He stood at his lectern, with the cameras on him and his company dying around him, and he announced, in a flat dead voice, that the Ark programme was suspended pending review, and that the dog — "the animal in question" — was "a pet of no scientific significance, and the earlier remarks were aspirational marketing for which the company apologises," and that Lazarus "had no interest of any kind" in me, or my family, or Itch, "now or ever."

He had to say it. To save himself, he had to stand up in front of the world and swear that my dog was nothing special, that there was no miracle in him, that he was just an ordinary greyhound a girl loved.

Which was, of course, the truest thing anybody said all day. And the only thing I'd ever wanted him to understand.

We walked out the front door of the Helix, all of us, into the daylight, with Itch trotting on a lead between us, an ordinary pet of no scientific significance, the most valuable thing in the entire world.

Behind us, the launch was over. Within a month, so was Lazarus Biosciences, and so — I read it in the paper, in a small column near the back — was the career of Mr Damian Sole, who "left the company to pursue other opportunities," which is the thing they write about a man named after the part of a foot that touches the ground when at last it doesn't anymore.

I never saw him again. I think about him sometimes, though. I think he genuinely never understood the thing that beat him. He thought he'd lost to a scientist's evidence, or a clever trick with the screens. He never once realised he'd lost to a dog. To the simple fact of how much a dog can be loved, and how that love, it turns out, is the one thing in the universe you can't put in a freezer, or a machine, or a brochure.

You can only do it. One bit at a time. The way the dog taught us.

We got him home. We got him safe. And then, at last, with the whole truth out and the tin dug up and the family unbroken and the dog asleep in the patch of sun where he belonged, there was only one thing left in the entire world that I still had to do.

I had to find my dad.

## Chapter Thirty-Three — The Number Grandad Had

It turned out Grandad had a number.

I should have known. I think, somewhere, I did know. Because if my dad had told Grandad where the tin was, *give it to her one day, when she's ready,* then my dad must have stayed reachable, mustn't he, all these years — must have left a way, in case the day ever came. And it had. And Grandad had carried it, the way he'd carried everything, alone, up the allotment, in the WORLD'S OKAYEST GRANDAD mug, for six years.

He gave it to me in the shed, the day after the launch, written on the back of a seed packet in his careful old-fashioned hand. A mobile number, and under it, in brackets, the words *(Galloway Greyhound Sanctuary — that's where he is, pet, last I knew. Saving the dogs. Like the one he saved for you.)*

"He sends a card at Christmas," Grandad said, not looking at me, fussing with a leek he didn't need to fuss with. "Every year. Same words near enough. *Tell our Bonnie I think of her every day. Tell her when she's ready.* And every year I've sat with it and thought, is she ready, is *he* ready, am *I* — and every year I've put the card in the tin in my drawer and said nowt, because saying nowt was easier, and because I was frightened of what your mam would — " He stopped. He put the leek down. "I've been a coward twice over, Bonnie. Once in a lane and once every Christmas since. But you're braver than the whole lot of us put together, you've proved that this last month, so here." He pressed the seed packet into my hand and closed my fingers round it, the way he had with the tin, only this time it wasn't a thing buried in the past, it was a thing pointing at the future. "You ring him. You're ready even if none of the rest of us ever will be. Howay. Go on."

*Howay.*

I rang him from the allotment. I don't know why. I think because it was the place with the most of him in it — the apple tree he climbed, the ground where he buried his three best things, the shed where his dad kept his guilt — and because Itch was there, sniffing along the leek bed, my dad's dog made new, and I wanted him near me when I did it.

I sat on an upturned bucket in the gold evening light, the same light as the burial, the same light as the tin, and I dialled the number off the seed packet, and I put the phone to my ear, and it rang twice, and a man's voice said, "Hello?"

And it was him. Older than the tape. Rougher, the gravel further up it now, more of Grandad in it. But him. The voice that said *howay and look what I've got.* The voice that whistled the house to sleep.

"Hello?" he said again. "Who's this?"

And I opened my mouth and nothing came out, seven years of nothing, the whole closed door in the middle of me, and then Itch — who'd heard the voice, tinny and small out of the phone's little speaker, who'd come trotting over and put his head on my knee, ears up, electric, *certain* — Itch lifted his nose to the evening sky and he howled. The joyful one. The rising singing one. The one he sang back to the whistle in Mick's van. He howled down the phone at the sound of my dad's voice, my dad's dog, across death and a machine and six years of silence, singing *you came back, you came back, you came back.*

And there was a silence on the line. A long, cracking-open silence.

And then my dad said, in a whisper, in a voice that had fallen straight through the floor of itself: "...Scratch? That's — I'd know that fella anywhere, that's — but he'd be — " and then, because there is only one person in the entire world that dog would be howling next to, because some sums come out all at once: "...*Bonnie?* Is that — pet, is that *you?*"

"Hi, Dad," I said.

I'd planned a hundred things to say. I'd lain awake building speeches — angry ones, where I made him feel every year of it; cold ones, where I was very grown-up and made him work for it; whole long careful ones, where I explained the tin and the dog and the science. In the end I said none of them. In the end, on an upturned bucket in the gold light with my dead dog howling joy beside me, I said the only true thing, the smallest one, the one that had been sitting under everything for seven years waiting to be let out:

"I kept the door open. Like you said. I kept it open the whole time."

And three hundred miles away, in a sanctuary full of rescued greyhounds in the hills of Galloway, my dad made the sound my grandad made at the funeral, the sound men in my family make about twice in their lives, and through it, breaking, joyful, ruined, he said, "Oh, Bonnie. Oh, my Bonnie. *Good girl.* Oh, you *good girl.* I'm so sorry. I'm so sorry, pet. I never stopped — not for one day — I'm so sorry — "

And we talked. For an hour, on an upturned bucket, until the light went and the blackbirds gave it up and Grandad brought me a cup of tea and a blanket without a word and went and stood by the apple tree to give us the dark. We talked, my dad and me, the first of a thousand talks, the start of the long, slow, unfinishable work of knowing each other, which we are still doing, which we will be doing for the rest of our lives, which is the best work I have ever done.

And before we hung up, he said, "Can I — would it be all right — could I see you? In the flesh? I don't want to push, I've no right to push, I'll take whatever you'll give me and be grateful — but could I — "

"We'll come to you," I said. "Mick's got a van. He'll drive us. I want to see where you are. I want to see the dogs."

A pause. "Who's Mick?"

"He's Mum's fella," I said. "He stays."

Another pause, longer. And then my dad, who understood, who'd spent six years understanding, said softly, "Good. That's — I'm glad, Bonnie. I'm glad somebody stayed. Tell him — " his voice went thick — "tell him thank you. From the one who didn't."

We went up the following Saturday. All of us, in Mick's van, three hundred miles north, to a sanctuary in the hills, to a man who saved dogs because he couldn't save himself in time.

And I brought Itch.

## Chapter Thirty-Four — The Sanctuary

Galloway is the far south-west of Scotland, hills and forest and a cold bright sea, about as far from Kelsey Bay as you can go and still have the same gulls. Mick drove the whole way, six hours, in the rattling van with the cassette slot, and we played my dad's tape on the motorway, all of us, the Fastest Dog in the World and the whistle and *night night, eyes shut,* and nobody said much, and Itch slept on my lap and twitched his legs, running, in his dreams, the way Scratch used to.

Mum came. I'd been frightened about that — Mum, meeting Joe again, after everything — but Mum had said, in the kitchen, the night before, "Bonnie, I made my peace with your dad a long time ago, the wrong way, by deciding he was a villain, and now I've got to make it again, the right way, by deciding he was just a frightened daft lad who loved you and got it all wrong. That's harder. But you don't get to send your bairn three hundred miles to meet her father and not be there. I'm coming. And I'll be civil, and I might even be kind, because — " she'd looked at Mick, who was pretending very hard to read the side of a cereal box — "because I've got everything I need now, haven't I, and a person who's got everything they need can afford to be kind to a person who lost it all. Howay. Pack the dog's lead."

The sanctuary was a long low farm with kennels and paddocks and forty rescued greyhounds dozing in the autumn sun, lean and gentle and worried-looking, every one of them a Scratch, every one of them somebody's washed-up no-longer-fast-enough dog who'd been given, here, a soft place to land. And standing at the gate, in a battered waxed jacket with mud on his boots, twisting a flat cap round and round in his hands the way you twist a thing when your whole heart is in your throat, was my dad.

I'd thought I wouldn't know him. I knew him before the van had stopped. The chin. My chin. The gap-toothed grin from the marrow photo, older now, greyer, but there, breaking across his face like sunrise as he saw me climb down out of the van, and then breaking the other way, into pieces, as the years all landed on him at once.

We didn't run into each other's arms. It wasn't that kind of meeting; real ones never are. We stood about six feet apart in a muddy farmyard, a man and a girl who were father and daughter and total strangers both at once, and we looked at each other, and nobody knew what to do with their hands or their face, and it was awkward, and it was enormous, and it was real.

And then Itch decided it for us.

I'd put him down out of the van, and he'd been overwhelmed at first — forty greyhounds, all those smells — but now he went still. His nose came up. And he found it, on the cold autumn air, the thing he'd been waiting for at a gate every dawn of his life: a scent. A particular scent. The scent off a man in a waxed jacket twisting a cap in a farmyard.

And Itch went off like he'd been fired from a gun.

He didn't walk to my dad. He *flew* — that nine-second beach-flat-out greyhound flight, all folded speed, across the muddy yard — and he hit my dad's legs and went straight up him, crying, that high happy keening, the whole-body joy, spinning, leaning, licking, *cannot-believe-it, you-came-back, you-came-back, YOU CAME BACK,* the dawn the dog never forgot finally, finally come true, the corner finally turned, the waiting finally, after death and a machine and six long years, *over* —

— and my dad dropped to his knees in the mud and caught the dog, this strange impossible puppy he'd never met, and buried his face in his neck, and I heard him say, muffled, broken, baffled, "but you can't — you can't be — he'd be gone by now, this can't be — *Scratch?* — how can you — how do you *know* me, how do you — " 

And I knelt down next to him, my dad, in the mud, with the dog going wild between us, and I told him.

I told him everything — the diagnosis and the break-in and the button and the machine and Dr Lillico and the sea slugs and the deepest groove, the whole mad impossible true story — and he listened with the dog in his arms and the tears running into his stubble, and at the end of it he didn't say *that's impossible,* and he didn't say *that's wonderful.* He looked at the dog, and then he looked at me, and he understood the thing that took me a whole summer and a whole novel to understand, and he said it simpler than I ever could:

"So the dog loved me so much," my dad said slowly, wonderingly, holding the proof of it in his two muddy hands, "that even after he died — even made new, by a machine, by *you* — the loving me came through. It was strong enough to come through." He looked at me, and his face was wide open, more open than any grown-up's face I've ever seen. "And it led you to the tin. To the truth. The dog brought you the truth about me, because the loving was that strong." He gave a wet, wrecked, wondering laugh. "Bonnie. Do you not see what that means? It means I was wrong. The whole time, six years, I told myself the kindest thing was to stay away, to let you forget me, that the love would fade and you'd heal clean." He shook his head, the tears coming freely now. "But love doesn't fade clean. It's not *built* to fade. It's built to come through. It came through a *dead dog,* pet. Through death and a machine. It was always going to find its way back to you. I should have trusted that. I should have just — knocked on the door. The bravest thing was never leaving. It was staying. The dog knew that. The dog always knew that. I'm the only one who had to learn it the hard way."

And then my dad reached out, at last, across the six feet and the six years, with the dog still wild between us, and he put his hand against the side of my face, the way you would with something precious, and he said, "Can I have a go at staying now? I'll be rubbish at it. I've had no practice. But I'd like to start, if you'll let me. I'd like to start today."

And I leaned into his hand. The lean. *I'm here and you're here.*

I got it from the dog.

## Chapter Thirty-Five — The First Sundays

Here is a thing nobody tells you about getting your dad back: it is not like the films, where there's a hug and some music and then the credits roll on a mended family. It is much slower than that, and much harder, and much better, and it is mostly made of Sundays.

Because that's how we did it, in the end. My dad couldn't just move back in — that was never going to happen, and I had to do some growing up to understand why, and it took me a while, and I won't pretend there wasn't a night or two where I shouted at my mum about it and slammed a door, which is allowed, by the way, when you're twelve and your heart is doing something it has never had to do before. He couldn't move back because Mum was with Mick now, and that was real, and good, and Dad had no claim on the life Mum had built out of the wreckage he'd left, and he knew it, and he never once tried to. And the sanctuary was three hundred miles away, and it was his whole life now, the rescued dogs, and you can't ask a man who finally found a way to be useful to throw it away the week he gets a second chance.

So we did Sundays. He came down on the train one weekend a month, at first, and stayed in a B&B on the front run by a woman called Mrs Pearce who does a fearsome breakfast, and on the Sunday he'd come for his dinner at ours, and I went up to Galloway in the school holidays. That was the shape of it. It's still roughly the shape of it. It's not the shape I'd have drawn, aged five, on the step, watching the corner. But it's a real shape, and a real shape that holds is worth a hundred perfect ones that don't.

The first Sunday dinner was the strangest meal of my life, and I have eaten a hot chocolate with a woman who'd just told me she'd accidentally cloned my dog, so that's saying something. Picture it: our little kitchen, the good plates out, Mum doing a roast and being so determinedly normal about it that the normal-ness had its own weather system. My dad, in a shirt, with his hair combed, sitting at the table he used to sit at, holding a knife and fork like he'd forgotten how they worked, looking around at a kitchen that was the same and completely different, with a man named Mick in it, and his own father, and a daughter he was meeting and remembering at the same time, and a dog at his feet who was somehow the dog he'd carried home on a rope a lifetime ago.

Nobody knew what to say. We got through about four minutes on the subject of the trains. Then Mick — daft, kind, watching Mick — put down his fork and said, into the awful silence, "I just want to say, Joe, man to man, that I think it's a canny thing you're doing, coming back, and I'm not — I'm not in your way, like. I'm not trying to be the lass's dad. That's your job. I'm just the feller who fixes the boiler and runs them places. There's no — " he went red — "there's no competition, is what I'm trying to say. There's plenty of room. A bairn can't have too many people in her corner. That's all." And he picked his fork back up.

And my dad looked at Mick for a long moment, this big soft slow man who had stayed when he hadn't, who had kept his daughter and the woman he'd loved safe and warm and fed for two years while he was three hundred miles away saving other people's dogs, and my dad's eyes went bright, and he said, "Aye. Thank you. From the one who — aye. Thank you, Mick." And the two of them did a thing men do instead of crying, which is nod at each other very seriously about nothing, and then both look at the dog, and the terrible silence broke, and the meal became, slowly, just a meal, which was the best thing it could possibly have become.

Dad and Grandad were the hardest, and the quietest, and in the end the most mended. There were no speeches there either. There was just, one afternoon, the two of them up at the allotment, where it had all gone wrong in a lane at dawn six years before, weeding the leek bed, side by side, on their knees in the dirt, not talking, for about three hours. I went up with Itch to bring them a flask and I stood at the gate and watched them for a while before they saw me — two Maguire men, the old one and the less-old one, pulling weeds out of the ground in the same rhythm, shoulder to shoulder, six years of silence going quietly back into the soil where, Grandad says, the only place silence is any use is as something for things to grow out of. They never did have the big conversation, as far as I know. They didn't need to. They had three hours of weeding, which for men like mine is the same as three hours of weeping and saying everything, only with leeks at the end of it. I left the flask on the wall and didn't interrupt.

And Mum and Dad — that one I worried about most, and it turned out to be the one that taught me the most. Because one Sunday, after dinner, I came downstairs and they were in the kitchen, just the two of them, talking low, and I stopped on the stairs, the way you do, the way I'd stopped on the stairs the night I learned about Sole. And they weren't shouting, and they weren't crying, and they weren't falling back in love. They were just — sorting it out. Two grown-ups who'd hurt each other badly a long time ago, saying the true things at last, plainly, with no children to protect anymore because the child had gone and dug up all the truth herself. I heard Mum say, "I'm not angry anymore, Joe. I was, for years, but I gave it back. You can't carry that much for that long, it's too heavy, you have to put it down or it puts you down." And I heard my dad say, "I'm not asking for anything, Donna. I lost the right to ask for things in a lane in 2018. I'm just asking to be let near her. That's all I'll ever ask." And I heard my mum say, "You don't have to ask. She found you herself. She's been asking for *you,* her whole life, through a closed door, and now the door's open and I'd not shut it again for the world. Just — Joe. Don't you dare leave her twice. Whatever else. If you're in, you're *in.* A clean break heals; it's the breaking and breaking again that — " and her voice caught, and my dad said, "I know. Your Wattie said the exact same words to me at the exact same gate. I'll not break her twice. I'd rather die." And then they were quiet, and I crept back up the stairs and let them have it, because some doors you hold open for other people the way they've been held open for you.

As for me and my dad — that's the slowest one, and the one that isn't finished, and won't be for years, and that's exactly right. You don't get a dad back like flicking a switch. You get him back like learning a language: a word at a time, lots of awkward silences, getting it wrong, starting again. There were whole visits, early on, where we barely knew what to say to each other, where I'd be shy and he'd be careful and we'd end up just walking Itch along the front not talking, and I'd go to bed afterwards feeling cheated, like *that wasn't it, that wasn't the big reunion I was owed.* And then slowly I worked out that the walking-not-talking *was* it. That's what having a dad actually is, most of the time. It isn't the big reunion. It's the boring Sundays. It's somebody who'll walk the dog with you in the rain and not need you to perform being happy. The big stuff — the howling in the van, the flying across the farmyard, the *Bonnie, is that you, pet* — that's real, but it's rare, it's the once-or-twice. The thing that actually mends you is the ordinary, the Sundays, the going-on, the staying.

The staying. It always comes back to the staying. I learned it from a dog, and it turns out it's the only lesson there is.

## Chapter Thirty-Six — What Happens When You Clone Your Dog

So. Here's the answer. The one the title's been promising you this whole time. I've earned the right to give it to you straight now, and you've earned the right to hear it, because we've come a long way together, you and me, all the way from a green towel in a vet's back room.

What happens when you clone your dog?

*You do not get your dog back.*

I need you to hear that, the way Dr Lillico needed me to hear it in the Beach Hut café with the cream sliding down the mug. It's the truest and most important thing in the whole book, and it's the thing every grieving person who ever picked up a brochure that said *bring them back* most needs to be told. You don't get them back. You can't. Scratch is under a paving slab at the allotment, between the leek bed and the apple tree, with Mr Ball and his collar and a bit of frying steak for the journey, and no machine on earth will ever undo that, and it would be a lie and a cruelty to pretend otherwise. Death is not optional. Mr Sole was selling a thing that does not exist, and it ruined him, and it deserved to.

What you get — what I got — is not your dog back. It's something stranger, and smaller, and in the end I think bigger.

You get a *new* dog. A real one. His own one, with his own wrong toe and his own splash of white and his own brand-new heart with nothing wrong in it, who will live his own whole life and make his own whole story and is *not* a photocopy, is *not* a forgery, is *not* a way of refusing to say goodbye. Itch is not Scratch. Itch is Itch. He is his own actual dog, and loving him is its own actual thing, and the day I understood that — the day I stopped trying to make him be Scratch and started letting him be himself — was the day he stopped being a ghost in the house and started being a joy in it.

But here's the other half, the half Mr Sole could never have sold because you can't put it in a freezer or a brochure, the half that is, I've decided, the actual answer to the actual question.

What you get, if you're very stubborn and a bit criminal and extremely lucky, is that a little of the old one *comes through.* Not all of it. Not even most of it. Just the deepest grooves — the lean, the fear of the wheelie bin, the inexplicable hatred of a cartoon meerkat, and, deepest of all, the love. The love came through. Across death, across a machine, across everything. Not as a copy. As an *inheritance.* The way your grandad's gravel comes through in your dad's voice. The way my chin turns up in my dad's face and his face. The way my whole family forgives like it's going out of fashion, all of us, down the generations, because somewhere back along the line a dog taught us how and we never stopped.

That's what love does, it turns out. It doesn't photocopy. It *inherits.* It comes through. It finds its way home — through a dead dog, through six buried years, through a girl who wouldn't stop standing in front of the impossible like the sea in front of a cliff. You can't clone a soul. Dr Lillico was right, and Mr Sole was wrong, and the warning at the bottom of that midnight article was the truest line in it. You cannot clone a soul.

But you don't have to.

Because a soul was never a thing you could lose in the first place. It's not in the cells. It's not in the freezer. It's in the *coming through.* It's in the lean, and the door kept open, and the bravest-thing-is-staying, and the dog who runs all the way round death itself because you're the thing he runs towards.

That's what happens when you clone your dog.

You find out you never needed to.

## Epilogue — The Following Spring

It's a year, near enough, since the green towel. Spring again. The funfair's back on the links — I can hear it from my window, the waltzers, the smell of diesel and candyfloss coming up the bank — and there are fireworks promised for Saturday, the last night, the grand finale.

Itch isn't frightened of them.

That's the thing I have to tell you, the last thing, because it's the strangest and the most hopeful and it's exactly what Dr Lillico said would happen, back in her flat full of books and dog hair, the day she explained the rules. *They fade, Bonnie. The borrowed memories fade, as the clone builds his own life, the way grief fades, the way it's supposed to. The new writes over the old. That's not sad. That's the whole point.*

She was right. She usually is. Over this last year, one by one, very gently, the deepest grooves of the old dog have softened in the new one. Itch doesn't wait at the back gate at dawn anymore — partly because the memory's faded, and partly, I think, because there's nothing to wait for now: the man the dog was waiting for isn't a ghost round a corner anymore, he's a real person who rings every Sunday and comes down on the train one weekend a month and is, slowly, terribly, learning to stay. You don't keep watch on a corner once the person's come through it. The waiting's over. For both of us.

And the fear of fireworks is gone, because that was Scratch's, from the track, from the bad old life, and Itch never had a bad old life — Itch got the good recipe with none of the scars, remember — so as the borrowed fear faded, nothing underneath it was frightened, and now my dog sleeps straight through the bangs, sprawled in the patch of sun, legs sticking out like he's been dropped from a height. He's got his own quirks now, that are nobody's but his. He's terrified of the recycling box but fine with the wheelie bin (the opposite of Scratch, which makes Grandad laugh every time). He loves the sea — actually *loves* it, charges into it, where Scratch was scared stiff of the water — and there is nothing, nothing in the whole world, like watching a grey dog who is partly my dead dog and partly my dad's last gift and entirely, gloriously himself, galloping into the North Sea with his daft ears flying, being no one's copy, being only and completely Itch.

The inherited bits aren't all gone. Some never will be, I think. He still does the lean. He still knows the patch of sun. And once, last month, when my dad was down and said *howay* in exactly the old way, soft, two notes, the lift at the end — Itch's ears did the flicker, the lift, the old wired-in thing, and he looked up at my dad with something ancient and adoring in his face, just for a second, before he went back to being a daft young dog chewing a slipper. The deepest groove of all. The love. That one's staying. That one came through to stay.

Mum and Mick are getting married in the autumn. I'm a bridesmaid, which I have complaints about (the dress) and no complaints about (Mick). He moved in at Christmas. He gave me his van keys, that night with the cassette, and asked no questions, and that turned out to be the whole of him, the entire man: the one who hands you the warmth and the quiet and stays at the window keeping watch and never once tries to be something he isn't. There's more than one way to be somebody's father. I know that now. I've got, if you can believe it, slightly more than my fair share of the ways, these days. Some kids get none. I went looking for the one I lost and came home with two. Greedy, Lola says. Lola's the other bridesmaid. We're plotting about the dress.

Grandad won the Leek Show. Eric Stamp came second. I have never seen a human being as happy as my grandad was holding that cup, except possibly my grandad the first time my dad came down on the train and the two of them stood in the allotment not knowing what to say and then just got on with weeding, side by side, in the gold evening light, six years of silence going quietly back into the ground where it could finally rot down into something that grows things. That's where we re-buried the tin, by the way. Empty now — I keep the photo and the tape and the letters and the little silver greyhound on its chain, which I wear every day — but we put the empty tin back under the apple tree, because Grandad said a thing should be allowed to finish its job in the place it did it, and because some day, a long time from now, somebody might dig there and wonder, and I like that. I like leaving a small mystery in the ground for the future. It's a good thing to do with a hole.

And Dr Petra Lillico comes for Sunday dinner once a month. She's got a job at the sanctuary now, my dad's sanctuary, doing the science that actually helps — health-testing the rescue dogs, the good kind of genetics, the kind that mends instead of sells. She's got a dog of her own at last, a three-legged lurcher called Hypothesis. She told me, one Sunday, washing up, not looking at me, that she'd had a dog when she was a girl, a dog she'd loved more than anything, and that he'd died when she was twelve, and that she'd spent her entire life — the whole career, the machine, all of it — secretly, without ever quite admitting it, trying to find a way to bring him back. *And I never could, Bonnie,* she said. *Twenty years and the best machine in the world and I never could. And then a criminal child broke into my lab and did it by accident and proved the only thing worth proving, which is that it can't be done, not really, and that the trying is what eats your life, and that the only way out of grief is straight through the middle of it, one bit at a time.* She dried her hands. *You cured me, you know. You daft, brilliant, law-breaking girl. You're the only successful experiment I ever ran, and I didn't even run you. You ran yourself.*

I don't know about that. I'm not extraordinary. I keep telling people that and they keep not believing me. I just spent a lot of time with a dog, and dogs teach you the important things, if you let them, and the important things turn out to be very simple and very hard: Stay. Lean in. Forgive. Keep watch on the corner, but don't forget to come in for your tea. Run into the sea even though your dad was scared of it, because you're not him, you're you. Love something so much that a piece of it comes through, no matter what. And live one bit at a time, which is the only way anything's ever lived, the only way anything ever can be.

The fireworks are starting. I can hear the first ones going up over the sea.

Itch is asleep in the patch of sun, snoring like a faulty radiator, legs in the air, not frightened of anything, dreaming his own dreams now, running in them — towards what, I'll never know, because they're his, the way Scratch's were his, the way mine are mine.

I'm going to go and lie down on the floor next to him anyway. Not because he needs me to. He doesn't, not for fireworks, not anymore.

Because I want to. Because he's warm, and he's here, and I'm here, and that — it turns out, after everything, after the whole impossible year — that's the entire thing. That's the answer to every question worth asking.

He's here. I'm here.

Howay. Eyes shut.

*The End.*

## The Science Bit

*(I told you not to read this first. If you've read the book, here's the deal I promised you on the very first page: which of the impossible things are real.)*

**Dolly the sheep is real.** In 1996, scientists at the Roslin Institute near Edinburgh made a lamb called Dolly from a single cell taken from an adult sheep — the first mammal ever cloned from an adult body cell. It really did take them 277 tries. Dolly really is stuffed and on display at the National Museum of Scotland, looking faintly embarrassed. You can go and see her.

**Cloning pets is real.** CC the cat ("Copy Cat") was cloned in Texas in 2001. Today, real companies will really clone your dog or cat from a tissue sample for a very large amount of money, and — exactly as in this book — the clone is a genetic twin born later, not a resurrection. The coat patterns really do come out different (CC looked nothing like her donor), because the *exact placement* of markings isn't fully written in the DNA. Acquired scars, like Scratch's torn ear, really wouldn't carry over either. A clone is the recipe baked fresh, with new accidents.

**The memory part is the bit that's (almost) not real — but only almost, and that's the maddening part.** Most scientists are confident that your memories live in the *wiring* of your brain — the connections between cells, built by experience — and so cannot be copied or transferred. By that rule, a clone's mind is a blank, and everything Itch does should be impossible.

*But.* In the 1960s, a scientist named James McConnell really did claim to transfer memory between flatworms by grinding up trained ones and feeding them to untrained ones. Everyone laughed; nobody could repeat it. And then, in 2018, a serious and respected neuroscientist named David Glanzman, at the University of California, really did publish an experiment in which he took RNA from a trained sea slug (*Aplysia*) and injected it into an untrained one — and the untrained slug behaved as if it had learned something that only ever happened to the other animal. It made headlines around the world. It is real, and it is published, and it suggests — to a small, stubborn, mostly-disbelieved corner of science — that *some* trace of *some* kinds of memory might, just possibly, live in molecules after all.

Nobody has ever done what Dr Lillico did. Transferring a memory-residue into a cloned mammal so that the clone "remembers" the original's life is, as far as anyone knows, completely impossible.

*As far as anyone knows.*

That's the gap this whole story climbed through. It's a very small gap. But it's a real one. And I rather think that the difference between a true story and a made-up one is often just exactly that small, and that the people who change the world are usually the ones daft enough — or stubborn enough, or sad enough, or in love enough — to keep standing in front of the gap, like the sea in front of a cliff, until something gives.

Usually it's them that gives.

But not always.

Sometimes it's the cliff.

— B.M.
